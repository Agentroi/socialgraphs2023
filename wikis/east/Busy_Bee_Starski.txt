<p><b>David James Parker</b> (born October 26, 1962), known by the stage name <b>Busy Bee</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Old_school_hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Old school hip hop">old-school hip hop</a> musician from <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York, NY</a>. First coming on the New York City music scene in 1977, Busy Bee worked with many of hip-hop's founding fathers, including <a href="/wiki/Melle_Mel" title="Melle Mel">Melle Mel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afrika_Bambaataa" title="Afrika Bambaataa">Afrika Bambaataa</a>, and Kool DJ AJ.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Busy_Bee_Starski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Known for his comedic rhymes, Busy Bee originally gained a large following through MC rap battles in <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>. He was famously roasted by <a href="/wiki/Kool_Moe_Dee" title="Kool Moe Dee">Kool Moe Dee</a> at Harlem World in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan, NY</a>, December 1981, in one of the earliest documented rap battles.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1985, he won the <a href="/wiki/New_Music_Seminar" title="New Music Seminar">New Music Seminar's</a> MC World Supremacy Belt.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the early 1980s Afrika Bambaataa asked Busy to join his <a href="/wiki/Universal_Zulu_Nation" title="Universal Zulu Nation">Zulu Nation</a> where the young MC would DJ for Bambaataa's Zulu Nation parties.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Busy Bee continues to rhyme today, most recently appearing on <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">KRS-One</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marley_Marl" title="Marley Marl">Marley Marl</a>'s collaborative 2007 album <i><a href="/wiki/Hip-Hop_Lives" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip-Hop Lives">Hip-Hop Lives</a></i>.
</p><p>Busy Bee was featured in the 1983 film <i><a href="/wiki/Wild_Style" title="Wild Style">Wild Style</a></i>, billed as the first hip-hop motion picture, directed by <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Ahearn_(director)" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlie Ahearn (director)">Charlie Ahearn</a>. More recently, he played himself in the 2002 inner-city drama <i><a href="/wiki/Paid_in_Full_(2002_film)" title="Paid in Full (2002 film)">Paid in Full</a></i>. In 2007, he was included in the video documentary <i>Hip Hop Legends</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Thirty years after the song's initial release, in 2018 <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rippberger" title="Robert Rippberger">Robert Rippberger</a> directed the official music video for “Suicide” by Busy Bee, the single off his gold album <i>Running Thangs</i>. The video features a cameo by artist <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Busy_Bee_Starski&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He currently resides in Las Vegas Nevada, with Michelle, his wife of over 20 years. He has two daughters.
</p>
