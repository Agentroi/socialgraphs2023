<p><b>Lakiyra Williams</b>, known professionally as <b>Oompa</b>, is a rapper, poet, and vocalist from <a href="/wiki/Roxbury,_Boston" title="Roxbury, Boston">Roxbury, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oompa_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oompa_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oompa was put into <a href="/wiki/Foster_care" title="Foster care">foster care</a> at six weeks old.<sup id="cite_ref-:16_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She grew up in Academy Homes, a low-income housing development near <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Square_station" title="Jackson Square station">Jackson Square</a>. When she was 5, she and her two biological sisters were adopted.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Oompa started rapping in middle school, competing in <a href="/wiki/Battle_rap" title="Battle rap">battle rap</a> in the cafeteria for lunch money.  As a kid, she played basketball at Washington Park in Roxbury, where she was given the nickname, "Oompa Loompa" because she was short and speedy. This inspired her stage name.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In high school, Oompa's sister, Nicky, died from <a href="/wiki/Lupus" title="Lupus">lupus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:16_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oompa_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oompa attended <a href="/wiki/Bucknell_University" title="Bucknell University">Bucknell University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, on a full ride scholarship.<sup id="cite_ref-:16_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She performed rap and poetry at open mics on campus and taught herself how to professionally record music in her college's computer lab.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:6_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, when she was a freshman in college, Oompa's mom died, which she described in 2019 as "the hardest thing in my life, thus far." Her debut album "November 3rd" was named for the day her mom died, and features the track, "Dear Mama" in dedication. After her mother's death, Oompa took a leave of absence from school to care for her younger sister while she was still a minor. They struggled with food insecurity and homelessness.<sup id="cite_ref-:16_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:6_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Oompa returned to Bucknell later on, changed her <a href="/wiki/Academic_major" title="Academic major">major</a> from <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_education" title="Mathematics education">mathematics</a>, and earned an undergraduate degree in <a href="/wiki/English_studies" title="English studies">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">Education</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:16_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetry_career">Poetry career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oompa_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Poetry career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2016, Oompa won a spot on the Boston House Slam team. The team qualified for finals at the <a href="/wiki/National_Poetry_Slam" title="National Poetry Slam">National Poetry Slam</a>. She also coached a team of youth poets for <a href="/wiki/Louder_Than_a_Bomb" title="Louder Than a Bomb">Louder than a Bomb Massachusetts</a>, who placed 5th in the state. In 2017, Oompa won the <a href="/wiki/Women_of_the_World_Poetry_Slam" title="Women of the World Poetry Slam">Women of the World Poetry Slam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas, Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>She has stated about her experience doing slam poetry, "A lot of slam was competition over who has the most important oppression and how awful things are. The truth is suffering is part of the human condition. We all hurt. It's not a competition."<sup id="cite_ref-:16_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oompa_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2022, Oompa performed at <a href="/wiki/Boston_Calling_Music_Festival" title="Boston Calling Music Festival">Boston Calling Music Festival</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> was accepted into <a href="/wiki/The_Recording_Academy" title="The Recording Academy">The Recording Academy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:16_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and was one of 12 artists selected to be a part of <a href="/wiki/Pepsi" title="Pepsi">Pepsi</a> Music Lab Academy class of 22–23'.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:7_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 2023, Oompa performed at the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Celtics" title="Boston Celtics">Boston Celtics</a> half-time performance at <a href="/wiki/TD_Garden" title="TD Garden">TD Garden</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and at <a href="/wiki/Maura_Healey" title="Maura Healey">Massachusetts Governor Maura Healy</a>'s inauguration.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:14_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:14-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In August 2023, Oompa's song, "Outta Patience", was featured in a <a href="/wiki/Beats_Electronics" title="Beats Electronics">Beats</a> commercial starring <a href="/wiki/Alessia_Russo" title="Alessia Russo">Alessia Russo</a> for the <a href="/wiki/2023_FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup" title="2023 FIFA Women&#39;s World Cup">Women's World Cup</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Oompa has won four <a href="/wiki/Boston_Music_Awards" title="Boston Music Awards">Boston Music Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> She has performed with <a href="/wiki/Jill_Scott_(singer)" title="Jill Scott (singer)">Jill Scott</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/2_Chainz" title="2 Chainz">2-Chainz</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marc_Rebillet" title="Marc Rebillet">Marc Rebillet</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Palehound" title="Palehound">Palehound</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Brandi_Carlile" title="Brandi Carlile">Brandi Carlile</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:14_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:14-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oompa_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oompa has cited <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauryn_Hill" title="Lauryn Hill">Lauryn Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._Cole" title="J. Cole">J. Cole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kendrick_Lamar" title="Kendrick Lamar">Kendrick Lamar</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Brown" title="James Brown">James Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">Gospel music</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Black_music" title="Black music">Black music</a> across genres as her influences.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_acclaim">Critical acclaim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oompa_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical acclaim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2016, Oompa joined HipStory (founded by Cliff Notez and Tim Hall), a Boston collective and multimedia production company that worked with her to record and release her first album, "November 3rd". A review from <i>Sound of Boston</i> called Oompa's rhymes "deadly" and referred to the project as "a deeply confessional collection of songs over smooth soul beats".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The album was listed as one of <i>Allston Pudding's</i> favorite albums of the year and <i><a href="/wiki/DigBoston" title="DigBoston">DigBoston</a>'s</i> Top 30 Local Albums of the year in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:11_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2019, Oompa released the album, "Cleo", named for <a href="/wiki/Queen_Latifah" title="Queen Latifah">Queen Latifah</a>'s character in <a href="/wiki/Set_It_Off_(film)" title="Set It Off (film)">Set It Off</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/WBUR-FM" title="WBUR-FM"><i>WBUR</i></a> commented, that the songs on "Cleo" "all benefit from Oompa's keen sense of melody, her knack for grabbing the ear with a certain phrase or rhythm and telling it something funny and incisive and memorable".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Telegram_%26_Gazette" title="Telegram &amp; Gazette"><i>Telegram</i></a> called the album captivating and viscerally inspiring.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> <i>Allston Pudding</i> attended the album release and called the performance of "Cleo" "powerful from beginning to end".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> With "Cleo", Oompa landed on <a href="/wiki/NPR_Music" title="NPR Music"><i>NPR</i></a>'s Slingshot Artist to Watch list.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2021, Oompa released the album, "Unbothered".<sup id="cite_ref-:9_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Harvard_Crimson" title="The Harvard Crimson">The Harvard Crimson</a></i> characterized the album as, "personal, unapologetic, inspirational, and incredibly deserving of all the glowing receptions it will receive".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <i>Vanyaland</i> called the album, "a sacred space, the Boston's emcee commitment to herself — and her listeners — to cultivate a life worth living".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> <i>Galore Mag</i> hailed Oompa as having "a cadence and energy akin to the legendary <a href="/wiki/Missy_Elliott" title="Missy Elliott">Missy Elliott</a> and the poetic finesse of <a href="/wiki/Noname_(rapper)" title="Noname (rapper)">Noname</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Three-Sixty Mag</i> called "Unbothered", "an album brimming with gems".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Okayplayer" title="Okayplayer">Okayplayer</a></i> named the project to their 15 New Hip-Hop &amp; R&amp;B Albums You Should Be Listening To list, and called the project, "an album riddled with self-reflective undertones and vibrant lyrics".<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_impact">Cultural impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oompa_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cultural impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oompa is on the leadership council at The Record Co., a nonprofit that provides affordable rehearsal and recording space for Boston-based music makers.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Oompa partnered with the <a href="/wiki/Somerville,_Massachusetts" title="Somerville, Massachusetts">Somerville</a> Arts Council to create Black, Brown &amp; Queer Fest.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Gumbo</i> has praised Oompa's music for uplifting women.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Boston_(magazine)" title="Boston (magazine)"><i>Boston</i></a>, in 2022, "she was one of 12 people and organizations awarded $500,000 in workforce development contracts as part of a program established by the city's Mayor's Office of Arts and Culture. She's using the funding to establish Outlaud Entertainment, an artists' collective and incubator that will mentor five emerging artists over the course of fifteen months."<sup id="cite_ref-:16_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oompa_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2023, Oompa reconnected with her birth father, who was a popular R &amp; B singer in the '90s.<sup id="cite_ref-:16_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
