<p><b>Cameron Ezike Giles</b> (born February 4, 1976),<sup id="cite_ref-Curr_Bio_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Curr_Bio-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> better known by his stage name <b>Cam'ron</b>, is an American rapper. Beginning his career in the mid-1990s as <b>Killa Cam</b>, Giles signed to <a href="/wiki/Lance_Rivera" title="Lance Rivera">Lance "Un" Rivera</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lance_Rivera" title="Lance Rivera">Untertainment</a> under the aegis of <a href="/wiki/Epic_Records" title="Epic Records">Epic</a> and released his first two studio albums <i><a href="/wiki/Confessions_of_Fire" title="Confessions of Fire">Confessions of Fire</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/S.D.E._(album)" class="mw-redirect" title="S.D.E. (album)">S.D.E. (Sports Drugs &amp; Entertainment)</a></i> in 1998 and 2000 respectively; the former achieved <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_certification" title="Music recording certification">gold</a> status by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">RIAA</a>. After leaving Epic, Giles signed to <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a> in 2001 and released his third studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Come_Home_with_Me" title="Come Home with Me">Come Home with Me</a></i> the following year; it achieved <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_certification" title="Music recording certification">platinum</a> status by the RIAA, and also contained Cam'ron's highest-charting singles to date; "<a href="/wiki/Oh_Boy_(Cam%27ron_song)" title="Oh Boy (Cam&#39;ron song)">Oh Boy</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Hey_Ma_(Cam%27ron_song)" title="Hey Ma (Cam&#39;ron song)">Hey Ma</a>", which peaked at No. 4 and No. 3 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> respectively. His fourth studio album and final release on Roc-A-Fella, <i><a href="/wiki/Purple_Haze_(album)" title="Purple Haze (album)">Purple Haze</a></i> was released in 2004 to critical acclaim and commercial success, being certified gold by the RIAA.
</p><p>After separating his label, <a href="/wiki/Diplomat_Records" title="Diplomat Records">Diplomat Records</a> from Roc-A-Fella in 2005 due to disagreements between himself and label-head <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, Cam'ron signed the label to a distribution deal with <a href="/wiki/Asylum_Records" title="Asylum Records">Asylum Records</a>. In 2006, Cam'ron released his fifth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Killa_Season" title="Killa Season">Killa Season</a></i>, which contained a <a href="/wiki/Killa_Season_(film)" title="Killa Season (film)">film of the same name</a>, in which Cam'ron made his director-screenwriter debut and starred as the main character. In 2009, after taking a hiatus due to his mother's health, Cam'ron returned to music and released his sixth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Crime_Pays_(Cam%27ron_album)" title="Crime Pays (Cam&#39;ron album)">Crime Pays</a></i>. It reached number 3 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>.
</p><p>Aside from his solo career, Cam'ron is the leader of <a href="/wiki/The_Diplomats" title="The Diplomats">the Diplomats</a> (also known as Dipset), a group he formed in 1997 with his childhood friend <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jones_(rapper)" title="Jim Jones (rapper)">Jim Jones</a> and his cousin <a href="/wiki/Freekey_Zekey" title="Freekey Zekey">Freekey Zekey</a>. He was also one half of the duo <a href="/wiki/U.N._(group)" title="U.N. (group)">U.N.</a> (Us Now), and a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Children_of_the_Corn_(group)" title="Children of the Corn (group)">Children of the Corn</a> before they disbanded in 1997. Giles has also occasionally worked as an actor, starring in the <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella</a> films <i><a href="/wiki/Paper_Soldiers" title="Paper Soldiers">Paper Soldiers</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Paid_in_Full_(2002_film)" title="Paid in Full (2002 film)">Paid in Full</a></i> in 2002.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cam%27ron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1976.E2.80.931997:_Early_life_and_career_beginnings"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1976–1997:_Early_life_and_career_beginnings">1976–1997: Early life and career beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cam%27ron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1976–1997: Early life and career beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Giles was born and raised in the <a href="/wiki/East_Harlem" title="East Harlem">East Harlem</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Manhattan,_New_York_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper Manhattan, New York City">Upper Manhattan, New York City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was raised by his mother, Fredericka Giles (July 10, 1955 – February 9, 2023). He went to school at the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Center_for_Science_and_Mathematics" title="Manhattan Center for Science and Mathematics">Manhattan Center for Science and Mathematics</a>, where he met his longtime friends <a href="/wiki/Mase" title="Mase">Mase</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jones_(rapper)" title="Jim Jones (rapper)">Jim Jones</a>. He was a promising basketball player alongside Mase; however, he was unable to take advantage of scholarship offers due to his poor academic standing. Instead, he enrolled in a college in Texas, without even graduating from high school, but soon dropped out and returned to Harlem where he began selling drugs before starting his rap career. Giles was eventually introduced to <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> through his childhood friend Mase. B.I.G. introduced Giles to <a href="/wiki/Lance_Rivera" title="Lance Rivera">Lance Rivera</a>, who signed him to his label, Untertainment.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He began his musical career in the mid-1990s, rapping alongside <a href="/wiki/Big_L_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Big L (rapper)">Big L</a>, Mase, and his cousin Bloodshed, in a group called <a href="/wiki/Children_of_the_Corn_(group)" title="Children of the Corn (group)">Children of the Corn</a>. After Bloodshed's death in a car accident on March 2, 1997, the group disbanded and the remaining members pursued solo careers.
</p>
<h3><span id="1998.E2.80.932002:_Confessions_of_Fire.2C_S.D.E._and_Come_Home_with_Me"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1998–2002:_Confessions_of_Fire,_S.D.E._and_Come_Home_with_Me">1998–2002: <i>Confessions of Fire</i>, <i>S.D.E.</i> and <i>Come Home with Me</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cam%27ron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1998–2002: Confessions of Fire, S.D.E. and Come Home with Me">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two years before Big L's murder in 1999, Cam'ron was introduced to <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> by Mase who was signed to <a href="/wiki/Bad_Boy_Records" title="Bad Boy Records">Bad Boy Records</a> at the time. Biggie was so impressed by Cam'ron that he introduced him to his partner Lance "Un" Rivera who signed Cam'ron to his Untertainment label, distributed by Epic Records. His debut album, <i>Confessions of Fire</i>, was released a year later in July 1998 and included singles such as "3-5-7" (which was also featured in the movie <a href="/wiki/Woo_(film)" title="Woo (film)">Woo</a>), and "Horse and Carriage" featuring Mase, which reached the R&amp;B Top Ten. The album achieved gold status and made the Top 10 of both the pop and R&amp;B charts.
</p><p>In 2000, Cam'ron was working with music executive <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Mottola" title="Tommy Mottola">Tommy Mottola</a> and released his second album <i><a href="/wiki/S.D.E." title="S.D.E.">S.D.E. (Sports Drugs &amp; Entertainment)</a></i> on Sony/Epic Records. With features from <a href="/wiki/Destiny%27s_Child" title="Destiny&#39;s Child">Destiny's Child</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juelz_Santana" title="Juelz Santana">Juelz Santana</a>, Jim Jones, <a href="/wiki/N.O.R.E." title="N.O.R.E.">N.O.R.E.</a>, and producer Digga, it included the relatively successful singles, "Let Me Know" and "What Means The World To You". The album reached Number 2 on the R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums chart, and Number 14 on the Billboard 200.
</p><p>After demanding a release from Sony/Epic Records, Cam'ron signed with his childhood friend and new manager <a href="/wiki/Damon_Dash" title="Damon Dash">Damon Dash</a> to <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a> in 2001, alongside artists such as <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beanie_Sigel" title="Beanie Sigel">Beanie Sigel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freeway_(rapper)" title="Freeway (rapper)">Freeway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Bleek" title="Memphis Bleek">Memphis Bleek</a>. A reported $4.5 million record deal was agreed upon with and Damon Dash and his Roc-A-Fella partners Kareem Biggs and Jay-Z in the form of a record advance.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> His third and most successful album <i>Come Home with Me</i> was released in 2002 featuring guests such as Jay-Z, <a href="/wiki/Beanie_Sigel" title="Beanie Sigel">Beanie Sigel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Bleek" title="Memphis Bleek">Memphis Bleek</a>, and production from <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Heatmakerz" title="The Heatmakerz">The Heatmakerz</a>. It included the hit singles "<a href="/wiki/Oh_Boy_(Cam%27ron_song)" title="Oh Boy (Cam&#39;ron song)">Oh Boy</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Hey_Ma_(Cam%27ron_song)" title="Hey Ma (Cam&#39;ron song)">Hey Ma</a>", which both featured <a href="/wiki/The_Diplomats" title="The Diplomats">the Diplomats</a> newest member <a href="/wiki/Juelz_Santana" title="Juelz Santana">Juelz Santana</a>. The album achieved platinum status and served as a stepping stone for Cam'ron's group the Diplomats to sign with Roc-A-Fella.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, Cam'ron went on to appear in the <a href="/wiki/Damon_Dash" title="Damon Dash">Damon Dash</a> produced film, <i><a href="/wiki/Paid_in_Full_(2002_film)" title="Paid in Full (2002 film)">Paid in Full</a></i>, in which he played one of three main characters alongside <a href="/wiki/Mekhi_Phifer" title="Mekhi Phifer">Mekhi Phifer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wood_Harris" title="Wood Harris">Wood Harris</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 2006 he started shooting his movie for his album titled <i><a href="/wiki/Killa_Season_(film)" title="Killa Season (film)">Killa Season</a></i>; the film would mark both Cam'ron's screenwriting and directorial debuts, as well as his return to acting. Killa Season was released to DVD on April 25, 2006, after a special two-day theatrical release.<sup id="cite_ref-theatre_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theatre-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2003.E2.80.932009:_Purple_Haze.2C_Killa_Season_and_Crime_Pays"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2003–2009:_Purple_Haze,_Killa_Season_and_Crime_Pays">2003–2009: <i>Purple Haze</i>, <i>Killa Season</i> and <i>Crime Pays</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cam%27ron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2003–2009: Purple Haze, Killa Season and Crime Pays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 2003, Cam'ron teamed up with his fellow Diplomats Members <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jones_(rapper)" title="Jim Jones (rapper)">Jim Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juelz_Santana" title="Juelz Santana">Juelz Santana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Freekey_Zeeky" class="mw-redirect" title="Freekey Zeeky">Freekey Zeeky</a> to release the <a href="/wiki/The_Diplomats" title="The Diplomats">Diplomats</a>' debut double disc album, <i><a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_Immunity_(The_Diplomats_album)" title="Diplomatic Immunity (The Diplomats album)">Diplomatic Immunity</a></i>, under Roc-A-Fella/Diplomat Records, which was quickly certified <a href="/wiki/Oro_album" class="mw-redirect" title="Oro album">gold</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">RIAA</a>. The album featured the lead single "Dipset Anthem", a remix to Cam'rons hit "Hey Ma", and the (street anthem) single "I Really Mean It", as well as featuring production from <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Heatmakerz" title="The Heatmakerz">The Heatmakerz</a>. A year later, the Diplomats released their second album, <i>Diplomatic Immunity 2</i>.
</p><p>On December 7, 2004, Cam'ron's fourth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Purple_Haze_(album)" title="Purple Haze (album)">Purple Haze</a></i>, was released on <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam" class="mw-redirect" title="Def Jam">Def Jam</a>/<a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>. It featured collaborations with Kanye West, <a href="/wiki/Jaheim" title="Jaheim">Jaheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twista" title="Twista">Twista</a>, Juelz Santana, and various other artists and ultimately reached gold status.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The album was also a critical success, being ranked 114th on <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a>'s Top 200 Albums of the first decade of the 21st century List, and 10th on <a href="/wiki/Rhapsody_(online_music_service)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhapsody (online music service)">Rhapsody</a>'s Hip Hop's Best Albums Of The Decade List. However, after feeling that the album was poorly promoted and that his projects were not receiving enough attention, Cam'ron requested his release from <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>.
</p><p>On April 28, 2005, Cam'ron officially joined the <a href="/wiki/Warner_Music_Group" title="Warner Music Group">Warner Music Group</a> under the Asylum Records imprint.<sup id="cite_ref-asylum_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asylum-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He began work on what would be his first project for the new label. Cam'ron's fifth studio album, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Killa_Season" title="Killa Season">Killa Season</a></i>, was released on May 16, 2006, featuring production from long-term collaborators The Heatmakerz, Charlmagne and <a href="/wiki/Ty_Fyffe" title="Ty Fyffe">Ty Fyffe</a>, as well as others such as <a href="/wiki/Alchemist" class="mw-redirect" title="Alchemist">Alchemist</a> and <a href="/wiki/I.N.F.O." title="I.N.F.O.">I.N.F.O.</a> Along with the album, Cam'ron released his first film, in which he wrote, directed and starred in, also titled "Killa Season". Despite selling 112,000 units in the first week and debuting at number 2 on the charts, <i>Killa Season</i> failed to have the same sales strength as his two previous releases, but <i>Killa Season</i> became certified gold.
</p><p>After the release of <i>Killa Season</i> and his feud with <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a> in 2007, Cam'ron took a three-year hiatus from music after his mother suffered three strokes which left her paralyzed on her left side. He moved to Florida with her to set up her rehabilitation and therapy, and stayed there until she had fully recovered.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Cam'ron's 2009 album, <i><a href="/wiki/Crime_Pays_(Cam%27ron_album)" title="Crime Pays (Cam&#39;ron album)">Crime Pays</a></i> was released on Asylum/Diplomat Records, featuring the majority of the production handled by <a href="/wiki/Skitzo" title="Skitzo">Skitzo</a> and <a href="/wiki/AraabMuzik" title="AraabMuzik">AraabMuzik</a>. Although none of the singles from the album managed to chart, the album still reached number 3 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a> but only sold 150,000 units, making it the lowest selling album of his career. In 2009 Cam'ron formed a new label, Dipset West and new group the <a href="/wiki/U.N._(group)" title="U.N. (group)">U.N.</a>
</p>
<h3><span id="2010.E2.80.93present:_Mixtapes.2C_EPs_and_collaborations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2010–present:_Mixtapes,_EPs_and_collaborations">2010–present: Mixtapes, EPs and collaborations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cam%27ron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010–present: Mixtapes, EPs and collaborations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="50_Cent">50 Cent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cam%27ron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 50 Cent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On February 1, 2007, Cam'ron and <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a> had a live argument on <i>The <a href="/wiki/Angie_Martinez" title="Angie Martinez">Angie Martinez</a> Show</i> on <a href="/wiki/WQHT" title="WQHT">Hot 97</a> radio. 50 Cent commented that he felt <a href="/wiki/Koch_Entertainment" title="Koch Entertainment">Koch Entertainment</a> was a "graveyard", meaning major record labels would not work with their artists.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Cam'ron then ridiculed the record sales of <a href="/wiki/G-Unit" title="G-Unit">G-Unit</a> members <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Banks" title="Lloyd Banks">Lloyd Banks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a> by pointing out that <a href="/wiki/The_Diplomats" title="The Diplomats">Dipset</a> member <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jones_(rapper)" title="Jim Jones (rapper)">Jim Jones</a> outsold both of their albums despite not being signed to a major label, and also went on to clarify that his group, The Diplomats, had a distribution deal from several labels.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Both rappers released diss songs with videos on <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>. 50 Cent released "Funeral Music", and suggested in the song that Cam'ron is no longer able to lead The Diplomats and that Jim Jones should take his place. Cam'ron responded with "Curtis" and "Curtis Pt. II", in which he makes fun of 50 Cent's appearance, calling him "a gorilla, with rabbit teeth". 50 Cent responded by releasing "<a href="/wiki/Hold_On_(Young_Buck_song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hold On (Young Buck song)">Hold On</a>" with <a href="/wiki/Young_Buck" title="Young Buck">Young Buck</a>. Since 2009, the feud slowly died down, and they eventually reconciled in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jim_Jones">Jim Jones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cam%27ron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Jim Jones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cam'ron revealed in 2007 that he was no longer speaking to his fellow Diplomat members Juelz Santana and Jim Jones, leading to speculation that the group had officially broken up. However, despite admitting that he did not want to contact Jim Jones, he said that he had no hard feelings towards him. In an interview with Miss Info, Cam'ron said: "I still haven't spoken to Jim. But Jim ran with me for over 10 years, he worked hard, and I wish him the best of luck. Everybody thinks I'm mad at Jim. Why am I mad? I told people for years that Jimmy was gonna be a star. So it's better on my resume. I wish him the best."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> After three years of not speaking, Cam'ron and Jim Jones mended their differences in April 2010. In late 2011, both appeared together on <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Gartner" title="Wolfgang Gartner">Wolfgang Gartner</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Weekend_in_America" title="Weekend in America">Weekend in America</a></i>, on the track "Circus Freaks".
</p>
<h3><span id="Stop_Snitchin.27"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Stop_Snitchin'">Stop Snitchin'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cam%27ron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Stop Snitchin&#039;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On April 22, 2007, Cam'ron was interviewed on <i><a href="/wiki/60_Minutes" title="60 Minutes">60 Minutes</a></i> as part of a piece on the <a href="/wiki/Stop_Snitchin%27" title="Stop Snitchin&#39;">Stop Snitchin'</a> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-60min_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60min-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> He also stated that he would "not help the police" try to locate the shooter saying he is "not a snitch" and helping the police would probably hurt his record sales. He stated in the interview, "Because with the type of business I'm in, it would definitely hurt my business. And the way that I was raised, I just don't do that. I was raised differently, not to tell... It's about business but it's still also a code of ethics" When asked by <a href="/wiki/Anderson_Cooper" title="Anderson Cooper">Anderson Cooper</a> if he would tell the police if a serial killer was living next to him, Cam'ron replied "I would probably move" but would not inform the police.
</p><p>Cam'ron later issued an apology for his comments, calling them an "error in judgement":<sup id="cite_ref-60min_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60min-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> "Where I come from, once word gets out that you've cooperated with the police that only makes you a bigger target of criminal violence. That is a dark reality in so many neighborhoods like mine across America. I'm not saying its right, but its reality. And it's not unfounded. There's a harsh reality around violence and criminal justice in our inner cities." Cam'ron has had contact with the police in the past. According to <a href="/wiki/The_Smoking_Gun" title="The Smoking Gun">The Smoking Gun</a>, New York Police Department records indicate that Giles filed a report with police after he was assaulted at a park in Harlem in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kanye_West">Kanye West</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cam%27ron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Kanye West">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both Cam'ron and Jim Jones took out their frustrations on former label-mate Kanye West in defense of former CEO Dame Dash (due to their longtime friendship dating back to growing up in Harlem)<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> by releasing a song titled "Toast" rhyming over Kanye West's song "<a href="/wiki/Runaway_(Kanye_West_song)" title="Runaway (Kanye West song)">Runaway</a>". The feud eventually ended, evidenced by Cam'ron, Jim Jones, and Kanye West collaborating on a song called "<a href="/wiki/G.O.O.D._Fridays" class="mw-redirect" title="G.O.O.D. Fridays">Christmas in Harlem</a>".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cam%27ron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On October 23, 2005, Cam'ron was leaving a nightclub in Washington D.C., having performed the day before at <a href="/wiki/Howard_University" title="Howard University">Howard University</a>. While stopped at a traffic light at the intersection of New York and New Jersey Avenue shortly after midnight, a passenger of a nearby car threatened Cam'ron to "give up" his 2006 <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini" title="Lamborghini">Lamborghini</a>. Cam'ron resisted, and the man then shot him. Cam'ron was struck at least once as he was holding the steering wheel, but he was able to drive, going the wrong way on streets and flashing his lights, until a fan drove him to Howard University Hospital. The gunman and passenger drove off, crashed into a parked car, and fled the scene. D.C. Metro Police recovered a cell phone from the scene of the crash, which they tried to use to trace the suspects. He stated that he does not know who shot him, although later, in the song "Gotta Love It" featuring <a href="/wiki/Max_B" title="Max B">Max B</a>, Cam'ron claims that he saw the gunman throw up the <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a> diamond hand signal before shots were fired.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
