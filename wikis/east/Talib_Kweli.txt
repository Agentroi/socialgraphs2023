<p><b>Talib Kweli Greene</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span></span><span class="wrap"> </span><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="&#39;w&#39; in &#39;wind&#39;">w</span><span title="/ɑː/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;father&#39;">ɑː</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born October 3, 1975) is an American rapper. He earned recognition early on through his collaboration with fellow <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> rapper <a href="/wiki/Mos_Def" title="Mos Def">Mos Def</a> in 1997, when they formed the group <a href="/wiki/Black_Star_(rap_duo)" title="Black Star (rap duo)">Black Star</a>. Kweli's musical career continued with solo success including collaborations with producers and rappers <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pharrell_Williams" title="Pharrell Williams">Pharrell Williams</a>. His most recent album, <i>Gotham</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> was released in 2020. In 2011, Kweli founded his own record label, Javotti Media.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, Kweli grew up in a household in Brooklyn's <a href="/wiki/Park_Slope" title="Park Slope">Park Slope</a>. His mother, <a href="/wiki/Brenda_M._Greene" title="Brenda M. Greene">Brenda Greene</a>, is an English professor at <a href="/wiki/Medgar_Evers_College" title="Medgar Evers College">Medgar Evers College</a> of the <a href="/wiki/City_University_of_New_York" title="City University of New York">City University of New York</a>, and his father is an administrator at <a href="/wiki/Adelphi_University" title="Adelphi University">Adelphi University</a>. His younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Jamal_Greene" title="Jamal Greene">Jamal Greene</a>, is a professor of constitutional law at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Law_School" title="Columbia Law School">Columbia Law School</a> and a graduate of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yale_Law_School" title="Yale Law School">Yale Law School</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_law_clerks_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States_(Seat_3)" title="List of law clerks of the Supreme Court of the United States (Seat 3)">former clerk</a> to Justice <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Stevens" title="John Paul Stevens">John Paul Stevens</a> on the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Supreme Court">Supreme Court</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>As a youth, Kweli was drawn to <a href="/wiki/Afrocentrism" title="Afrocentrism">Afrocentric</a> rappers, such as <a href="/wiki/De_La_Soul" title="De La Soul">De La Soul</a> and other members of the <a href="/wiki/Native_Tongues_Posse" class="mw-redirect" title="Native Tongues Posse">Native Tongues Posse</a> whom he had met in high school. Kweli was a student at <a href="/wiki/Cheshire_Academy" title="Cheshire Academy">Cheshire Academy</a>, a boarding school in Connecticut. He was previously a student at <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Technical_High_School" title="Brooklyn Technical High School">Brooklyn Technical High School</a>. He later studied <a href="/wiki/Experimental_theater" class="mw-redirect" title="Experimental theater">experimental theater</a> at <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Early_career_.281997.E2.80.932001.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career_(1997–2001)">Early career (1997–2001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early career (1997–2001)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kweli made his debut in 1997, with featured five appearances on <i><a href="/wiki/Doom_(Mood_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Doom (Mood album)">Doom</a></i>, an album by <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>, group <a href="/wiki/Mood_(band)" title="Mood (band)">Mood</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In Cincinnati, Kweli met <a href="/wiki/Hi-Tek" title="Hi-Tek">Hi-Tek</a> and the two collaborated on a few underground recordings as <a href="/wiki/Reflection_Eternal" title="Reflection Eternal">Reflection Eternal</a>, including "Fortified Live" (1997), and "B-Boy Document 99/Chaos" (1999, featuring <a href="/wiki/The_High_%26_Mighty" title="The High &amp; Mighty">The High &amp; Mighty</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Talib_kweli-01-mika.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Talib_kweli-01-mika.jpg/220px-Talib_kweli-01-mika.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Talib_kweli-01-mika.jpg/330px-Talib_kweli-01-mika.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Talib_kweli-01-mika.jpg/440px-Talib_kweli-01-mika.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1326" data-file-height="888" /></a><figcaption>Kweli in 1999, New York City</figcaption></figure>
<h3><span id="Rawkus_Records_collaborative_projects_.281998.E2.80.932000.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rawkus_Records_collaborative_projects_(1998–2000)">Rawkus Records collaborative projects (1998–2000)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rawkus Records collaborative projects (1998–2000)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shortly afterwards, upon returning to New York, he reconnected with <a href="/wiki/Mos_Def" title="Mos Def">Mos Def</a> and formed <a href="/wiki/Black_Star_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Star (group)">Black Star</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Kweli brought along Hi-Tek to produce 1998's <i><a href="/wiki/Mos_Def_%26_Talib_Kweli_Are_Black_Star" title="Mos Def &amp; Talib Kweli Are Black Star">Mos Def &amp; Talib Kweli Are Black Star</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-citypages_October_1998_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-citypages_October_1998-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The album, released amidst a late '90s renaissance of conscious, Afrocentric hip hop, was hailed by critics and achieved modest mainstream success.<sup id="cite_ref-chicagotribune_talib_spot_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chicagotribune_talib_spot-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Kweli and Hi-Tek continued their <a href="/wiki/Reflection_Eternal" title="Reflection Eternal">Reflection Eternal</a> partnership on the 2000 album <i><a href="/wiki/Train_of_Thought_(Reflection_Eternal_album)" title="Train of Thought (Reflection Eternal album)">Train of Thought</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The album was recorded at <a href="/wiki/Electric_Lady_Studios" title="Electric Lady Studios">Electric Lady Studios</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span id="Hip_Hop_For_Respect_.282000.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hip_Hop_For_Respect_(2000)"><i>Hip Hop For Respect</i> (2000)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hip Hop For Respect (2000)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An EP, <i>Hip Hop for Respect</i>, was organized by <a href="/wiki/Mos_Def" title="Mos Def">Mos Def</a> and Kweli to speak out against <a href="/wiki/Police_brutality" title="Police brutality">police brutality</a>, specifically, the case of <a href="/wiki/Amadou_Diallo" class="mw-redirect" title="Amadou Diallo">Amadou Diallo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AM_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AM-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The project released one EP for Rawkus Entertainment.<sup id="cite_ref-AM_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AM-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> On February 4, 1999, Amadou Diallo was shot 41 times by four police officers while reaching into his pocket for his wallet. Diallo was unarmed. The project aimed to assemble 41 emcees to represent the 41 shots fired. Rappers featured on the EP include Owen Brown, Evil Dee, <a href="/wiki/Kool_G._Rap" class="mw-redirect" title="Kool G. Rap">Kool G. Rap</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rah_Digga" title="Rah Digga">Rah Digga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sporty_Thievz" title="Sporty Thievz">Sporty Thievz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shabaam_Sahdeeq" title="Shabaam Sahdeeq">Shabaam Sahdeeq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharoahe_Monch" title="Pharoahe Monch">Pharoahe Monch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kelvin_Mercer" title="Kelvin Mercer">Posdnuos</a>, Donte and Main Flow of <a href="/wiki/Mood_(band)" title="Mood (band)">Mood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nine_(rapper)" title="Nine (rapper)">Nine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiye_Phoenix" class="mw-redirect" title="Tiye Phoenix">Tiye Phoenix</a>, Breezly Brewn' of the Juggaknots, <a href="/wiki/Punchline_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punchline (rapper)">Punchline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imani_Uzuri" title="Imani Uzuri">Imani Uzuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/El-P" title="El-P">El-P</a> and Mr. Len of <a href="/wiki/Company_Flow" title="Company Flow">Company Flow</a>, Jah-Born of Medina Green, <a href="/wiki/John_Fort%C3%A9" title="John Forté">John Forté</a>, Mr. Khaliyl, Fre, <a href="/wiki/J-Live" title="J-Live">J-Live</a>, Rubix, Invincible, <a href="/wiki/Wordsworth_(rapper)" title="Wordsworth (rapper)">Wordsworth</a>, A.L., Kofi Taha, <a href="/wiki/Tame_One" title="Tame One">Tame One</a>, Jane Doe, <a href="/wiki/Grafh" title="Grafh">Grafh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shyheim" title="Shyheim">Shyheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Channel_Live" title="Channel Live">Channel Live</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wise_Intelligent" title="Wise Intelligent">Wise Intelligent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cappadonna" title="Cappadonna">Cappadonna</a>, Crunch Lo, Rock, <a href="/wiki/Nonchalant" title="Nonchalant">Nonchalant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ras_Kass" title="Ras Kass">Ras Kass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dead_Prez" title="Dead Prez">Dead Prez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parrish_Smith" class="mw-redirect" title="Parrish Smith">Parrish Smith</a>. Producers included Mr. Khaliyl, <a href="/wiki/Organized_Noize" title="Organized Noize">Organized Noize</a>, and <a href="/wiki/88-Keys" title="88-Keys">88-Keys</a>.

<h3><span id="Red_Hot_Organization_.282001.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Red_Hot_Organization_(2001)">Red Hot Organization (2001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Red Hot Organization (2001)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, Kweli and Mos Def, contributed to the <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_%2B_Indigo" title="Red Hot + Indigo">Red Hot + Indigo</a></i> compilation album created by the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Organization" title="Red Hot Organization">Red Hot Organization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The compilation was a tribute to <a href="/wiki/Duke_Ellington" title="Duke Ellington">Duke Ellington</a>, that raised money for various charities devoted to increasing AIDS awareness and fighting the disease. Black Star collaborated with fellow artists <a href="/wiki/John_Patton_(musician)" title="John Patton (musician)">John Patton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ron_Carter" title="Ron Carter">Ron Carter</a> to record "Money Jungle". In 2002, Kweli contributed to the critically acclaimed <i>Red Hot + Riot</i>, a compilation CD created by the Red Hot Organization in tribute to the music and work of Nigerian musician <a href="/wiki/Fela_Kuti" title="Fela Kuti">Fela Kuti</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He collaborated with fellow hip hop artists <a href="/wiki/Dead_Prez" title="Dead Prez">Dead Prez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Ben" title="Jorge Ben">Jorge Ben</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bilal_(American_singer)" title="Bilal (American singer)">Bilal</a> to remake the Fela Kuti song "<a href="/wiki/Shuffering_and_Shmiling" title="Shuffering and Shmiling">Shuffering and Shmiling</a>", for the album.
</p>
<h3><span id="Rawkus_Records_solo_albums_.282001.E2.80.932006.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rawkus_Records_solo_albums_(2001–2006)">Rawkus Records solo albums (2001–2006)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rawkus Records solo albums (2001–2006)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Star_-_Ilosaarirock_2012.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Black_Star_-_Ilosaarirock_2012.jpg/220px-Black_Star_-_Ilosaarirock_2012.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="331" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Black_Star_-_Ilosaarirock_2012.jpg/330px-Black_Star_-_Ilosaarirock_2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Black_Star_-_Ilosaarirock_2012.jpg/440px-Black_Star_-_Ilosaarirock_2012.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1630" data-file-height="2454" /></a><figcaption>Kweli performing alongside fellow <a href="/wiki/Black_Star_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Star (group)">Black Star</a> member <a href="/wiki/Mos_Def" title="Mos Def">Mos Def</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>In 2002, Kweli released his first solo album, <i>Quality</i> which featured production from a variety of producers, including <a href="/wiki/DJ_Quik" title="DJ Quik">DJ Quik</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  The album was met with widespread critical acclaim and received some mainstream attention thanks to the West-produced single "Get By" which peaked at No. 77 on the Billboard Hot 100.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <i>Quality</i> peaked at No. 21 on the US Billboard 200 and at No. 6 on the R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums Chart.
</p><p>In February 2004, Kweli featured on Kanye West's "Get 'Em High" off of West's debut album <i><a href="/wiki/The_College_Dropout" title="The College Dropout">The College Dropout</a></i>.
</p><p>Kweli made a total of three appearances on <a href="/wiki/Dave_Chappelle" title="Dave Chappelle">Dave Chappelle</a>'s <i>Chappelle's Show</i>. He was the second musical guest on the show, which aired on January 29, 2003.  For his second appearance, Kweli partnered with Yasiin Bey, as Black Star, for the finale of the show's first season on April 9, 2003. His third and final appearance on the show aired one year later on April 7, 2004.
</p><p>In the summer of 2004, Kweli, along with Bob Moore's Amazing Mongrels, supported the <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie Boys</a> on their "Challah at Your Boy World Tour". That same summer, appeared on a <a href="/wiki/Dilated_Peoples" title="Dilated Peoples">Dilated Peoples</a> song called "Live on Stage". A live remix was later featured on the video game <i><a href="/wiki/NBA_Street_Vol._2" title="NBA Street Vol. 2">NBA Street Vol. 2</a></i>.
</p><p>In the summer of 2004, Kweli also performed at <i><a href="/wiki/Dave_Chappelle%27s_Block_Party" title="Dave Chappelle&#39;s Block Party">Dave Chappelle's Block Party</a>,</i> both as a solo act and as one half of Black Star, and he was later featured in the film and soundtrack.
</p><p>In November 2004, he released his second solo album and final Rawkus release, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beautiful_Struggle" title="The Beautiful Struggle">The Beautiful Struggle</a></i>, which debuted at No. 14 on the Billboard 200.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Kweli responded to <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s 2003 song "Moment of Clarity" in which Jay-Z rapped: "If skills sold, truth be told/I'd probably be, lyrically, Talib Kweli",<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> in his own track, "Ghetto Show" featuring <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Hamilton_(musician)" title="Anthony Hamilton (musician)">Anthony Hamilton</a> by stating "If lyrics sold then truth be told/I'd probably be just as rich and famous as Jay-Z." The album featured much more commercial production, including efforts from <a href="/wiki/The_Neptunes" title="The Neptunes">The Neptunes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 2005, Kweli had appeared in an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Trippin" title="Trippin">Trippin</a></i>, a 2005 <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> environmental documentary television series hosted by <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Diaz" title="Cameron Diaz">Cameron Diaz</a>, also featuring <a href="/wiki/Justin_Timberlake" title="Justin Timberlake">Justin Timberlake</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Fallon" title="Jimmy Fallon">Jimmy Fallon</a>. On the show, said celebrities visit various ecological locales around the world, in particular underprivileged areas of the world. The four of them traveled to <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> to visit one of the world's largest and most thriving ecosystems left on the planet, the <a href="/wiki/Serengeti_Plain" class="mw-redirect" title="Serengeti Plain">Serengeti Plain</a>. There, they had gone into the depths of the Serengeti Plain with a few local zoologists at the time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span id="BlackSmith_Records_.282005.E2.80.932009.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="BlackSmith_Records_(2005–2009)">BlackSmith Records (2005–2009)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: BlackSmith Records (2005–2009)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, Kweli released a <a href="/wiki/Mixtape" title="Mixtape">mixtape</a>-CD with his newly formed <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith_Records" title="Blacksmith Records">Blacksmith Records</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Right_About_Now:_The_Official_Sucka_Free_Mix_CD" title="Right About Now: The Official Sucka Free Mix CD">Right About Now: The Official Sucka Free Mix CD</a></i>, a title which is considered a response to the criticism of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beautiful_Struggle" title="The Beautiful Struggle">The Beautiful Struggle</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-washingtonpost_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Some people regard <i>Right About Now</i> as an album because of its availability through commercial sites and its release of two singles. <i>Right About Now</i> also lacks the DJ overlays often accompanied by mixtapes which makes the CD feel more like an album. The album sold 16,000 copies in its first week of release, debuting at No. 113 on the Billboard 200.<sup id="cite_ref-MTV_November_2005_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV_November_2005-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Sparking some controversy, on <i>Right About Now</i> Kweli sampled <a href="/wiki/Ben_Kweller" title="Ben Kweller">Ben Kweller</a>'s "In Other Words" for his own song "Ms. Hill". In part seven of Kweller's video podcast series "One Minute Pop Song", Kweller said he found Kweli's use of the song "a little fucked up" due to the fact that it was sampled without permission.<sup id="cite_ref-YouTube_=SCX54oh0p2o_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YouTube_=SCX54oh0p2o-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In a similar incident, Kweli reacted with outrage on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> when a verse from "Fly Away" was sampled in Peter Andre's track of the same name. Kweli stated, "I protect the integrity of my music like a grizzly protects cubs. Woe to those who actively stand in the way of that."<sup id="cite_ref-KweliTwitter_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KweliTwitter-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Kweli has not publicly addressed his unauthorized use of Kweller's "In Other Words".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>

album: "Bedroom Lights" and "Black Snake Moan".<sup id="cite_ref-rothstien_December_2009_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rothstien_December_2009-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx_November_2008_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx_November_2008-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2009, it was announced that Kweli would be featured in the <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a>-turned-animated series <i><a href="/wiki/Blokhedz" title="Blokhedz">Blokhedz</a></i> on Missiong.com, voicing the lead part of the character Blak.<sup id="cite_ref-blokhedz_February_2009_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blokhedz_February_2009-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vimeo_5991734_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vimeo_5991734-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, Kweli is a spokesperson and mentor for P'Tones Records a non-profit after school music program whose mission is "to create constructive opportunities for urban youth through no-cost music programs."<sup id="cite_ref-ptonesrecords_Spokespersons_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ptonesrecords_Spokespersons-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kweli and DJ Hi-Tek released a second Reflection Eternal album titled <i><a href="/wiki/Revolutions_Per_Minute_(Reflection_Eternal_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Revolutions Per Minute (Reflection Eternal album)">Revolutions Per Minute</a></i> on May 18, 2010. <i>Revolutions per Minute</i> was recorded at <a href="/wiki/Electric_Lady_Studios" title="Electric Lady Studios">Electric Lady Studios</a>. It is their second album after a 10-year hiatus. The album received generally positive reviews from most music critics, based on an aggregate score of 80/100 from <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Metacritic_Revolutions_Per_Minute_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic_Revolutions_Per_Minute-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Javotti_Media_.282010.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Javotti_Media_(2010–present)">Javotti Media (2010–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Javotti Media (2010–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Gutter_Rainbows" title="Gutter Rainbows">Gutter Rainbows</a></i>, Kweli's fourth solo album, was the first to be released by his newly formed Javotti Media. The album was originally intended to be a digital-only release, however, on November 16, 2010, Duck Down Records announced its plans to offer <i>Gutter Rainbows</i> a CD release outside US.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx_December_2010_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx_December_2010-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> This included an import edition and a special edition with extras.<sup id="cite_ref-MTV_1657237_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV_1657237-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In its first week, the album sold 13,900 copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-xxlmag_February_2011_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmag_February_2011-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In December 2010, Kweli appeared with <a href="/wiki/Darryl_McDaniels" title="Darryl McDaniels">Darryl McDaniels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mix_Master_Mike" title="Mix Master Mike">Mix Master Mike</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ahmet_Zappa" title="Ahmet Zappa">Ahmet Zappa</a> on a cover of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Zappa" title="Frank Zappa">Frank Zappa</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Willie_the_Pimp" title="Willie the Pimp">Willie the Pimp</a>" for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Frank_Zappa_AAAFNRAAAA_Birthday_Bundle_2010" title="The Frank Zappa AAAFNRAAAA Birthday Bundle 2010">The Frank Zappa AAAFNRAAAA Birthday Bundle 2010</a></i>.
</p><p>In 2011, Kweli was featured on the soundtrack for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Beat_the_World" title="Beat the World">Beat the World</a></i> with the song "Infinite Love".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kweli's fifth solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_Conscious" title="Prisoner of Conscious">Prisoner of Conscious</a></i>, earned its title because of Kweli's constant labeling as a "<a href="/wiki/Conscious_hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Conscious hip hop">conscious rapper</a>." It also pays tribute to <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigerian</a> reggae artist <a href="/wiki/Majek_Fashek" title="Majek Fashek">Majek Fashek</a>'s album <i>Prisoner of Conscience</i>. Before the release of <a href="/wiki/Gutter_Rainbows" title="Gutter Rainbows">Gutter Rainbows</a>, Kweli began working on <i>P.O.C.</i>, and put cuts originally intended for latter on <i>Rainbows</i>. Upon its May 7, 2013, release <i>Prisoner of Conscious</i> was met with generally favorable reviews from music critics, and debuted at number 48 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The album featured guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Nelly" title="Nelly">Nelly</a>, Miguel, <a href="/wiki/Currensy" title="Currensy">Currensy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kendrick_Lamar" title="Kendrick Lamar">Kendrick Lamar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>. With production by <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._Cole" title="J. Cole">J. Cole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oh_No_(musician)" title="Oh No (musician)">Oh No</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Fraud" title="Harry Fraud">Harry Fraud</a>, and others. The selection of the tracks on this album were more experimental and worked towards illuminating musicality. Kweli went on to release music videos for "Hamster Wheel", "Upper Echelon", and "Come Here" and more.
</p><p>In August 2013, Kweli announced that in 2014 he would release his next studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Gravitas_(Talib_Kweli_album)" title="Gravitas (Talib Kweli album)">Gravitas</a></i>. The album included guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Thought" title="Black Thought">Black Thought</a>, and The Under Achievers with production by OhNo, <a href="/wiki/Statik_Selektah" title="Statik Selektah">Statik Selektah</a>, and <a href="/wiki/J_Dilla" title="J Dilla">J Dilla</a>.
</p><p>In the summer of 2015, Kweli released another free album entitled Fuck The Money featuring guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Fall_Out_Boy" title="Fall Out Boy">Fall Out Boy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Stump" title="Patrick Stump">Patrick Stump</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miguel_(singer)" title="Miguel (singer)">Miguel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Styles_P" title="Styles P">Styles P</a> and others.
</p><p>In November 2016, he released a compilation mixtape with to promote Javotti Media's developing artists, <i>Awful People Are Great at Parties</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>2017 saw two releases from Kweli; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seven_(EP)" title="The Seven (EP)">The Seven</a>,</i><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> a joint effort with <a href="/wiki/Styles_P" title="Styles P">Styles P</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Lox" title="The Lox">The Lox</a> in April and his eighth solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Radio_Silence_(Talib_Kweli_album)" title="Radio Silence (Talib Kweli album)">Radio Silence</a></i> in November.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kweli published his memoir, <i>Vibrate Higher: A Rap Story</i>, in 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Partnering with Uproxx in June 2019, Talib Kweli launched a weekly podcast called The People's Party with co-host Jasmin Leigh. The show has aired over one hundred episodes to date. Guests include <a href="/wiki/DMX" title="DMX">DMX</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Game_(rapper)" title="The Game (rapper)">The Game</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 2020 COVID-19 Pandemic, Talib teamed up with former collaborator <a href="/wiki/Mos_Def" title="Mos Def">Yasiin Bey</a> and longtime friend <a href="/wiki/Dave_Chappelle" title="Dave Chappelle">Dave Chappelle</a> to work on Talib's second podcast venture, The Midnight Miracle.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He also confirmed that he plans to reunite with Madlib to offer <i>Liberation 2</i> and Mos Def for a sophomore <a href="/wiki/Black_Star_(rap_duo)" title="Black Star (rap duo)">Black Star</a> release.<sup id="cite_ref-social_entertainment_msn_January_2011_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-social_entertainment_msn_January_2011-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Javotti_Media">Javotti Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Javotti Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2011, Kweli founded Javotti Media, an independent label. Signed artists have included Cory Mo, Jessica Care Moore, K'Valentine and Brazilian MC <a href="/wiki/Niko_Is" title="Niko Is">Niko Is</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2014, Kweli's former collaborator, <a href="/wiki/Res_(singer)" title="Res (singer)">Res</a> was offering a song featuring Kweli, without his permission, in exchange for donations from fans on a <a href="/wiki/Crowdfunding" title="Crowdfunding">crowdfunding</a> page. In 2018, Res posted screenshots of Kweli apologizing for attempting to kiss her to her <a href="/wiki/Instagram" title="Instagram">Instagram</a> account. She also claimed Kweli held her career hostage for rejecting his sexual advances.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The case was ultimately dismissed by Judge Carolyn Wade.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kweli was accused of harassing <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> user Maya Moody in July 2020 after Moody intended to call out Talib, along with other popular figures in music, for only dating or marrying <a href="/wiki/Light-skinned" class="mw-redirect" title="Light-skinned">light-skinned</a> black women. Moody tweeted a message that did not specifically mention  Kweli by name.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>  Kweli later announced on  Instagram that he was leaving Twitter "for the green pastures of <a href="/wiki/Patreon" title="Patreon">Patreon</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> A spokesperson for Twitter stated that Kweli's account had been permanently suspended "after repeated violations of the Twitter rules."<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_activism">Politics and activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Politics and activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Views">Views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kweli is known for his strong political views and activism, specifically on topics of racial stereotypes and police brutality.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_2_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_2-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He is an advocate on behalf of political prisoners and a supporter of community organizations like the Malcolm X Grassroots Movement. He has spoken often to inner-city high school and college students.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_3_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_3-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kweli urged people to ask hip-hop artists questions in order to engage them politically, but warned not to get frustrated if artists do not "give you what you want" in their replies. He said that musicians bear an unfair burden to use their music as a platform because they have the ability to influence people. Kweli mentions that artists, just like their audience, fall victim to their upbringing.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_6_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_6-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> In 2017, rapper <a href="/wiki/Remedy_(rapper)" title="Remedy (rapper)">Remedy</a> and Kweli feuded on Twitter regarding Remedy's pro-Israel political stance, which conflicts with Kweli's criticism of <a href="/wiki/Israeli-occupied_territories" title="Israeli-occupied territories">Israel's occupation of Palestine</a>, and his criticism of <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">Zionism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i>, Kweli stated that young people can make a difference because they have the energy to actually go out to the public and do something. He has also stated his belief that activism cannot be done by just sitting at a computer.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_4_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_4-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 1, 2019, Kweli was disinvited from the Open Source Festival in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> because of his support for <a href="/wiki/Boycott,_Divestment_and_Sanctions" title="Boycott, Divestment and Sanctions">BDS</a> which has been classified as <a href="/wiki/Antisemitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Antisemitic">antisemitic</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">German parliament</a> and his tour of Germany was cancelled.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Kweli responded to this on his Facebook page, where he stated, "I would like to perform in Germany but I don't need to. I'd rather be a decent human being and stand up for what's right than censor myself and lie about BDS for a check."<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Activism">Activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Talib_Kweli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with Mos Def, Kweli organized the <i>Hip Hop for Respect</i> CD and video in 2000, which spoke out against police brutality and specifically about the death of <a href="/wiki/Amadou_Diallo" class="mw-redirect" title="Amadou Diallo">Amadou Diallo</a> at the hands of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_police" class="mw-redirect" title="New York City police">New York City police</a>. Profits from the CD were given to the Hip Hop For Respect Foundation, a nonprofit organization that encouraged celebrities to become involved with their fans. The Hip Hop For Respect contained 41 different artists for each shot that the police took at Diallo.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_7_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_7-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2005, Kweli and Mos Def gathered with supporters at City Hall in New York to demand that the federal government drop the million dollar bounty that was placed on political activist <a href="/wiki/Assata_Shakur" title="Assata Shakur">Assata Shakur</a> and remove her from the <a href="/wiki/Terrorist_Screening_Database" title="Terrorist Screening Database">terrorist watch list</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_5_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_5-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kweli visited the <a href="/wiki/Occupy_Wall_Street" title="Occupy Wall Street">Occupy Wall Street</a> camp in October 2011 to show support for the protestors.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_8_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_8-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 1, 2012, Kweli spoke at a rally at city hall to urge the NYPD police to end their <a href="/wiki/Stop-and-frisk" class="mw-redirect" title="Stop-and-frisk">stop-and-frisk</a> policy.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_9_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_9-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 7, 2013, Kweli traveled to Tallahassee to spend a night in the capital building with the Dream Defenders, a group of students that created a sit-in at the Florida governor's office to protest the state's <a href="/wiki/Stand-your-ground_law" title="Stand-your-ground law">stand-your-ground law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_10_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_10-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 19, 2014, Kweli traveled to <a href="/wiki/Ferguson,_Missouri" title="Ferguson, Missouri">Ferguson, Missouri</a>, to join the community in protesting the <a href="/wiki/Shooting_of_Michael_Brown" class="mw-redirect" title="Shooting of Michael Brown">shooting of Michael Brown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_11_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_11-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kweli joined South African artist <a href="/wiki/Cassper_Nyovest" title="Cassper Nyovest">Cassper Nyovest</a> on a new <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remix</a> of his single "<a href="/wiki/Doc_Shebeleza_(song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Doc Shebeleza (song)">Doc Shebeleza</a>", a tribute to the <a href="/wiki/Kwaito" title="Kwaito">Kwaito</a> music icon of the same name. The original appeared on <a href="/wiki/Cassper_Nyovest" title="Cassper Nyovest">Nyovest</a>'s album <a href="/wiki/Tsholofelo_(album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tsholofelo (album)">Tsholofelo</a>. The remix was produced by <a href="/wiki/Anatii" title="Anatii">Anatii</a> and was released on November 3, 2014, as a free <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">digital download</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_Kweli_12_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Talib_Kweli_12-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, Kweli held two concerts to mark the one year anniversary of Michael Brown's death. The line up for the benefit show "Ferguson is Everywhere" featured <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bun_B" title="Bun B">Bun B</a>, M1 from <a href="/wiki/Dead_Prez" title="Dead Prez">Dead Prez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rapsody" title="Rapsody">Rapsody</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tef_Poe" title="Tef Poe">Tef Poe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kendra_Ross" title="Kendra Ross">Kendra Ross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rebel_Diaz" title="Rebel Diaz">Rebel Diaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jasiri_X" title="Jasiri X">Jasiri X</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immortal_Technique" title="Immortal Technique">Immortal Technique</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pharoahe_Monch" title="Pharoahe Monch">Pharoahe Monch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
