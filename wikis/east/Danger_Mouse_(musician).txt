<p><b>Brian Joseph Burton</b> (born July 29, 1977), known professionally as <b>Danger Mouse</b>, is an American musician and record producer. He came to prominence in 2004 when he released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grey_Album" title="The Grey Album">The Grey Album</a></i>, which combined vocal performances from <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Black_Album_(Jay-Z)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Black Album (Jay-Z)">The Black Album</a></i> with instrumentals from the Beatles' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beatles_(album)" title="The Beatles (album)">The Beatles</a></i>, also known as <i>The White Album</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 2008, <i>Esquire</i> named him one of the "75 most influential people of the 21st century".
</p><p>He formed <a href="/wiki/Gnarls_Barkley" title="Gnarls Barkley">Gnarls Barkley</a> with <a href="/wiki/CeeLo_Green" title="CeeLo Green">CeeLo Green</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and produced its albums <i><a href="/wiki/St._Elsewhere_(album)" title="St. Elsewhere (album)">St. Elsewhere</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Odd_Couple_(album)" title="The Odd Couple (album)">The Odd Couple</a></i>. In 2009, he collaborated with <a href="/wiki/James_Mercer_(musician)" title="James Mercer (musician)">James Mercer</a> of the indie rock band <a href="/wiki/The_Shins" title="The Shins">The Shins</a> to form the band <a href="/wiki/Broken_Bells" title="Broken Bells">Broken Bells</a>; the band released three albums since then, with <a href="/wiki/Broken_Bells" title="Broken Bells"><i>Into the Blue (2022)</i></a> being the most recent one. Burton collaborated with rapper <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Dumile" class="mw-redirect" title="Daniel Dumile">MF Doom</a> as <a href="/wiki/Danger_Doom" title="Danger Doom">Danger Doom</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mouse_and_the_Mask" title="The Mouse and the Mask">The Mouse and the Mask</a></i> and with emcee <a href="/wiki/Black_Thought" title="Black Thought">Black Thought</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/Cheat_Codes_(album)" title="Cheat Codes (album)">Cheat Codes</a></i>.
</p><p>As a producer Danger Mouse produced the second <a href="/wiki/Gorillaz" title="Gorillaz">Gorillaz</a> album, 2005's <i><a href="/wiki/Demon_Days" title="Demon Days">Demon Days</a></i>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Beck" title="Beck">Beck</a>'s 2008 record <i><a href="/wiki/Modern_Guilt" title="Modern Guilt">Modern Guilt</a></i> and four albums with <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Keys" title="The Black Keys">The Black Keys</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Attack_%26_Release" title="Attack &amp; Release">Attack &amp; Release</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_(The_Black_Keys_album)" title="Brothers (The Black Keys album)">Brothers</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/El_Camino_(The_Black_Keys_album)" title="El Camino (The Black Keys album)">El Camino</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Turn_Blue_(album)" title="Turn Blue (album)">Turn Blue</a></i>). In 2016, Danger Mouse produced, performed on and co-wrote songs for the eleventh studio album by the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers" title="Red Hot Chili Peppers">Red Hot Chili Peppers</a> titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Getaway_(Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers_album)" title="The Getaway (Red Hot Chili Peppers album)">The Getaway</a></i>. Danger Mouse has also produced and co-written albums by <a href="/wiki/Norah_Jones" title="Norah Jones">Norah Jones</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Little_Broken_Hearts" title="Little Broken Hearts">Little Broken Hearts</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Electric_Guest" title="Electric Guest">Electric Guest</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Mondo_(album)" title="Mondo (album)">Mondo</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Portugal._The_Man" title="Portugal. The Man">Portugal. The Man</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Evil_Friends" title="Evil Friends">Evil Friends</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Adele" title="Adele">Adele</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/25_(Adele_album)" title="25 (Adele album)">25</a></i>), and <a href="/wiki/ASAP_Rocky" title="ASAP Rocky">ASAP Rocky</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/At.Long.Last.ASAP" class="mw-redirect" title="At.Long.Last.ASAP">At.Long.Last.ASAP</a></i>). He has been nominated for 22 <a href="/wiki/Grammy_award" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammy award">Grammy Awards</a> and has won six. Danger Mouse has been nominated in the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Producer_of_the_Year,_Non-Classical" title="Grammy Award for Producer of the Year, Non-Classical">Producer of the Year</a> category five times, and won the award in 2011.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_music_career_beginnings">Early life and music career beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danger_Mouse_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and music career beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brian Joseph Burton was born on July 29, 1977 in <a href="/wiki/White_Plains,_New_York" title="White Plains, New York">White Plains, New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He spent much of his childhood in <a href="/wiki/Spring_Valley,_New_York" title="Spring Valley, New York">Spring Valley</a>, New York. Burton moved to <a href="/wiki/Stone_Mountain,_Georgia" title="Stone Mountain, Georgia">Stone Mountain, Georgia</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, where he attended and graduated from Redan High School.<sup id="cite_ref-danger_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-danger-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He lived in <a href="/wiki/Athens,_Georgia" title="Athens, Georgia">Athens, Georgia</a>, where he pursued a degree in telecommunications at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Georgia" title="University of Georgia">University of Georgia</a> on scholarship,<sup id="cite_ref-danger_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-danger-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mouse_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mouse-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and where his <a href="/wiki/Trip_hop" title="Trip hop">trip hop</a> works (<i>The Chilling Effect</i> (1999), <i>Rhode Island</i> (2000), and <i>Pelican City / Scanner - Pelican City vs. Scanner</i> (2002)) were released while he was a student.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> While at the University of Georgia he was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Nirvana_(band)" title="Nirvana (band)">Nirvana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Portishead_(band)" title="Portishead (band)">Portishead</a>, and came to know the <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">indie rock</a> scene in Athens,<sup id="cite_ref-danger_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-danger-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> remixed work by several local artists, including <a href="/wiki/Neutral_Milk_Hotel" title="Neutral Milk Hotel">Neutral Milk Hotel</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and DJ'd for University of Georgia radio station <a href="/wiki/WUOG-FM" class="mw-redirect" title="WUOG-FM">WUOG-FM</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mouse_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mouse-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> From 1998 to 2003, Burton created a series of remix CDs and records under the stage name Danger Mouse. He performed in a mouse outfit because he was too shy to show his face, and took his name from the English cartoon series <i><a href="/wiki/Danger_Mouse_(1981_TV_series)" title="Danger Mouse (1981 TV series)">Danger Mouse</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danger_Mouse_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While in Athens, Burton took second place in a 1998 talent contest and was asked to open for a concert at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Georgia" title="University of Georgia">University of Georgia</a> featuring <a href="/wiki/OutKast" class="mw-redirect" title="OutKast">OutKast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goodie_Mob" title="Goodie Mob">Goodie Mob</a>. Afterwards, Burton claims he approached CeeLo Green, a member of Goodie Mob, and gave him an instrumental demo tape. CeeLo has no recollection of this and the instrumental demo tape's existence has never been confirmed. Trey Reames who had promoted CeeLo's first solo shows in Georgia, moved to London and became Burton's first manager. He suggested and negotiated CeeLo's appearance on the 26' Remix of the Danger Mouse and Jemini record. When Burton met CeeLo for this project, he shared some beats which led them to collaborate as Gnarls Barkley.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2001.E2.80.932004"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2001–2004">2001–2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danger_Mouse_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2001–2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Burton moved to Britain for a couple of years, living in <a href="/wiki/New_Cross" title="New Cross">New Cross</a> in London and working at the Rose pub<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  near London Bridge. While there, he sent a demo to <a href="/wiki/Lex_Records" title="Lex Records">Lex Records</a>, which signed him. Burton relocated to Los Angeles; his first original releases under the name Danger Mouse were his collaborations with <a href="/wiki/Rapper" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapper">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/Jemini_the_Gifted_One" title="Jemini the Gifted One">Jemini</a>, including the album <i><a href="/wiki/Ghetto_Pop_Life" title="Ghetto Pop Life">Ghetto Pop Life</a></i>, released in 2003 on Lex Records. The Danger Mouse debut was well received by critics, but he did not rise to fame until he created <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grey_Album" title="The Grey Album">The Grey Album</a></i>, mixing <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a> versions of <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Black_Album_(Jay-Z)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Black Album (Jay-Z)">The Black Album</a></i> over beats crafted from samples of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>' <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles_(album)" title="The Beatles (album)">eponymous album</a>, also known as <i>The White Album</i>. The remix album, originally created just for his friends, spread over the Internet and became very popular with both the general audience and critics, with <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> calling it the ultimate remix record<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> ranking it the best record of that year.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He discussed his feelings about any controversy the album may have created in the documentary <i>Alternative Freedom</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Danger Mouse was named among the Men of the Year by <i><a href="/wiki/GQ" title="GQ">GQ</a></i> in 2004 and won a 2005 <a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)"><i>Wired</i></a> Rave Award.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2004.E2.80.932007"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2004–2007">2004–2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danger_Mouse_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2004–2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Grey Album</i> got the attention of <a href="/wiki/Damon_Albarn" title="Damon Albarn">Damon Albarn</a>, who enlisted Danger Mouse to produce <a href="/wiki/Gorillaz" title="Gorillaz">Gorillaz</a>' second studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Demon_Days" title="Demon Days">Demon Days</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <i>Demon Days</i> earned Burton a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> nomination for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Producer_of_the_Year,_Non-Classical" title="Grammy Award for Producer of the Year, Non-Classical">Producer of the Year</a>.
</p><p>Danger Mouse's next project was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mouse_and_the_Mask" title="The Mouse and the Mask">The Mouse and the Mask</a></i>, a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/MF_Doom" title="MF Doom">MF Doom</a> (as <a href="/wiki/Danger_Doom" title="Danger Doom">Danger Doom</a>) about and for <a href="/wiki/Cartoon_Network" title="Cartoon Network">Cartoon Network</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Adult_Swim" title="Adult Swim">Adult Swim</a>. The two had previously collaborated on the Danger Mouse remix of <a href="/wiki/Zero_7" title="Zero 7">Zero 7</a>'s "Somersault", on the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Po" title="Prince Po">Prince Po</a> track "Social Distortion", and on <a href="/wiki/Gorillaz" title="Gorillaz">Gorillaz</a>' "November Has Come". A year later, Danger Doom released a follow-up EP called <i><a href="/wiki/Occult_Hymn" title="Occult Hymn">Occult Hymn</a></i>. The 7-track EP featured new songs as well as remixes of tracks from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mouse_%26_The_Mask" class="mw-redirect" title="The Mouse &amp; The Mask">The Mouse &amp; The Mask</a></i> and was released as a free download on Adult Swim's site.
</p><p>In 2006, Danger Mouse and CeeLo as <a href="/wiki/Gnarls_Barkley" title="Gnarls Barkley">Gnarls Barkley</a> released their first album, <i><a href="/wiki/St._Elsewhere_(album)" title="St. Elsewhere (album)">St. Elsewhere</a></i>, which included the international hit single "<a href="/wiki/Crazy_(Gnarls_Barkley_song)" title="Crazy (Gnarls Barkley song)">Crazy</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> "Crazy" became the first <a href="/wiki/List_of_number-one_singles_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="List of number-one singles (UK)">UK number-one single</a> based solely on <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">downloads</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Gnarls Barkley set out on tour and was one of the main opening acts on the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers" title="Red Hot Chili Peppers">Red Hot Chili Peppers</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Stadium_Arcadium_World_Tour" title="Stadium Arcadium World Tour">Stadium Arcadium World Tour</a></i>. The Gnarls Barkley touring lineup featured future Chili Peppers guitarist, <a href="/wiki/Josh_Klinghoffer" title="Josh Klinghoffer">Josh Klinghoffer</a>. He produced two tracks on <a href="/wiki/The_Rapture_(band)" title="The Rapture (band)">The Rapture</a>'s 2006 album <i><a href="/wiki/Pieces_of_the_People_We_Love" title="Pieces of the People We Love">Pieces of the People We Love</a></i>. In the autumn of 2006, Sparklehorse released his fourth album, <i><a href="/wiki/Dreamt_for_Light_Years_in_the_Belly_of_a_Mountain" title="Dreamt for Light Years in the Belly of a Mountain">Dreamt for Light Years in the Belly of a Mountain</a></i>, a collaboration with Danger Mouse and Steven Drozd of The Flaming Lips. In August and September 2006, Danger Mouse collaborated with British graffiti artist <a href="/wiki/Banksy" title="Banksy">Banksy</a> to replace 500 copies of <a href="/wiki/Paris_Hilton" title="Paris Hilton">Paris Hilton</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Paris_(Paris_Hilton_album)" title="Paris (Paris Hilton album)">Paris</a></i> in British music stores with altered album artwork and a 40-minute instrumental song containing various statements she had made.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2007.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2007–present">2007–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danger_Mouse_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2007–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Gnarls_Barkley.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Gnarls_Barkley.jpg/220px-Gnarls_Barkley.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Gnarls_Barkley.jpg/330px-Gnarls_Barkley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Gnarls_Barkley.jpg/440px-Gnarls_Barkley.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="893" /></a><figcaption>Danger Mouse (left) and CeeLo Green performing as <a href="/wiki/Gnarls_Barkley" title="Gnarls Barkley">Gnarls Barkley</a> in 2007</figcaption></figure>
<p>In January 2007, Danger Mouse produced another collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Damon_Albarn" title="Damon Albarn">Damon Albarn</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Good,_the_Bad_%26_the_Queen_(album)" title="The Good, the Bad &amp; the Queen (album)">The Good, the Bad &amp; the Queen</a></i>, along with <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">The Clash</a> bassist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Simonon" title="Paul Simonon">Paul Simonon</a>, former <a href="/wiki/The_Verve" title="The Verve">The Verve</a> guitarist <a href="/wiki/Simon_Tong" title="Simon Tong">Simon Tong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afrobeat" title="Afrobeat">afrobeat</a> pioneer and Africa 70 drummer <a href="/wiki/Tony_Allen_(musician)" title="Tony Allen (musician)">Tony Allen</a>.
</p><p>In March 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Odd_Couple_(album)" title="The Odd Couple (album)">The Odd Couple</a></i>, the second album of his and CeeLo Green's Gnarls Barkley project, was released. In May 2008, an album with <a href="/wiki/Martina_Topley-Bird" title="Martina Topley-Bird">Martina Topley-Bird</a>, titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blue_God" title="The Blue God">The Blue God</a></i>, was released. Martina collaborated on "All Alone", one song on the Danger Mouse produced <a href="/wiki/Gorillaz" title="Gorillaz">Gorillaz</a> second LP, <i><a href="/wiki/Demon_Days" title="Demon Days">Demon Days</a></i>. Also released in May 2008 was <i><a href="/wiki/Replica_Sun_Machine" title="Replica Sun Machine">Replica Sun Machine</a></i>, an album with the band <a href="/wiki/The_Shortwave_Set" title="The Shortwave Set">The Shortwave Set</a>, including a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Van_Dyke_Parks" title="Van Dyke Parks">Van Dyke Parks</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Velvet_Underground" class="mw-redirect" title="Velvet Underground">Velvet Underground</a>'s <a href="/wiki/John_Cale" title="John Cale">John Cale</a>, according to British music magazine <i>New Musical Express</i>.
</p><p>Upcoming releases include a follow-up to <i><a href="/wiki/Ghetto_Pop_Life" title="Ghetto Pop Life">Ghetto Pop Life</a></i> entitled <i>Kill Your Heroes</i>. It was scheduled to be released in summer of 2006, but its release was pushed back to an undetermined date. He was also working on an album with <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Keys" title="The Black Keys">The Black Keys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ike_Turner" title="Ike Turner">Ike Turner</a>. Turner's death was expected to cancel the album, but The Black Keys and Danger Mouse released <i><a href="/wiki/Attack_%26_Release" title="Attack &amp; Release">Attack &amp; Release</a></i> in April 2008. Some songs must have been recorded by Turner, however, as a posthumous Danger Mouse produced album has been mentioned, as well as another collaboration with MF Doom.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Danger Mouse produced and crafted beat structures with Beck for <a href="/wiki/Beck" title="Beck">Beck</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Modern_Guilt" title="Modern Guilt">Modern Guilt</a></i>, which was released in July 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In April 2009, he and Helena Costas released an album as <a href="/wiki/Joker%27s_Daughter_(band)" title="Joker&#39;s Daughter (band)">Joker's Daughter</a> titled <i>The Last Laugh</i>. Danger Mouse was also listed as one of <i>Esquire</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s 75 most influential people of the 21st century.
</p><p>Since 2005, Danger Mouse (as a producer as well as an artist with Gnarls Barkley) has been nominated for 12 Grammy Awards: Producer of the Year (2005, 2006 and 2008), Record of Year (2006), Album of Year (2006), Best Alternative Album (2006 and 2008), Best Urban Alternative Performance (2006), Best Short Form Music Video (2007 and 2008) and Best Pop Performance (2008). He won two Grammy Awards in 2006 for Best Alternative Album and Best Urban Alternative Performance. He won in 2017 for his work as a producer on Adele's <i>25</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Danger Mouse and <a href="/wiki/Sparklehorse" title="Sparklehorse">Sparklehorse</a> were due to release an album in the summer of 2009 entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Danger_Mouse_and_Sparklehorse_Present:_Dark_Night_of_the_Soul" class="mw-redirect" title="Danger Mouse and Sparklehorse Present: Dark Night of the Soul">Danger Mouse and Sparklehorse Present: Dark Night of the Soul</a></i> (together with a 100+ photo book with photographs by <a href="/wiki/David_Lynch" title="David Lynch">David Lynch</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-dnots_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dnots-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NPR_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Due to a dispute with <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> the album was not released officially until July 12, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> However, the BBC reported that Danger Mouse planned to release a full illustrated jewel case with a blank CD-R included in it.<sup id="cite_ref-dnots_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dnots-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NPR_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Danger_Mouse_to_Release_Blank_CD_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danger_Mouse_to_Release_Blank_CD-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The CD-R was to be labeled: "For Legal Reasons, enclosed CD-R contains no music. Use it as you will."<sup id="cite_ref-dnots_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dnots-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NPR_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Danger_Mouse_to_Release_Blank_CD_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danger_Mouse_to_Release_Blank_CD-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 8, 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/Paste_(magazine)" title="Paste (magazine)">Paste</a></i> magazine named Danger Mouse the "Best Producer of the Decade (2000–2009)."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Together with <a href="/wiki/James_Mercer_(musician)" title="James Mercer (musician)">James Mercer</a>, Danger Mouse (billed by his real name, Brian Burton) formed <a href="/wiki/Broken_Bells" title="Broken Bells">Broken Bells</a>. The project was first announced on September 29, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-DiS_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DiS-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> On December 21, 2009, the band informed fans in an e-mail message of the release of their debut single "The High Road", which was made available as a free download on their official site.<sup id="cite_ref-NME_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NME-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Broken_Bells_(album)" title="Broken Bells (album)">self-titled debut album</a> was released in the U.S. on March 9, 2010.
</p><p>In 2010, Danger Mouse began producing <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a>'s thirteenth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Songs_of_Innocence_(U2_album)" title="Songs of Innocence (U2 album)">Songs of Innocence</a></i>, released in 2014. U2's frontman, <a href="/wiki/Bono" title="Bono">Bono</a>, said in 2010, "We have about 12 songs with him. At the moment that looks like the album we will put out next because it's just happening so easily."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately, Danger Mouse was credited as co-producer on 7 of the album's 11 tracks, and sole producer on "Sleep Like a Baby Tonight" and "This Is Where You Can Reach Me Now". During this period, he also produced the band's single "<a href="/wiki/Ordinary_Love_(U2_song)" title="Ordinary Love (U2 song)">Ordinary Love</a>", released in 2013 as part of the soundtrack to <i><a href="/wiki/Mandela:_Long_Walk_to_Freedom" title="Mandela: Long Walk to Freedom">Mandela: Long Walk to Freedom</a></i>, as well as their single "<a href="/wiki/Invisible_(U2_song)" title="Invisible (U2 song)">Invisible</a>", which was released on Super Bowl Sunday, February 2, 2014.
</p><p>In 2005, Danger Mouse began composing a "<a href="/wiki/Spaghetti_Western" title="Spaghetti Western">Spaghetti Western</a>" album with composer <a href="/wiki/Daniele_Luppi" title="Daniele Luppi">Daniele Luppi</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Jack_White" title="Jack White">Jack White</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norah_Jones" title="Norah Jones">Norah Jones</a> performing the main vocals on the album. Instrumentation was done mainly by musicians who played on the original <a href="/wiki/Ennio_Morricone" title="Ennio Morricone">Ennio Morricone</a> scores.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Danger Mouse does not play any instruments on the album. The style of the album reflects much of Danger Mouse's work since 2005, such as segments of Beck's <i>Modern Guilt</i>, aspects of <i>Dark Night of the Soul</i>, and songs like "Mongrel Heart" off of the self-titled <i>Broken Bells</i> album. The album was titled <i><a href="/wiki/Rome_(Danger_Mouse_and_Daniele_Luppi_album)" title="Rome (Danger Mouse and Daniele Luppi album)">Rome</a></i> and was released on May 16, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 13, 2011, Danger Mouse won a Grammy for Best Producer for his work on the <a href="/wiki/Black_Keys" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Keys">Black Keys</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Brothers_(The_Black_Keys_album)" title="Brothers (The Black Keys album)">Brothers</a></i>, Broken Bells' self-titled album, and the Danger Mouse and <a href="/wiki/Sparklehorse" title="Sparklehorse">Sparklehorse</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Night_of_the_Soul_(album)" title="Dark Night of the Soul (album)">Dark Night of the Soul</a></i>.
</p><p>On November 1, 2011, Los Angeles-based indie band <a href="/wiki/Electric_Guest" title="Electric Guest">Electric Guest</a> released their first single, "Troubleman/American Daydream", produced by Danger Mouse. Danger Mouse produced the band's whole debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Mondo_(album)" title="Mondo (album)">Mondo</a></i> which was released on April 24, 2012.
</p><p>On May 1, 2012, Norah Jones released her Danger Mouse-produced fifth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Broken_Hearts" title="Little Broken Hearts">Little Broken Hearts</a></i> (Blue Note/EMI).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Danger Mouse co-wrote the song "Keep It for Your Own" by <a href="/wiki/POP_ETC" class="mw-redirect" title="POP ETC">POP ETC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Danger Mouse produced <a href="/wiki/Portugal._The_Man" title="Portugal. The Man">Portugal. The Man</a>'s seventh studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Evil_Friends" title="Evil Friends">Evil Friends</a></i>, released in June 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Danger Mouse produced <i><a href="/wiki/At.Long.Last.ASAP" class="mw-redirect" title="At.Long.Last.ASAP">At.Long.Last.ASAP</a></i>, the 2015 album by rapper <a href="/wiki/ASAP_Rocky" title="ASAP Rocky">ASAP Rocky</a>.
<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2015 Danger Mouse launched his own record label, 30th Century Records.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Danger Mouse was announced as the producer for the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers" title="Red Hot Chili Peppers">Red Hot Chili Peppers</a> eleventh album and that he would replace <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rubin" title="Rick Rubin">Rick Rubin</a>, the producer of the band's previous six albums dating back to 1991. Production began in February 2015. The production was halted due to an injury the band's bassist <a href="/wiki/Flea_(musician)" title="Flea (musician)">Flea</a> suffered during a skiing trip. It resumed in August 2015 and continued into early 2016. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Getaway_(Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers_album)" title="The Getaway (Red Hot Chili Peppers album)">The Getaway</a></i> was released in June 2016 with Danger Mouse performing on and having co-writing credits on many of the songs.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2017, <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Chili_Peppers" title="Red Hot Chili Peppers">Red Hot Chili Peppers</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Kiedis" title="Anthony Kiedis">Anthony Kiedis</a> indicated that the band would likely want to work again with Danger Mouse on the follow-up to <i>The Getaway</i>. Kiedis stated "I think it's good to work with the same guy again to um, you know you had your freshman experience. I think we owe it to ourselves to start from the beginning with this guy and see what we can accomplish."<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> However, their follow-up albums, 2022's <i><a href="/wiki/Unlimited_Love" title="Unlimited Love">Unlimited Love</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Dream_Canteen" title="Return of the Dream Canteen">Return of the Dream Canteen</a></i>, featured the return of longtime producer <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rubin" title="Rick Rubin">Rick Rubin</a>.
</p><p>In April 2017, he released <i>Resistance Radio: The Man in The High Castle</i>, a compilation produced alongside 30th Century Records-signee producer Sam Cohen. Connected to <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Studios" title="Amazon Studios">Amazon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Man_in_the_High_Castle_(TV_series)" title="The Man in the High Castle (TV series)">eponymous TV series</a>, this album contains covers of 1960s classics by contemporary artists like Beck, Norah Jones, <a href="/wiki/Kelis" title="Kelis">Kelis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_VanWyngarden" title="Andrew VanWyngarden">Andrew VanWyngarden</a>, The Shins, and more.
<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2017, Portugal. The Man released <i><a href="/wiki/Woodstock_(Portugal._The_Man_album)" title="Woodstock (Portugal. The Man album)">Woodstock</a></i>, their follow-up to 2013's <i>Evil Friends</i>; both of which featured production from Danger Mouse.
</p><p>In June 2017, he collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Run_the_Jewels" title="Run the Jewels">Run the Jewels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Big_Boi" title="Big Boi">Big Boi</a> to create the song "Chase Me" for the 2017 film <i><a href="/wiki/Baby_Driver" title="Baby Driver">Baby Driver</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2018, he produced the acclaimed album <i><a href="/wiki/Wide_Awake!" title="Wide Awake!">Wide Awake!</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Parquet_Courts" title="Parquet Courts">Parquet Courts</a>.
</p><p>In March 2019, he collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Karen_O" title="Karen O">Karen O</a> to create the album <i><a href="/wiki/Lux_Prima" title="Lux Prima">Lux Prima</a></i>. The album has been met with generally favorable review by critics.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 12 2022, he released a collaborative studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Cheat_Codes_(album)" title="Cheat Codes (album)">Cheat Codes</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a> emcee <a href="/wiki/Black_Thought" title="Black Thought">Black Thought</a>.
</p><p>In October 2022, he released a third Broken Bells album with James Mercer entitled Into The Blue (stylized all caps).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danger_Mouse_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an interview for <i>The New York Times</i> magazine, Danger Mouse was compared to a film <a href="/wiki/Auteur" title="Auteur">auteur</a>, basing his music production philosophy on the cinematic philosophy of directors like <a href="/wiki/Woody_Allen" title="Woody Allen">Woody Allen</a>. "Woody Allen was an auteur: he did his thing, and that particular thing was completely his own", he said. "That's what I decided to do with music. I want to create a director's role within music, which is what I tried to do on this album (<i><a href="/wiki/St._Elsewhere_(album)" title="St. Elsewhere (album)">St. Elsewhere</a></i>)... I can create different kinds of musical worlds, but the artist needs the desire to go into that world... Musically, there is no one who has the career I want. That's why I have to use film directors as a model."<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
