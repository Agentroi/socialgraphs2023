<p><b>Marlon Lu'Ree Williams</b> (born September 30, 1962), better known by his stage name <b>Marley Marl</b>, is an American DJ, record producer, rapper and record label founder, primarily operating in <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LarkinDM_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LarkinDM-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Marlon grew up in <a href="/wiki/Queensbridge_Houses" title="Queensbridge Houses">Queensbridge</a> housing projects<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> located in <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a>, New York. He performed in local talent shows during the early days of rap music, further fueling his interest.
</p><p>He was also featured on <a href="/wiki/Eric_B._%26_Rakim" title="Eric B. &amp; Rakim">Eric B. &amp; Rakim</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Paid_in_Full_(Eric_B._%26_Rakim_song)" title="Paid in Full (Eric B. &amp; Rakim song)">Paid in Full</a>" from their <a href="/wiki/Paid_in_Full_(album)" title="Paid in Full (album)">debut album</a>, which was also recorded in his studio.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 
He is credited with influencing a number of hip hop icons such as <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">Biggie Smalls</a>, <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madlib" title="Madlib">Madlib</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rock" title="Pete Rock">Pete Rock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ALLMUSIC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ALLMUSIC-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Producer Madlib stated in an interview that Marley was the first producer who inspired him to make beats.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> magazine wrote that he, "forever changed the sound of hip-hop with his unique beat barrages."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marley_Marl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>
Marley's career started with an interest in <a href="/wiki/Electronica" title="Electronica">electronica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> As a young intern at <a href="/wiki/Unique_Recording_Studios" title="Unique Recording Studios">Unique Recording Studios</a> in the early 1980s, he had an opportunity to experiment with very expensive samplers like the <a href="/wiki/Fairlight_CMI" title="Fairlight CMI">Fairlight CMI</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> One day during a studio session with an artist named Captain Rock he discovered sampling by accident. </p><blockquote><p>"I was actually trying to get a riff off of a record. I made a mistake and got the snare in there before the sound came. I was truncating the vocal part but the snare was playing with the beat — we was truncating while the beat was playing. Thank God the beat was playing, because it probably wouldn't have happened if the beat wasn't playing. So I was playing it and the snare sounded better than the snare that I had from the drum machine when I was popping it."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>A short time later pioneering hip-hop radio DJ <a href="/wiki/Mr._Magic" title="Mr. Magic">Mr. Magic</a> heard Marley Marl's remix of <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_McLaren" title="Malcolm McLaren">Malcolm McLaren</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Gals_(Malcolm_McLaren_song)" title="Buffalo Gals (Malcolm McLaren song)">Buffalo Gals</a>, leading to Marley becoming his DJ.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> They eventually started the hip-hop collective the <a href="/wiki/Juice_Crew" title="Juice Crew">Juice Crew</a> together in 1983. 1986 saw the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Cold_Chillin%27_Records" title="Cold Chillin&#39; Records">Cold Chillin' Records</a>, where Marley served as in-house producer for many projects. He earned $250,000 per year for his production work.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The label was also home to many Juice Crew artists.
</p><p>Marley caught his big break in 1984, with artist <a href="/wiki/Roxanne_Shante" title="Roxanne Shante">Roxanne Shante</a>'s hit "<a href="/wiki/Roxanne%27s_Revenge" title="Roxanne&#39;s Revenge">Roxanne's Revenge</a>". In a 2008 interview Shante noted how seriously he took recording despite his limited setup. "We'd be recording in his living room on a reel-to-reel and four-tracks. I really just wanted to go to the mall after one take, but Marley always made me do it again."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another significant early record was 1985's "Marley Marl Scratch" featuring MC Shan. The song was recorded on a four-track cassette recorder and Shan used a mic with a missing ball to record his lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several of his early record feature inventive use of the <a href="/wiki/Roland_TR-808" title="Roland TR-808">Roland TR-808</a> drum machine. On <a href="/wiki/MC_Shan" title="MC Shan">MC Shan</a>'s 1986 Pop Art single "The Bridge', which later appeared on his 1987 album <i><a href="/wiki/Down_by_Law_(MC_Shan_album)" title="Down by Law (MC Shan album)">Down By Law</a></i>, Marley used the 808 pulse to trigger different samplers.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> According to Biz Markie, the button on Marley's 808 stuck during the recording of his hit "Make the Music with Your Mouth, Biz", leading to sound heard on the record today.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Juice Crew member <a href="/wiki/Big_Daddy_Kane" title="Big Daddy Kane">Big Daddy Kane</a> praised his ability to pair 808 drum sounds with sampled drums. "Regardless of how clean or brand-new the record was that he was sampling, or light the production may have been, he always gave it a really gritty feel when he sampled it. He always put the 808 to it and gave it a heavy bottom and warm feel."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the late 1980s, the Juice Crew gained increased attention from mainstream publications. <a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)"><i>Spin</i> magazine</a> wrote, "they've produced some of the genre's toughest, most uncompromising music."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> At the time Marley began a streak of producing entire albums for several Juice Crew members. He produced all of the tracks on <a href="/wiki/Craig_G" title="Craig G">Craig G</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kingpin_(album)" title="The Kingpin (album)">The Kingpin</a></i> (1989), <a href="/wiki/Big_Daddy_Kane" title="Big Daddy Kane">Big Daddy Kane</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Long_Live_the_Kane" title="Long Live the Kane">Long Live The Kane</a></i> (1988)<i>,</i> <a href="/wiki/Biz_Markie" title="Biz Markie">Biz Markie</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Goin%27_Off" title="Goin&#39; Off">Goin' Off</a></i> (1988), <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a> &amp; DJ Polo's <i><a href="/wiki/Road_to_the_Riches" title="Road to the Riches">Road to the Riches</a></i> (1989), <a href="/wiki/MC_Shan" title="MC Shan">MC Shan</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Down_by_Law_(MC_Shan_album)" title="Down by Law (MC Shan album)"><i>Down By Law</i></a> (1987) and <i><a href="/wiki/Born_to_Be_Wild_(MC_Shan_album)" title="Born to Be Wild (MC Shan album)">Born to Be Wild</a></i> (1988), and <a href="/wiki/Roxanne_Shante" title="Roxanne Shante">Roxanne Shante</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_Sister_(album)" title="Bad Sister (album)">Bad Sister</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Bad_Sister_(album)" title="Bad Sister (album)">(1989)</a>.
</p><p>In 1988, he produced the Juice Crew posse cut "The Symphony" by using a <a href="/wiki/Hal_Jackson" title="Hal Jackson">Hal Jackson</a> record from the <a href="/wiki/WBLS" title="WBLS">WBLS</a> record library as a sample source.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Post-Juice_Crew_and_Cold_Chillin.27"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Juice_Crew_and_Cold_Chillin'">Post-Juice Crew and Cold Chillin'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marley_Marl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Post-Juice Crew and Cold Chillin&#039;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1996, Marley filed a suit against Cold Chillin' for unpaid royalties.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007 he produced the entire <i><a href="/wiki/Hip_Hop_Lives" title="Hip Hop Lives">Hip Hop Lives</a></i> album for former rival <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">KRS-One</a>.
</p>
