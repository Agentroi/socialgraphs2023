<p><b>Malik Izaak Taylor</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (November 20, 1970&#160;&#8211;&#32;March 22, 2016), known professionally as <b>Phife Dawg</b> (or simply <b>Phife</b>), was an American <a href="/wiki/Rapper" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapper">rapper</a> and a member of the group <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">A Tribe Called Quest</a> with <a href="/wiki/Q-Tip_(musician)" title="Q-Tip (musician)">Q-Tip</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ali_Shaheed_Muhammad" title="Ali Shaheed Muhammad">Ali Shaheed Muhammad</a> (and for a short time <a href="/wiki/Jarobi_White" title="Jarobi White">Jarobi White</a>). He was also known as the "Five-Foot Assassin" and "the Five-Footer", because he stood at 5&#160;feet 3&#160;inches (1.60&#160;m).
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phife_Dawg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phife Dawg was born Malik Izaak Taylor on November 20, 1970, in <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidadian</a> immigrant parents Cheryl Boyce-Taylor, a poet, and Walt Taylor.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ubuntu_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ubuntu-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Born prematurely, his twin brother Mikal died shortly after birth.<sup id="cite_ref-heavy_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heavy-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> His mother settled in the <a href="/wiki/St._Albans,_Queens" title="St. Albans, Queens">St. Albans</a> neighborhood of Queens, where Phife Dawg was raised, when she was 13 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-ubuntu_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ubuntu-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He was a cousin of writer <a href="/wiki/Zinzi_Clemmons" title="Zinzi Clemmons">Zinzi Clemmons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He first met his friend <a href="/wiki/Q-Tip_(musician)" title="Q-Tip (musician)">Q-Tip</a> at the age of two, and at nine years old, Phife Dawg suggested that they should rap, after hearing "<a href="/wiki/Rapper%27s_Delight" title="Rapper&#39;s Delight">Rapper's Delight</a>" by <a href="/wiki/The_Sugarhill_Gang" title="The Sugarhill Gang">the Sugarhill Gang</a> for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-beats_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beats-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He attended <a href="/wiki/Pine_Forge_Academy" title="Pine Forge Academy">Pine Forge Academy</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist</a> boarding school near <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, for his freshman year of high school, later transferring to <a href="/wiki/Springfield_Gardens_High_School" title="Springfield Gardens High School">Springfield Gardens High School</a> in Queens.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phife_Dawg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phife Dawg formed <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">A Tribe Called Quest</a>, then simply named Quest, with Q-Tip and DJ <a href="/wiki/Ali_Shaheed_Muhammad" title="Ali Shaheed Muhammad">Ali Shaheed Muhammad</a> in 1985;<sup id="cite_ref-thesource_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thesource-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the group was later expanded with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Jarobi_White" title="Jarobi White">Jarobi White</a>. A Tribe Called Quest were closely associated with fellow hip-hop acts <a href="/wiki/De_La_Soul" title="De La Soul">De La Soul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jungle_Brothers" title="Jungle Brothers">Jungle Brothers</a>, with the groups collectively known as the <a href="/wiki/Native_Tongues" title="Native Tongues">Native Tongues</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-avc_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A Tribe Called Quest was initially offered a demo deal by <a href="/wiki/Geffen_Records" title="Geffen Records">Geffen Records</a> in 1989, but signed to <a href="/wiki/Jive_Records" title="Jive Records">Jive Records</a> to release its 1990 début <i><a href="/wiki/People%27s_Instinctive_Travels_and_the_Paths_of_Rhythm" title="People&#39;s Instinctive Travels and the Paths of Rhythm">People's Instinctive Travels and the Paths of Rhythm</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-avc_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Phife Dawg's contributions to the group increased on its second album, 1991's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Low_End_Theory" title="The Low End Theory">The Low End Theory</a></i>, which saw Phife—often calling himself "the Five-Foot Assassin"—rapping about social and political issues; the record has since been acclaimed by critics and musicians.<sup id="cite_ref-avc_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The group released three more albums that decade—<i><a href="/wiki/Midnight_Marauders" title="Midnight Marauders">Midnight Marauders</a></i> in 1993, <i><a href="/wiki/Beats,_Rhymes_and_Life" title="Beats, Rhymes and Life">Beats, Rhymes and Life</a></i> in 1996, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Love_Movement" title="The Love Movement">The Love Movement</a></i> in 1998—before disbanding as a result of conflict both with their record label and internally. The group's troubles, especially the sometimes tense relationship between Phife and Q-Tip, were featured in the 2011 documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Beats,_Rhymes_%26_Life:_The_Travels_of_A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="Beats, Rhymes &amp; Life: The Travels of A Tribe Called Quest">Beats, Rhymes &amp; Life: The Travels of A Tribe Called Quest</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Rapaport" title="Michael Rapaport">Michael Rapaport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-avc_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Phife also performed with other artists. He was featured on the <a href="/wiki/Fu-Schnickens" title="Fu-Schnickens">Fu-Schnickens</a> song "<a href="/wiki/La_Schmoove" title="La Schmoove">La Schmoove</a>", <a href="/wiki/Diamond_D" title="Diamond D">Diamond D</a>'s "Painz &amp; Strife" with <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rock" title="Pete Rock">Pete Rock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chi-Ali" title="Chi-Ali">Chi-Ali</a>'s "Let the Horns Blow" with <a href="/wiki/Dres_(rapper)" title="Dres (rapper)">Dres</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Beatnuts" title="The Beatnuts">Al' Tariq</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Jolicoeur" title="David Jolicoeur">Trugoy</a>. In 2000, he released his debut solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Ventilation:_Da_LP" title="Ventilation: Da LP">Ventilation: Da LP</a></i>. In 2013, it was reported that Phife was working on another solo album, <i>MUTTYmorPHosis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A single, "Sole Men", was released one day after Phife's death (March 23, 2016) along with a posthumously released video.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Another single, "Nutshell", was released online in April 2016 along with a posthumously released video.<sup id="cite_ref-avc_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 13, 2015, A Tribe Called Quest reunited for a performance on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show_Starring_Jimmy_Fallon" title="The Tonight Show Starring Jimmy Fallon">The Tonight Show Starring Jimmy Fallon</a></i> in commemoration of the 25th anniversary of <i>People's Instinctive Travels and the Paths of Rhythm</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-toure_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toure-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> That night, Phife and Q-Tip decided to put aside their differences and record a new group album, <i><a href="/wiki/We_Got_It_from_Here..._Thank_You_4_Your_Service" title="We Got It from Here... Thank You 4 Your Service">We Got It from Here... Thank You 4 Your Service</a></i>, in secrecy.<sup id="cite_ref-toure_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toure-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Phife spent four months working on the album before his death; it was completed by the surviving members and released on November 11, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-toure_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toure-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In February 2017, it was announced that Phife's second studio album would be released later in the year. The single "Wanna Dance" was released that month and features <a href="/wiki/Dwele" title="Dwele">Dwele</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mike_City" title="Mike City">Mike City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hip-Hop_DX_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hip-Hop_DX-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Phife Dawg's second solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Forever_(Phife_Dawg_album)" title="Forever (Phife Dawg album)">Forever</a></i>, was released on March 22, 2022, the sixth anniversary of his death.<sup id="cite_ref-forever_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forever-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phife_Dawg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Taylor was married to Deisha Head-Taylor and had two children, a daughter and a son.<sup id="cite_ref-thefader_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thefader-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-heavy_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heavy-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vv_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vv-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> He was a fan of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Knicks" title="New York Knicks">New York Knicks</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and was a playable character in the video games <i><a href="/wiki/NBA_2K7" title="NBA 2K7">NBA 2K7</a></i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/NBA_2K9" title="NBA 2K9">NBA 2K9</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_problems_and_death">Health problems and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phife_Dawg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Health problems and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Taylor was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" class="mw-redirect" title="Diabetes mellitus">diabetes</a> in 1990. Conflicting reports indicated it as <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_1" class="mw-redirect" title="Diabetes mellitus type 1">type 1</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> while other sources reported it as <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Diabetes mellitus type 2">type 2</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> He described himself as a "funky diabetic" in the single "<a href="/wiki/Oh_My_God_(A_Tribe_Called_Quest_song)" title="Oh My God (A Tribe Called Quest song)">Oh My God</a>" from A Tribe Called Quest's 1993 album <i><a href="/wiki/Midnight_Marauders" title="Midnight Marauders">Midnight Marauders</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> After the group disbanded, he continued playing live shows to help cover medical costs, and revealed in the 2011 documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/Beats,_Rhymes_%26_Life:_The_Travels_of_A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="Beats, Rhymes &amp; Life: The Travels of A Tribe Called Quest">Beats, Rhymes &amp; Life</a></i> that he was "just addicted to sugar ... it's really a sickness".<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 2008, Taylor developed <a href="/wiki/Renal_failure" class="mw-redirect" title="Renal failure">renal failure</a> and received a <a href="/wiki/Kidney_transplant" class="mw-redirect" title="Kidney transplant">kidney transplant</a> from his wife, but it was unsuccessful, and by 2012 he again required a transplant.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 22, 2016, Taylor died at age 45 in his <a href="/wiki/Oakley,_California" title="Oakley, California">Oakley, California</a>, home due to <a href="/wiki/Complications_of_diabetes" title="Complications of diabetes">complications of diabetes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phife_Dawg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phife has been described as having had a "self-deprecating swagger", and his work with A Tribe Called Quest helped challenge the "macho posturing" of rap and hip-hop music during the late 1980s and early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Phife's work has been cited as an influence on <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jill_Scott_(singer)" title="Jill Scott (singer)">Jill Scott</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-avc_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> while the 1991 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Low_End_Theory" title="The Low End Theory">The Low End Theory</a></i> is considered one of the greatest hip-hop albums ever.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 19, 2016, a portion of <a href="/wiki/Linden_Boulevard" title="Linden Boulevard">Linden Boulevard</a>, at the intersection of 192nd Street in St. Albans, was honorarily renamed "Malik 'Phife Dawg' Taylor Way".<sup id="cite_ref-thefader_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thefader-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The location is significant as the site of the video for A Tribe Called Quest's song "<a href="/wiki/Check_the_Rhime" title="Check the Rhime">Check the Rhime</a>".
</p>
