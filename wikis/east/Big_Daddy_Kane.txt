<p><b>Antonio Hardy</b> (born September 10, 1968), better known by his stage name <b>Big Daddy Kane</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapper</a>, producer and actor who began his career in 1986 as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Juice_Crew" title="Juice Crew">Juice Crew</a>. He is widely regarded as one of the most influential and skilled <a href="/wiki/MC" class="mw-redirect" title="MC">MCs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> ranked his song "<a href="/wiki/Ain%27t_No_Half-Steppin%27" title="Ain&#39;t No Half-Steppin&#39;">Ain't No Half-Steppin'</a>" number 25 on its list of <i>The 50 Greatest Hip-Hop Songs of All Time</i>, calling him "a master wordsmith of rap's late-<a href="/wiki/Golden_age_hip_hop" title="Golden age hip hop">golden age</a> and a huge influence on a generation of MCs".
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Daddy_Kane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Daddy_Kane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1984, Kane became friends with <a href="/wiki/Biz_Markie" title="Biz Markie">Biz Markie</a>, and he would co-write some of Biz's best-known lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-BDK_interview_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BDK_interview-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Both eventually became important members of the <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a>-based Juice Crew, a collective headed by renowned producer <a href="/wiki/Marley_Marl" title="Marley Marl">Marley Marl</a>. Kane signed with Tyrone Williams's (Marl's manager) and Len Fichtelberg's <a href="/wiki/Cold_Chillin%27_Records" title="Cold Chillin&#39; Records">Cold Chillin' Records</a> label in 1987 and debuted the same year with the 12" single "Raw", which was an underground hit. The name Big Daddy Kane came from a variation on <a href="/wiki/Kwai_Chang_Caine" title="Kwai Chang Caine">Caine</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Carradine" title="David Carradine">David Carradine</a>'s character from the TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Kung_Fu_(TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kung Fu (TV series)">Kung Fu</a></i> (1972–1975), and a character called "Big Daddy" whom <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Price" title="Vincent Price">Vincent Price</a> played in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Beach_Party" title="Beach Party">Beach Party</a></i> (1963).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kane is known for his ability to syncopate over faster hip hop beats, and despite his asthmatic condition,<sup id="cite_ref-BDK_interview_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BDK_interview-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> he is acknowledged as one of the pioneering masters of fast rhyming. His sense of style is renowned and set a number of late-1980s and early-1990s hip hop trends (<a href="/wiki/Hi-top_fade" title="Hi-top fade">high-top fades</a> hairstyles, velour suits, and four-finger rings). The <a href="/wiki/Backronym" title="Backronym">backronym</a> "King Asiatic Nobody's Equal" is often applied to his moniker.
</p><p>He released his debut album on Cold Chillin' Records in the early summer of 1988 called <i><a href="/wiki/Long_Live_the_Kane" title="Long Live the Kane">Long Live the Kane</a></i>, which featured the hit "<a href="/wiki/Ain%27t_No_Half_Steppin%27" class="mw-redirect" title="Ain&#39;t No Half Steppin&#39;">Ain't No Half Steppin'</a>". The following year, Kane released his second album and biggest hit to date, <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_a_Big_Daddy_Thing" title="It&#39;s a Big Daddy Thing">It's a Big Daddy Thing</a></i>, which included 1970s sample throwbacks like "<a href="/wiki/Smooth_Operator_(Big_Daddy_Kane_song)" title="Smooth Operator (Big Daddy Kane song)">Smooth Operator</a>" and the <a href="/wiki/Teddy_Riley_(producer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Teddy Riley (producer)">Teddy Riley</a>-produced track "I Get the Job Done", which hit the R&amp;B top 40 during the closing of the 1980s. He also had a memorable verse on the <a href="/wiki/Marley_Marl" title="Marley Marl">Marley Marl</a>-produced track "<a href="/wiki/The_Symphony_(song)" title="The Symphony (song)">The Symphony</a>" released in late 1988, which included Juice Crew members Craig G, <a href="/wiki/Masta_Ace" title="Masta Ace">Masta Ace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Daddy_Kane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Big_Daddy_Kane-03.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Big_Daddy_Kane-03.jpg/170px-Big_Daddy_Kane-03.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="254" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Big_Daddy_Kane-03.jpg/255px-Big_Daddy_Kane-03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Big_Daddy_Kane-03.jpg/340px-Big_Daddy_Kane-03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="717" data-file-height="1071" /></a><figcaption>Big Daddy Kane in 1998</figcaption></figure>
<p>Big Daddy Kane appeared on <a href="/wiki/Patti_LaBelle" title="Patti LaBelle">Patti LaBelle</a>'s 1991 effort, "Burnin<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>". He provided the rap chorus to the single "Feels Like Another One". He also appeared on the video release "Live in New York".
</p><p>He contributed the song "'Nuff Respect" to <a href="/wiki/Juice_(soundtrack)" title="Juice (soundtrack)">the soundtrack</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Dickerson" title="Ernest Dickerson">Ernest Dickerson</a>'s feature film debut <i><a href="/wiki/Juice_(1992_film)" title="Juice (1992 film)">Juice</a></i>, which starred <a href="/wiki/Omar_Epps" title="Omar Epps">Omar Epps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a>, further demonstrating his fast lyrical delivery.
</p><p>In 1991, Kane won the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Performance_by_a_Duo_or_Group" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group">Grammy Award for Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group</a> for his performance on the <a href="/wiki/Quincy_Jones" title="Quincy Jones">Quincy Jones</a> collaborative track "Back on the Block" from the <a href="/wiki/Back_on_the_Block" title="Back on the Block">album of the same name</a>.
</p><p>Widely regarded as one of the greatest rappers during the <a href="/wiki/Golden_age_hip_hop" title="Golden age hip hop">golden age of hip hop</a>, Kane's experimentation with <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">R&amp;B</a> beats and his alignment to the <a href="/wiki/The_Nation_of_Gods_and_Earths" class="mw-redirect" title="The Nation of Gods and Earths">Five Percent Nation</a> drew criticism.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Later albums, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Looks_Like_a_Job_For..." title="Looks Like a Job For...">Looks Like a Job For...</a></i>, were acclaimed, but he was never able to return to the commercial and artistic success of <i>It's a Big Daddy Thing</i>. However, he still tours extensively.
</p><p>As an actor, he debuted in <a href="/wiki/Mario_Van_Peebles" title="Mario Van Peebles">Mario Van Peebles</a>' 1993 western <i><a href="/wiki/Posse_(1993_film)" title="Posse (1993 film)">Posse</a></i>, and appeared in <a href="/wiki/Robert_Townsend_(actor)" title="Robert Townsend (actor)">Robert Townsend</a>'s 1993 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Meteor_Man_(film)" title="The Meteor Man (film)">The Meteor Man</a></i>. He also posed for <i><a href="/wiki/Playgirl" title="Playgirl">Playgirl</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Madonna" title="Madonna">Madonna</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/Sex_(book)" title="Sex (book)">Sex</a></i> during the 1990s.
</p><p>During the early 1990s, <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> toured with Kane, and Kane helped him early on in his career. Ice-T says: "I actually met Jay-Z with Kane. Kane brought Jay-Z over to my house."<sup id="cite_ref-mtv.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
Kane himself says that Jay-Z was not technically his hypeman in the true sense of the term: "he wasn't a hypeman, he basically made cameo appearances on stage. When I would leave the stage to go change outfits, I would bring out Jay-Z and Positive K and let them freestyle until I came back to the stage."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
Jay-Z was also featured on Big Daddy Kane's track "Show &amp; Prove" from <i>Daddy's Home</i> (1994), as well as in the video.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1995, Kane recorded with <a href="/wiki/MC_Hammer" title="MC Hammer">MC Hammer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a> on the rap song "Too Late Playa" (along with <a href="/wiki/Danny_Boy_(singer)" title="Danny Boy (singer)">Danny Boy</a>). He was also mentioned to signing with <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_East" class="mw-redirect" title="Death Row East">Death Row East</a> in 1996.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 1997, Kane teamed up with <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Cutlass" title="Frankie Cutlass">Frankie Cutlass</a> on his third single title "The Cypher Part 3" and some of <a href="/wiki/Marley_Marl" title="Marley Marl">Marley Marl</a> <a href="/wiki/Juice_Crew" title="Juice Crew">Juice Crew</a> veterans. In 1998, he released his final solo album to date, <i><a href="/wiki/Veteranz%27_Day" title="Veteranz&#39; Day">Veteranz' Day</a></i>. It received mixed reviews and did not sell well. However, Kane did not give up rapping and made many appearances in the 2000s.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Daddy_Kane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Big Daddy Kane appeared on Tony Touch's "<a href="/wiki/The_Piece_Maker" title="The Piece Maker">The Piece Maker</a>" mixtape alongside <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a> and <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">KRS-One</a>. A rejuvenated Kane occasionally collaborated with a variety of hip-hop artists, including <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">A Tribe Called Quest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_5" title="Jurassic 5">Jurassic 5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Little_Brother_(band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Little Brother (band)">Little Brother</a>, and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Babu" title="DJ Babu">DJ Babu</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Beat_Junkies" title="Beat Junkies">Beat Junkies</a>. He released two singles, the <a href="/wiki/The_Alchemist_(musician)" title="The Alchemist (musician)">Alchemist</a>-produced "The Man, The Icon", and the <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>-produced "Any Type of Way" (on which he discusses urban collapse in post-<a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">9/11</a> New York City ("<a href="/wiki/Rudy_Giuliani" title="Rudy Giuliani">Giuliani</a> got New York lookin' like it's <a href="/wiki/La_Amistad" title="La Amistad">Amistad</a>")) and the erosion of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a>.
</p><p>Big Daddy Kane appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Trip_hop" title="Trip hop">trip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Morcheeba" title="Morcheeba">Morcheeba</a>'s 2003 single "What's Your Name".
</p><p>In 2005, Big Daddy Kane was honored during the <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a> Hip-Hop Honors. After a <a href="/wiki/Medley_(music)" title="Medley (music)">medley</a> of hits performed by <a href="/wiki/T.I." title="T.I.">T.I.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Thought" title="Black Thought">Black Thought</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a>, he came out to perform "Warm It Up, Kane" with his old dancers, Scoob and Scrap. Kane and Kool G Rap can both be seen briefly in <i><a href="/wiki/Dave_Chappelle%27s_Block_Party" title="Dave Chappelle&#39;s Block Party">Dave Chappelle's Block Party</a></i>.
</p><p>In 2006, he appeared as a guest MC on the track "Get Wild Off This", produced by the <a href="/wiki/Stanton_Warriors" title="Stanton Warriors">Stanton Warriors</a> for their <i>Stanton Sessions Vol. 2</i> breaks mix. He also appeared alongside the <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rakim" title="Rakim">Rakim</a>, and his longtime friends <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Q-Tip_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Q-Tip (rapper)">Q-Tip</a> in a segment of the 2006 <a href="/wiki/Summer_Jam_(festival)" title="Summer Jam (festival)">Summer Jam</a> concert (June 7, 2006), as part of an initiative by Busta Rhymes to honor the legacy of New York City hip-hop.
</p><p>In 2007, a new track, "BK Mentality", was released on the mixtape compilation <i>Official Joints</i>. Kane also appeared on <a href="/wiki/Joell_Ortiz" title="Joell Ortiz">Joell Ortiz</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Brick:_Bodega_Chronicles" title="The Brick: Bodega Chronicles">The Brick: Bodega Chronicles</a></i> mixtape.
</p><p>Big Daddy Kane made a cameo in the 2008 video for "<a href="/wiki/Game%27s_Pain" title="Game&#39;s Pain">Game's Pain</a>", a track by rapper <a href="/wiki/The_Game_(rapper)" title="The Game (rapper)">The Game</a>. The video also featured appearances by <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Three_6_Mafia" title="Three 6 Mafia">Three 6 Mafia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>. The Game also referenced <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>'s former occupation as Kane's hypeman: "Ask a Jay-Z fan about Big Daddy Kane: Don't know him, Game gon' show 'em".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="previous source was not reliable (October 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> He also appeared on the remix of "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Touch_Me_(Throw_da_Water_on_%27Em)" title="Don&#39;t Touch Me (Throw da Water on &#39;Em)">Don't Touch Me</a>" by Busta Rhymes.
Also in 2009, Kane played the role of Clay in the film <i>Just Another Day</i>. The film is the tale of two rappers, one poor and young, one older and successful. The movie follows them through a day of their lives.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Daddy_Kane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Big Daddy Kane appears on one track on <i><a href="/wiki/MA_Doom:_Son_of_Yvonne" title="MA Doom: Son of Yvonne">MA Doom: Son of Yvonne</a></i>—the collaboration between <a href="/wiki/MF_Doom" title="MF Doom">MF Doom</a> and former Juice Crew member <a href="/wiki/Masta_Ace" title="Masta Ace">Masta Ace</a>.
As of 2013, Kane was recruited by New York City power group Lifted Crew and R&amp;B singer Showtyme to form a band called "Las Supper". The album, <i>Back to the Future</i>, was released on March 26, 2013.
</p><p>On November 24, 2014, Big Daddy Kane discussed his upbringing, childhood influences, relationships, sexual experiences and <a href="/wiki/Madonna" title="Madonna">Madonna</a>'s 1992 book <i><a href="/wiki/Sex_(book)" title="Sex (book)">Sex</a></i> in an interview on the <i>Dr. Zoe Today</i> show.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 2016, he was featured on <a href="/wiki/Tito_Jackson" title="Tito Jackson">Tito Jackson's</a> single "Get It Baby" in addition to appearing in the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Hip-Hop_Evolution" title="Hip-Hop Evolution">Hip-Hop Evolution</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2020s">2020s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Daddy_Kane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2020s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 2020, Kane released the single "Enough", aimed at addressing issues surrounding police brutality.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Daddy_Kane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Big Daddy Kane is regarded as one of the most influential and skilled <a href="/wiki/Golden_age_hip_hop" title="Golden age hip hop">golden age</a> rappers.<sup id="cite_ref-mtv.com_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated303_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated303-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-allhiphop1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allhiphop1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-allmusic1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-allmusic2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic2-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone2001_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone2001-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated17_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated17-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2004_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated29_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated29-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-allmusic3_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic3-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> put him at No. 7 in their "Greatest MCs of All Time" list.<sup id="cite_ref-mtv.com_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He is placed at No. 4 in <a href="/wiki/Kool_Moe_Dee" title="Kool Moe Dee">Kool Moe Dee</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/There%27s_a_God_on_the_Mic" title="There&#39;s a God on the Mic">There's a God on the Mic</a>: The True 50 Greatest MCs</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated303_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated303-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/About.com" class="mw-redirect" title="About.com">About.com</a> ranked him No. 3 on its list of the "Top 50 MCs of Our Time",<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a> listed him as one of his "Top 5 best MCs".<sup id="cite_ref-allhiphop1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allhiphop1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 2012, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Source (magazine)">The Source</a></i> ranked him No. 8 on their list of the "Top 50 Lyricists of All Time".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> says "his best material ranks among the finest hip-hop of its era, and his sex-drenched persona was enormously influential on countless future would-be players",<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic1_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and describes him as "an enormously talented battle MC",<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> "one of rap's major talents",<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> refers to his "near-peerless technique"<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic.com_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic.com-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and "first-rate technique and rhyming skills",<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and says he "had the sheer verbal facility and razor-clean dexterity to ambush any MC and exhilarate anyone who witnessed or heard him perform".<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic3_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic3-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Kool_Moe_Dee" title="Kool Moe Dee">Kool Moe Dee</a> describes him as "one of the most imitated emcees ever in the game"<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and "one of the true greatest emcees ever".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a> stated:
</p>
<blockquote><p>"To me, Big Daddy Kane is still today one of the best rappers. I would put Big Daddy Kane against any rapper in a battle. Jay-Z, Nas, Eminem, any of them. I could take his 'Raw' "swagger" from 88 and put it up against any record [from today]. Kane is one of the most incredible lyricists... and he will devour you on the mic. I don't want to try to out-rap Big Daddy Kane. Big Daddy Kane can rap circles around cats."<sup id="cite_ref-mtv.com_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>His first two albums are also considered hip hop classics<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic2_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic2-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> says, "he has received consistent critical kudos".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
In the book <i>Rap-Up: The Ultimate Guide to Hip-Hop and R&amp;B</i>, Cameron and Devin Lazerine say Big Daddy Kane is "widely seen as one of the best lyricists of his time and even today regularly gets name-checked by younger dudes",<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and music journalist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Shapiro_(journalist)" title="Peter Shapiro (journalist)">Peter Shapiro</a> says Kane is "perhaps the most complete MC ever".<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated29_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated29-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a> references Big Daddy Kane in the lyrics to his song "Yellow Brick Road" from his album <i><a href="/wiki/Encore_(Eminem_album)" title="Encore (Eminem album)">Encore</a></i>, saying, "we (Eminem and <a href="/wiki/Proof_(rapper)" title="Proof (rapper)">Proof</a>) was on the same shit, that Big Daddy Kane shit, where compound syllables sound combined",<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2004_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and he quotes the same lines in his book <a href="/wiki/The_Way_I_Am_(autobiography)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Way I Am (autobiography)"><i>The Way I Am</i></a>—this illustrates how Big Daddy Kane had an influence on both Eminem's and Proof's rhyme technique.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
