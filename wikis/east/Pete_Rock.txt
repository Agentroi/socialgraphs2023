<p><b>Peter O. Phillips<sup id="cite_ref-amoeba.com_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amoeba.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></b> (born June 21, 1970),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> better known by his stage name <b>Pete Rock</b>, is an American music producer, <a href="/wiki/DJ" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ">DJ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapper</a>. He is widely recognized as one of the greatest hip hop producers of all time,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and is often mentioned alongside <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>, <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Q-Tip_(musician)" title="Q-Tip (musician)">Q-Tip</a> as one of the mainstays of 1990s <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_hip_hop" title="East Coast hip hop">East Coast hip hop</a> production. He rose to prominence in the early 1990s as one half of the critically acclaimed group <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rock_%26_CL_Smooth" title="Pete Rock &amp; CL Smooth">Pete Rock &amp; CL Smooth</a>. Early on in his career, he was also famed for his remix work.
</p><p>After the duo went their separate ways, Rock continued with a solo career that has garnered him worldwide respect, though little in the way of mainstream success.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Along with groups such as <a href="/wiki/Stetsasonic" title="Stetsasonic">Stetsasonic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gang_Starr" title="Gang Starr">Gang Starr</a>, <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">A Tribe Called Quest</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a>, Rock played a major role in the merging of elements from <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> into hip hop music (also known as <a href="/wiki/Jazz_rap" title="Jazz rap">jazz rap</a>). Pete Rock is also the older brother and younger cousin, respectively, of rappers <a href="/wiki/Grap_Luva" class="mw-redirect" title="Grap Luva">Grap Luva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_D" title="Heavy D">Heavy D</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Henry_Adaso_8_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Henry_Adaso_8-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pete Rock was born in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">The Bronx</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="New York City, New York">New York City, New York</a><sup id="cite_ref-peterock.tribe.net_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peterock.tribe.net-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> the fourth of five children born to <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaican</a> immigrant parents.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> His family moved to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Vernon,_New_York" title="Mount Vernon, New York">Mount Vernon, New York</a> when he was seven years old. During high school, he met his future recording partner <a href="/wiki/CL_Smooth" title="CL Smooth">CL Smooth</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> He briefly attended <a href="/wiki/John_Jay_College_of_Criminal_Justice" title="John Jay College of Criminal Justice">John Jay College of Criminal Justice</a> but was not a serious student and left to focus on his music career.<sup id="cite_ref-Complex_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Complex-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> According to Rock, his father was also a part-time DJ who had an impressive record collection. Rock would often accompany his father to a <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> club called Wembley in The Bronx and watch as he spun records for the guests.<sup id="cite_ref-peterock.tribe.net_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peterock.tribe.net-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> His first job was as a <a href="/wiki/Paperboy_(newspaper_delivery)" class="mw-redirect" title="Paperboy (newspaper delivery)">paperboy</a>, in his neighborhood.<sup id="cite_ref-peterock.tribe.net_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peterock.tribe.net-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_career">Solo career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solo career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Pete_Rock_at_Launch_Party.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Pete_Rock_at_Launch_Party.jpg/170px-Pete_Rock_at_Launch_Party.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Pete_Rock_at_Launch_Party.jpg/255px-Pete_Rock_at_Launch_Party.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Pete_Rock_at_Launch_Party.jpg/340px-Pete_Rock_at_Launch_Party.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3744" data-file-height="5616" /></a><figcaption>Pete Rock performing at <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel</a>/<a href="/wiki/St%C3%BCssy" title="Stüssy">Stüssy</a> launch party in Los Angeles, 2011</figcaption></figure>
<p>Rock oversaw the production of <i><a href="/wiki/Jay_Stay_Paid" title="Jay Stay Paid">Jay Stay Paid</a></i>, a posthumous album by the producer <a href="/wiki/J_Dilla" title="J Dilla">J Dilla</a>, released June 2, 2009, on <a href="/wiki/Nature_Sounds" title="Nature Sounds">Nature Sounds</a>. Following that, Pete Rock joined <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> in Hawaii, who traveled there to work on the latter's fifth album, <i><a href="/wiki/My_Beautiful_Dark_Twisted_Fantasy" title="My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy">My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In addition, he and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a> have announced that they are working on a joint album together, although further details are unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In London, he confirmed that <a href="/wiki/Big_Pooh" title="Big Pooh">Big Pooh</a> and <a href="/wiki/C.L._Smooth" class="mw-redirect" title="C.L. Smooth">C.L. Smooth</a> will be on his half of the VS album and he planned on releasing five albums in 2011, including reuniting with C.L. Smooth for a third album, and dropping his fourth album on <a href="/wiki/Nature_Sounds" title="Nature Sounds">Nature Sounds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> His next few collaborative albums are both due for a summer release with <a href="/wiki/Monumental_(album)" title="Monumental (album)">Monumental</a> first then with <a href="/wiki/Camp_Lo" title="Camp Lo">Camp Lo</a>'s "80 Blocks From Tiffanys" LP.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In an April 2011 interview on Conspiracy Worldwide Radio, Pete Rock discussed his new solo work including his album with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>, as well as exploring the fact that he has had numerous beats rejected by <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a> over the years <sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Pete Rock Uncensored Radio Interview. In an August 2011 interview, he has confirmed the completion of the <a href="/wiki/Camp_Lo" title="Camp Lo">Camp Lo</a> album "80 Blocks from Tiffany's" and that he is currently working on production for <a href="/wiki/Torae" title="Torae">Torae</a>'s album, <a href="/wiki/Elzhi" title="Elzhi">Elzhi</a> &amp; his own solo album <i><a href="/wiki/PeteStrumentals_2" title="PeteStrumentals 2">PeteStrumentals 2</a></i>. Pete Rock announced on Twitter that <i><a href="/wiki/PeteStrumentals_2" title="PeteStrumentals 2">PeteStrumentals 2</a></i> is indeed confirmed finished and scheduled for a 2015 release.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The project was released on June 23, 2015, on the indie label <a href="/wiki/Mello_Music_Group" title="Mello Music Group">Mello Music Group</a>. On January 2, 2019, Rock posted a trailer video on his Instagram page announcing that new works will be coming soon, including <i><a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_SP1200" title="Return of the SP1200">Return of the SP1200</a></i> (which was released on April 13, 2019, exclusively on <a href="/wiki/Phonograph_record" title="Phonograph record">vinyl</a> (<a href="/wiki/Record_Store_Day" title="Record Store Day">Record Store Day</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and released digitally on April 26, 2019), <i>PeteStrumentals 3</i> (a continuation to 2001's <i><a href="/wiki/PeteStrumentals" title="PeteStrumentals">PeteStrumentals</a></i> and 2015's <i><a href="/wiki/PeteStrumentals_2" title="PeteStrumentals 2">2</a></i>), <i>Don't Smoke Rock 2</i> (also a continuation to <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Smoke_Rock" title="Don&#39;t Smoke Rock">Don't Smoke Rock</a></i>) featuring <a href="/wiki/Smoke_DZA" title="Smoke DZA">Smoke DZA</a>, and an album with rapper <a href="/wiki/Skyzoo" title="Skyzoo">Skyzoo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Rock confirms to <a href="/wiki/Okayplayer" title="Okayplayer">Okayplayer</a> that he is working on his sixth instrumental album called <i>PeteStrumentals 4</i>, another continuation to Rock's previous PeteStrumentals series releases.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The project was finished on June 30 and it's scheduled for a 2022 release on Tru Soul records.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The project was released on March 31, 2022.<sup id="cite_ref-NEW_PETESTRUMENTALS_4_DROPS_MARCH_3_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEW_PETESTRUMENTALS_4_DROPS_MARCH_3-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Affiliates">Affiliates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Affiliates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proteges">Proteges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proteges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Through the years, Rock has helped to jump-start the careers of several artists. His first project outside of Pete Rock &amp; CL Smooth was the hardcore duo <a href="/wiki/YG%27z" title="YG&#39;z">YG'z</a>, who released an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> called <i>Street Nigga</i> in 1993, with four out of the six tracks produced by Rock; however, they were quickly dropped from their deal with Reprise Records. His next venture, <a href="/wiki/INI_(hip_hop_group)" class="mw-redirect" title="INI (hip hop group)">INI</a>, was a group featuring Rock, his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Grap_Luva" class="mw-redirect" title="Grap Luva">Grap Luva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rass_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rass (rapper)">Ras G</a> and rapper <a href="/wiki/Rob-O" class="mw-redirect" title="Rob-O">Rob-O</a>. They released a single, "Fakin' Jax", through <a href="/wiki/Elektra_Records" title="Elektra Records">Elektra Records</a> in 1995, before their debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_%26_Found:_Hip_Hop_Underground_Soul_Classics" title="Lost &amp; Found: Hip Hop Underground Soul Classics">Center of Attention</a></i>, was shelved by the label. The other two members continue to record solo material, albeit only sporadically. In an interview Rock elaborated on the situation:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We finished the album, turned it into Elektra and they never put it out, they only put out a single. <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Rhone" title="Sylvia Rhone">Sylvia [Rhone]</a> really didn't cooperate, she didn't break bread with me when it came down to resolving that. It was all about her changing everything around. She wanted to change my whole sound. When she said, "You gotta make a beat like <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">Puffy</a>", I just knew it wasn't going to work out.<sup id="cite_ref-peterock.tribe.net_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peterock.tribe.net-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_CL_Smooth">Relationship with CL Smooth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship with CL Smooth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since their split in 1995, Pete Rock's relationship with CL Smooth has been highly unpredictable. Although the pair briefly united for the reflective "Da Two" from Rock's <i>Soul Survivor</i> album in 1998, they avoided entertaining requests for a reunion album until 2001, when they once again teamed up for "Back on Da Block" from Rock's <i>PeteStrumentals</i>. In their interviews during this period, it appeared as though a new album was underway. As Rock would explain:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We've been on tour, we know every rhymer and producer in this business. We've influenced people, even people we've never met have said that we changed the face of hip-hop. So we're going to try to do some more.<sup id="cite_ref-peterock.tribe.net_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peterock.tribe.net-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The pair went on a short international tour culminating in their well-received show at London's Jazz Cafe; however, soon after this they declined to comment any further on the new album, which never materialized (although Smooth did make three separate appearances on <i>Soul Survivor II</i>).  Eventually, Smooth would confirm rumors of a rift in an interview with AllHipHop.com,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> in which he appeared angry and frustrated with his former partner, saying "I didn't ask him to be a superhero" and "I'm not the problem". In an interview taken in December 2006, Rock ruled out any further collaborations with Smooth but stated that he holds no grudges against his former partner.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_style">Musical style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Musical style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:E-mu_SP-1200_(111607sp1200).jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/E-mu_SP-1200_%28111607sp1200%29.jpg/220px-E-mu_SP-1200_%28111607sp1200%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="175" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/E-mu_SP-1200_%28111607sp1200%29.jpg/330px-E-mu_SP-1200_%28111607sp1200%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/46/E-mu_SP-1200_%28111607sp1200%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="319" /></a><figcaption>An <a href="/wiki/E-mu_SP-1200" title="E-mu SP-1200">E-mu SP-1200</a> that Pete Rock used to create music and beats on one of his many early recordings</figcaption></figure>
<p>Pete Rock creates beats from samples, the majority of which are taken from obscure <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">R&amp;B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Funk_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Funk music">funk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jazz_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Jazz music">jazz</a> records. This paying true homage to the Black American musical traditions that are the foundation and origins of Hip-Hop music. Early on in his career he would also sample drum breaks such as <a href="/wiki/Black_Heat" title="Black Heat">Black Heat</a>'s "Zimba Ku" for <a href="/wiki/Heavy_D_%26_The_Boyz" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy D &amp; The Boyz">Heavy D &amp; The Boyz</a>'s "Letter To The Future". Pete Rock heavily used the <a href="/wiki/E-mu_SP-1200" title="E-mu SP-1200">E-mu SP-1200</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Akai_S900" title="Akai S900">Akai S950</a>—later moving onto using the MPC—for his productions. Pete Rock tends to use the samples as palettes for his beats, chopping (cutting the sample into smaller parts), filtering (altering the frequencies of the sample), and layering several samples, often within the same song. While this technique was applied long before Rock (on <a href="/wiki/De_La_Soul" title="De La Soul">De La Soul</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Feet_High_and_Rising" class="mw-redirect" title="Three Feet High and Rising">Three Feet High and Rising</a></i> or the work of <a href="/wiki/The_Bomb_Squad" title="The Bomb Squad">The Bomb Squad</a> for example), Rock's work is distinctive for the way in which he uses samples to achieve a hazy, droning effect. He is also noted for his resonant basslines, horn samples, and gritty sounding drums. His beats often sound as though they were being played from an old <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Gramophone record">vinyl</a> record; he samples many of his sounds straight off these records.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> He frequently recorded at <a href="/wiki/Greene_St._Recording" title="Greene St. Recording">Greene St. Recording</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, having liked the <a href="/wiki/Equalization_(audio)" title="Equalization (audio)">equalizer</a> that was used there, which gave many of his productions a <a href="/wiki/Wah-wah_(music)" title="Wah-wah (music)">wah-wah</a> effect.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another trait of his, more so in the earlier part of his career, is the way he uses horn samples to supplement his grooves. On "<a href="/wiki/They_Reminisce_Over_You_(T.R.O.Y.)" title="They Reminisce Over You (T.R.O.Y.)">They Reminisce Over You (T.R.O.Y.)</a>", Rock uses a horn sample from <a href="/wiki/Tom_Scott_(saxophonist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tom Scott (saxophonist)">Tom Scott</a>'s "Today"; he has also used horns on  "Straighten It Out", <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Enemy (band)">Public Enemy</a>'s "Shut 'Em Down", <a href="/wiki/Rah_Digga" title="Rah Digga">Rah Digga</a>'s "What They Call Me", and A.D.O.R.'s "Let It All Hang Out".
</p><p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Gang_Starr" title="Gang Starr">Gang Starr</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a> and <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">A Tribe Called Quest</a>, Pete Rock played a large role in the fusing of jazz and funk music into hip hop. The aforementioned "Reminisce..." withstanding, Rock used many jazz samples on his album <i>Mecca and The Soul Brother</i>, such as <a href="/wiki/Cannonball_Adderley" title="Cannonball Adderley">Cannonball Adderley</a>'s "Country Preacher", for the song "Return of the Mecca", or "Capricorn" for the song "In the House" from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Main_Ingredient_(Pete_Rock_%26_CL_Smooth_album)" title="The Main Ingredient (Pete Rock &amp; CL Smooth album)">The Main Ingredient</a></i>. Pete Rock's heavy use of intro and outro beats has also been widely influential. To introduce feature songs, he often plays a short instrumental excerpt, completely different from the rest of the song.  Aside from their role as transitions, these are widely regarded as a way of displaying his large collection and as a challenge to other hip-hop producers to identify the records that the <a href="/wiki/Break_(music)" title="Break (music)">breaks</a> come from.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <i>Mecca &amp; the Soul Brother</i> and <i>The Main Ingredient</i> use intro/outro beats on nearly every track to great effect, and the tradition continues to the present on Rock's recent releases.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remixes">Remixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Remixes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Another Pete Rock Remix" is Pete Rock's trademark catchphrase, heard on countless singles that he has <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remixed</a>. In addition to hip-hop artists he has done remix work for artists from other genres such as his 1995 remix of "<a href="/wiki/Before_You_Walk_Out_Of_My_Life" class="mw-redirect" title="Before You Walk Out Of My Life">Before You Walk Out Of My Life</a>" for <a href="/wiki/R%26B" class="mw-redirect" title="R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Monica_(singer)" title="Monica (singer)">Monica</a>. In 1992 he collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Mary_J._Blige" title="Mary J. Blige">Mary J. Blige</a> on the <i><a href="/wiki/What%27s_the_411%3F" title="What&#39;s the 411?">What's the 411?</a></i> single "Reminisce", which utilized the same sample from his own single "They Reminisce Over You (T.R.O.Y.)". Rock claims to have done several high-profile remixes that remain unreleased, including one of <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Secret_(Madonna_song)" title="Secret (Madonna song)">Secret</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> He also claims to have produced the original beat for <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Juicy_(The_Notorious_B.I.G._song)" title="Juicy (The Notorious B.I.G. song)">Juicy</a>" and that it was recreated by <a href="/wiki/P._Diddy" class="mw-redirect" title="P. Diddy">P. Diddy</a> and Poke (of <a href="/wiki/Trackmasters_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="Trackmasters Entertainment">Tone &amp; Poke</a> fame), without consent. However, he was invited to produce the remix, which utilizes the same sample as the original—<a href="/wiki/Mtume" title="Mtume">Mtume</a>'s "Juicy Fruit".<sup id="cite_ref-peterock.tribe.net_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peterock.tribe.net-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Although he received no official producer credit, he made the original demo beat for <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">A Tribe Called Quest</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Jazz_(We%27ve_Got)" title="Jazz (We&#39;ve Got)">Jazz (We've Got)</a>", which was then recreated by <a href="/wiki/Q-Tip_(musician)" title="Q-Tip (musician)">Q-Tip</a> on the album <i>The Low End Theory</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-peterock.tribe.net_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peterock.tribe.net-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He remixed <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Enemy (band)">Public Enemy</a>'s "Shut 'em Down" and "Nighttrain" in the same day, starting at 12pm and finishing at 12am.<sup id="cite_ref-peterock.tribe.net_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peterock.tribe.net-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Up until 2003, he created all of his productions on the <a href="/wiki/E-mu_Systems" title="E-mu Systems">E-mu</a> SP-1200, thereafter using the <a href="/wiki/Akai" title="Akai">Akai</a> <a href="/wiki/Music_Production_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Music Production Center">MPC2000XL</a>. He also has a collection of about 90,000 records and looks for records at least once a week.<sup id="cite_ref-peterock.tribe.net_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peterock.tribe.net-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Pete Rock was one of nine artists who participated in <a href="/wiki/Thetruth.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Thetruth.com">thetruth.com</a>'s Remix Project, where he remixed the Sunny Side song "Magical Amount".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pete_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pete Rock himself being overwhelmingly influenced by Black American musical traditions of Soul, Jazz, and R&amp;B music, was able to pay it forward. Pete Rock has had a considerable impact on a number of record producers who have emerged in the hip hop scene since the late 1990s. Critics have favorably compared Detroit producer <a href="/wiki/J_Dilla" title="J Dilla">J Dilla</a> and North Carolina's <a href="/wiki/9th_Wonder" title="9th Wonder">9th Wonder</a> to Rock; both of them worked with Rock during their recording careers. Several of the comparisons stem from the fact that these producers have created the bulk of their productions out of samples, as well as the warm, mellow, and exuberant undertones apparent in their work. Pete Rock himself has added validation to the comparisons with J Dilla by stating "he's the only producer in this game that was just as serious [as me]."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
