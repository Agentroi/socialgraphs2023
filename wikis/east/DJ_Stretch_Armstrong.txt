<p><b>Adrian Bartos</b> (born September 29, 1969) known professionally as <b>DJ Stretch Armstrong</b> is a New York-based <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">DJ</a> and music producer, known as a former co-host of <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> radio show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stretch_Armstrong_and_Bobbito_Show" title="The Stretch Armstrong and Bobbito Show">The Stretch Armstrong and Bobbito Show</a></i>, alongside <a href="/wiki/Bobbito_Garcia" title="Bobbito Garcia">Bobbito Garcia</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DJ_Stretch_Armstrong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bartos grew up in the Upper East Side of New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-Owerko_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Owerko-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He was obsessed with <a href="/wiki/Boombox" title="Boombox">boomboxes</a> as a child and had an older sister who was into early disco music in the seventies, bringing records home to listen to.<sup id="cite_ref-NPR.org_2020_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR.org_2020-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He started DJing in downtown New York City, making his own concert flyers out of cardboard, scissors, and glue.<sup id="cite_ref-Owerko_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Owerko-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-powerHouse_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powerHouse-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Bartos graduated from <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DJ_Stretch_Armstrong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_and_music">Radio and music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DJ_Stretch_Armstrong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radio and music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1990 to 1998, Bartos co-hosted <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stretch_Armstrong_and_Bobbito_Show" title="The Stretch Armstrong and Bobbito Show">The Stretch Armstrong and Bobbito Show</a></i> on Columbia University's <a href="/wiki/WKCR" class="mw-redirect" title="WKCR">WKCR</a>. It featured exclusive demo tapes and in-studio freestyles from many then-unsigned pop artist such as The Brinson Club and hip hop artists such as <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Big_Pun" title="Big Pun">Big Pun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat_Joe" title="Fat Joe">Fat Joe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cam%27ron" title="Cam&#39;ron">Cam'ron</a>, <a href="/wiki/DMX_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="DMX (rapper)">DMX</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fugees" title="Fugees">Fugees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talib_Kweli" title="Talib Kweli">Talib Kweli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_L" title="Big L">Big L</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> who later found great success on major record labels.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 2020 the pair produced an album called <i>No Requests</i> with a group of musicians called the M19, named for a bus in Manhattan connecting the Upper East Side to the Upper West Side.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornish_Lonsdorf_2020_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornish_Lonsdorf_2020-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The album is a reimagining of hip-hop's foundational songs with some updated lyrics and no sampling.<sup id="cite_ref-CBC_2020_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBC_2020-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bartos co-hosted <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR's</a> podcast <i>What's Good with Stretch and Bobbito</i> which began in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-NPR_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nieman_Lab_2017_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nieman_Lab_2017-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WAMU_2017_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WAMU_2017-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The show which was about art, politics, and sports, as well as music, interviewed people such as <a href="/wiki/Dave_Chappelle" title="Dave Chappelle">Dave Chappelle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Blistein_Blistein_2017_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blistein_Blistein_2017-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His musical career, along with Garcia, was made into a movie <a href="/wiki/Stretch_and_Bobbito:_Radio_That_Changed_Lives" title="Stretch and Bobbito: Radio That Changed Lives">Stretch and Bobbito: Radio That Changed Lives</a>, which was picked up by Netflix in 2015 on the 25th anniversary of the pair's radio show.<sup id="cite_ref-Vibe_2016_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibe_2016-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-officialdon_2015_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-officialdon_2015-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vibe_2020_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibe_2020-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Source" title="The Source">The Source Magazine</a> called their show "The Best Hip Hop Radio Show of All Time" in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-Hip-Hop_Radio_Archive_1992_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hip-Hop_Radio_Archive_1992-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Bartos' first book, with archivist Evan Auerbach, <i>No Sleep: NYC Nightlife Flyers 1988-1999 </i>, was released through Powerhouse Books.<sup id="cite_ref-powerHouse_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powerHouse-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bee_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bee-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He explains that it's "a book that chronicles basically the history of New York City nightclubs from ‘88 to ‘99 as told through club flyer art."<sup id="cite_ref-Vibe_2016_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibe_2016-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
