<p><b>Russell Tyrone Jones</b> (November 15, 1968 – November 13, 2004),<sup id="cite_ref-ODB_bio_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODB_bio-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> better known by his <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage name</a> <b>Ol' Dirty Bastard</b> (often abbreviated as <b>ODB</b>), was an American rapper. He was one of the founding members of the <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, a rap group primarily from <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a>, New York City, which rose to mainstream prominence with its 1993 debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Wu-Tang_(36_Chambers)" title="Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)">Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-wutang-corp1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wutang-corp1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His professional success was hampered by frequent legal troubles, including <a href="/wiki/Imprisonment" title="Imprisonment">incarceration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODB_bio_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODB_bio-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He died on November 13, 2004, of an accidental drug overdose, two days before his 36th birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-overdose_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-overdose-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ol' Dirty Bastard was noted for his "outrageously profane, free-associative rhymes delivered in a distinctive half-rapped, half-sung style".<sup id="cite_ref-ODB_bio_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODB_bio-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> His stage name was derived from the 1980 Chinese martial arts film <i>Ol' Dirty and the Bastard</i> (also called <i>An Old Kung Fu Master</i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Yuen_Siu-tien" title="Yuen Siu-tien">Yuen Siu-tien</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> According to fellow Wu-Tang Clan member <a href="/wiki/Method_Man" title="Method Man">Method Man</a>, Ol' Dirty Bastard's name was also a reference to the unique nature of his rapping and, specifically, the fact "there ain't no father to his style."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ol%27_Dirty_Bastard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Early_life.2C_formation_of_the_Wu-Tang_Clan"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life,_formation_of_the_Wu-Tang_Clan">Early life, formation of the Wu-Tang Clan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ol%27_Dirty_Bastard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life, formation of the Wu-Tang Clan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Russell Tyrone Jones was born on November 15, 1968, in the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Greene,_Brooklyn" title="Fort Greene, Brooklyn">Fort Greene</a> section of <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-Vice_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vice-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He and his cousins <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">Robert Diggs</a> and <a href="/wiki/GZA" title="GZA">Gary Grice</a> shared a taste for rap music and martial arts-style movies.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Jones, Diggs, and Grice (later known as Ol' Dirty Bastard, RZA, and GZA respectively) formed the group Force of the Imperial Master, which became known as All in Together Now after their successful underground single of the same name. They eventually added six more members to their group, calling it the Wu-Tang Clan. The group released their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Wu-Tang_(36_Chambers)" title="Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)">Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)</a></i> in 1993, receiving notable commercial and critical success.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ol%27_Dirty_Bastard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ol' Dirty Bastard's solo career began March 28, 1995, at the age of 26. His first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Return_to_the_36_Chambers:_The_Dirty_Version" title="Return to the 36 Chambers: The Dirty Version">Return to the 36 Chambers: The Dirty Version</a></i>, spawned the hit singles "<a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Zoo_(song)" title="Brooklyn Zoo (song)">Brooklyn Zoo</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Shimmy_Shimmy_Ya" title="Shimmy Shimmy Ya">Shimmy Shimmy Ya</a>", which helped propel the album to <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_certification" title="Music recording certification">platinum status</a>.  The album's sound was noted by several music writers as being as "raw and gritty" as <i>36 Chambers</i>, with RZA and <a href="/wiki/4th_Disciple" title="4th Disciple">4th Disciple</a> producing beats of an even more minimalist and stripped-down style than on the group's debut album. In this same year, Ol' Dirty Bastard collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Mariah_Carey" title="Mariah Carey">Mariah Carey</a> for the remix version of her single "<a href="/wiki/Fantasy_(Mariah_Carey_song)#Remixes" title="Fantasy (Mariah Carey song)">Fantasy</a>".
</p><p>It was around this time that Ol' Dirty Bastard gained notoriety when, as he was being profiled for an <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> biography, he took two of his three children by <a href="/wiki/Limousine" title="Limousine">limousine</a> to a New York State welfare office to cash a $375 welfare check and receive <a href="/wiki/Supplemental_Nutrition_Assistance_Program" title="Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program">food stamps</a><sup id="cite_ref-foodstamps_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foodstamps-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> while his latest album was still in the top 10 of the US charts. The entire incident was filmed by an MTV camera crew and was broadcast nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Although he had recently received a $45,000 cash advance for his first solo album and was earning a cut of the profits from the Wu-Tang Clan's debut album, Ol' Dirty Bastard was still listed as eligible for welfare and food stamps due to the fact that he had not yet filed his taxes for the current year. His caseworker revoked his eligibility after seeing the MTV segment, and the incident was presented by critics of <a href="/wiki/Welfare" title="Welfare">welfare</a> as representative of the allegedly widespread <a href="/wiki/Welfare_fraud" title="Welfare fraud">abuse and fraud</a> that led to the significant <a href="/wiki/Personal_Responsibility_and_Work_Opportunity_Act" title="Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Act">welfare reforms enacted in 1996</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1997, Ol' Dirty Bastard appeared on the Wu-Tang Clan's second and most commercially successful work, the double album <i><a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Forever" title="Wu-Tang Forever">Wu-Tang Forever</a></i>. He had fewer appearances on this album than the group's debut, contributing to one solo track ("Dog Shit"), three verses ("Maria", "Reunited", "Heaterz"), one hook ("As High as Wu-Tang Get"), and a spoken introduction/refrain ("<a href="/wiki/Triumph_(song)" title="Triumph (song)">Triumph</a>").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In February 1998, Ol' Dirty Bastard witnessed a car accident from the window of his <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> recording studio. He and a friend ran to the accident scene and organized about a dozen onlookers, who assisted in lifting the 1996 <a href="/wiki/Ford_Mustang" title="Ford Mustang">Ford Mustang</a>—rescuing a 4-year-old girl from the wreckage. She was taken to a hospital with first and second-degree <a href="/wiki/Burn" title="Burn">burns</a>. Using a <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">false name</a>, Ol' Dirty Bastard visited the girl in the hospital frequently until he was spotted by members of the media.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The evening following the traffic accident, Ol' Dirty Bastard rushed on-stage unexpectedly as <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Colvin" title="Shawn Colvin">Shawn Colvin</a> took the stage to give her acceptance speech for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Song_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Song of the Year">Song of the Year</a> at the <a href="/wiki/40th_Annual_Grammy_Awards" title="40th Annual Grammy Awards">1998 Grammy Awards</a>, and he announced he had recently purchased expensive clothes in anticipation of winning the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Grammy Award for Best Rap Album</a> that he lost to <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">Puff Daddy</a>. As Ol' Dirty Bastard took the stage to a round of applause, he asked the audience, "Please calm down, the music and everything. It's nice that I went and bought me an outfit today that costed a lot of money today, you know what I mean? 'Cause I figured that Wu-Tang was gonna win. I don't know how you all see it, but when it comes to the children, Wu-Tang is for the children. We teach the children. You know what I mean? Puffy is good, but Wu-Tang is the best. Okay? I want you all to know that this is ODB, and I love you all. Peace!" The incident was widely covered in the media.<sup id="cite_ref-Time13_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time13-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The morning after the Grammy Awards, he appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Howard_Stern_Show" title="The Howard Stern Show">The Howard Stern Show</a></i>, to discuss the incident.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1999, Ol' Dirty Bastard wrote and recorded his second studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Nigga_Please" title="Nigga Please">Nigga Please</a></i>, between jail sentences. The album received notable commercial success, although it failed to parallel the critical praise of his debut. This release included the single "<a href="/wiki/Got_Your_Money" title="Got Your Money">Got Your Money</a>", which garnered worldwide chart success. The song was produced by <a href="/wiki/The_Neptunes" title="The Neptunes">The Neptunes</a> and featured chorus vocals by <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Kelis" title="Kelis">Kelis</a>.
</p><p>In 1999, Ol' Dirty Bastard was paid $30,000 to appear on <a href="/wiki/Insane_Clown_Posse" title="Insane Clown Posse">Insane Clown Posse</a>'s fifth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Amazing_Jeckel_Brothers" title="The Amazing Jeckel Brothers">The Amazing Jeckel Brothers</a></i>. Completing his track in two days, his recording consisted of his "rambling about bitches". Insane Clown Posse re-recorded the track and re-edited Ol' Dirty Bastard's vocals in order to form four rhymes out of his rambling, titling the song "Bitches".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2001, with Ol' Dirty Bastard again in jail for <a href="/wiki/Crack_cocaine" title="Crack cocaine">crack cocaine</a> possession, his record label <a href="/wiki/Elektra_Records" title="Elektra Records">Elektra Records</a> made the decision to release a greatest hits album (despite there being only two albums in his back catalog) in order to both end their contract with the artist (<a class="mw-selflink-fragment" href="#Legal_issues">see below section</a>), and to profit from the publicity generated by his legal troubles. After the contract with Elektra was terminated, the label D-3 Records released the album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Trials_and_Tribulations_of_Russell_Jones" title="The Trials and Tribulations of Russell Jones">The Trials and Tribulations of Russell Jones</a></i> in 2002, composed of tracks compiled without Ol' Dirty Bastard's input.
</p><p>In 2003, the day he was released from prison, Ol' Dirty Bastard signed a contract with <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>. Living at his mother's home under <a href="/wiki/House_arrest" title="House arrest">house arrest</a> and with a court-ordered <a href="/wiki/Probation" title="Probation">probation</a>, he used his criminal record to title his <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a> special, <i>Inside Out: Ol' Dirty Bastard on Parole</i>. He also managed to record his third album <i><a href="/wiki/A_Son_Unique" title="A Son Unique">A Son Unique</a></i>, which was originally scheduled to be released through <a href="/wiki/Damon_Dash" title="Damon Dash">Dame Dash Music Group</a> in 2004; as of 2020, however, the album has never been released. In October 2004, one month before his death, his last collaboration was with <a href="/wiki/Jon_B." title="Jon B.">Jon B.</a> on the track "Everytime" from the album, <i><a href="/wiki/Stronger_Everyday" title="Stronger Everyday">Stronger Everyday</a></i>. In 2005, five months after his death, he appeared posthumously on the song "Blah-Blah-Blah" by <a href="/wiki/Brooke_Valentine" title="Brooke Valentine">Brooke Valentine</a> on her debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Chain_Letter_(album)" title="Chain Letter (album)">Chain Letter</a></i>.
</p><p>On July 17, 2004, Ol' Dirty Bastard had his second to last live performance at the <a href="/wiki/Rock_the_Bells" title="Rock the Bells">Rock the Bells</a> hip-hop festival in <a href="/wiki/San_Bernardino,_California" title="San Bernardino, California">San Bernardino, California</a>, with the rest of the Wu-Tang Clan.
</p><p>On July 18, 2004, his final live performance was at the <a href="/wiki/Gathering_of_the_Juggalos" title="Gathering of the Juggalos">Gathering of the Juggalos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Garrettsville,_Ohio" title="Garrettsville, Ohio">Garrettsville, Ohio</a>.
</p><p>In August 2017 in an interview on <a href="/wiki/WQHT" title="WQHT">Hot 97</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a> member <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a> confirmed that the new Wu-Tang album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Saga_Continues_(Wu-Tang_Clan_album)" title="The Saga Continues (Wu-Tang Clan album)">The Saga Continues</a></i>, will contain unreleased vocals by Ol' Dirty Bastard.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To celebrate Ol' Dirty Bastard's birthday, "Intoxicated" from the unreleased album <i>A Son Unique</i> was released as a single on November 15, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ol%27_Dirty_Bastard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1993, Ol' Dirty Bastard was convicted of second-degree <a href="/wiki/Assault" title="Assault">assault</a> for an attempted <a href="/wiki/Robbery" title="Robbery">robbery</a> and in 1994, he was shot in the abdomen following an argument with another rapper.<sup id="cite_ref-ODB_bio_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODB_bio-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  In 1997, he was arrested for failure to pay <a href="/wiki/Child_support" title="Child support">child support</a> for three of his children.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 1998, he pleaded guilty to attempted assault on his wife and was the victim of a <a href="/wiki/Home_invasion" title="Home invasion">home invasion</a> robbery at his girlfriend's house. He was shot in the back and arm but the wounds were superficial.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 1998, only days after being shot in a push-in robbery at his girlfriend's house in Brooklyn, he was arrested for <a href="/wiki/Shoplifting" title="Shoplifting">shoplifting</a> a pair of $50 shoes from a Sneaker Stadium store in <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Beach,_Virginia" title="Virginia Beach, Virginia">Virginia Beach, Virginia</a>, although he was carrying close to $500 in cash at the time. He was issued <a href="/wiki/Bench_warrant" class="mw-redirect" title="Bench warrant">bench warrants</a> by the Virginia Beach Sheriff's Department to stand trial after he failed to appear in court numerous times. He was arrested for <a href="/wiki/Intimidation" title="Intimidation">criminal threatening</a> after a series of confrontations in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> a few weeks later, and was then re-arrested for similar charges not long after that.  During a traffic stop, the details of which remain clouded in multiple versions of events, he was arrested for <a href="/wiki/Attempted_murder" title="Attempted murder">attempted murder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Criminal_possession_of_a_weapon" title="Criminal possession of a weapon">criminal weapon possession</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vice_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vice-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The case was later dismissed.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 14, 1999,<sup id="cite_ref-Lowebook_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lowebook-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> shortly before the killing of <a href="/wiki/Shooting_of_Amadou_Diallo" class="mw-redirect" title="Shooting of Amadou Diallo">Amadou Diallo</a>, two officers from the Street Crimes Unit fired eight shots at Ol' Dirty Bastard and accused him of firing at them after they stopped his car in Bedford-Stuyvesant. Ol' Dirty Bastard was cleared by a grand jury and insisted that the officers had been scared by his cellular phone. No weapons or shell casings (besides those of the officers) were found in the vehicle or near the scene.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 1999, he was arrested for <a href="/wiki/Driver%27s_license" title="Driver&#39;s license">driving without a license</a> and for being a <a href="/wiki/Felony" title="Felony">convicted felon</a> wearing a <a href="/wiki/Bulletproof_vest" title="Bulletproof vest">bulletproof vest</a>. At the time, it was illegal for felons to own body armor.<sup id="cite_ref-vice_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vice-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Back in New York weeks later, he was arrested for <a href="/wiki/Drug_possession" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug possession">drug possession</a> of <a href="/wiki/Crack_cocaine" title="Crack cocaine">crack cocaine</a> and for traffic offenses. With multiple cases in the past and present, he was arrested with <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">marijuana</a> and 20 vials of crack.<sup id="cite_ref-NME01_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NME01-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2000, he escaped from his court-mandated <a href="/wiki/Drug_rehabilitation" title="Drug rehabilitation">drug treatment facility</a> and spent one month as a fugitive. During his time on the run, he met with RZA and spent some time in their recording studio. He then appeared onstage at the <a href="/wiki/Hammerstein_Ballroom" title="Hammerstein Ballroom">Hammerstein Ballroom</a> in New York drinking from a bottle at the record release party for <i><a href="/wiki/The_W" title="The W">The W</a></i>, the third Wu-Tang Clan album. In late November 2000, while still a fugitive, he was arrested outside a <a href="/wiki/South_Philadelphia" title="South Philadelphia">South Philadelphia</a> <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald&#39;s">McDonald's</a> (at 29th and Gray's Ferry Ave.), after he drew a crowd while signing autographs. He spent several days in a <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> jail and was later extradited to New York City. A <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> court sentenced him to two to four years incarceration. He was released on parole on May 1, 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, his <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">FBI</a> file was released to the public after a <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_Information_Act_(United_States)" title="Freedom of Information Act (United States)">Freedom of Information Act</a> request.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> It contains details of numerous crimes, such as alleged connections to three murders, a <a href="/wiki/Shootout" title="Shootout">shootout</a> with the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department" title="New York City Police Department">New York City Police Department</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Racketeer_Influenced_and_Corrupt_Organizations_Act" title="Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act">Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act</a> investigation against the Wu-Tang Clan.<sup id="cite_ref-FBIfile_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FBIfile-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ol%27_Dirty_Bastard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:ODB_Mural.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/ODB_Mural.jpg/220px-ODB_Mural.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/ODB_Mural.jpg/330px-ODB_Mural.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/ODB_Mural.jpg/440px-ODB_Mural.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a><figcaption>Mural of Ol' Dirty Bastard</figcaption></figure>
<p>Leading up to his death, Ol' Dirty Bastard's legal troubles and eccentric behavior made him "something of a folk hero", according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> writer Michael Agger.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Music writer Steve Huey wrote: "it was difficult for observers to tell whether Ol' Dirty Bastard's wildly erratic behavior was the result of serious drug problems or genuine <a href="/wiki/Mental_disorder" title="Mental disorder">mental instability</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-ODB_bio_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODB_bio-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">The Atlantic</a></i> contributing editor and music biographer James Parker, Ol' Dirty Bastard had been diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_disorder" title="Bipolar disorder">bipolar disorder</a> around 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ol' Dirty Bastard collapsed at approximately 4:35<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>p.m. (EST) on November<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>13, 2004 (two days before his 36th birthday), at <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a>'s recording studio (36 Chambers Records LLC on West 34th Street in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>); he was pronounced dead at the scene.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> His funeral was held at Brooklyn's <a href="/wiki/Christian_Cultural_Center" title="Christian Cultural Center">Christian Cultural Center</a> and drew a crowd of thousands.
</p><p>The official cause of death was a <a href="/wiki/Drug_overdose" title="Drug overdose">drug overdose</a>; an autopsy found a lethal mixture of <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> and the prescription opioid <a href="/wiki/Tramadol" title="Tramadol">tramadol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The overdose was ruled accidental and witnesses say Ol' Dirty Bastard complained of chest pain on the day he died.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
Mourning the decline of Jones' mental and physical health, RZA wrote in his 2009 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tao_of_Wu" title="The Tao of Wu">The Tao of Wu</a></i>:</p><blockquote><p>Trust me, the man who became ODB, Ason Unique, my cousin, he was a scientist and a minor prophet... People may not know this from the outrageous character he played, but ODB was a visionary. But he decayed, he lost that vision... From the time they put him in jail to all the drugs he was doing to all the stress he went through with his family, it took away his ability to see. And this night, he sat there and looked me in the eye and said, "RZA, I don't understand." ... Now, I know that right there, right when he said that—we lost him. Eight hours later, ODB was gone.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
