<p><b>Gregory Edward Jacobs</b> (August 25, 1963 – April 22, 2021), known professionally as  <b>Shock G</b> and by his alter ego <b>Humpty Hump</b>, was an American rapper and musician who was best known as the lead vocalist of the <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Digital_Underground" title="Digital Underground">Digital Underground</a>. He was responsible for Digital Underground's "<a href="/wiki/The_Humpty_Dance" title="The Humpty Dance">The Humpty Dance</a>", <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">2Pac</a>'s breakthrough single "<a href="/wiki/I_Get_Around_(Tupac_Shakur_song)" title="I Get Around (Tupac Shakur song)">I Get Around</a>", and co-producer of 2Pac's debut album <i><a href="/wiki/2Pacalypse_Now" title="2Pacalypse Now">2Pacalypse Now</a></i>.
</p>

<p>Gregory Edward Jacobs was born on August 25, 1963, in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He spent most of his childhood moving around the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> with his family, eventually settling in <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">Tampa, Florida</a>. As a drummer he won the 1978 "Most Talented" trophy at Greco Junior High School, but after relocating to <a href="/wiki/Queens,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Queens, New York">Queens, New York</a> (as a result of his parents' divorce), he traded his drums in for a set of turntables upon discovering and marvelling over <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> while the art form was still in an underground developmental stage. He was mentored in the craft by his cousin Rene Negron (a.k.a. DJ-Stretch), and their close friend Shawn Trone (a.k.a. MC Shah-T of the <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a>-rap group <a href="/wiki/No_Face_(rap_duo)" title="No Face (rap duo)">No Face</a>) who suggested Greg use the name "Shah-G". Jacobs liked the idea, but mistakenly thought his friend said "<i>Shock</i>-G", and began using that name instead.
</p><p>After returning to Tampa less than two years later, he dropped out of <a href="/wiki/Chamberlain_High_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Chamberlain High School">Chamberlain High School</a> to form the Master Blasters, a mobile DJ crew which featured three DJs and four <a href="/wiki/Emcees" class="mw-redirect" title="Emcees">emcees</a> at its height.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> They performed at parties, and also for the crowds at Riverfront Park's outdoor Sunday gatherings, eventually capturing the interest of Tony Stone, a program director at WTMP radio, which was the city's primary <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> station. Tony offered Jacobs, who was sixteen at the time, a job <a href="/wiki/DJing" class="mw-redirect" title="DJing">DJing</a> on the air, and for a short while, as "Gregory Racker", he was the youngest <a href="/wiki/Radio_personality" title="Radio personality">radio personality</a> in central Florida with a regular time slot.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
After being fired for playing the fifteen-minute-long album version of "<a href="/wiki/(Not_Just)_Knee_Deep" title="(Not Just) Knee Deep">(Not Just) Knee Deep</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Funkadelic" title="Funkadelic">Funkadelic</a> in a five-minute time slot, and also after tensions with his father escalated, Jacobs found himself <a href="/wiki/Backpacking_(hiking)" title="Backpacking (hiking)">backpacking</a> the United States for a few years, drifting through odd jobs and petty criminal adventures. It was during this excursion that his focus switched from DJing to keyboard playing, and while utilizing piano practice-rooms at music stores and colleges around the country, he effectively taught himself to play the piano.
</p><p>Deciding to pursue music seriously, he returned home, quickly obtained a <a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">diploma</a>, and began attending <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough_Community_College" title="Hillsborough Community College">Hillsborough Community College</a>, where he studied <a href="/wiki/Music_theory" title="Music theory">music theory</a> under Jim Burge and piano under Patricia J. Trice. It was there at HCC that he met and formed a bond with Kenneth Waters, and the two began performing together under various names including The Chill Factor,<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia.com_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and also The Four Horsemen, which included MC Skoobie-D, and the MD Dazzlin Doc-P who had recently moved to Tampa from the Bronx, <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a>'s birthplace. Then in 1985, after two years of producing local artists for hire, playing solo piano gigs around town, performing with Kenny, and being a keyboardist in Warren Allen Brooks' band, Greg and his aspiring-actress girlfriend (Davita Watts) set their sights beyond Tampa, and eloped to Los Angeles in search of greater opportunity. There he played keyboards in Kenny McCloud's <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>-<a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a> band Onyx before leaving Los Angeles and finally arriving in the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a> where he found work in an Oakland music store, and where his group Digital Underground would form a few years later.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia.com_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_Underground">Digital Underground</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Digital Underground">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soon after relocating to Oakland, California, Shock G formed <a href="/wiki/Digital_Underground" title="Digital Underground">Digital Underground</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Chopmaster_J" class="mw-redirect" title="Chopmaster J">Chopmaster J</a>, and the late Kenneth Waters (a.k.a. Kenny-K). After around 15 months of unsuccessful negotiations with various small record companies, in 1988 the trio finally released a <a href="/wiki/12-inch_single" class="mw-redirect" title="12-inch single">12-inch single</a> on Macola Records. It featured "Your Life's a Cartoon" as the A-side and "Underwater Rimes" as the B-side. Both songs were penned, produced, and performed by Jacobs, who also sketched the cartoonish cover illustrations. The record included the logo for Digital Underground's startup label, TNT, as well as Macola's logo. TNT was also founded by Tupac Shakur's management CEO Atron Gregory. In 1989, the group signed with <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Boy_Records" title="Tommy Boy Records">Tommy Boy Records</a> and released "<a href="/wiki/Doowutchyalike" title="Doowutchyalike">Doowutchyalike</a>", receiving minimal radio airplay but became an underground hit. Its video was more successful, reaching number 40 on the MTV's top 100 videos of the year. "Doowutchyalike" paved the way for Digital Underground's debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Sex_Packets" title="Sex Packets">Sex Packets</a></i> and the highest-charting song of their career "<a href="/wiki/The_Humpty_Dance" title="The Humpty Dance">The Humpty Dance</a>" both released in early 1990, and both achieving platinum sales certifications by the <a href="/wiki/RIAA" class="mw-redirect" title="RIAA">RIAA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The latter was rapped by "Humpty Hump," the most flamboyant of Shock G's several <a href="/wiki/Alter_ego" title="Alter ego">alter egos</a>. By that time, Digital Underground had expanded significantly, with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Fuze" title="DJ Fuze">DJ Fuze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Money-B" title="Money-B">Money-B</a>, and Schmoovy-Schmoov joining the group, and with Ramone "Pee Wee" Gooden and Tupac Shakur joining by 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_identities">Other identities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other identities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jacobs performed under many aliases he developed over his career, resulting in characters that were maintained with such reality that they were believed to be separate people by both fans and industry insiders.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> While he rapped in his normal voice as Shock G, as "Humpty Hump" he adopted a more nasal sound as part of this character's exaggerated buffoon persona that included garish clothes and <a href="/wiki/Groucho_glasses" title="Groucho glasses">Groucho glasses</a>. A fictional biography was included in Digital Underground's press kit stating that Humpty Hump's real name was Edward Ellington Humphrey III and he wore the Groucho glasses after burning his nose in a <a href="/wiki/Deep_fryer" title="Deep fryer">deep-fryer</a> accident.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Jacobs made public appearances as one person or the other, but at live shows and video shoots he would use a stand-in or camera tricks to maintain the illusion.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Jacobs also sometimes performed as other characters including MC Blowfish, Icey-Mike, The Computer Woman, ButtaFly, and Peanut Hakeem.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_and_film_work">Television and film work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Television and film work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shock G's TV appearances include <i><a href="/wiki/Showtime_at_the_Apollo" title="Showtime at the Apollo">Showtime at the Apollo</a></i> in 1992, several <i><a href="/wiki/The_Arsenio_Hall_Show" title="The Arsenio Hall Show">The Arsenio Hall Show</a></i> performances between 1990 and 1994, and several live MTV performances, including MTV Spring Break 1990 in Daytona Beach, <i>Yo MTV Raps</i> (performing live with <a href="/wiki/Ed_Lover" title="Ed Lover">Ed Lover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Dr%C3%A9" title="Doctor Dré">Doctor Dré</a>) in 1991, <i><a href="/wiki/Club_MTV" title="Club MTV">Club MTV Live</a></i> (with Downtown Julie Brown) in 1992, and MTV Jams in 1994. Most of these consisted of music performances with either Digital Underground or 2Pac; however, on an episode of the 1991 sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Drexell%27s_Class" title="Drexell&#39;s Class">Drexell's Class</a></i>, Jacobs played a small acting role as a furnace repairman. Within the show's story, the title character, Otis Drexell, insists that the furnace repairman looks exactly like Humpty Hump, but neither he nor his coworker (<a href="/wiki/Jason_Priestley" title="Jason Priestley">Jason Priestley</a>) have heard of any such hip-hop artist, especially not one with such a ridiculous name. The episode ends with a live performance of Digital Underground's "No Nose Job" on a cruise ship full of <i>Sports Illustrated</i> swimsuit models, which is presented as a scene from one of Mr. Drexell's dreams.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With his Digital Underground band members, Jacobs appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Dan_Aykroyd" title="Dan Aykroyd">Dan Aykroyd</a>-directed comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/Nothing_but_Trouble_(1991_film)" title="Nothing but Trouble (1991 film)">Nothing but Trouble</a></i> (1991) appearing as both Shock G and Humpty Hump. The group (including Tupac Shakur) makes a cameo music performance, as well as play a small character role in the film as themselves. Since then, Jacobs has appeared in a handful of music documentaries, including <i>Thug Angel: Life of an Outlaw</i> (2000) about Tupac Shakur, and <i>Parliament Funkadelic: One Nation Under a Groove</i> (1996) about George Clinton &amp; P-Funk, both of which received heavy TV rotation, and both of which relied heavily on Jacobs' commentary.
</p><p>On June 24, 2011, Shock G was featured on an episode of the podcast "You Had To Be There" with comedians <a href="/wiki/Nikki_Glaser" title="Nikki Glaser">Nikki Glaser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sara_Schaefer" title="Sara Schaefer">Sara Schaefer</a>.
</p>

<h3><span id="Production.2C_solo_work.2C_and_miscellaneous"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Production,_solo_work,_and_miscellaneous">Production, solo work, and miscellaneous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Production, solo work, and miscellaneous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to his work with Digital Underground, Shock G found moderate success as a solo artist and music producer. In 1993, Shock G produced <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a>'s breakthrough platinum single "I Get Around" as well as guest starred on the single and music video, and went on to produce Tupac's "So Many Tears" from his multi-platinum 1995 album <i><a href="/wiki/Me_Against_the_World" title="Me Against the World">Me Against the World</a></i>. Tupac's first published work was while still a member of Digital Underground when he appeared on the 1991 song and video "Same Song", which also appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Chevy_Chase" title="Chevy Chase">Chevy Chase</a>, Dan Aykroyd and <a href="/wiki/Demi_Moore" title="Demi Moore">Demi Moore</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Nothing_but_Trouble_(1991_film)" title="Nothing but Trouble (1991 film)">Nothing but Trouble</a></i>. Shock co-produced Tupac's debut album <i><a href="/wiki/2Pacalypse_Now" title="2Pacalypse Now">2Pacalypse Now</a></i>. Shock G appeared as a producer and guest artist on fellow <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a>-based rap group <a href="/wiki/The_Luniz" class="mw-redirect" title="The Luniz">The Luniz</a> platinum debut release <i><a href="/wiki/Operation_Stackola" title="Operation Stackola">Operation Stackola</a></i> in 1995, also appearing as a guest emcee in the "I Got 5 on It" Bay Ballers Remix and video.
</p><p>In 1996 the <a href="/wiki/Wayans_brothers" class="mw-redirect" title="Wayans brothers">Wayans brothers</a>' film <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Be_a_Menace_to_South_Central_While_Drinking_Your_Juice_in_the_Hood" title="Don&#39;t Be a Menace to South Central While Drinking Your Juice in the Hood">Don't Be a Menace to South Central While Drinking Your Juice in the Hood</a></i> featured the Shock G song "We Got More". The song, which featured Oakland rappers Luniz was used for three different scenes in the film, and is featured in two different places on the soundtrack, making it the only song to appear twice on one soundtrack. In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a> included the Shock G produced "Love Sign" on his triple-CD <i><a href="/wiki/Crystal_Ball_(album_set)" class="mw-redirect" title="Crystal Ball (album set)">Crystal Ball</a></i> album. Shock G has toured and performed on stage with <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)">George Clinton</a> and <a href="/wiki/P-Funk" class="mw-redirect" title="P-Funk">P-Funk</a> including a guest performance with Clinton at <a href="/wiki/Woodstock_1999" class="mw-redirect" title="Woodstock 1999">Woodstock 1999</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, Shock G produced the single "Risky Business" for Los Angeles underground artist <a href="/wiki/Murs_(rapper)" title="Murs (rapper)">Murs</a>, and also appeared in the video, as himself and as Humpty Hump. Murs performed this song live with Shock G at the Paid Dues festival, and also featured him as his stage DJ/music conductor on a 2-month extensive Definitive Jux label U.S. and Canada tour.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  On January 20, 2009, Shock G's single "Cherry Flava'd Email" was renamed and released as a special edition called "Cherry Flava'd Election" to commemorate the inauguration of President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On April 22, 2021, Jacobs was found dead in a motel room in Tampa, Florida, at age 57.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> On June 10, 2021, the Hillsborough County Medical Examiner announced that Shock G's death was caused by an accidental overdose of <a href="/wiki/Fentanyl" title="Fentanyl">fentanyl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methamphetamine_in_the_United_States" title="Methamphetamine in the United States">methamphetamine</a> and ethanol (alcohol).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-obituarry_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obituarry-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Katie_Atkinson_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katie_Atkinson-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jacobs was interred at Parklawn Memorial Cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Dunedin,_Florida" title="Dunedin, Florida">Dunedin, Florida</a>.
</p>
