<p><b>Lance Taylor</b> (born on April 17, 1957), also known as <b>Afrika Bambaataa</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;f&#39; in &#39;find&#39;">f</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span><span class="wrap"> </span><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/ɑː/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;father&#39;">ɑː</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oxfordmusiconline.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxfordmusiconline.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/DJ" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ">DJ</a>, rapper, and producer from the <a href="/wiki/South_Bronx" title="South Bronx">South Bronx</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_guide_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_guide-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oxfordmusiconline.com_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxfordmusiconline.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He is notable for releasing a series of genre-defining <a href="/wiki/Electro_(music)" title="Electro (music)">electro</a> tracks in the 1980s that influenced the development of <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> culture.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Afrika Bambaataa is one of the originators of <a href="/wiki/Breakbeat" title="Breakbeat">breakbeat</a> <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">DJing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Modulations_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Modulations-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Through his co-opting of the street gang the <a href="/wiki/Black_Spades" title="Black Spades">Black Spades</a> into the music and culture-oriented <a href="/wiki/Universal_Zulu_Nation" title="Universal Zulu Nation">Universal Zulu Nation</a>, he has helped spread hip hop culture throughout the world.<sup id="cite_ref-zulu_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zulu-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
In May 2016, Bambaataa left his position as head of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Zulu_Nation" title="Universal Zulu Nation">Universal Zulu Nation</a> due to multiple allegations of <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">child sexual abuse</a> dating as far back as the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afrika_Bambaataa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born Lance Taylor to <a href="/wiki/Jamaicans" title="Jamaicans">Jamaican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbadians" title="Barbadians">Barbadian</a> immigrants,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Bambaataa grew up in the <a href="/wiki/Bronx_River_Houses" title="Bronx River Houses">Bronx River Projects</a>, with an <a href="/wiki/Activist" class="mw-redirect" title="Activist">activist</a> mother and uncle. As a child, he was exposed to the <a href="/wiki/Black_theology" title="Black theology">black liberation movement</a> and witnessed debates between his mother and uncle regarding the conflicting ideologies in the movement. He was exposed to his mother's extensive and eclectic record collection.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Street_gang" class="mw-redirect" title="Street gang">Gangs</a> in the area became the law, clearing their <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/turf" class="extiw" title="wikt:turf">turf</a> of drug dealers, assisting with community health programs and both fighting and partying to keep members and turf.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bambaataa was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Spades" title="Black Spades">Black Spades</a>. He quickly rose to the position of "warlord" in one of the divisions. As warlord, it was his job to build ranks and expand the turf of the young Spades. He was not afraid to cross turfs to forge relationships with other gangs, and their members. As a result, the Spades became the biggest gang in the city in terms of both membership and turf.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After Bambaataa won an essay contest that earned him a trip to Africa, his worldview shifted.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He had seen the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Zulu_(1964_film)" title="Zulu (1964 film)">Zulu</a></i> and was impressed with the solidarity exhibited by the <a href="/wiki/Zulu_people" title="Zulu people">Zulu</a> in that film. During his trip to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, the communities he visited inspired him to create one in his own neighborhood.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He changed his name to Afrika Bambaataa Aasim, adopting the name of the Zulu chief <a href="/wiki/Bhambatha" title="Bhambatha">Bhambatha</a>, who led an armed rebellion against unfair economic practices in early 20th-century South Africa. He told people that his name was Zulu for "affectionate leader".<sup id="cite_ref-chang_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Bambaataa formed The "Bronx River Organization" as an alternative to the Black Spades.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afrika_Bambaataa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the oft nebulous timeline of hip-hop origins, there are conflicting accounts of when Bambaataa began hosting parties. Some suggest he began as early as 1970, predating noted "father of hip hop" <a href="/wiki/DJ_Kool_Herc" title="DJ Kool Herc">Kool Herc</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> while others contend he began after Herc in 1976, in fact, attending and drawing inspiration from Kool Herc parties.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He vowed to use hip-hop to draw angry kids out of gangs and form the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Zulu_Nation" title="Universal Zulu Nation">Universal Zulu Nation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-efioee_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-efioee-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Keef_Cowboy" title="Keef Cowboy">Robert Keith Wiggins</a>, a.k.a. "Cowboy" of <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Flash_and_the_Furious_Five" title="Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five">Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five</a>, is credited with naming hip-hop. The term became a common phrase used by MCs as part of a <a href="/wiki/Scat_singing" title="Scat singing">scat</a>-inspired style of rhyming.<sup id="cite_ref-Total_Chaos_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Total_Chaos-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>The term was first used in print to refer to the music by reporter Robert Flipping, Jr. in a February 1979 article in the New Pittsburgh Courier,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and to refer to the culture in a January 1982 interview of Afrika Bambaataa by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Holman_(filmmaker)" title="Michael Holman (filmmaker)">Michael Holman</a> in the East Village Eye.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The term gained further currency in September of that year in another Bambaataa interview in The Village Voice,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Hager" title="Steven Hager">Steven Hager</a>.
</p><p>In 1982, Bambaataa and his followers – a group of dancers, artists, and <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">DJs</a> – went outside the United States on the first hip-hop tour.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He saw that the hip hop tours would be the key to help expand hip hop and his Universal Zulu Nation. In addition it would help promote the values of hip hop that he believed are based on peace, unity, love, and having fun. He brought peace to the gangs; many artists and gang members say that "hip hop saved a lot of lives".<sup id="cite_ref-efioee_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-efioee-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> His influence inspired many overseas artists like the <a href="/wiki/French_hip_hop" title="French hip hop">French rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/MC_Solaar" title="MC Solaar">MC Solaar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-efioee_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-efioee-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was a popular DJ in The South Bronx rap scene and became known not only as Afrika Bambaataa but also as the "Master of Records".<sup id="cite_ref-Hip_Hop_America_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hip_Hop_America-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> He established two rap crews: the <a href="/wiki/Jazzy_5" class="mw-redirect" title="Jazzy 5">Jazzy 5</a> including MCs Master Ice, Mr. Freeze, Master Bee, Master D.E.E, and AJ Les, and the second crew referred to as <a href="/wiki/Soulsonic_Force" title="Soulsonic Force">Soulsonic Force</a> including Mr. Biggs, Pow Wow and Emcee G.L.O.B.E.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1982, Bambaataa, who was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Kraftwerk" title="Kraftwerk">Kraftwerk</a>'s futuristic electronic music, debuted at <a href="/wiki/Roxy_NYC" class="mw-redirect" title="Roxy NYC">The Roxy</a> a test cassette of <a href="/wiki/EBN-OZN" class="mw-redirect" title="EBN-OZN">EBN-OZN</a>'s ground breaking, 12-inch white rap/spoken word "AEIOU Sometimes Y". It was the first commercially released American single ever made on a computer, a <a href="/wiki/Fairlight_CMI" title="Fairlight CMI">Fairlight CMI</a>, ushering in the era of music computer sampling.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In that same year, Bambaataa and Soulsonic Force stopped performing with a live band, and began to use only technology. Bambaataa credited the pioneering Japanese <a href="/wiki/Electropop" title="Electropop">electropop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Magic_Orchestra" title="Yellow Magic Orchestra">Yellow Magic Orchestra</a>, whose work he sampled, as an inspiration.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_ymo_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian_ymo-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wire_1996_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wire_1996-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He also borrowed a keyboard hook from German <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic</a> pioneers <a href="/wiki/Kraftwerk" title="Kraftwerk">Kraftwerk</a> and was provided the electronic <a href="/wiki/Roland_TR-808" title="Roland TR-808">Roland TR-808</a> "beat-box" by producer <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Baker_(musician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur Baker (musician)">Arthur Baker</a> and synthesizer player <a href="/wiki/John_Robie" title="John Robie">John Robie</a>. That resulted in "<a href="/wiki/Planet_Rock_(song)" title="Planet Rock (song)">Planet Rock</a>", which went to gold status and generated an entire school of "electro-boogie" rap and dance music. Bambaataa formed his own label to release the Time Zone Compilation. He created "<a href="/wiki/Turntablism" title="Turntablism">turntablism</a>" as its own subgenre and the ratification of "<a href="/wiki/Electronica" title="Electronica">electronica</a>" as an industry-certified trend in the late 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_of_the_Zulu_Nation">Birth of the Zulu Nation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afrika_Bambaataa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Birth of the Zulu Nation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the late 1970s, Bambaataa formed what became known as the Universal Zulu Nation, a group of socially and politically aware rappers, <a href="/wiki/B-boy" class="mw-redirect" title="B-boy">B-boys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> artists and other people involved in <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip hop culture">hip hop culture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hip_Hop_America_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hip_Hop_America-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> By 1977, inspired by DJ Kool Herc and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Dee" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ Dee">DJ Dee</a>, and after <a href="/wiki/Disco_King_Mario" title="Disco King Mario">Disco King Mario</a> loaned him his first equipment, Bambaataa began organizing <a href="/wiki/Block_party" title="Block party">block parties</a> all around The South Bronx. He even faced his long-time friend, Disco King Mario in a DJ battle. He then began performing at <a href="/wiki/Adlai_E._Stevenson_High_School_(New_York_City)" title="Adlai E. Stevenson High School (New York City)">Adlai E. Stevenson High School</a> and formed the Bronx River Organization, then later simply "The Organization".<sup id="cite_ref-hood_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hood-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bambaataa had deejayed with his own sound system at <a href="/wiki/Bronx_River_Houses" title="Bronx River Houses">The Bronx River Houses</a>' Community Center, with Mr. Biggs, Queen Kenya, and Cowboy, who accompanied him in performances in the community. Because of his prior status in the Black Spades, he already had an established Army party crowd drawn from former members of the gang. Hip hop culture was spreading through the streets via house parties, block parties, gym dances and mix tapes.<sup id="cite_ref-hood_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hood-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>About a year later Bambaataa reformed the group, calling it the Zulu Nation (inspired by his wide studies on African history at the time). Specifically, Bambaataa watched the 1964 film <i><a href="/wiki/Zulu_(1964_film)" title="Zulu (1964 film)">Zulu</a></i>, which sparked the name for the group. Five b-boys (<a href="/wiki/Breakdance" class="mw-redirect" title="Breakdance">break dancers</a>) joined him, whom he called the Zulu Kings, and later formed the Zulu Queens, and the Shaka Zulu Kings and Queens. As he continued deejaying, more DJs, rappers, <a href="/wiki/B-boy" class="mw-redirect" title="B-boy">b-boys</a>, <a href="/wiki/B-girl" class="mw-redirect" title="B-girl">b-girls</a>, graffiti writers, and artists followed him, and he took them under his wing and made them all members of his Zulu Nation.
</p><p>He was also the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Soulsonic_Force" title="Soulsonic Force">Soulsonic Force</a>, which originally consisted of approximately 20 Zulu Nation members: Mr. Biggs, Queen Kenya, DJ Cowboy Soulsonic Force (#2), Pow Wow, G.L.0.B.E. (creator of the "MC popping" rap style), <a href="/wiki/DJ_Jazzy_Jay" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ Jazzy Jay">DJ Jazzy Jay</a>, Cosmic Force, Queen Lisa Lee, Prince Ikey C, Ice Ice (#1), Chubby Chub; <a href="/wiki/Jazzy_Five" title="Jazzy Five">Jazzy Five</a>-DJ Jazzy Jay, Mr. Freeze, Master D.E.E., Kool DJ Red Alert, Sundance, Ice Ice (#2), Charlie Choo, Master Bee, Busy Bee Starski, Akbar (Lil Starski), and <a href="/wiki/Raheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Raheim">Raheim</a>. The personnel for the Soulsonic Force were groups within groups with whom he would perform and make records.
</p><p>In 1980, Bambaataa's groups made <i>Death Mix</i>, their first recording with <a href="/wiki/Paul_Winley_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul Winley Records">Paul Winley Records</a>. According to Bambaata, this was an unauthorized release.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Winley recorded two versions of Soulsonic Force's landmark single, "Zulu Nation Throwdown", with authorization from the musicians. Disappointed with the results of the single, Bambaataa left the company. The arranger credit on these recordings is correctly attributed to Harlem Underground Band leader, Kevin Donovan. This led to the false assumption that Bambaataa's real name was Kevin Donovan, which was widely accepted by the hip hop community until recently, following sexual abuse allegations, when Bronx River residents spoke out and revealed in oral testimonies that Bambaataa's real name was in fact Lance Taylor.<sup id="cite_ref-youtube.com_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youtube.com-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Zulu Nation was the first hip-hop organization, with an official birth date of November 12, 1977. Bambaataa's plan with the Universal Zulu Nation was to build a movement out of the creativity of a new generation of outcast youths with an authentic, liberating worldview.<sup id="cite_ref-chang_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition">Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afrika_Bambaataa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Afrika_Bambaataa_and_DJ_Yutaka_(2004).jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Afrika_Bambaataa_and_DJ_Yutaka_%282004%29.jpg/240px-Afrika_Bambaataa_and_DJ_Yutaka_%282004%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="240" height="179" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Afrika_Bambaataa_and_DJ_Yutaka_%282004%29.jpg/360px-Afrika_Bambaataa_and_DJ_Yutaka_%282004%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Afrika_Bambaataa_and_DJ_Yutaka_%282004%29.jpg/480px-Afrika_Bambaataa_and_DJ_Yutaka_%282004%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="739" data-file-height="551" /></a><figcaption>Afrika Bambaataa (left) in 2004</figcaption></figure>
<p>In 1981, hip hop artist <a href="/wiki/Fab_Five_Freddy" title="Fab Five Freddy">Fab Five Freddy</a> was putting together music packages in the largely white downtown <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> clubs, and invited Bambaataa to perform at one of them, the <a href="/wiki/Mudd_Club" title="Mudd Club">Mudd Club</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It was the first time Bambaataa had performed before a predominantly white crowd. Attendance for his parties downtown became so large that he had to move to larger venues, first to the Ritz, in a show organized by hip hop pioneer, Michael Holman, with <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_McLaren" title="Malcolm McLaren">Malcolm McLaren</a>'s group <a href="/wiki/Bow_Wow_Wow" title="Bow Wow Wow">Bow Wow Wow</a>, then to the <a href="/wiki/Peppermint_Lounge" title="Peppermint Lounge">Peppermint Lounge</a>, The Jefferson, Negril, <a href="/wiki/Danceteria" title="Danceteria">Danceteria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Roxy_NYC" class="mw-redirect" title="Roxy NYC">Roxy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-efioee_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-efioee-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"<a href="/wiki/Planet_Rock_(song)" title="Planet Rock (song)">Planet Rock</a>", a popular single produced by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Baker_(musician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur Baker (musician)">Arthur Baker</a> and the keyboardist <a href="/wiki/John_Robie" title="John Robie">John Robie</a>, came out that June under the name Afrika Bambaataa and the <a href="/wiki/Soulsonic_Force" title="Soulsonic Force">Soulsonic Force</a>. The song borrowed musical motifs from German <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a>, funk, and rock. Different elements and musical styles were used together. The song became an immediate hit and stormed the music charts worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-efioee_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-efioee-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The song melded the main melody from Kraftwerk's "<a href="/wiki/Trans-Europe_Express_(album)" title="Trans-Europe Express (album)">Trans-Europe Express</a>" with electronic beats based on their track "<a href="/wiki/Numbers_(Kraftwerk_song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Numbers (Kraftwerk song)">Numbers</a>" as well as portions from records by <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">Babe Ruth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Captain_Sky" title="Captain Sky">Captain Sky</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> thus creating a new style of music altogether, <a href="/wiki/Electro_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Electro music">electro funk</a>.
</p><p>Afrika Bambaataa was booked on the first ever European hip hop tour presented by Europe One and Fnac France.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-holyroller_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holyroller-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Along with himself were rapper and <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Rammellzee" title="Rammellzee">Rammellzee</a>, Zulu Nation DJ <a href="/wiki/Grand_Mixer_DXT" title="Grand Mixer DXT">Grand Mixer DXT</a> (formerly Grand Mixer D.St), B-boy and B-girl crews the <a href="/wiki/Rock_Steady_Crew" title="Rock Steady Crew">Rock Steady Crew</a>, and the Double Dutch Girls, as well as legendary graffiti artists <a href="/wiki/Fab_5_Freddy" class="mw-redirect" title="Fab 5 Freddy">Fab 5 Freddy</a>, <a href="/wiki/PHASE_2" class="mw-redirect" title="PHASE 2">PHASE 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Futura_2000" class="mw-redirect" title="Futura 2000">Futura 2000</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dondi_White" class="mw-redirect" title="Dondi White">Dondi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-holyroller_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holyroller-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bambaataa's second release around 1983 was "<a href="/wiki/Looking_for_the_Perfect_Beat" title="Looking for the Perfect Beat">Looking for the Perfect Beat</a>", then later, "<a href="/wiki/Renegades_of_Funk" title="Renegades of Funk">Renegades of Funk</a>", both with the same Soulsonic Force. He began working with producer <a href="/wiki/Bill_Laswell" title="Bill Laswell">Bill Laswell</a> at Jean Karakos's <a href="/wiki/Celluloid_Records" title="Celluloid Records">Celluloid Records</a>, where he developed and placed two groups on the label: Time Zone and Shango. Bambaataa recorded "Wildstyle" with <a href="/wiki/Time_Zone_(band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Time Zone (band)">Time Zone</a>, and he recorded a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rocker</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Lydon" title="John Lydon">John Lydon</a> and Time Zone in 1984, titled "World Destruction". Shango's album, <i>Shango Funk Theology</i>, was released by the label in 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-Star_Time:_Song_by_Song_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Star_Time:_Song_by_Song-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1984, Bambaataa and other hip hop celebrities appeared in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Beat_Street" title="Beat Street">Beat Street</a></i>. He also made a landmark recording with <a href="/wiki/James_Brown" title="James Brown">James Brown</a>, titled "<a href="/wiki/Unity_(Afrika_Bambaataa_and_James_Brown_song)" title="Unity (Afrika Bambaataa and James Brown song)">Unity</a>". It was billed in music industry circles as "the Godfather of Soul meets the Godfather of Hip Hop".<sup id="cite_ref-Star_Time:_Song_by_Song_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Star_Time:_Song_by_Song-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Around October 1985, Bambaataa and other music stars worked on the anti-<a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Sun_City_(album)" title="Sun City (album)">Sun City</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Little_Steven_Van_Zandt" class="mw-redirect" title="Little Steven Van Zandt">Little Steven Van Zandt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joey_Ramone" title="Joey Ramone">Joey Ramone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Run%E2%80%93D.M.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Run–D.M.C.">Run–D.M.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lou_Reed" title="Lou Reed">Lou Reed</a>, <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a>, and others. During 1988, he recorded "Afrika Bambaataa and Family" for <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a>, titled The Light, featuring <a href="/wiki/Nona_Hendryx" title="Nona Hendryx">Nona Hendryx</a>, <a href="/wiki/UB40" title="UB40">UB40</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boy_George" title="Boy George">Boy George</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)">George Clinton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bootsy_Collins" title="Bootsy Collins">Bootsy Collins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yellowman" title="Yellowman">Yellowman</a>. He had recorded a few other works with Family three years earlier, one titled "Funk You" in 1985, and the other titled "Beware (The Funk Is Everywhere)" in 1986. In 1986 he discovered an artist in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>. (Through MC SHY D) by the name of Kenya Miler a.k.a. MC Harmony (Known producer now as Kenya Fame Flames Miller), that was later signed to Criminal Records and Arthur Baker.<sup id="cite_ref-hood_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hood-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The group was Harmony and LG. The first single, 1987's "Dance To The Drums/No Joke", was produced by Bambaataa and Baker with musicians Keith LeBlanc and Doug Wimbish. Bambaataa was involved in the Stop the Violence Movement, and with other hip hop artists recorded "Self Destruction", a 12" single which hit number one on the Hot Rap Singles Chart in March 1989. The single went gold and raised $400,000 for the National Urban League to be used for community anti-violence education programs.<sup id="cite_ref-hood_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hood-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1990, Bambaataa made <i><a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">Life</a></i> magazine's "Most Important Americans of the 20th Century" issue. He was also involved in the anti-apartheid work "Hip Hop Artists Against Apartheid" for Warlock Records. He teamed with the <a href="/wiki/Jungle_Brothers" title="Jungle Brothers">Jungle Brothers</a> to record the album <i>Return to Planet Rock (The Second Coming)</i>.
</p><p>Gee Street Records, Bambaataa and John Baker organized a concert at <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium_(1923)" title="Wembley Stadium (1923)">Wembley Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in 1990 for the <a href="/wiki/African_National_Congress" title="African National Congress">African National Congress</a> (ANC), in honor of <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Mandela" title="Nelson Mandela">Nelson Mandela</a>'s release from prison. The concert brought together performances by British and American rappers, and also introduced both Nelson and <a href="/wiki/Winnie_Mandela" class="mw-redirect" title="Winnie Mandela">Winnie Mandela</a> and the ANC to hip hop audiences. In relation to the event, the recording Ndodemnyama (Free South Africa) helped raise approximately $30,000 for the ANC.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the mid-1990s, Bambaataa returned to his electro roots. In 1998, he produced a remix of "<a href="/wiki/Planet_Rock_(song)" title="Planet Rock (song)">Planet Rock</a>" combining electro and <a href="/wiki/House_music" title="House music">house music</a> elements, called "Planet Rock '98", which is regarded as an early example of the <a href="/wiki/Electro_house" title="Electro house">electro house</a> genre.<sup id="cite_ref-Eurodance_Hits_Polystar_Electro_House_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eurodance_Hits_Polystar_Electro_House-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Rage_Against_the_Machine" title="Rage Against the Machine">Rage Against the Machine</a> covered his song "<a href="/wiki/Renegades_of_Funk" title="Renegades of Funk">Renegades of Funk</a>" for their album, <i><a href="/wiki/Renegades_(Rage_Against_the_Machine_album)" title="Renegades (Rage Against the Machine album)">Renegades</a></i>. The same year, he collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Leftfield" title="Leftfield">Leftfield</a> on the song "<a href="/wiki/Afrika_Shox" title="Afrika Shox">Afrika Shox</a>", the first single from Leftfield's <i><a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_Stealth" title="Rhythm and Stealth">Rhythm and Stealth</a></i>. "<a href="/wiki/Afrika_Shox" title="Afrika Shox">Afrika Shox</a>" also appeared on the soundtrack to <i><a href="/wiki/Vanilla_Sky" title="Vanilla Sky">Vanilla Sky</a></i>.
</p><p>In 2004, he collaborated with <a href="/wiki/WestBam" title="WestBam">WestBam</a>, a group that was named after him, on the 2004 album <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Matter_Moving_at_the_Speed_of_Light" title="Dark Matter Moving at the Speed of Light">Dark Matter Moving at the Speed of Light</a></i> which also featured <a href="/wiki/Gary_Numan" title="Gary Numan">Gary Numan</a>. In 2006, he was featured on the British singer <a href="/wiki/Jamelia" title="Jamelia">Jamelia</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Walk_with_Me_(Jamelia_album)" title="Walk with Me (Jamelia album)">Walk with Me</a></i> on a song called "Do Me Right", and on <a href="/wiki/Mekon_(musician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mekon (musician)">Mekon</a>'s album <i>Some Thing Came Up</i>, on the track "D-Funktional". He performed the lyrics on the track "Is There Anybody Out There" by the <a href="/wiki/Bassheads" title="Bassheads">Bassheads</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UK2_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UK2-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> As an actor, he has played a variety of <a href="/wiki/Voice-over" title="Voice-over">voice-over</a> character roles on <i><a href="/wiki/Kung_Faux" title="Kung Faux">Kung Faux</a></i>.
</p><p>Bambaataa was a judge for the 6th annual <a href="/wiki/Independent_Music_Awards_(IMAs)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent Music Awards (IMAs)">Independent Music Awards</a> to support independent artists' careers.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> On September 27, 2007, it was announced that Afrika Bambaataa was one of the nine nominees for the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> Inductions.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> On December 22, 2007, he made a surprise appearance performing at the <i>First Annual Tribute Fit For the King of King Records, Mr. Dynamite <a href="/wiki/James_Brown" title="James Brown">James Brown</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Covington,_Kentucky" title="Covington, Kentucky">Covington, Kentucky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 14, 2012, Bambaataa was given a three-year appointment as a visiting scholar at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The appointment was made in collaboration between <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University_Library" title="Cornell University Library">Cornell University Library</a>'s Hip Hop Collection, the largest collection of historical hip hop music in North America, and the university's department of Music.<sup id="cite_ref-news.library.cornell.edu_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.library.cornell.edu-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> His archives, including his vinyl collection, original audio and video recordings, manuscripts, books, and papers arrived at the Cornell University Hip Hop Collection in December 2013.
</p>

<p>In April 2016, Bronx political activist <a href="/wiki/Ronald_%22Bee-Stinger%22_Savage" class="mw-redirect" title="Ronald &quot;Bee-Stinger&quot; Savage">Ronald "Bee-Stinger" Savage</a> accused Bambaataa of <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">molesting</a> him in 1980, when Savage was 15.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Following Savage's allegations, three more men accused Bambaataa of sexual abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Bambaataa issued a statement to <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> denying the allegations.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In early May 2016, the Universal Zulu Nation disassociated themselves from Bambaataa as part of an organizational restructuring that saw the group removing "all accused parties and those accused of covering up the current allegations of child molestation" from their current roles in the organization.<sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone.com_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone.com-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 6 that year, Bambaataa left his position as head of The Zulu Nation.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Then, a month later, The Universal Zulu Nation issued an open letter apologizing to the people alleging Bambaataa had sexually abused them while expressing responsibility for the organization's "poor response".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The letter was signed by nearly three dozen members of the Zulu Nation, including leaders from as far as New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2016, <i><a href="/wiki/Vice_(magazine)" title="Vice (magazine)">Vice</a></i> published an <a href="/wiki/Investigative_journalism" title="Investigative journalism">investigative</a> article titled "Afrika Bambaataa Allegedly Molested Young Men For Decades" and reported stories from the alleged victims and witnesses. The article stated the accusers "claim that these accounts of alleged abuse have been common knowledge in the Bronx River community and beyond since the early 1980s, including among many of Bambaataa's closest friends and Zulu soldiers."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In a March 2021 interview, <a href="/wiki/Melle_Mel" title="Melle Mel">Melle Mel</a> said "everyone knew" about the accusations, calling it "hip hop's best kept secret" but would not respond when asked if he knew specifically.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
