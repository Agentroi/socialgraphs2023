<p><b>Gary Eldridge Grice</b> (born August 22, 1966), better known by his stage names <b>GZA</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">JIZ</span>-ə</i></a>) and <b>The Genius</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is an American rapper. A founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, GZA is the group's "spiritual head", being both the first member in the group to receive a record deal and being the oldest member.<sup id="cite_ref-ViceLarson_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ViceLarson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He has appeared on his fellow Wu-Tang members' solo projects, and has maintained a successful solo career starting with <i><a href="/wiki/Liquid_Swords" title="Liquid Swords">Liquid Swords</a></i> (1995).
</p><p>His lyrical style often dismisses typical rap story lines in favor of science and wide-ranging philosophies and has been characterized as "armed with sharp metaphors and a smooth flow".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Breaks_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Breaks-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> An analysis of GZA's lyrics found that he has the second largest vocabulary in popular hip hop music.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He teamed up with an education group to promote science education in New York City through hip hop.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GZA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After he was born in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, New York City, on August 22, 1966, Gary Grice developed an interest in hip-hop by attending block parties as a child in the early 1970s. He was involved in <a href="/wiki/B-boying" class="mw-redirect" title="B-boying">breakdance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a>, and DJing. Early on he developed an interest in writing, studying <a href="/wiki/Mother_Goose" title="Mother Goose">Mother Goose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nursery_rhyme" title="Nursery rhyme">nursery rhyme</a>. In 1974–1976, before moving from <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a> to <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, he listened to <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Poets" title="The Last Poets">The Last Poets</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-GuardianGibsone_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianGibsone-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> however, he was attracted by <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">profanity</a> rather than content, and didn't understand the underlying messages until many years later. The Last Poets, as well as songs by <a href="/wiki/The_Isley_Brothers" title="The Isley Brothers">The Isley Brothers</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Commodores" title="Commodores">Commodores</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/O%27Jays" class="mw-redirect" title="O&#39;Jays">O'Jays</a> had a big part in his early life, especially regarding the tone and cadence of speech.<sup id="cite_ref-Village2012_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Village2012-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Later on, he began to listen to hip hop and R&amp;B, for example by <a href="/wiki/Secret_Weapon_(group)" title="Secret Weapon (group)">Secret Weapon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Flash_and_the_Furious_Five" title="Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five">Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MTV2012_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV2012-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 1982, Grice dropped out of high school in the tenth grade.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He formed a three-man group with his cousins, Robert Diggs and Russell Jones, who would later be known as <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ol%27_Dirty_Bastard" title="Ol&#39; Dirty Bastard">Ol' Dirty Bastard</a>. At the time he didn't like the sound of his voice.<sup id="cite_ref-Village2012_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Village2012-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The group, FOI: Force of the Imperial Master, and later All in Together,<sup id="cite_ref-Spin2015_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spin2015-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> saw the three rapping and DJing, switching names and performing local shows; since they lived in different boroughs, GZA and Ol' Dirty would travel from <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> out to <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a> to meet up with their cousin RZA, after which the three would travel across New York City and challenge other MCs to battles.<sup id="cite_ref-Crimson2011_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson2011-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He left school in the 10th grade, but nurtured his affection for science as he developed his skills as a lyricist.<sup id="cite_ref-TWSJ12_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TWSJ12-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After some years of this, GZA was signed to <a href="/wiki/Cold_Chillin%27_Records" title="Cold Chillin&#39; Records">Cold Chillin' Records</a> as a solo artist under the name <b>The Genius</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-ForbesSuarez_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ForbesSuarez-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> He released his debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Words_from_the_Genius" title="Words from the Genius">Words from the Genius</a></i> (1991), produced mostly by <a href="/wiki/Easy_Mo_Bee" title="Easy Mo Bee">Easy Mo Bee</a>, but it failed to sell due to lack of promotion. This, combined with his rocky experience on tour, fed up GZA and he asked to be released from the label.<sup id="cite_ref-GuardianGibsone_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianGibsone-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Exclaim_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exclaim-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The album's beat selection is similar to the classic <a href="/wiki/Boom_bap" title="Boom bap">boom-bap</a> sound of the early 1990s, while the lyricism subject is the everyday situations of an individual living in the parts of New York.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> At the time he worked as a bicycle messenger.<sup id="cite_ref-Crimson2011_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson2011-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wu-Tang_Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GZA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wu-Tang Clan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1992, GZA joined the <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, a group of nine, formed by his cousin <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a>. GZA had some high-profile appearances on the group's debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Wu-Tang_(36_Chambers)" title="Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)">Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)</a></i> (1993), including a solo track, "Clan in da Front".<sup id="cite_ref-LDebut_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LDebut-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> This, combined with appearances on other Clan members' albums such as <i><a href="/wiki/Return_to_the_36_Chambers:_The_Dirty_Version" title="Return to the 36 Chambers: The Dirty Version">Return to the 36 Chambers: The Dirty Version</a></i> (1995) and <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx..." title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...</a></i> (1995) brought him much recognition. According to <a href="/wiki/Method_Man" title="Method Man">Method Man</a>, "we form like <a href="/wiki/Voltron" title="Voltron">Voltron</a> and GZA happens to be the head".<sup id="cite_ref-LDebut_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LDebut-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_career">Solo career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GZA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solo career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1995.E2.80.931999:_Liquid_Swords.2C_Beneath_the_Surface"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1995–1999:_Liquid_Swords,_Beneath_the_Surface">1995–1999: <i>Liquid Swords</i>, <i>Beneath the Surface</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GZA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1995–1999: Liquid Swords, Beneath the Surface">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These guest appearances were followed up in 1995 with GZA's second studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Liquid_Swords" title="Liquid Swords">Liquid Swords</a></i>, produced and recorded entirely in the basement studio by RZA, with the participation of all original Clan members. It was released on November 7 by <a href="/wiki/Geffen_Records" title="Geffen Records">Geffen Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LDebut_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LDebut-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The album's complex composition included dialogue sampled from the film <i><a href="/wiki/Shogun_Assassin" title="Shogun Assassin">Shogun Assassin</a></i>. The album was met with positive reviews and sold well. It is considered to be one of the best albums from the Wu-Tang camp. It peaked at the #9 spot on the Billboard 200 chart and #2 on the Top R&amp;B/Hip Hop Albums chart.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> On October 8, 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> announced that the album had earned a <a href="/wiki/Platinum_certification" class="mw-redirect" title="Platinum certification">Platinum certification</a> for having sold more than 1 million copies. It became the first Wu-Tang-related album to get certified since 2004, when <a href="/wiki/Method_Man" title="Method Man">Method Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ghostface_Killah" title="Ghostface Killah">Ghostface Killah</a> both earned plaques.<sup id="cite_ref-ForbesSuarez_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ForbesSuarez-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In addition, he directed music videos for four album singles.<sup id="cite_ref-Spin2015_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spin2015-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After appearing on the Wu-Tang Clan's second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Forever" title="Wu-Tang Forever">Wu-Tang Forever</a></i> (1997), his third studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Beneath_the_Surface_(GZA_album)" title="Beneath the Surface (GZA album)">Beneath the Surface</a></i> was released by <a href="/wiki/MCA_Records" title="MCA Records">MCA Records</a> in 1999. The album and its singles "Crash Your Crew" and "Breaker, Breaker" met with critical acclaim.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The album earned a <a href="/wiki/Gold_certification" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold certification">Gold certification</a> by RIAA,<sup id="cite_ref-ForbesSuarez_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ForbesSuarez-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> peaked at #9 on the Billboard 200 chart, and topped the Top R&amp;B/Hip Hop Albums chart.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2000.E2.80.932009:_Legend_of_the_Liquid_Sword.2C_Pro_Tools"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000–2009:_Legend_of_the_Liquid_Sword,_Pro_Tools">2000–2009: <i>Legend of the Liquid Sword</i>, <i>Pro Tools</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GZA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000–2009: Legend of the Liquid Sword, Pro Tools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Gza-01-mika.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Gza-01-mika.jpg/220px-Gza-01-mika.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="222" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Gza-01-mika.jpg/330px-Gza-01-mika.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Gza-01-mika.jpg/440px-Gza-01-mika.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1118" data-file-height="1127" /></a><figcaption>GZA in 2000</figcaption></figure>
<p>In 2000 the single "<a href="/wiki/Ich_lebe_f%C3%BCr_Hip_Hop" title="Ich lebe für Hip Hop">Ich lebe für Hip Hop</a>" was released with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Tomekk" title="DJ Tomekk">DJ Tomekk</a> and placed 11th in the German charts. 
</p><p>In 2002, a sequel to his second solo album was released, <i><a href="/wiki/Legend_of_the_Liquid_Sword_(album)" title="Legend of the Liquid Sword (album)">Legend of the Liquid Sword</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Village2012_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Village2012-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It was well-received by critics, yet did not sell well and failed to go Gold. GZA spent 2004 touring, both solo and with the Clan, and made an appearance with RZA in <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jarmusch" title="Jim Jarmusch">Jim Jarmusch</a>'s film <i><a href="/wiki/Coffee_and_Cigarettes" title="Coffee and Cigarettes">Coffee and Cigarettes</a></i>, opposite <a href="/wiki/Bill_Murray" title="Bill Murray">Bill Murray</a>. The two also appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Chappelle_Show" class="mw-redirect" title="Chappelle Show">Chappelle Show</a>'s skits "Wu Tang Financial" and "Racial Draft".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, GZA and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Muggs" title="DJ Muggs">DJ Muggs</a> (the producer for hip-hop group <a href="/wiki/Cypress_Hill" title="Cypress Hill">Cypress Hill</a>) released collaboration album <i><a href="/wiki/Grandmasters_(album)" title="Grandmasters (album)">Grandmasters</a></i>. Muggs provided all the production for the album, which saw GZA using chess as a metaphor for the rap game, with most of the songs having a chess-themed title.<sup id="cite_ref-ViceLarson_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ViceLarson-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:GZA_at_Paid_Dues_3.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/GZA_at_Paid_Dues_3.jpg/220px-GZA_at_Paid_Dues_3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/GZA_at_Paid_Dues_3.jpg/330px-GZA_at_Paid_Dues_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/GZA_at_Paid_Dues_3.jpg/440px-GZA_at_Paid_Dues_3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4272" data-file-height="2848" /></a><figcaption>GZA performing at <a href="/wiki/Paid_Dues" title="Paid Dues">Paid Dues</a> in New York City, 2008</figcaption></figure>
<p>In the summer of 2008, his fifth solo studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Pro_Tools_(album)" title="Pro Tools (album)">Pro Tools</a></i> was released by <a href="/wiki/Babygrande_Records" title="Babygrande Records">Babygrande Records</a>. It featured production from <a href="/wiki/Black_Milk" title="Black Milk">Black Milk</a> and Jay "Waxxx" Garfield, RZA, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_(producer)" title="Mathematics (producer)">Mathematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/True_Master" title="True Master">True Master</a>. A very controversial song on the album was entitled "Paper Plates", a smooth flowing response aimed at rival rapper <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Exclaim_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exclaim-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> After the release, GZA toured various parts of Europe on a "Liquid Swords Tour", where he performed songs from <i>Liquid Swords</i> as well as <i>Pro Tools</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Exclaim_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exclaim-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He later toured parts of the US.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2010.E2.80.93present:_Dark_Matter"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2010–present:_Dark_Matter">2010–present: <i>Dark Matter</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GZA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2010–present: Dark Matter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, he spoke at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Crimson2011_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crimson2011-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and since then has visited, among others, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Academy_(California)" title="Oxford Academy (California)">Oxford</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">USC</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">NYU</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell</a>, where he met with scientists such as <a href="/wiki/David_Kaiser" title="David Kaiser">David Kaiser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sallie_W._Chisholm" title="Sallie W. Chisholm">Sallie W. Chisholm</a><sup id="cite_ref-MTV2012_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV2012-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-StonePerpetua_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StonePerpetua-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> to seek inspiration.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LAWeeklyWeiss_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LAWeeklyWeiss-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Whitlock_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whitlock-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In 2012, he talked "hip-hop and science" with <a href="/wiki/Neil_deGrasse_Tyson" title="Neil deGrasse Tyson">Neil deGrasse Tyson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-factmag-com_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-factmag-com-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TWSJ12_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TWSJ12-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To improve science education in New York City and beyond in late 2012, with the assistance of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>'s professor <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Emdin" title="Christopher Emdin">Christopher Emdin</a> and website <a href="/wiki/Rap_Genius" class="mw-redirect" title="Rap Genius">Rap Genius</a>, he began work on a pilot program called Science Genius B.A.T.T.L.E.S. (Bringing Attention to Transforming, Teaching and Learning Science). This initiative motivates young high school students, especially African-American and Latino students, who together make up 70 percent of New York City's student body, to learn science through hip hop, creating scientific raps and engaging in a rap competition.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the meantime, he continued to work on a related, new studio album called <i>Dark Matter</i>, which is based on a journey through <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-StonePerpetua_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StonePerpetua-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In late 2015, GZA canceled European tour dates to focus on working on the album with composer <a href="/wiki/Vangelis" title="Vangelis">Vangelis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Spin2015_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spin2015-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> It is planned to be one in a series of albums related to Earth, with the following conceived around <a href="/wiki/Oceans" class="mw-redirect" title="Oceans">oceans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TWSJ12_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TWSJ12-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> GZA hinted that the albums will be without parental advisory, profanity and nudity.<sup id="cite_ref-TWSJ12_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TWSJ12-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2015, GZA released a single "The Mexican" (a cover of <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth_(band)" title="Babe Ruth (band)">Babe Ruth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Mexican_(song)" title="The Mexican (song)">song</a>), featuring guitarist <a href="/wiki/Tom_Morello" title="Tom Morello">Tom Morello</a> and singer Kara Lane. They performed the song live along with <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show_Starring_Jimmy_Fallon" title="The Tonight Show Starring Jimmy Fallon">The Tonight Show Starring Jimmy Fallon</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> On June 30, 2016, he released a new space-themed track, "The Spark", produced by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ryder" title="Paul Ryder">Paul Ryder</a>, for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s project "Destination: Jupiter", in celebration of the agency's ongoing <i><a href="/wiki/Juno_(spacecraft)" title="Juno (spacecraft)">Juno Mission</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2020, GZA provided a voice role in Netflix's <i><a href="/wiki/Kipo_and_the_Age_of_Wonderbeasts" title="Kipo and the Age of Wonderbeasts">Kipo and the Age of Wonderbeasts</a></i> as Bad Billions, and contributed a song to the series.
</p>
