<p><b>Dennis David Coles</b> (born May 9, 1970),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> better known by his stage name <b>Ghostface Killah</b>, is an American rapper and a member of the hip hop group <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> After the group achieved breakthrough success in the aftermath of <i><a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Wu-Tang_(36_Chambers)" title="Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)">Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)</a></i>, the members went on to pursue solo careers to varying levels of success. Ghostface Killah debuted his solo career with <i><a href="/wiki/Ironman_(Ghostface_Killah_album)" title="Ironman (Ghostface Killah album)">Ironman</a></i>, which was well-received by music critics, in 1996. He has enjoyed continued success in the years that have followed, releasing critically acclaimed albums such as <i><a href="/wiki/Supreme_Clientele" title="Supreme Clientele">Supreme Clientele</a></i> (2000) and <i><a href="/wiki/Fishscale" title="Fishscale">Fishscale</a></i> (2006). His stage name was taken from one of the characters in the 1979 <a href="/wiki/Kung_fu" class="mw-redirect" title="Kung fu">kung fu</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mystery_of_Chess_Boxing" title="The Mystery of Chess Boxing">The Mystery of Chess Boxing</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He is the founder of his own <a href="/wiki/Record_label" title="Record label">record label</a>, Starks Enterprises.
</p><p>Ghostface Killah is critically acclaimed<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> for his loud, fast-paced <a href="/wiki/Flow_(rapping)" class="mw-redirect" title="Flow (rapping)">flow</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and his emotional <a href="/wiki/Stream_of_consciousness" title="Stream of consciousness">stream-of-consciousness</a> narratives containing cryptic <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">slang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non_sequitur_(literary_device)" title="Non sequitur (literary device)">non-sequiturs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 2006, <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> included him as an "honourable mention" on their list of the "Greatest MCs of All Time",<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> while the editors of <a href="/wiki/About.com" class="mw-redirect" title="About.com">About.com</a> placed him on their list of the "Top 50 MCs of Our Time (1987–2007)", calling him "one of the most imaginative storytellers of our time."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Q_(magazine)" title="Q (magazine)"><i>Q</i> magazine</a> called him "rap's finest storyteller."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> has stated that "Ghostface has unparalleled storytelling instincts; he might be the best, most colorful storyteller rap has ever seen."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a> has called him "a compulsive storyteller", and asserts that "his fiction is <a href="/wiki/Painterliness" title="Painterliness">painterly</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghostface_Killah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ghostface grew up in the <a href="/wiki/Stapleton_Houses" title="Stapleton Houses">Stapleton Houses</a> housing project in <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a>, New York City,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> helping with daily care of two younger brothers who had muscular dystrophy.<sup id="cite_ref-" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The 2019 series <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang:_An_American_Saga" title="Wu-Tang: An American Saga">Wu-Tang: An American Saga</a> depicts a feud between him and fellow Clan member <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>; however the feud was purely fictional and never took place in reality. Raekwon and Ghostface attended junior high school together.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghostface_Killah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_work">Early work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghostface_Killah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A roommate of Wu-Tang founder <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a>, Ghostface helped bring together the other seven members. In 1995, Ghostface guest-starred extensively on fellow Clan member <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>'s debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx..." title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...</a></i>, appearing on almost every song and receiving nearly equal billing.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> "It felt good to have my boy next to me, Ghostface, who basically comes from the same lifestyle as I come from. And we were able to sit down and concoct an idea that we both were able to respect and basically get everybody in the crew involved...", said Raekwon about Ghostface's involvement in the project. Raekwon intended <i>Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...</i> to play like a film, with himself as the "star," Ghostface Killah as the "guest star" and producer RZA as the "director."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> He also contributed songs to the <i><a href="/wiki/Sunset_Park_(film)" title="Sunset Park (film)">Sunset Park</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Be_a_Menace_to_South_Central_While_Drinking_Your_Juice_in_the_Hood" title="Don&#39;t Be a Menace to South Central While Drinking Your Juice in the Hood">Don't Be a Menace to South Central While Drinking Your Juice in the Hood</a></i> soundtracks; both songs were included on his first solo LP, <i><a href="/wiki/Ironman_(Ghostface_Killah_album)" title="Ironman (Ghostface Killah album)">Ironman</a></i>, in 1996. The album, which debuted at #2 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, had a more pronounced <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> influence (particularly 1970s soul) than previous Wu-Tang releases, and Ghostface's future albums would continue to feature this stylistic trait.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the year 2000, Ghostface released his second studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Supreme_Clientele" title="Supreme Clientele">Supreme Clientele</a></i>. The album was well received by critics, and peaked at #7 on the Billboard 200|<i>Billboard</i> 200.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It included "<a href="/wiki/Apollo_Kids_(Ghostface_Killah_single)" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollo Kids (Ghostface Killah single)">Apollo Kids</a>", a popular single which featured Raekwon and had a sample of "Cool Breeze" by <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Burke" title="Solomon Burke">Solomon Burke</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Cherchez_La_Ghost" title="Cherchez La Ghost">Cherchez La Ghost</a>", another single off the album, became a minor club hit.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <i>Supreme Clientele</i> marked a turning point in terms of RZA's influence on Ghostface's sound, as only six songs on it are produced by RZA, whereas on <i>Ironman</i>, its predecessor, every song but one is produced by RZA. Although he contributed fewer beats to the project than to <i>Ironman</i>, RZA personally oversaw the mixing and production of the album as a whole, thereby contributing to <i>Supreme Clientele</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s unified sound.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ghostface wasted little time in recording his next album, the heavily <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>-influenced <i><a href="/wiki/Bulletproof_Wallets" title="Bulletproof Wallets">Bulletproof Wallets</a></i>, released a year after <i>Supreme Clientele</i>. Its main single, "<a href="/wiki/Never_Be_the_Same_Again_(Ghostface_Killah_song)" title="Never Be the Same Again (Ghostface Killah song)">Never Be the Same Again</a>", featured Raekwon and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Thomas_(singer)" title="Carl Thomas (singer)">Carl Thomas</a>. It produced another minor club hit, "Flowers", which featured guest vocals from fellow Wu-Tang members <a href="/wiki/Method_Man" title="Method Man">Method Man</a> and Raekwon, and a popular single, "<a href="/wiki/Ghost_Showers" title="Ghost Showers">Ghost Showers</a>" which featured Madame Majestic, whose other key claim to fame is that she sings on the popular Wu-Tang track "<a href="/wiki/Gravel_Pit" title="Gravel Pit">Gravel Pit</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Def_Jam">Def Jam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghostface_Killah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Def Jam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, Ghostface signed with <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Def Jam Records">Def Jam Records</a>. After temporarily dropping "Killah" from his stage name, Ghostface released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pretty_Toney_Album" title="The Pretty Toney Album">The Pretty Toney Album</a></i> in April 2004. The album, while containing two RZA productions, featured none of the Clan; instead, it featured collaborations with <a href="/wiki/Missy_Elliott" title="Missy Elliott">Missy Elliott</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Lox" title="The Lox">D-Block</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jacki-O" title="Jacki-O">Jacki-O</a>. The singles "<a href="/wiki/Tush_(Ghostface_Killah_song)" title="Tush (Ghostface Killah song)">Tush</a>" and "Run", which were collaborations with Missy and <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a> respectively, achieved moderate success in the clubs and on the charts, and the album was featured on numerous "best of the year" lists; for instance, it was Pitchfork Media's #9 album of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Ghostface also appeared on the track "On My Knees" by <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> R&amp;B group <a href="/wiki/The_411" title="The 411">the 411</a>; the song became a hit in the UK and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Ghostface then released an album entitled <i><a href="/wiki/718_(album)" title="718 (album)">718</a></i> (after the Staten Island <a href="/wiki/Area_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Area code">area code</a>) with a group of his protégés, the <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Unit" title="Theodore Unit">Theodore Unit</a>. Ghostface also appeared on "He Comes" by De La Soul, on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Grind_Date" title="The Grind Date">The Grind Date</a></i>. In November 2005, Ghostface and Theodore Unit's breakout star <a href="/wiki/Trife_Da_God" class="mw-redirect" title="Trife Da God">Trife Da God</a> released a joint project, <i><a href="/wiki/Put_It_on_the_Line" title="Put It on the Line">Put It on the Line</a></i>.
</p>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Ghostface_-_Highline_Ballroom_2.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Ghostface_-_Highline_Ballroom_2.jpg/290px-Ghostface_-_Highline_Ballroom_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="290" height="218" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Ghostface_-_Highline_Ballroom_2.jpg/435px-Ghostface_-_Highline_Ballroom_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Ghostface_-_Highline_Ballroom_2.jpg/580px-Ghostface_-_Highline_Ballroom_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a><figcaption>Ghostface Killah at <a href="/wiki/Highline_Ballroom" title="Highline Ballroom">Highline Ballroom</a> in New York in 2007</figcaption></figure>
<p>In 2006, Ghostface teamed up with underground artist <a href="/wiki/MF_Doom" title="MF Doom">MF Doom</a> for a still-unreleased album entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Swift_%26_Changeable" class="mw-redirect" title="Swift &amp; Changeable">Swift &amp; Changeable</a></i>. MF Doom also produced several songs for Ghostface's 2006 album <i><a href="/wiki/Fishscale" title="Fishscale">Fishscale</a></i>, on which he chose to revert to the stage name "Ghostface Killah", rather than just "Ghostface". The album debuted strongly, appearing at the #4 position on the U.S. <i>Billboard</i> 200 and at #2 on the R&amp;B charts,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> the rapper's most auspicious chart showing since the heyday of the Wu-Tang Clan and the release of his solo debut. The album also received near-unanimous positive reviews. Ghostface embarked on a limited-date tour of U.S. venues in support of the album, performing several of his concerts together with most of the other members of the Wu-Tang Clan.
</p><p>In 2007, Ghostface provided the theme song for the Irish animated series <i><a href="/wiki/Skunk_Fu!" title="Skunk Fu!">Skunk Fu!</a></i> that ran on <i><a href="/wiki/Kids%27_WB" title="Kids&#39; WB">Kids' WB</a></i>. On December 4, 2007, Ghostface released his seventh solo studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Doe_Rehab" title="The Big Doe Rehab">The Big Doe Rehab</a></i>.
</p><p>In a May 2008 interview, Ghostface Killah stated that he would make an <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>-inspired album in the vein of certain tracks he had done before with artists such as <a href="/wiki/Ne-Yo" title="Ne-Yo">Ne-Yo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jodeci" title="Jodeci">Jodeci</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> That album would become his eighth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Ghostdini:_Wizard_of_Poetry_in_Emerald_City" title="Ghostdini: Wizard of Poetry in Emerald City">Ghostdini: Wizard of Poetry in Emerald City</a></i>, which won good reviews from music critics. It featured singles such as "Baby" and "Do Over". In March 2009, Ghostface responded to the <a href="/wiki/Rihanna" title="Rihanna">Rihanna</a>/<a href="/wiki/Chris_Brown_(American_singer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chris Brown (American singer)">Chris Brown</a> controversy by recording a song, "Message from Ghostface", which was dedicated to women in abusive relationships.
</p><p>In a May 2009 interview with <i>Rolling Stone</i>, Raekwon indicated that Ghostface Killah was preparing to release a new album. In response to a question asking if the Wu-Tang Clan were going to release a follow-up to <i><a href="/wiki/8_Diagrams" title="8 Diagrams">8 Diagrams</a></i>, Raekwon stated, "<i>Everybody's doing different things right now — you got Meth [Method Man] coming out with <a href="/wiki/Blackout!_2" title="Blackout! 2">an album</a>, you got Ghostface coming out with an album, some guys working on their projects, some guys getting into the film world, everybody is multi-tasking right now.</i>"<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Ghostface appeared on a total of 8 songs on Raekwon's highly anticipated release of <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx..._Pt._II" title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx... Pt. II">Only Built 4 Cuban Linx... Pt. II</a></i>.
</p><p>Shortly after the release of <i>Only Built 4 Cuban Linx... Pt. II</i>, Def Jam contracted Raekwon to work with their label artists Method Man and Ghostface Killah on an album which later received the title <i><a href="/wiki/Wu-Massacre" title="Wu-Massacre">Wu-Massacre</a></i>. Production began in November 2009. <i>Wu-Massacre</i> was released March 30, 2010 to generally positive reviews from music critics; this time, however, reviews were more mixed, with some critics objecting to the 30-minute-long album's rushed feel. With heavy promotion, it sold 37,900 units in its first week; it has sold 64,000 units as of May 12, 2010. It features production from <a href="/wiki/Scram_Jones" title="Scram Jones">Scram Jones</a>, Mathematics, and The RZA, who produced the album's lead single, "Our Dreams". 
</p><p>In 2010, Ghostface confirmed that he would be releasing two new studio albums, <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_Kids_(Ghostface_Killah_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollo Kids (Ghostface Killah album)">Apollo Kids</a></i> and one which would serve as a sequel to his 2000 album <i>Supreme Clientele</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In 2011, Ghostface Killah featured on UK artist <a href="/wiki/Josh_Osho" title="Josh Osho">Josh Osho</a>'s debut single "Redemption Days".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_Def_Jam">Post Def Jam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghostface_Killah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post Def Jam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ghostface released a collaborative album with <a href="/wiki/The_Lox" title="The Lox">D-Block</a> member <a href="/wiki/Sheek_Louch" title="Sheek Louch">Sheek Louch</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Wu_Block" title="Wu Block">Wu Block</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The album was released on November 27, 2012, on <a href="/wiki/E1_Music" class="mw-redirect" title="E1 Music">E1 Music</a> and debuted at number 73 on the US Billboard 200 chart, with first-week sales of 8,600 copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> It fell to #152 in its second week selling 4,200 more copies.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In January of 2017, Ghostface and Sheek Louch announced on their respective social media accounts that a second collaborative album was in the works.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In an interview with <a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Complex (magazine)"><i>Complex Magazine</i></a> on November 17, 2012, Ghostface confirmed that he had left Def Jam, making <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_Kids_(album)" title="Apollo Kids (album)">Apollo Kids</a></i> his last album on the label. In the same interview, he also stated that <i>Blue &amp; Cream</i>, the sequel to his critically acclaimed album <i><a href="/wiki/Supreme_Clientele" title="Supreme Clientele">Supreme Clientele</a></i>, was 80-85 percent done. On April 16, 2013, Ghostface released his tenth album <i><a href="/wiki/Twelve_Reasons_to_Die" title="Twelve Reasons to Die">Twelve Reasons to Die</a></i> which was produced by <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Younge" title="Adrian Younge">Adrian Younge</a> and executive produced by RZA.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx.com_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx.com-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The album was released in various formats such as <a href="/wiki/CD" class="mw-redirect" title="CD">CD</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Gramophone record">vinyl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compact_Cassette" class="mw-redirect" title="Compact Cassette">cassette</a> under RZA's <a href="/wiki/Soul_Temple_Records" title="Soul Temple Records">Soul Temple Records</a>. The deluxe digital and CD versions also came with a comic book.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx.com_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx.com-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He would later announce that the sequel to <i>Supreme Clientele</i> would be released between July and September 2013<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and that his collaborative album with MF Doom would be released around Halloween 2013<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> though neither projects materialized. In January 2014, he appeared on the <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Couples_Therapy_(2012_TV_series)" title="Couples Therapy (2012 TV series)">Couples Therapy</a></i> with his girlfriend Kelsey Nykole.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Later that year Ghostface announced he would be releasing his eleventh album titled <i><a href="/wiki/36_Seasons" title="36 Seasons">36 Seasons</a></i> in December 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> He collaborated with Canadian jazz band <a href="/wiki/BADBADNOTGOOD" class="mw-redirect" title="BADBADNOTGOOD">BADBADNOTGOOD</a> on an album titled <i><a href="/wiki/Sour_Soul_(album)" title="Sour Soul (album)">Sour Soul</a></i> which was released in 2015.
</p><p>A sequel to <i><a href="/wiki/Twelve_Reasons_to_Die" title="Twelve Reasons to Die">Twelve Reasons to Die</a></i>, simply titled <i><a href="/wiki/Twelve_Reasons_to_Die_II" title="Twelve Reasons to Die II">Twelve Reasons to Die II</a></i>, was released on July 10, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ghostface had a string of UK and European tour dates<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> scheduled in 2016.
</p><p>In 2022 he provided vocals on the song "Purple Hearts" from Kendrick Lamar's album <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Morale_%26_The_Big_Steppers" class="mw-redirect" title="Mr. Morale &amp; The Big Steppers">Mr. Morale &amp; The Big Steppers</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghostface_Killah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lawsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 2011, Coles was sued by <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Urbont" title="Jacques Urbont">Jack Urbont</a> for copyright violation stemming from the "improper use" of the 1960s "<a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_in_other_media" title="Iron Man in other media">Iron Man</a>" theme song, which Urbont claimed as his own. Urbont also took issue with Coles' appropriation of the Iron Man brand name.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Coles was granted summary judgment on the lawsuit in April 2015, which stated that <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Entertainment" title="Marvel Entertainment">Marvel Entertainment</a>, owners of the Iron Man character and brand, owned the "Iron Man" theme song, not Urbont.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2011, Coles filed a lawsuit against Universal Music for unpaid royalties.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghostface_Killah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like fellow Wu-Tang Clan member <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>, Ghostface is a Muslim, having converted to <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Ghostface is the father of rappers Sun God,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Supreme,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> and singer Infinite.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
