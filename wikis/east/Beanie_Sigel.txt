<p><b>Dwight Equan Grant</b> (born March 6, 1974),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> better known by his stage name <b>Beanie Sigel</b>, is an American rapper. He first became known for his association with <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>, releasing his debut studio album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Truth_(Beanie_Sigel_album)" title="The Truth (Beanie Sigel album)">The Truth</a></i> through Roc-A-Fella in February 2000 to critical and commercial success.<sup id="cite_ref-MTV.com_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV.com-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sigel's second studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Reason_(Beanie_Sigel_album)" title="The Reason (Beanie Sigel album)">The Reason</a></i>, saw similar commercial success, but received mixed reviews from critics. His third album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_B._Coming" title="The B. Coming">The B. Coming</a></i>, was critically acclaimed and peaked at number 3 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMG_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> After a short break from music, Sigel returned to Roc-A-Fella in 2007 and released his fourth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Solution_(Beanie_Sigel_album)" title="The Solution (Beanie Sigel album)">The Solution</a></i> in December 2007 to positive reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> After leaving Roc-A-Fella once again, Sigel's fifth and sixth studio albums, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Broad_Street_Bully" title="The Broad Street Bully">The Broad Street Bully</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/This_Time_(Beanie_Sigel_album)" title="This Time (Beanie Sigel album)">This Time</a></i> were released independently in 2009 and 2012, respectively.
</p><p>Sigel is also known for his numerous legal issues, including a 2004–2005 trial for attempted murder (he was found not guilty).<sup id="cite_ref-WENN_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WENN-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MTV_News_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV_News-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beanie_Sigel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Roc-A-Fella_and_Def_Jam_years_.281999.E2.80.932006.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Roc-A-Fella_and_Def_Jam_years_(1999–2006)">Roc-A-Fella and Def Jam years (1999–2006)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beanie_Sigel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Roc-A-Fella and Def Jam years (1999–2006)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a> released Beanie Sigel's debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Truth_(Beanie_Sigel_album)" title="The Truth (Beanie Sigel album)">The Truth</a></i>  on February 29, 2000, to critical and commercial success.
</p><p>In 2002, Sigel and much of the Roc-A-Fella roster starred in <i><a href="/wiki/State_Property_(film)" title="State Property (film)">State Property</a></i>. Its release coincided with the creation and promotion of <a href="/wiki/State_Property_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="State Property (group)">State Property</a>, a group of Philadelphia artists signed to Roc-A-Fella, who were organized by Sigel and <a href="/wiki/Freeway_(rapper)" title="Freeway (rapper)">Freeway</a>. Its members included <a href="/wiki/Peedi_Crakk" class="mw-redirect" title="Peedi Crakk">Peedi Crakk</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Young_Gunz" title="Young Gunz">Young Gunz</a> (<a href="/wiki/Neef_Buck" class="mw-redirect" title="Neef Buck">Neef Buck</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Young_Chris" title="Young Chris">Young Chris</a>), Oschino and <a href="/wiki/Omillio_Sparks" title="Omillio Sparks">Omillio Sparks</a>. Their first collaboration was for the movie's soundtrack, an eponymous release that featured the original "<a href="/wiki/Roc_the_Mic" title="Roc the Mic">Roc the Mic</a>" by Sigel and Freeway. 2003's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chain_Gang_Vol._2" title="The Chain Gang Vol. 2">The Chain Gang Vol. 2</a></i> followed, featuring the single "<a href="/wiki/Can%27t_Stop,_Won%27t_Stop_(Young_Gunz_Song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Can&#39;t Stop, Won&#39;t Stop (Young Gunz Song)">Can't Stop, Won't Stop</a>" by the Young Gunz. The record was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for Best Rap Performance by a Duo or Group.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, before serving a one-year prison sentence stemming from an earlier arrest, Sigel shot multiple videos, directed by Joe Briscella, and finished his third album <i><a href="/wiki/The_B._Coming" title="The B. Coming">The B. Coming</a></i>. During this time, Sigel's label head at Roc-A-Fella, <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, became president of its parent label, <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Recordings" title="Def Jam Recordings">Def Jam</a>, which caused former business partners Damon Dash and Kareem "Biggs" Burke to leave and form the Dame Dash Music Group. Dame Dash Music Group and Def Jam released Sigel's <i>The B. Coming</i> rather than Roc-A-Fella, but it still contained production from a slew of Roc/Jay Z-affiliated producers including Chad West, <a href="/wiki/The_Neptunes" title="The Neptunes">The Neptunes</a>, Just Blaze, <a href="/wiki/Alrad_Lewis" title="Alrad Lewis">Boola</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Buckwild_(music_producer)" title="Buckwild (music producer)">Buckwild</a>. Sigel recruited cross–label MC talent for his album as well, including <a href="/wiki/Cam%27ron" title="Cam&#39;ron">Cam'ron</a> and Jay-Z. Sigel's record produced a hit track, "<a href="/wiki/Feel_It_in_the_Air" title="Feel It in the Air">Feel It in the Air</a>", and sold 131,000 copies in its first week.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Advertising his friendship with the incarcerated Sigel, Dash publicly claimed that he was leaving the Roc-A-Fella roster to join him. Sigel's group, State Property was thrown into turmoil, but eventually chose to remain at Roc-A-Fella—apparently against Sigel's wishes. On his release, Sigel questioned the loyalty of his group and said that he was signing with Dame and Biggs due to a stronger relationship with the pair than with Jay-Z:
</p>
<blockquote><p>I've never been around Jay on an off day. It ain't like I made a choice of running with Dame and Biggs or 'Dame and Biggs held me down through my whole trial.' It's not that. I would be a sucker if I said I [signed with the Damon Dash Music Group] because they did that for me. It's love. You see how we chillin' now, this ain't about no business.<sup id="cite_ref-Disloyal_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Disloyal-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Soon after, he clarified his comments, saying he simply did not want to be involved in the conflict, and elected to re-open talks with Roc-A-Fella instead of moving to Dame Dash Music Group.<sup id="cite_ref-Disloyal_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Disloyal-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, Sigel re-signed with Roc-A-Fella Records and started recording for his fourth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Solution_(Beanie_Sigel_album)" title="The Solution (Beanie Sigel album)">The Solution</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-xxl_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxl-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> On October 29, 2007, Sigel released the first single from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Solution_(Beanie_Sigel_album)" title="The Solution (Beanie Sigel album)">The Solution</a></i> titled "All The Above" which featured <a href="/wiki/R._Kelly" title="R. Kelly">R. Kelly</a>. It debuted on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">U.S. R&amp;B charts</a> at number 83. On May 22, 2007, Sigel released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Solution_(Beanie_Sigel_album)" title="The Solution (Beanie Sigel album)">The Solution</a></i>  which debuted on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard charts</a> at number 37.
</p>
<h3><span id="Post_Roc-A-Fella_.282007.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_Roc-A-Fella_(2007–present)">Post Roc-A-Fella (2007–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beanie_Sigel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Post Roc-A-Fella (2007–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, Sigel released his first independent album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Broad_Street_Bully" title="The Broad Street Bully">The Broad Street Bully</a></i> after his contracts with Roc-A-Fella Records and <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Def Jam Records">Def Jam Records</a> had expired.
</p><p>In 2010, Sigel announced that he was working on his sixth studio album titled <i>The Closure</i> which was to be released on <a href="/wiki/Universal_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal Republic">Universal Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/G-Unit_Records" title="G-Unit Records">G-Unit Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> However, on May 31, 2011, Sigel cancelled the album and retired from the music industry for two weeks, before deciding to return and work on the album again, re-titling it <i>The Classic</i>. In 2011, Sigel signed with <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>'s subsidiary label <a href="/wiki/G-Unit_Philly" class="mw-redirect" title="G-Unit Philly">G-Unit Philly</a> and renewed his 2009 contract with G-Unit Records.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> On July 13, 2011, Sigel released his first single from the album titled "B-Boy Stance".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
On March 30, 2012, Sigel told <a href="/wiki/Statik_Selektah" title="Statik Selektah">Statik Selektah</a> on <a href="/wiki/Shade_45" title="Shade 45">Shade 45</a> that he would be working on a collaborative album with his longtime close friend, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_rap" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern rap">Southern rap</a> veteran <a href="/wiki/Scarface_(rapper)" title="Scarface (rapper)">Scarface</a>; the album was to be called <i>Mac and Brad</i>. Scarface tweeted the announcement at the same time via mobile. On that same day, while distribution deals with <a href="/wiki/E1_Entertainment_Distribution" class="mw-redirect" title="E1 Entertainment Distribution">E1</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Orchard_(company)" title="The Orchard (company)">The Orchard</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asylum_Records" title="Asylum Records">Asylum</a> were on the table, Sigel also decided to sign a distribution deal with <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> after one of his mutual friends garnered the interest of Chris Schwartz. Under Schwartz's guidance, Sigel released a new <a href="/wiki/Mixtape" title="Mixtape">mixtape</a>, followed by his studio album <i><a href="/wiki/This_Time_(Beanie_Sigel_album)" title="This Time (Beanie Sigel album)">This Time</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-EMI_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EMI-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting">Acting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beanie_Sigel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sigel first appeared in the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Backstage_(2000_film)" title="Backstage (2000 film)">Backstage</a>,</i> and his first main acting role was in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/State_Property_(film)" title="State Property (film)">State Property</a></i> which spawned a sequel <i><a href="/wiki/State_Property_2" title="State Property 2">State Property 2</a></i>. He also appeared along with <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Hart_(actor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kevin Hart (actor)">Kevin Hart</a> in the 2002 film <i><a href="/wiki/Paper_Soldiers" title="Paper Soldiers">Paper Soldiers</a></i>. Sigel released a DVD titled <i>The B. Coming of Beanie Sigel</i> in 2005, shortly after his third solo album release <i>The B. Coming</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, Sigel was interviewed in the <i>Rap Sheet: Hip-Hop and the Cops</i> documentary, which exposed rappers to the dossiers maintained by NYPD as part of their effort to surveil hip-hop artists and executives.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He also auditioned for the lead role in the 2009 film <i><a href="/wiki/Notorious_(2009_film)" title="Notorious (2009 film)">Notorious</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sigel appeared in the 2011 film <i>Rhyme and Punishment</i>, a documentary about hip-hop artists who have served time in county jail or state/federal prison. The film features an interview with Sigel in which he discusses his conviction and life while incarcerated.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feuds">Feuds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beanie_Sigel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Feuds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beanie_Sigel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Jay-Z">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sigel recorded a song dissing his former boss and mentor Jay-Z on the track "I Go Off" with <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a> who had signed him to his G-Unit Records label in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> On May 31, 2011, it was reported that Sigel had apologized to Jay-Z. However, in an interview with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Green_Lantern" title="DJ Green Lantern">DJ Green Lantern</a>, he said that he had never "apologized" to Jay-Z, and that he was still making music.<sup id="cite_ref-beansgreenlantern_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beansgreenlantern-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, Sigel and Jay-Z reunited at the <a href="/wiki/Tidal_(service)" title="Tidal (service)">Tidal</a> B-Sides Concert and performed together, ending the feud.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beanie_Sigel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Jadakiss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the beginning of the new millennium, Sigel and various other Roc artists engaged in a conflict with artists from the <a href="/wiki/Ruff_Ryders" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruff Ryders">Ruff Ryders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Sigel publicly decried <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a>, the rest of <a href="/wiki/The_LOX" class="mw-redirect" title="The LOX">The LOX</a> and <a href="/wiki/DMX_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="DMX (rapper)">DMX</a>. Although Sigel and Jadakiss both denied any violent intent, they continued to exchange criticism until Sigel put out a freestyle rap over Jadakiss' hit "Put Ya Hands Up".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meek_Mill">Meek Mill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beanie_Sigel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Meek Mill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 26, 2016, Sigel and <a href="/wiki/Meek_Mill" title="Meek Mill">Meek Mill</a>, a former friend and neighbor, got into an altercation when Sigel ridiculed Mill's intelligence and claimed to be "the ruler of all of Philly" during a radio interview.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Minutes after the interview, someone who was believed to have been one of Meek Mill's affiliates, punched Sigel on the back of his head.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Three days later, Mill criticized Sigel via <a href="/wiki/Instagram" title="Instagram">Instagram</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> He also dissed Sigel again in a freestyle on <a href="/wiki/Funkmaster_Flex" title="Funkmaster Flex">Funkmaster Flex</a>'s radio show.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Sigel responded by releasing his own diss track titled "I'm Coming".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beanie_Sigel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sigel is a <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunni Muslim">Sunni Muslim</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beanie_Sigel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Sigel was arrested and charged with a federal weapons charge in <a href="/wiki/West_Philadelphia" title="West Philadelphia">West Philadelphia</a>. He would later serve a year in federal prison.<sup id="cite_ref-WENN_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WENN-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sigel was arrested in January 2003 after allegedly punching a 53-year-old man in the face, fracturing his eye socket.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> On July 3, 2003, Sigel turned himself in to Philadelphia police and was charged with attempted murder after allegedly firing six shots from a 9&#160;mm handgun at a club door, injuring two people. He was also charged with aggravated assault, simple assault and possession of a criminal instrument. His first trial ended in a <a href="/wiki/Hung_jury" title="Hung jury">hung jury</a>. A second trial resulted in a not-guilty verdict.<sup id="cite_ref-MTV_News_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV_News-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, Sigel was convicted of federal weapons charges and sentenced to one year and one day in federal prison.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sigel was shot multiple times during a robbery in Philadelphia on May 25, 2006. He was able to flee the scene and drive himself to a local hospital. One month prior to this incident, Sigel was released from prison after serving a brief stint for child support charges.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 15, 2009, Sigel was arrested on a drug possession charge while traveling to a concert in New Jersey. He was found in possession of nearly 50 grams (1.8&#160;oz) of marijuana in his vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sigel was charged in 2010 with three counts of failing to file tax returns on income of over $1 million between 2003 and 2005. He pled guilty to the charges and was sentenced to 25 months in prison.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 29, 2012, two weeks before reporting to prison, Sigel was arrested again in Philadelphia on drug, weapons, and conspiracy charges after being pulled over by police. Officers found a fully loaded .38-caliber <a href="/wiki/Smith_%26_Wesson" title="Smith &amp; Wesson">Smith &amp; Wesson</a> handgun inside the vehicle, as well as $4,500 in cash, an unlabeled bottle of codeine cough syrup, and various pills.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Sigel began his 24-month sentence for failing to file tax returns at the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Detention_Center,_Philadelphia" title="Federal Detention Center, Philadelphia">Federal Detention Center, Philadelphia</a> on September 12, 2012. Additionally, he was sentenced six to 23 months in county jail for illegal possession of prescription drugs, to be served concurrently with his federal sentence.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 14, 2014, Sigel was released from <a href="/wiki/Federal_Correctional_Institution,_Schuylkill" title="Federal Correctional Institution, Schuylkill">Federal Correctional Institution, Schuylkill</a> and was reported to be serving out the rest of his sentence in a <a href="/wiki/Halfway_house" title="Halfway house">halfway house</a> in Philadelphia.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Sigel was wounded in a drive-by shooting outside his Pleasantville, New Jersey home on December 5, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> He was rushed to the hospital where he had a <a href="/wiki/Pneumonectomy" title="Pneumonectomy">lung removed</a> due to complications during surgery. According to police, Sigel was not the intended victim.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
