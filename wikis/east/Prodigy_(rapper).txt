<p><b>Albert Johnson</b> (November 2, 1974 – June 20, 2017), better known by his stage name <b>Prodigy</b>, was an American rapper. With <a href="/wiki/Havoc_(musician)" title="Havoc (musician)">Havoc</a>, he was one half of the <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> duo <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prodigy was born on November 2, 1974, in <a href="/wiki/Hempstead_(village),_New_York" title="Hempstead (village), New York">Hempstead, New York</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>. He was raised in <a href="/wiki/LeFrak_City" title="LeFrak City">LeFrak City</a> in <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He had one elder brother, Greg Johnson.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He came from a musical family.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> His grandfather <a href="/wiki/Budd_Johnson" title="Budd Johnson">Budd Johnson</a> was a saxophonist who was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Big_Band_and_Jazz_Hall_of_Fame" title="Big Band and Jazz Hall of Fame">Big Band and Jazz Hall of Fame</a> in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> His grand-uncle, <a href="/wiki/Keg_Johnson" title="Keg Johnson">Keg Johnson</a>, was a <a href="/wiki/Trombonist" class="mw-redirect" title="Trombonist">trombonist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-To_Be,_Or_Not--_to_Bop_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-To_Be,_Or_Not--_to_Bop-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Both of them are remembered for their contributions to the <a href="/wiki/Bebop" title="Bebop">bebop</a> era of <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-heavy_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heavy-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> His mother, Fatima Frances (Collins) Johnson,<sup id="cite_ref-rsdeath_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rsdeath-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> was a member of <a href="/wiki/The_Crystals" title="The Crystals">The Crystals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-news_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> His father, Budd Johnson Jr., was a member of a <a href="/wiki/Doo-wop" title="Doo-wop">doo-wop</a> music group called The Chanters.<sup id="cite_ref-heavy_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heavy-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> His great-great-great-grandfather, <a href="/wiki/William_J._White_(journalist)" title="William J. White (journalist)">William Jefferson White</a>, founded Georgia's <a href="/wiki/Morehouse_College" title="Morehouse College">Morehouse College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-urban_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urban-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While attending the <a href="/wiki/High_School_of_Art_and_Design" title="High School of Art and Design">High School of Art and Design</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, he met his future music partner, <a href="/wiki/Havoc_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Havoc (rapper)">Havoc</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-theguardian2_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian2-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The duo became Poetical Prophets before choosing the name Mobb Deep.<sup id="cite_ref-rsdeath_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rsdeath-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Under the alias Lord-T (The Golden Child), the then-16-year-old Johnson landed an uncredited guest appearance on the <a href="/wiki/Boyz_n_the_Hood_(soundtrack)" title="Boyz n the Hood (soundtrack)"><i>Boyz n the Hood</i> soundtrack</a>, for his collaborative efforts on the song "Too Young" by <a href="/wiki/Hi-Five" title="Hi-Five">Hi-Five</a> in 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-rsdeath_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rsdeath-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1993, Mobb Deep released its debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Juvenile_Hell" title="Juvenile Hell">Juvenile Hell</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/4th_%26_B%27way_Records" title="4th &amp; B&#39;way Records">4th &amp; B'way</a>/<a href="/wiki/Island_Records" title="Island Records">Island</a>/<a href="/wiki/PolyGram_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="PolyGram Records">PolyGram Records</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1995.E2.80.931999:_The_Infamous.2C_Hell_on_Earth_and_Murda_Muzik"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1995–1999:_The_Infamous,_Hell_on_Earth_and_Murda_Muzik">1995–1999: <i>The Infamous,</i> <i>Hell on Earth</i> and <i>Murda Muzik</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1995–1999: The Infamous, Hell on Earth and Murda Muzik">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Initially compared to fellow rapper <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, who took a similar approach lyrically on his <i><a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">Illmatic</a></i> album from 1994,<sup id="cite_ref-ambrosiaforheads_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ambrosiaforheads-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Mobb Deep released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Infamous" title="The Infamous">The Infamous</a></i> in 1995, which was certified Gold by the RIAA within the first two months of its release.<sup id="cite_ref-Vibe2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibe2-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> 1995 was also the year that Prodigy began to raise his solo profile, by providing a guest appearance on <a href="/wiki/LL_Cool_J" title="LL Cool J">LL Cool J</a>'s controversial "<a href="/wiki/I_Shot_Ya_(remix)" class="mw-redirect" title="I Shot Ya (remix)">I Shot Ya (remix)</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-genius.com_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-genius.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The song became a minor part of the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast%E2%80%93West_Coast_hip_hop_rivalry" title="East Coast–West Coast hip hop rivalry">East Coast–West Coast hip hop rivalry</a>, due to <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a> believing the song to be a <a href="/wiki/Diss_song" class="mw-redirect" title="Diss song">diss</a> referring to his robbery/shooting in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Manhattan, New York">Manhattan, New York</a> at Quad Recording Studios – singling out the song's title (which many assumed was connected to <a href="/wiki/Biggie" class="mw-redirect" title="Biggie">Biggie</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Who_Shot_Ya%3F" title="Who Shot Ya?">Who Shot Ya?</a>"), certain lyrics, and the timing of its release – the year after the shooting incident.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the track was stated by Keith Murray to not have any lyrical shots aimed at Tupac, Mobb Deep responded in the following year to Tupac's "<a href="/wiki/Hit_Em_Up" class="mw-redirect" title="Hit Em Up">Hit Em Up</a>" with "<a href="/wiki/Drop_a_Gem_on_%27Em" class="mw-redirect" title="Drop a Gem on &#39;Em">Drop a Gem on 'Em</a>," a <a href="/wiki/Promotional_single" class="mw-redirect" title="Promotional single">promotional single</a> from their 1996 album <i><a href="/wiki/Hell_on_Earth_(Mobb_Deep_album)" title="Hell on Earth (Mobb Deep album)">Hell On Earth</a></i>. Ironically, "<a href="/wiki/I_Shot_Ya" title="I Shot Ya">I Shot Ya</a>" does feature a subliminal aim in Prodigy's verse to Murray, which continued friction that started sometime prior with an interlude from <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>'s 1995 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Infamous" title="The Infamous">The Infamous</a></i> album.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The rivalry continued until sometime in 2012, when the two ended it by taking a picture together.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A year and a half later, at the end of 1996, Prodigy and Havoc released <i><a href="/wiki/Hell_on_Earth_(Mobb_Deep_album)" title="Hell on Earth (Mobb Deep album)">Hell on Earth</a></i>, which debuted at number six on <a href="/wiki/SoundScan" class="mw-redirect" title="SoundScan">SoundScan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-F.E.D.S._Magazine:_Finally_Every_Dimension_of_the_Streets_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-F.E.D.S._Magazine:_Finally_Every_Dimension_of_the_Streets-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Their next release, <i><a href="/wiki/Murda_Muzik" title="Murda Muzik">Murda Muzik</a></i>, was heavily bootlegged while still in its demo stage, leaking, onto the streets and over the internet, rough versions of the nearly 30 songs the duo had recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-YouTube_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YouTube-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2000.E2.80.932006:_H.N.I.C."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000–2006:_H.N.I.C.">2000–2006: <i>H.N.I.C.</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000–2006: H.N.I.C.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2000, Prodigy released his debut solo album <i><a href="/wiki/H.N.I.C._(Prodigy_album)" title="H.N.I.C. (Prodigy album)">H.N.I.C.</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-The_Encyclopedia_of_Popular_Music_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Encyclopedia_of_Popular_Music-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> His followup solo album would be released in 2008.
</p><p>During the next six years, between the releases of his first two solo albums, Prodigy continued to work with Mobb Deep, releasing <i><a href="/wiki/Infamy_(album)" title="Infamy (album)">Infamy</a></i> in 2001, <i><a href="/wiki/Amerikaz_Nightmare" title="Amerikaz Nightmare">Amerikaz Nightmare</a></i> in 2004, and <i><a href="/wiki/Blood_Money_(Mobb_Deep_album)" title="Blood Money (Mobb Deep album)">Blood Money</a></i> in 2006.
</p>
<h3><span id="2007.E2.80.932011:_Collaborations_and_H.N.I.C._Part_2"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2007–2011:_Collaborations_and_H.N.I.C._Part_2">2007–2011: Collaborations and <i>H.N.I.C. Part 2</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2007–2011: Collaborations and H.N.I.C. Part 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During this time, Prodigy had started work on his second solo album, <i>H.N.I.C. Part 2</i>, which was first previewed on his official mixtape <i>The Return of the Mac</i>, and was later released on the independent label <a href="/wiki/Koch_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Koch Records">Koch Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rap4fame_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rap4fame-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The mixtape single, together with the mixtape video, was called "Mac 10 Handle". Prodigy then released <i><a href="/wiki/H.N.I.C._Pt._2" title="H.N.I.C. Pt. 2">H.N.I.C. Pt. 2</a></i> through Voxonic Inc., a company in which he was an <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">equity</a> holder.<sup id="cite_ref-XXL1_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL1-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In late 2009, Mobb Deep was released from their contract with <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>'s <a href="/wiki/G-Unit_Records" title="G-Unit Records">G-Unit</a> label.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-theurbandaily.cassiuslife_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theurbandaily.cassiuslife-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During this time, Prodigy was served with a three-year sentence in <a href="/wiki/Mid-State_Correctional_Facility" title="Mid-State Correctional Facility">Mid-State Correctional Facility</a> (a medium-security prison), following a plea agreement stemming from a gun-possession charge.<sup id="cite_ref-release_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-release-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> He was officially released on March 7, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-release_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-release-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prodigy was featured in the 2009 documentary <i>Rhyme and Punishment</i>, which documented hip-hop artists who had been incarcerated.<sup id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, Prodigy released a free EP called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ellsworth_Bumpy_Johnson_EP" title="The Ellsworth Bumpy Johnson EP">The Ellsworth Bumpy Johnson EP</a></i>, his first project after being released from prison.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 21, a song titled "The Type", with <a href="/wiki/Currensy" title="Currensy">Curren$y</a>, was released on Curren$y's free album <i>Covert Coup</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, Prodigy released his autobiography, <i>My Infamous Life: The Autobiography of Mobb Deep's Prodigy</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-My_Infamous_Life:_The_Autobiography_of_Mobb_Deep&#39;s_Prodigy_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-My_Infamous_Life:_The_Autobiography_of_Mobb_Deep&#39;s_Prodigy-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2013.E2.80.932014:_The_Infamous_Mobb_Deep"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2013–2014:_The_Infamous_Mobb_Deep">2013–2014: <i>The Infamous Mobb Deep</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2013–2014: The Infamous Mobb Deep">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2013, Prodigy released his second collaboration album with the Alchemist, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein_(album)" title="Albert Einstein (album)">Albert Einstein</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Albert_Einstein_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Albert_Einstein-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> On April 1, 2014, Mobb Deep released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Infamous_Mobb_Deep" title="The Infamous Mobb Deep">The Infamous Mobb Deep</a></i>, their eighth studio album.<sup id="cite_ref-complex_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mobb_Deep:_The_Infamous_Mobb_Depp_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mobb_Deep:_The_Infamous_Mobb_Depp-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In August 2016, he released an untitled EP of five tracks, in partnership with <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent" title="BitTorrent">BitTorrent</a>, an association that Prodigy had been working up for a while.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2011, Prodigy released his autobiography, <i>My Infamous Life: The Autobiography of Mobb Deep's Prodigy.</i> It was co-written with Laura Checkoway and published by Touchstone Books.<sup id="cite_ref-My_Infamous_Life:_The_Autobiography_of_Mobb_Deep&#39;s_Prodigy_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-My_Infamous_Life:_The_Autobiography_of_Mobb_Deep&#39;s_Prodigy-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013 Prodigy co-wrote the <a href="/wiki/Crime_fiction" title="Crime fiction">urban crime</a> <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novel</a> <i>H.N.I.C.</i> with British author <a href="/wiki/Steven_Savile" title="Steven Savile">Steven Savile</a>. It was published by Akashic / Infamous Books.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> They also co-wrote a second novel, <i> Ritual</i>, that was released in 2015 by Akashic.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prodigy co-wrote a <a href="/wiki/Cookbook" title="Cookbook">cookbook</a> with <a href="/wiki/Kathy_Iandoli" title="Kathy Iandoli">Kathy Iandoli</a> titled <i>Commissary Kitchen: My Infamous Prison Cookbook</i>. It features a foreword by chef and food personality <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Huang" title="Eddie Huang">Eddie Huang</a> and was published in 2016 by Infamous Books.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following is a brief timeline and chronology of some of the legal issues that Prodigy faced during his life:
</p>
<ul><li>November 6, 2003, Prodigy was arrested in <a href="/wiki/Cohoes,_New_York" title="Cohoes, New York">Cohoes, New York</a>, and charged with third degree criminal possession of a weapon and unlawful possession of cannabis. Police reportedly recovered a .25 caliber handgun and cannabis on his person.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>October 26, 2006, Prodigy was arrested in New York City and charged with criminal possession of a weapon. He was pulled over in a $120,000 customized bulletproof SUV after making an illegal u-turn around 2:15 AM. After conducting a search of the vehicle, police recovered a .22 caliber handgun in the center console.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>October 8, 2007, Prodigy was sentenced to serve three-and-a-half years in prison for illegal possession of a firearm. Originally facing a mandatory sentence of 15 years in prison, Prodigy struck a deal with the prosecution, and pleaded guilty in exchange for the shorter prison sentence.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>March 7, 2011, Prodigy was released from <a href="/wiki/Mid-State_Correctional_Facility" title="Mid-State Correctional Facility">Mid-State Correctional Facility</a> in <a href="/wiki/Marcy,_New_York" title="Marcy, New York">Marcy</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> after serving three years for criminal possession of a weapon. His sentence was reduced by six months for good behavior and he remained on parole until 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feuds">Feuds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Feuds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Coast">West Coast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: West Coast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1995 to 1997, the media-fueled "<a href="/wiki/East_Coast-West_Coast_hip_hop_rivalry" class="mw-redirect" title="East Coast-West Coast hip hop rivalry">East Coast-West Coast hip hop rivalry</a>" was occurring. This started when <a href="/wiki/Tha_Dogg_Pound" title="Tha Dogg Pound">Tha Dogg Pound</a> released "New York, New York," to which Mobb Deep took offense, as, in addition to the lyrics, the song's music video portrayed New York buildings being stomped on by Dogg Pound members. In response, Mobb Deep with <a href="/wiki/Capone-N-Noreaga" title="Capone-N-Noreaga">Capone-N-Noreaga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_Khadafi" title="Tragedy Khadafi">Tragedy Khadafi</a> released "LA, LA".<sup id="cite_ref-mtv_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">2Pac</a> "dissed" Mobb Deep (along with <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>) in "<a href="/wiki/Hit_%27Em_Up" title="Hit &#39;Em Up">Hit 'Em Up</a>" where, in the outro of the song, he made a remark in clear reference to Prodigy's ailment in having <a href="/wiki/Sickle_cell_anemia" class="mw-redirect" title="Sickle cell anemia">sickle cell anemia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rap-Up:_The_Ultimate_Guide_to_Hip-Hop_and_R&amp;B_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rap-Up:_The_Ultimate_Guide_to_Hip-Hop_and_R&amp;B-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Mobb Deep responded in a track called "<a href="/wiki/Drop_A_Gem_On_%27Em" class="mw-redirect" title="Drop A Gem On &#39;Em">Drop A Gem On 'Em</a>" which was released as a single 2 weeks before 2Pac was murdered.<sup id="cite_ref-variety_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-variety-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> 2Pac also dissed Mobb Deep on the song "<a href="/wiki/The_Don_Killuminati:_The_7_Day_Theory" title="The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory">Against All Odds</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-top_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> and "Bomb First (My Second Reply)" which were released after his death. But Prodigy later sampled 2Pac's voice from a freestyle for the chorus on the song "Return of the Mac" (a.k.a. "New York Shit") <a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Mac" title="Return of the Mac">on his album with the same name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-prefixmag_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prefixmag-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Def_Squad">Def Squad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Def Squad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On <i>The Infamous</i> track "The Infamous Prelude", Prodigy made remarks about rappers who rap about "smoking weed" and talk about "space shit".<sup id="cite_ref-keith_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-keith-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Def_Squad" title="Def Squad">Def Squad</a> took offense from this, but the feud was settled when Prodigy and <a href="/wiki/Keith_Murray_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Keith Murray (rapper)">Keith Murray</a> met at a video shoot.<sup id="cite_ref-keith_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-keith-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The feud was rekindled when Prodigy again referenced "space shit" in his appearance on <a href="/wiki/LL_Cool_J" title="LL Cool J">LL Cool J</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/I_Shot_Ya" title="I Shot Ya">I Shot Ya</a>" which also featured Murray. Murray saw Prodigy at a club one night and punched him.<sup id="cite_ref-keith_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-keith-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Prodigy recalled the altercation and threatened Murray in the song "In the Long Run" on <i>Hell on Earth</i>. Murray released a song "Call My Name" on his <i><a href="/wiki/Enigma_(Keith_Murray_album)" title="Enigma (Keith Murray album)">Enigma</a></i> album dissing Mobb Deep. The feud seemed to die down until Prodigy dissed Murray again in his 2004 song "Bad Blood." Murray has responded with numerous songs since.<sup id="cite_ref-keith_52-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-keith-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The feud has since died down, with the two sharing a photograph together with <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a> on social media.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> On Twitter, The reconciliation was confirmed by Prodigy in response to the photo not long after.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saigon">Saigon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Saigon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During an interview, Prodigy stated that he did not like <a href="/wiki/Saigon_(rapper)" title="Saigon (rapper)">Saigon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tru-Life" class="mw-redirect" title="Tru-Life">Tru-Life</a> (along with many other rappers).<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the night of September 19, 2007, after an impromptu performance by Saigon during a Mobb Deep show, words were exchanged between Saigon and Prodigy.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> This escalated into an argument, which resulted in a physical altercation when Saigon punched Prodigy twice before leaving the club.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Two video versions of the events have since emerged. One version, in slow motion footage, showed Saigon hiding under a table. Another released version of the video, showed Saigon running away from the club.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The feud, however, apparently died down, since (in an interview two months before Prodigy's release from prison) Saigon expressed happiness that Prodigy was coming home.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crooked_I">Crooked I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Crooked I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While in prison, Prodigy wrote a letter about his disillusionment with hip hop and rappers. He directly referenced <a href="/wiki/Crooked_I" title="Crooked I">Crooked I</a>'s name in the letter, commenting,
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Vibe says 920,000 people voted for it. I would personally b*tch slap all 920,000 of these voters if given the opportunity. Who in the f*ck picked Crooked I, Flo Rida and Rich Boy? How did Vibe approve this?</p></blockquote>
<p>Crooked I responded in a blog entry, and challenged Prodigy to a one-on-one fight upon the rapper's release.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Following Prodigy's death, Crooked I paid tribute in honor of him by posting an image of him on Instagram.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Havoc">Havoc</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Havoc">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 2012, Prodigy's musical partner, <a href="/wiki/Havoc_(musician)" title="Havoc (musician)">Havoc</a>, wrote a series of derogatory comments about Prodigy on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>, including accusing Prodigy of engaging in homosexual relationships in prison.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> At first, Havoc claimed that his Twitter account was hacked.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> However, he later confirmed that he wrote the tweets and expressed his frustrations with Prodigy in an interview with <a href="/wiki/AllHipHop" title="AllHipHop">AllHipHop</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> He stated that Mobb Deep was on an "indefinite hiatus" until the duo worked out their differences. Havoc later released a <a href="/wiki/Diss_track" class="mw-redirect" title="Diss track">diss track</a> aimed at Prodigy, which was titled "Separated (Real from the Fake)".<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Prodigy did not respond to Havoc's song and, even stated publicly, that Mobb Deep would eventually reconcile.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> In March 2013, the duo announced that they had reconciled and were going on tour.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Illuminati_theory">Illuminati theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Illuminati theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Illuminati_in_popular_culture" title="Illuminati in popular culture">Illuminati in popular culture</a></div>
<p>Some hip hop music has been inspired by the theory that a powerful international secret society exists. Often it is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Illuminati" title="Illuminati">Illuminati</a>, after the Bavarian secret society founded in 1776. <i><a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a></i> magazine has claimed it was Prodigy who started the interest in the theory.<sup id="cite_ref-complex.com_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex.com-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Prodigy had often spoken publicly against the alleged international secret society during his life.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prodigy rhymed about a <a href="/wiki/Secret_society" title="Secret society">secret society</a> in his collaboration with <a href="/wiki/LL_Cool_J" title="LL Cool J">LL Cool J</a> in the song "<a href="/wiki/I_Shot_Ya_(Remix)" class="mw-redirect" title="I Shot Ya (Remix)">I Shot Ya (Remix)</a>", from the 1995 album <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Smith_(album)" title="Mr. Smith (album)">Mr. Smith</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-genius.com_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-genius.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> From prison in 2007, not long before the release of <i>H.N.I.C. Part 2</i>, Prodigy wrote and published an open letter to <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> in which he made some cryptic allegations alluding to the theory.<sup id="cite_ref-complex.com_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex.com-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> In 2008, Prodigy titled a song "Illuminati", from <i>H.N.I.C. Part 2</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> In his final solo album released during his life, <i>The Hegelian Dialectic</i> (2017), Prodigy also referred to the theory.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> It was reported that Prodigy was working on a musical about his Illuminati theory at the time of his death.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_71-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Illness_and_death">Illness and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prodigy_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Illness and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> in November 2000, Prodigy spoke about what inspired him to directly address his battle with <a href="/wiki/Sickle_cell_disease" title="Sickle cell disease">sickle cell disease</a> in his song "You Can Never Feel My Pain", from his debut studio album <i>H.N.I.C</i>. He attributed his nihilism to the "permanent physical suffering" caused by his lifelong battle with the condition.<sup id="cite_ref-Vibe_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibe-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 20, 2017, it was reported that Prodigy had died at the Spring Valley Medical Center in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" class="mw-redirect" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">Las Vegas, Nevada</a>, while hospitalized for complications related to his sickle-cell anemia. He was performing in the Art of Rap Tour in Las Vegas with Havoc, <a href="/wiki/Ghostface_Killah" title="Ghostface Killah">Ghostface Killah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onyx_(hip_hop_group)" title="Onyx (hip hop group)">Onyx</a>, <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">KRS-One</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a> when he was hospitalized.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a coroner's report, Prodigy was admitted to Spring Valley Medical Center after suffering a significant medical episode arising from his life-long battle with sickle-cell anemia. Days later, on the morning of June 20, he was found unresponsive by hospital staff. It was reported at the time that Prodigy died from accidental choking.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> The complaint, which was filed on behalf of the family by the Gage Law Firm, alleges that the Spring Valley hospital breached their duty of care for Prodigy by "failing to maintain a working IV access," and by "failing to continuously monitor oxygen levels" as ordered by physicians in the hospital, and that those failures led to Prodigy's death.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
