<p><b>Robert Mandell</b>, professionally known as <b>G Koop</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Grammy_award" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammy award">Grammy</a>-nominated record producer, songwriter and musician.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G_Koop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>G Koop is originally from <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. His father had a large collection of albums, and his mother contends that G Koop's first word was "doobie," interpreted as a request for <a href="/wiki/The_Doobie_Brothers" title="The Doobie Brothers">The Doobie Brothers</a> to be played. His first job was at a record store. As a young child, he learned to play a number of instruments, including guitar, bass, piano, and drums.<sup id="cite_ref-DJBooth_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DJBooth-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>G Koop graduated from <a href="/wiki/Berklee_College_of_Music" title="Berklee College of Music">Berklee College of Music</a>. While there, he studied music theory and performance. G Koop plays some of these instruments in his own studio while making his samples. Also while at Berklee, G Koop met <a href="/wiki/Graham_Richards" title="Graham Richards">Graham Richards</a> and Anthony Caruso. The three have had an ongoing collaboration dubbed "G Koop and O-Man" since then.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Back_Packerz_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Back_Packerz-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=G_Koop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>G Koop moved to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in 2002 and formed a jazz band, "The People's Quintet," with drummer <a href="/wiki/Donald_Bailey_(musician)" title="Donald Bailey (musician)">Donald "Duck" Bailey</a>. Unable to find work for the group, G Koop instead began working with music production software, such as <a href="/wiki/Pro_Tools" title="Pro Tools">Pro Tools</a> and Reason.<sup id="cite_ref-DJBooth_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DJBooth-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He has been called a specialist in "<a href="/wiki/Interpolation_(popular_music)" title="Interpolation (popular music)">replaying samples</a>", in which an original <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sample</a> is reinterpreted and recreated in the studio. The resulting track is a new interpretation that is no longer under the original's copyright, but it still retains the original's character. Producers can use the new work in their own songs without having to pay the fees required to sample the original track.<sup id="cite_ref-hnhh_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hnhh-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sample_Replay_Society_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sample_Replay_Society-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>G Koop first produced for the Bay Area hip-hop group Foreign Legion. The group's <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Ceremonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Master of Ceremonies">MC</a>, Prozack Turner, introduced him to producer <a href="/wiki/Jake_One" title="Jake One">Jake One</a>, who in turn employed G Koop as a piano teacher. The two worked together on Jake's 2008 album, <i><a href="/wiki/White_Van_Music" title="White Van Music">White Van Music</a></i>, with G Koop performing instruments. He also worked on Jake's 2009 collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Freeway_(rapper)" title="Freeway (rapper)">Freeway</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stimulus_Package" title="The Stimulus Package">The Stimulus Package</a></i>. The two also partnered together on <a href="/wiki/Drake_(musician)" title="Drake (musician)">Drake</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Furthest_Thing" title="Furthest Thing">Furthest Thing</a>" for his 2013 <i>Nothing was the Same</i> album. Jake One found the original sample for "Furthest Thing" from a 1990s live <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a> album that Gene Brown, a record collector, recommended to him. However, the rights owners to the song requested high fees. Instead, Jake One asked G Koop to make an interpolation of the sample to avoid publishing issues. G Koop played all the instruments—guitar, piano, organ, drums, and bass—in his studio. The album's main producer, <a href="/wiki/Noah_%2240%22_Shebib" class="mw-redirect" title="Noah &quot;40&quot; Shebib">Noah "40" Shebib</a>, recorded the vocals for the track with a choir in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DJBooth_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DJBooth-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-npr_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npr-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>G Koop's first major placement as producer was <a href="/wiki/Nelly" title="Nelly">Nelly</a>'s "Self Esteem" featuring <a href="/wiki/Chuck_D" title="Chuck D">Chuck D</a>, which was on Nelly's 2008 gold album <i><a href="/wiki/Brass_Knuckles_(album)" title="Brass Knuckles (album)">Brass Knuckles</a></i>. He has worked with a number of artists, including <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(music_group)" title="Atmosphere (music group)">Atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/MF_Doom" title="MF Doom">DOOM</a>, Funkadelic, The Game, <a href="/wiki/G_Unit" class="mw-redirect" title="G Unit">G Unit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pusha_T" title="Pusha T">Pusha T</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhymesayers" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhymesayers">Rhymesayers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rihanna" title="Rihanna">Rihanna</a>, among others. G Koop also wrote, produced, and played a number of songs with <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)">George Clinton</a> on Funkadelic's 2014 <a href="/wiki/First_Ya_Gotta_Shake_the_Gate" title="First Ya Gotta Shake the Gate">First Ya Gotta Shake the Gate</a>. The album, the first for the band in 33 years, provided 33 tracks with a number of artists, including <a href="/wiki/Sly_Stone" title="Sly Stone">Sly Stone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soul_Clap" title="Soul Clap">Soul Clap</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bootsy_Collins" title="Bootsy Collins">Bootsy Collins</a>. The second single on the album, "Ain’t that Funkin’ Kinda Hard on You," was written and produced by G Koop and George Clinton. Hip Hop producer Ill Tal, in his review, called it "one of the best songs on the album and the name of George’s new auto-biography. The song is smooth, mellow, and funky with George doing most of the vocal leads." Nick DeRiso, in his review of the album for Something Else Reviews, called "Ain’t that Funkin’ Kinda Hard on You" the "perfect reintroduction of George Clinton … since it begins with a darkly ingratiating embrace rather that the expected turn-this-mother-out groove."  DeRiso goes on to call the track the "standout moment from Funkadelic’s sprawling new comeback effort." Other G Koop songs were singled out in reviews as well. Ill Tal wrote of "You Can’t Unring the Bell," that it is "probably my favorite hip-hop influenced song on the album." Ill Tal went on to refer to G Koop (although not by name): "Whoever made the beat did a great job sampling a break beat for the drums and what sounds like an old horn riff from George’s vaults." G Koop's bass playing was also recognized in E.E. Bradman's review for <i><a href="/wiki/Bass_Player_(magazine)" title="Bass Player (magazine)">Bass Player</a></i>, who commended G Koop's bass playing on a number of tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-HipHopDX_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HipHopDX-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-http&#58;//illtal.com_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-http://illtal.com-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SomethingElse!_Reviews_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SomethingElse!_Reviews-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Boston_Globe_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boston_Globe-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bass_Player_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bass_Player-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With Metro Boomin, G Koop co-produced the song "<a href="/wiki/Bad_and_Boujee" title="Bad and Boujee">Bad and Boujee</a>" by Migos, featuring <a href="/wiki/Lil_Uzi_Vert" title="Lil Uzi Vert">Lil Uzi Vert</a>—his first no. 1 hit. The song was released in October 2016, and it rose to no. 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> in January 2017. The song has gone <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">platinum</a> in the United States. The album the song appeared on, Migos' <i><a href="/wiki/Culture_(Migos_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Culture (Migos album)">Culture</a></i>, debuted at no. 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a>. The sample pack used on the song was originally sent to <a href="/wiki/Metro_Boomin" title="Metro Boomin">Metro Boomin</a>, and was used to make not only Bad and Boujee, but also <a href="/wiki/21_Savage" title="21 Savage">21 Savage</a>'s "Ocean Drive."<sup id="cite_ref-Thizzler_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thizzler-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>G Koop worked with producer <a href="/wiki/Southside_(record_producer)" title="Southside (record producer)">Southside</a> of <a href="/wiki/808_Mafia" title="808 Mafia">808 Mafia</a> on DJ Khaled's 2016 "<a href="/wiki/I_Got_the_Keys" title="I Got the Keys">I Got the Keys</a>," featuring <a href="/wiki/Jay_Z" class="mw-redirect" title="Jay Z">Jay Z</a> and Future. I Got the Keys has gone platinum, and rose to no. 30 on the Billboard Hot 100. The song is on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Major_Key_(album)" title="Major Key (album)">Major Key</a></i>, produced by Southside and co-produced by G Koop and Jake One as co-producers. The album was released at no. 1 on the Billboard 200, and was nominated for a Grammy for Best Rap Album.<sup id="cite_ref-DJBooth_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DJBooth-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Billboard_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-E!_online_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E!_online-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-XXL_Magazine_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-XXL_Magazine-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since then, he has worked with Ant, one of two members of Atmosphere, on Atmosphere's albums <i><a href="/wiki/Fishing_Blues" title="Fishing Blues">Fishing Blues</a></i> and <i>Southsiders</i>, and in a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Brother_Ali" title="Brother Ali">Brother Ali</a> on the latter's album, "All the Beauty in this Whole Life."<sup id="cite_ref-HipHopDX_Atmosphere_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HipHopDX_Atmosphere-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>G Koop's studio, "Fuzz Deluxxe," is based in Oakland. He also runs the website "Sample Replay Society," which works as a catalog for his work. He collects old instruments, such as the Clavinet and <a href="/wiki/Fender_Rhodes" class="mw-redirect" title="Fender Rhodes">Fender Rhodes</a>, and has several Fender guitars. He is a client of Pulse Recording.<sup id="cite_ref-gkoopandoman_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gkoopandoman-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pulse_Recordings_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pulse_Recordings-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
