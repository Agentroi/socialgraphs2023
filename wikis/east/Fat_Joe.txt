<p><b>Joseph Antonio Cartagena</b> (born August 19, 1970), better known by his stage name <b>Fat Joe</b>, is an American rapper from New York City. He began his music career as a member of <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Diggin%27_in_the_Crates_Crew" class="mw-redirect" title="Diggin&#39; in the Crates Crew">Diggin' in the Crates Crew</a> (D.I.T.C.) in 1992, he then embarked on a solo career a year later with his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Represent_(Fat_Joe_album)" title="Represent (Fat Joe album)">Represent</a></i> (1993). Joe formed the hip hop group <a href="/wiki/Terror_Squad_(group)" title="Terror Squad (group)">Terror Squad</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Terror_Squad_Entertainment" title="Terror Squad Entertainment">namesake record label</a> in the late 1990s, to which he would sign fellow New York artists including <a href="/wiki/Big_Pun" title="Big Pun">Big Pun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remy_Ma" title="Remy Ma">Remy Ma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Sunshine" title="Tony Sunshine">Tony Sunshine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Link" title="Cuban Link">Cuban Link</a>, as well as then-unknown producers <a href="/wiki/DJ_Khaled" title="DJ Khaled">DJ Khaled</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cool_%26_Dre" title="Cool &amp; Dre">Cool &amp; Dre</a>.
</p><p><i>Represent</i><sup id="cite_ref-all_music_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-all_music-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> spawned the single "<a href="/wiki/Flow_Joe" title="Flow Joe">Flow Joe</a>", which reached number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)"><i>Billboard</i></a> <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Songs</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was followed up with the albums <i><a href="/wiki/Jealous_One%27s_Envy" title="Jealous One&#39;s Envy">Jealous One's Envy</a></i> (1995), <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Cartagena" title="Don Cartagena">Don Cartagena</a></i> (1998), and <i><a href="/wiki/Jealous_Ones_Still_Envy_(J.O.S.E.)" title="Jealous Ones Still Envy (J.O.S.E.)">Jealous Ones Still Envy (J.O.S.E.)</a></i> (2001); the latter contained the hit song, "<a href="/wiki/What%27s_Luv%3F" title="What&#39;s Luv?">What's Luv?</a>" (featuring <a href="/wiki/Ja_Rule" title="Ja Rule">Ja Rule</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ashanti_(singer)" title="Ashanti (singer)">Ashanti</a>) and received <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/RIAA" class="mw-redirect" title="RIAA">RIAA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The song peaked at number 2 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100, while the 2004 single "<a href="/wiki/Lean_Back" title="Lean Back">Lean Back</a>" with his group <a href="/wiki/Terror_Squad_(group)" title="Terror Squad (group)">Terror Squad</a>, reached number 1 and became his most successful record to date. In the following years, Joe would also release the noteworthy singles "<a href="/wiki/Make_It_Rain_(Fat_Joe_song)" title="Make It Rain (Fat Joe song)">Make It Rain</a>" (featuring <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a>) in 2006, and "<a href="/wiki/All_the_Way_Up_(Fat_Joe_and_Remy_Ma_song)" title="All the Way Up (Fat Joe and Remy Ma song)">All the Way Up</a>" (with Remy Ma featuring <a href="/wiki/French_Montana" title="French Montana">French Montana</a> and Infared) a decade later.
</p><p>Fat Joe has appeared in several films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Scary_Movie_3" title="Scary Movie 3">Scary Movie 3</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Happy_Feet" title="Happy Feet">Happy Feet</a></i>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Spike_Lee" title="Spike Lee">Spike Lee</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/She%27s_Gotta_Have_It_(TV_series)" title="She&#39;s Gotta Have It (TV series)">She's Gotta Have It</a>.</i> In 2018 he began hosting a podcast on <a href="/wiki/Tidal_(service)" title="Tidal (service)">Tidal</a>, <i>Coca Vision</i>, where he discusses music, friendships, and pop culture with various guests.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> His album <a href="/wiki/Family_Ties_(Fat_Joe_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Family Ties (Fat Joe album)"><i>Family Ties</i></a> was released in December 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span></h2>
<p>Fat Joe was born Joseph Antonio Cartagena in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">the Bronx</a> borough of New York City, where he was raised by parents of Puerto Rican and Cuban descent.<sup id="cite_ref-all_music_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-all_music-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Faithful_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Faithful-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Different_Music_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Different_Music-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Living in the <a href="/wiki/Forest_Houses" title="Forest Houses">Forest Houses</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Public_housing" title="Public housing">public housing</a> project in the <a href="/wiki/South_Bronx" title="South Bronx">South Bronx</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Morrisania,_Bronx" title="Morrisania, Bronx">Morrisania</a>, Fat Joe began stealing at a young age to support his family.<sup id="cite_ref-LatinRappin_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LatinRappin-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He also admits that he was a <a href="/wiki/Bullying" title="Bullying">bully</a> in his childhood.<sup id="cite_ref-LatinRappin_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LatinRappin-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> His brother introduced him to hip hop music.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span></h2>
<h3><span id="1992.E2.80.931995:_early_years"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1992–1995:_early_years">1992–1995: early years</span></h3>
<p>Under stage name Fat Joe da Gangsta and part of the rap group <a href="/wiki/D.I.T.C." title="D.I.T.C.">D.I.T.C.</a>, Cartagena was signed to Relativity Records in the early 1990s, recording material and working with many artists whom he would later sign to his own label. In 1993, his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Represent_(Fat_Joe_album)" title="Represent (Fat Joe album)">Represent</a></i> was released, featuring production from <a href="/wiki/The_Beatnuts" title="The Beatnuts">The Beatnuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diamond_D" title="Diamond D">Diamond D</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Finesse" title="Lord Finesse">Lord Finesse</a>, and others. Its lead single, "<a href="/wiki/Flow_Joe" title="Flow Joe">Flow Joe</a>" peaked at number one on the Billboard Hot Rap Singles chart; other minor singles from the album included "Watch the Sound" and "This Shit Is Real".<sup id="cite_ref-all_music_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-all_music-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1995, Fat Joe released his second studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Jealous_One%27s_Envy" title="Jealous One&#39;s Envy">Jealous One's Envy</a></i>, which peaked at #71 on The Billboard 200 and at #7 on Top R&amp;B/Hip Hop Albums. The album featured a guest appearance from <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">KRS-One</a> and production from Diamond D. The lead single was "Success", which did not chart, but his second single, "Envy" peaked at #8 on the Hot Rap Tracks chart. One day while writing for the album at <a href="/wiki/Chung_King_Studios" title="Chung King Studios">Chung King Studios</a>, Fat Joe happened to find that his rap idol, <a href="/wiki/LL_Cool_J" title="LL Cool J">LL Cool J</a> was in another room with <a href="/wiki/Trackmasters" title="Trackmasters">Trackmasters</a>, working on the remix version of LL's single "<a href="/wiki/I_Shot_Ya" title="I Shot Ya">I Shot Ya</a>". After being welcomed to contribute a verse, Joe appeared on the record and in its accompanying video along with <a href="/wiki/Foxy_Brown_(rapper)" title="Foxy Brown (rapper)">Foxy Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keith_Murray_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Keith Murray (rapper)">Keith Murray</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Prodigy_(rapper)" title="Prodigy (rapper)">Prodigy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>. The track is considered by Joe to be one of his career highlights.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>During the recording of <i>Jealous One's Envy</i>, Joe discovered fellow <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_and_Latino_Americans" title="Hispanic and Latino Americans">Latino</a> rapper <a href="/wiki/Big_Pun" title="Big Pun">Big Pun</a>, who was featured on the song "Watch Out". Joe later explained the rapper's influence on him: "Latinos before us who had the opportunity to do it just didn't know how to do it. They came in trying to do this black music, waving flags. [But] we're trying to kick in the doors for other Latinos and represent our people, and it shows."<sup id="cite_ref-ChicagoTribune_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChicagoTribune-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1998.E2.80.932005:_signing_with_Atlantic.2C_Terror_Squad.2C_J.O.S.E..2C_50_Cent_feud"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1998–2005:_signing_with_Atlantic,_Terror_Squad,_J.O.S.E.,_50_Cent_feud">1998–2005: signing with Atlantic, Terror Squad, J.O.S.E., 50 Cent feud</span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Fat_joe-06-mika.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Fat_joe-06-mika.jpg/260px-Fat_joe-06-mika.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="179" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Fat_joe-06-mika.jpg/390px-Fat_joe-06-mika.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Fat_joe-06-mika.jpg/520px-Fat_joe-06-mika.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1306" data-file-height="899" /></a><figcaption>Fat Joe in 1999</figcaption></figure>
<p>In 1997, Fat Joe signed with Mystic Entertainment Group and <a href="/wiki/The_Warner_Music_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="The Warner Music Group">The Warner Music Group</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Records" title="Atlantic Records">Atlantic Records</a> through <a href="/wiki/Craig_Kallman" title="Craig Kallman">Craig Kallman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Big_Beat_Records_(American_record_label)" title="Big Beat Records (American record label)">Big Beat Records</a>. Under the deal, he was granted his own label, <a href="/wiki/Terror_Squad_Entertainment" title="Terror Squad Entertainment">Terror Squad Productions</a>. He subsequently began recording his third studio album. His third studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Don_Cartagena" title="Don Cartagena">Don Cartagena</a></i> was released on September 1, 1998. It was released in conjunction with his own label <a href="/wiki/Terror_Squad_Entertainment" title="Terror Squad Entertainment">Terror Squad Productions</a>. It peaked on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 at #7 and #2 on Top R&amp;B/Hip Hop albums, eventually being certified gold by the RIAA.<sup id="cite_ref-riaa_cert_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-riaa_cert-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The album featured three singles with accompanying music videos, "Bet Ya Man Can't Triz", "John Blaze", and "Don Cartagena". Guest appearances included <a href="/wiki/Nas_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nas (rapper)">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">Puff Daddy</a>, Big Pun, <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bone_Thugs-N-Harmony" class="mw-redirect" title="Bone Thugs-N-Harmony">Bone Thugs-N-Harmony</a>. On the album, Fat Joe debuted his own group Terror Squad consisting of the late Big Pun, as well as <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Link" title="Cuban Link">Cuban Link</a>, Triple Seis, Prospect, Armageddon, and later <a href="/wiki/Remy_Ma" title="Remy Ma">Remy Ma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-amg_terror_squad_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amg_terror_squad-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Former Terror Squad member Triple Seis also went on record when asked who had written Fat Joe's lyrics, stating that he and Pun were Joe's ghostwriters for Don Cartagena, and asserts that Joe continues to hire ghostwriters.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
Fat Joe executive produced and waa featured on Big Pun's 1998 album <i><a href="/wiki/Capital_Punishment_(Big_Pun_album)" title="Capital Punishment (Big Pun album)">Capital Punishment</a>. In 1999, he appeared on <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Lopez" title="Jennifer Lopez">Jennifer Lopez</a>'s single "<a href="/wiki/Feelin%27_So_Good" title="Feelin&#39; So Good">Feelin' So Good</a>" from her </i><a href="/wiki/On_the_6" title="On the 6">On the 6</a><i> album with late rapper Big Pun.</i>
</p><p>Fat Joe released his fourth album <i><a href="/wiki/Jealous_Ones_Still_Envy_(J.O.S.E.)" title="Jealous Ones Still Envy (J.O.S.E.)">Jealous Ones Still Envy (J.O.S.E.)</a></i> in 2001, with Fat Joe eexecutive producing the album. The album featured a star-studded lineup from the likes of Ashanti, <a href="/wiki/Ja_Rule" title="Ja Rule">Ja Rule</a>, N.O.R.E., <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>, Petey Pablo, M.O.P., <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>, <a href="/wiki/R._Kelly" title="R. Kelly">R. Kelly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buju_Banton" title="Buju Banton">Buju Banton</a>, and artists from his Terror Squad label. The lead single "<a href="/wiki/We_Thuggin%27" title="We Thuggin&#39;">We Thuggin'</a>" featuring R. Kelly was a big hit in late 2001, but would not reach the level of his hit single "<a href="/wiki/What%27s_Luv%3F" title="What&#39;s Luv?">What's Luv?</a>". At the time of the album's release, Fat Joe was managed by The Squad Music. The album was Fat Joe's biggest album commercially, being certified Platinum in America for shipping and selling over a million copies.<sup id="cite_ref-riaa_cert_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-riaa_cert-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> However, Fat Joe's fifth album <i><a href="/wiki/Loyalty_(Fat_Joe_album)" title="Loyalty (Fat Joe album)">Loyalty</a></i>, released later in 2002, was not as successful.
</p><p>In 2003, Fat Joe was featured in the pop single "<a href="/wiki/I_Want_You/Me_Pones_Sexy" class="mw-redirect" title="I Want You/Me Pones Sexy">I Want You</a>" by Mexican singer <a href="/wiki/Thal%C3%ADa" title="Thalía">Thalía</a>. The same year, he and Tony Sunshine performed the single "Crush Tonight" from <i>Loyalty</i> on the Comedy Central program <i><a href="/wiki/Chappelle%27s_Show" title="Chappelle&#39;s Show">Chappelle's Show</a></i>.
</p><p>Despite the setback, Fat Joe scored a number-one hit in 2004 with his group Terror Squad, collaborating with Remy Ma on the Scott Storch production "<a href="/wiki/Lean_Back" title="Lean Back">Lean Back</a>" from the album <i><a href="/wiki/True_Story_(Terror_Squad_album)" title="True Story (Terror Squad album)">True Story</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-amg_terror_squad_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amg_terror_squad-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Jason Birchmeier of <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> called the song "a perfect club-ready duet between Joe and Remy Ma that boasts a trademark <a href="/wiki/Scott_Storch" title="Scott Storch">Scott Storch</a> beat and a memorable singalong hook and dance-along step".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> He then began recording material for <a href="/wiki/Ivy_Queen" title="Ivy Queen">Ivy Queen</a>'s debut English-language album <i><a href="/wiki/Real_(Ivy_Queen_album)" title="Real (Ivy Queen album)">Real</a></i> in support of her goal to compete in the world of English-language hip hop music.<sup id="cite_ref-Caracol_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caracol-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Fat Joe portrayed himself and provided his own voice and likeness for the September 2004 video game, <i><a href="/wiki/Def_Jam:_Fight_For_NY" class="mw-redirect" title="Def Jam: Fight For NY">Def Jam: Fight For NY</a></i>. The video game also featured Fat Joe's 2002 song <i>Take A Look At My Life</i> from <i>Loyalty</i>.
</p><p>A year later, in 2005, Fat Joe released his sixth album <i><a href="/wiki/All_or_Nothing_(Fat_Joe_album)" title="All or Nothing (Fat Joe album)">All or Nothing</a></i>, noted for featuring the popular diss track "My Fofo", aimed at fellow New York rapper <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>, who had dissed Joe for recording with Ja Rule.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <i>All or Nothing</i> spawned the singles "So Much More" and "<a href="/wiki/Get_It_Poppin%27_(Fat_Joe_song)" title="Get It Poppin&#39; (Fat Joe song)">Get It Poppin</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/Nelly" title="Nelly">Nelly</a>, also with guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>, Mase, Remy Ma, Mashonda, and R. Kelly. Responding to "My Fofo", 50 Cent attacked Fat Joe in his song "<a href="/wiki/Piggy_Bank_(song)" title="Piggy Bank (song)">Piggy Bank</a>" from his best-selling 2005 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Massacre" title="The Massacre">The Massacre</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Fat Joe subsequently attacked 50's street credibility and called him a "coward" on a phone interview with Kay Slay of New York City hip-hop radio station WQHT.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The conflict carried on at the <a href="/wiki/2005_MTV_Video_Music_Awards" title="2005 MTV Video Music Awards">2005 MTV Video Music Awards</a>, while Fat Joe introduced the reggaeton act featuring Daddy Yankee, Joe remarked, "I feel safe with all the police protection—courtesy of <a href="/wiki/G-Unit" title="G-Unit">G-Unit</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Shortly after, when MTV switched to a commercial break, 50 Cent directed an obscenity at Joe, and 50 Cent jumped on stage as Fat Joe was leaving.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His recordings catalog for Atlantic Records is no longer under ownership of Warner Music Group, being divested in 2017 to RT Industries, a company founded by <a href="/wiki/Razor_%26_Tie" title="Razor &amp; Tie">Razor &amp; Tie</a> founders.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> However, Warner Music retains the digital distribution rights, now under its <a href="/wiki/Alternative_Distribution_Alliance" title="Alternative Distribution Alliance">ADA</a> division. This divestment was required as effect of a deal with <a href="/wiki/Independent_Music_Companies_Association" title="Independent Music Companies Association">IMPALA</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Merlin_Network" title="Merlin Network">Merlin Network</a>, related to Warner Music's acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Parlophone_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Parlophone Records">Parlophone Records</a> from <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="2006.E2.80.932008:_Me.2C_Myself_.26_I.2C_The_Elephant_in_the_Room.2C_the_50_Cent_feud_continued.2C_and_more"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2006–2008:_Me,_Myself_&amp;_I,_The_Elephant_in_the_Room,_the_50_Cent_feud_continued,_and_more">2006–2008: <i>Me, Myself &amp; I</i>, <i>The Elephant in the Room</i>, the 50 Cent feud continued, and more</span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Fat_Joe_in_Portugal.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Fat_Joe_in_Portugal.jpg/220px-Fat_Joe_in_Portugal.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Fat_Joe_in_Portugal.jpg/330px-Fat_Joe_in_Portugal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Fat_Joe_in_Portugal.jpg/440px-Fat_Joe_in_Portugal.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a><figcaption>Fat Joe performing in Portugal in 2006</figcaption></figure>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Me,_Myself_%26_I_(album)" title="Me, Myself &amp; I (album)">Me, Myself &amp; I</a></i>, released in 2006, is Fat Joe's seventh album. It was his first album released on his new deal with <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Records" title="Virgin Records">Virgin Records</a>. It featured the hit single "<a href="/wiki/Make_It_Rain_(Fat_Joe_song)" title="Make It Rain (Fat Joe song)">Make It Rain</a>" with southern rapper <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a>, followed by "No Drama (Clap and Revolve)". Fat Joe did a freestyle cipher segment for <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a>'s "Freestyle 59" competition in October 2006 prior to the VH1 Hip Hop Honors featuring New Jersey emcee Neuse.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2007, Catholic priest <a href="/wiki/Michael_Pfleger" title="Michael Pfleger">Michael Pfleger</a> targeted Fat Joe as among several rappers he believed promoted misogyny in his billboard campaign "Stop Listening to Trash", which was launched June 18, 2007, throughout <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, where Pfleger preaches.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Also that month, Fat Joe was featured in the DJ Khaled singles "<a href="/wiki/We_Takin%27_Over" title="We Takin&#39; Over">We Takin' Over</a>" alongside <a href="/wiki/Akon" title="Akon">Akon</a>, <a href="/wiki/T.I." title="T.I.">T.I.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Ross_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rick Ross (rapper)">Rick Ross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birdman_(rapper)" title="Birdman (rapper)">Birdman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a> and the remix to Khaled's "<a href="/wiki/I%27m_So_Hood" title="I&#39;m So Hood">I'm So Hood</a>" with Lil Wayne, <a href="/wiki/Young_Jeezy" class="mw-redirect" title="Young Jeezy">Young Jeezy</a>, Rick Ross, <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_Boi" title="Big Boi">Big Boi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Birdman_(rapper)" title="Birdman (rapper)">Birdman</a>. Verbal disputes between Fat Joe and 50 Cent continued during this time period: in September 2007, on the <a href="/wiki/Black_Entertainment_Television" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Entertainment Television">BET</a> program <i>Rap City</i>, 50 Cent accused Fat Joe of being cowardly for not willing to confront him, but Fat Joe dismissed this claim as nonsense.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Later in January, 50 Cent released another Fat Joe diss, called "Southside Nigga (I'm Leaving)". At the end of January 2008, Fat Joe and his longtime accountant Brian Dittrich both denied rumors spreading on the Internet that Fat Joe owed the <a href="/wiki/IRS" class="mw-redirect" title="IRS">IRS</a> taxes.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Fat Joe's eighth solo studio album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Elephant_in_the_Room_(album)" title="The Elephant in the Room (album)">The Elephant in the Room</a></i> was distributed by <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Records" title="Imperial Records">Imperial Records</a>, a division of <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a> and Terror Squad Entertainment,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and released on March 11, 2008; its lead single was "<a href="/wiki/I_Won%27t_Tell" title="I Won&#39;t Tell">I Won't Tell</a>" featuring singer <a href="/wiki/J_(singer)" class="mw-redirect" title="J (singer)">J</a>. Holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The album debuted at the sixth position on the Billboard Hot 100.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> "Ain't Sayin' Nuthin'" followed and featured <a href="/wiki/Plies_(rapper)" title="Plies (rapper)">Plies</a>.
</p><p>On March 20, 2008, shortly after record sales were released for Fat Joe's new album <i>The Elephant in the Room</i>, 50 Cent released a video via his <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> account, which features the "funeral" of Fat Joe, which shows 50 Cent crying in the fake footage. 50 Cent then talks about Fat Joe's record sales and states that he ended Fat Joe's career (like he says he did to Ja Rule's) and that his mixtape blew out Fat Joe's album.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009:_Jealous_Ones_Still_Envy_2">2009: <i>Jealous Ones Still Envy 2</i></span></h3>
<p>Fat Joe's ninth solo studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Jealous_Ones_Still_Envy_2_(J.O.S.E._2)" title="Jealous Ones Still Envy 2 (J.O.S.E. 2)">J.O.S.E. 2</a></i>, was released towards the end of June 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The project reprises the title of Joe's 2002 RIAA-Certified Platinum release, <i>Jealous Ones Still Envy (J.O.S.E.)</i>, and marked Joe's third release since bringing his <a href="/wiki/Terror_Squad_(record_label)" class="mw-redirect" title="Terror Squad (record label)">Terror Squad</a> imprint to the <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> family in 2006. For this album, Joe has reached out to many artists, landing assists from <a href="/wiki/Ron_Browz" title="Ron Browz">Ron Browz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fabolous" title="Fabolous">Fabolous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil%27_Kim" title="Lil&#39; Kim">Lil' Kim</a>, <a href="/wiki/T-Pain" title="T-Pain">T-Pain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Akon" title="Akon">Akon</a>. Producers include <a href="/wiki/Jim_Jonsin" title="Jim Jonsin">Jim Jonsin</a>, The Inkredibles, and frequent collaborator StreetRunner.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/One_(Fat_Joe_single)" class="mw-redirect" title="One (Fat Joe single)">One</a>", featuring <a href="/wiki/Akon" title="Akon">Akon</a>, was the first single. The album was released on October 6, 2009, and sold 11,000 copies in its first week. It debuted on The Billboard 200 at #73.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010:_signing_to_E1_Music_and_The_Darkside">2010: signing to E1 Music and <i>The Darkside</i></span></h3>
<p>In January 2010, Fat Joe announced that he was working on a new album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Darkside_Vol._1" title="The Darkside Vol. 1">The Darkside Vol. 1</a></i>. MTV News reported that Fat Joe intended "all the material...to be much harsher" than his previous album. Production comes from <a href="/wiki/The_Alchemist_(musician)" title="The Alchemist (musician)">The Alchemist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cool_%26_Dre" title="Cool &amp; Dre">Cool &amp; Dre</a>, Streetrunner, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>, Scoop DeVille, <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scram_Jones" title="Scram Jones">Scram Jones</a>, Raw Uncut and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Infamous" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ Infamous">DJ Infamous</a> with guest appearances by <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trey_Songz" title="Trey Songz">Trey Songz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a>, <a href="/wiki/R._Kelly" title="R. Kelly">R. Kelly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clipse" title="Clipse">Clipse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cam%27ron" title="Cam&#39;ron">Cam'ron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rico_Love" title="Rico Love">Rico Love</a>, <a href="/wiki/Too_$hort" class="mw-redirect" title="Too $hort">Too $hort</a>, TA, and <a href="/wiki/Young_Jeezy" class="mw-redirect" title="Young Jeezy">Young Jeezy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The first single from <i>The Darkside Vol. 1</i> is "<a href="/wiki/(Ha_Ha)_Slow_Down" title="(Ha Ha) Slow Down">(Ha Ha) Slow Down</a>", which features Young Jeezy. The second single off the album is "<a href="/wiki/If_It_Ain%27t_About_Money" title="If It Ain&#39;t About Money">If It Ain't About Money</a>" and features <a href="/wiki/Trey_Songz" title="Trey Songz">Trey Songz</a>.
</p><p>On March 28, 2010, Fat Joe signed a record deal with <a href="/wiki/E1_Music" class="mw-redirect" title="E1 Music">E1 Music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Harper_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harper-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
<i><a href="/wiki/The_Darkside_Vol._1" title="The Darkside Vol. 1">The Darkside Vol. 1</a></i> was released on July 27, 2010, and sold approximately 12,000 copies in the first week and entered the <i>Billboard</i> 200 at #27.
</p>
<h3><span id="2011.E2.80.93present:_Feature_on_Champagne_And_Cocaine.2C_Darkside_Vol._2..2C_Plata_O_Plomo_with_Remy_Ma.2C_and_Family_Ties_with_Dre"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2011–present:_Feature_on_Champagne_And_Cocaine,_Darkside_Vol._2.,_Plata_O_Plomo_with_Remy_Ma,_and_Family_Ties_with_Dre">2011–present: <i>Feature on Champagne And Cocaine, Darkside Vol. 2</i>., <i>Plata O Plomo</i> with Remy Ma, and <i>Family Ties</i> with Dre</span></h3>
<p>On April 7, 2011, Jamie Drastik released his second mixtape Champagne And Cocaine which contains the song "One Hundred And Ten" featuring Fat Joe.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Joe was featured on a remix to <a href="/wiki/DJ_Khaled" title="DJ Khaled">DJ Khaled</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Welcome_to_My_Hood" title="Welcome to My Hood">Welcome to My Hood</a>", which also features <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>, <a href="/wiki/T-Pain" title="T-Pain">T-Pain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twista" title="Twista">Twista</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mavado_(singer)" title="Mavado (singer)">Mavado</a>, Birdman, <a href="/wiki/Ace_Hood" title="Ace Hood">Ace Hood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Game_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Game (rapper)">Game</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bun_B" title="Bun B">Bun B</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Waka_Flocka_Flame" title="Waka Flocka Flame">Waka Flocka Flame</a>. It is included as the final track on Khaled's fifth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/We_the_Best_Forever" title="We the Best Forever">We the Best Forever</a></i>.
</p><p>In an interview with XXL Magazine on September 21, 2011, Fat Joe stated <i><a href="/wiki/The_Darkside_Vol._2" title="The Darkside Vol. 2">The Darkside Vol. 2</a></i> is going to be his first ever official mixtape and will feature the Mark Henry-produced songs "Massacre on Madison" and "Drop a Body", both of which were released earlier in the year. Joe went on to say he is also working on an album which is yet to be named but the first single is called "<a href="/wiki/Another_Round_(Fat_Joe_song)" title="Another Round (Fat Joe song)">Another Round</a>" produced by <a href="/wiki/Cool_and_Dre" class="mw-redirect" title="Cool and Dre">Cool and Dre</a> and Young Lad and features <a href="/wiki/Chris_Brown_(American_entertainer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chris Brown (American entertainer)">Chris Brown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-xxlmag1_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmag1-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 19, 2011, "<a href="/wiki/Another_Round_(Fat_Joe_song)" title="Another Round (Fat Joe song)">Another Round</a>" the first single off Joe's yet to be named eleventh studio album was released on iTunes.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The second single released from the album is "Yellow Tape" which features Lil Wayne, <a href="/wiki/ASAP_Rocky" title="ASAP Rocky">ASAP Rocky</a>, and French Montana. In September 2012, Joe featured in Grammy awards winner <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Sanz" title="Alejandro Sanz">Alejandro Sanz</a>'s new album, <a href="/wiki/La_M%C3%BAsica_No_Se_Toca" title="La Música No Se Toca">La Música No Se Toca</a> in a music named <i>Down</i>. Joe would then release another single, "Ballin'" on March 18, 2013. The song features <a href="/wiki/Wiz_Khalifa" title="Wiz Khalifa">Wiz Khalifa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teyana_Taylor" title="Teyana Taylor">Teyana Taylor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Fat Joe joined D.I.T.C. for their album <i>Sessions</i>. The album was released in October 2016 and was preceded by the lead single "Rock Shyt".<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Via Hiphop Wired, Joe revealed that he and <a href="/wiki/Remy_Ma" title="Remy Ma">Remy Ma</a> are releasing a joint album. The first single, "<a href="/wiki/All_the_Way_Up_(Fat_Joe_and_Remy_Ma_song)" title="All the Way Up (Fat Joe and Remy Ma song)">All the Way Up</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/French_Montana" title="French Montana">French Montana</a> and Infared, peaked at #27 on the Hot 100, becoming his first top 40 hit in nearly a decade. The album, <i><a href="/wiki/Plata_O_Plomo_(Fat_Joe_and_Remy_Ma_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Plata O Plomo (Fat Joe and Remy Ma album)">Plata O Plomo</a></i>, and was released in February 2017.
</p><p>On August 2, 2017, Fat Joe released "So Excited" with <a href="/wiki/Cool_%26_Dre" title="Cool &amp; Dre">Dre</a>.
On July 24, 2018, he released "Attention" with <a href="/wiki/Chris_Brown" title="Chris Brown">Chris Brown</a>.
Fat Joe's second collaborative album called <i>Family Ties</i>, with Dre, was released in December 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2020, Fat Joe appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Complex_Networks" title="Complex Networks">ComplexLand</a> virtual event and talked about the best sneakers of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span></h3>
<p>Fat Joe is married with three children, and has lived in <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Tenafly,_New_Jersey" title="Tenafly, New Jersey">Tenafly, New Jersey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_work">Community work</span></h3>
<p>Fat Joe has been back to his old school in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">the Bronx</a>, New York to donate computers for students.
</p><p>In 2008, he attended the grand opening of the Hip Hop Soda Shop in Miami which was a community outreach project set up by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Chavis" title="Benjamin Chavis">Ben Chavis</a> for the youth to hang out and do things such as record music, use the computers and play on Xbox 360s.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At a "School is Cool" assembly in <a href="/wiki/Jersey_City_Public_Schools" title="Jersey City Public Schools">Public School 5</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jersey_City,_New_Jersey" title="Jersey City, New Jersey">Jersey City, New Jersey</a> on June 11, 2009, Fat Joe was a speaker.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 23, 2011, Fat Joe appeared with <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark</a> mayor <a href="/wiki/Cory_Booker" title="Cory Booker">Cory Booker</a> and fitness expert <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Halevy" title="Jeff Halevy">Jeff Halevy</a> at an event to promote the Newark branch of <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Obama" title="Michelle Obama">Michelle Obama</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Move!" title="Let&#39;s Move!">Let's Move!</a></i> initiative against childhood obesity.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weight_loss">Weight loss</span></h3>
<p>By 1996, he weighed 300 pounds (140&#160;kg).<sup id="cite_ref-Faithful_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Faithful-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, <i>Stuff</i> magazine<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> and <i>ContactMusic.com</i><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> profiled Fat Joe's weight loss efforts.
</p><p>In 2011, Fat Joe unveiled his latest weight loss efforts in the video for his song "Drop a Body"<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> after losing 88 pounds (40&#160;kg) off his previous weight of 350 pounds (160&#160;kg).<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, he follows a <a href="/wiki/Low-carbohydrate_diet" title="Low-carbohydrate diet">low-carb approach</a>, eating <a href="/wiki/Sweet_potatoes" class="mw-redirect" title="Sweet potatoes">sweet potatoes</a> but not eating certain <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a> such as white bread, white rice, and white pasta.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2022, Fat Joe said that he would not change his stage name from Fat Joe despite his weight loss, because it would not be a good marketing move.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span></h3>
<p>On September 8, 1998, Fat Joe and Big Pun were arrested on assault charges for hitting a man with a baseball bat and stealing the man's gold chain on June 14 that year.<sup id="cite_ref-Different_Music_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Different_Music-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Joe was arrested again on May 12, 2002, for allegedly fighting with another man at B.B. King's Blues Club in <a href="/wiki/Times_Square" title="Times Square">Times Square</a>, but the charges were dropped on January 10, 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In two murder cases, Fat Joe has been named a witness:
</p><p>Joe's former bodyguard, Jose Mulero (also known as Sing Sing), was arrested on September 17, 2004, for the April 15, 1994, shooting death of 16-year-old Ernesto Rivera at a Bronx nightclub. Responding to a <a href="/wiki/Subpoena" title="Subpoena">subpoena</a>, Fat Joe claimed to have heard the shooting and seen people fleeing the scene, but investigators argued that he was standing closer to Mulero, by a door.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Miami_Beach_Police_Department" title="Miami Beach Police Department">Miami Beach police</a> also named Fat Joe as a witness in a 2007 Memorial Day double murder outside David's Cafe II in <a href="/wiki/South_Beach" title="South Beach">South Beach</a>. Jermaine <i>Wufgang</i> Chamberline of <a href="/wiki/Miami_Gardens" class="mw-redirect" title="Miami Gardens">Miami Gardens</a> was accused of shooting Lessli Paz and Joey Navarro to death on that morning; Fat Joe and the two victims were sitting in a rented <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_Escalade" title="Cadillac Escalade">Cadillac Escalade</a> parked outside the restaurant when a fight broke out between passengers and another man.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2012, Fat Joe pleaded guilty to <a href="/wiki/Tax_evasion" title="Tax evasion">tax evasion</a> for not paying income tax on over $3<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>million from 2007 to 2010. On June 24, 2013, he was sentenced to four months in prison for tax evasion.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> He began the sentence on August 26, 2013, and was released on November 28, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LGBT_beliefs_and_support">LGBT beliefs and support</span></h3>
<p>During an interview with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Vlad" title="DJ Vlad">Vlad TV</a>, Joe stated that he believes that gay people should not hide their sexuality and should stand by who they are. He mentioned that it's possible that he has done songs with gay rappers and that there are likely several gay people besides rappers in the hip hop industry who are in the closet, describing it as a "Gay Mafia".<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His comments came after he was asked to comment on radio personality <a href="/wiki/Mister_Cee" title="Mister Cee">Mister Cee</a> being arrested for public lewdness with a transgender sex worker.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
