<p><b>Jonathan Burks</b> (born October 4, 1964), better known by his stage name <b>Jaz-O</b> (formerly <b>The Jaz</b> and <b>Big Jaz</b>), is an American <a href="/wiki/Rapper" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapper">rapper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Record_producer" title="Record producer">record producer</a>. Active in the late 1980s through the 1990s, and best known for being the mentor of Brooklyn rapper <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, Burks is nicknamed "the Originator", and had a song titled "<a href="/wiki/The_Originators_(The_Jaz_song)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Originators (The Jaz song)">The Originators</a>" that featured a young Jay-Z in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jonathan Burks was born in the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> borough of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and raised in <a href="/wiki/Marcy_Houses" title="Marcy Houses">Marcy Houses</a>, a housing project in the <a href="/wiki/Bedford-Stuyvesant" class="mw-redirect" title="Bedford-Stuyvesant">Bedford-Stuyvesant</a> neighborhood. Originally attending college to become an <a href="/wiki/Accountant" title="Accountant">accountant</a>, he decided to become a rapper after being encouraged by a friend, and being inspired after hearing <a href="/wiki/UTFO" title="UTFO">UTFO</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Roxanne,_Roxanne" class="mw-redirect" title="Roxanne, Roxanne">Roxanne, Roxanne</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years_and_signing_with_EMI_USA">Early years and signing with EMI USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early years and signing with EMI USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Being from the <a href="/wiki/Marcy_Projects" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcy Projects">same projects</a> as Jay-Z, the two would meet and become friends, with Jaz-O being a rhyme mentor to Jay-Z. Sometime during the year of 1986, the two lyricists would soon form a short-lived group by the name of High Potent. Despite the group being short-lived, the two recorded and released several songs throughout 1986, before Jaz-O would go on and get a record deal with <a href="/wiki/EMI_America_Records" title="EMI America Records">EMI USA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In regards to the signing, he stated:
</p>
<blockquote><p>I had been rhyming since I was 14. Do the math on your own! I became a legend on the east coast - which in the 'eighties' was quite remarkable, in about six years. I was always told I was the best most had ever heard. I put out a song in 1985 called "HP Gets Busy" on our own label (my manager at the time, Jack Walker and I), featuring two of my comrades from <a href="/wiki/Long_Island,_NY" class="mw-redirect" title="Long Island, NY">Long Island, NY</a>, and my young apprentice, Jay Z. We did shows regionally; NY and Pittsburgh mostly. Meanwhile, I was recording with friend and producer, Fresh Gordon. He helped me get a single deal on <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Boy_Records" title="Tommy Boy Records">Tommy Boy Records</a> after I collaborated on a song with him called "My <a href="/wiki/Fila_(company)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fila (company)">Fila</a>" (a response to <a href="/wiki/Run_DMC" class="mw-redirect" title="Run DMC">Run DMC</a>'s, "<a href="/wiki/My_Adidas" title="My Adidas">My Adidas</a>"). A mutual friend of Gordon and I, Marlon Prescott, introduced me to my soon-to-be manager at that time, Stan Poses. In four months Stan solidified a major artist deal for me, and I became the first rap artist ever to sign with EMI Records. I also became the recipient of the largest advance and recording budget of any rap artist at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1989:_Word_to_the_Jaz">1989: <i>Word to the Jaz</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1989: Word to the Jaz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several months after signing with EMI USA, Jaz-O went on a trip to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, where he would record his debut album. Joining him on the trip was Jay-Z, as well as future <a href="/wiki/Murder_Inc_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Murder Inc Records">Murder Inc Records</a> CEO and producer <a href="/wiki/Irv_Gotti" title="Irv Gotti">Irv Gotti</a>, who was Jaz-O's DJ at the time. After completion, Jaz-O released his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Word_to_the_Jaz" title="Word to the Jaz">Word to the Jaz</a>.</i> The album was produced by Bryan "Chuck" New, Pete Q. Harris and Jaz-O himself. The album peaked at number 87 on the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It featured a guest appearance from <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> on the song "Hawaiian Sophie", which peaked at number 18 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Songs</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The single "Let's Play House"/"Buss the Speaker" peaked at number 26 on the <i>Billboard</i> <a href="/wiki/Dance_Club_Songs" title="Dance Club Songs">Dance Club Songs</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jaz-O also made a <a href="/wiki/Guest_appearance" title="Guest appearance">guest appearance</a> on <a href="/wiki/The_O%27Jays" title="The O&#39;Jays">The O'Jays</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Have_You_Had_Your_Love_Today" title="Have You Had Your Love Today">Have You Had Your Love Today</a>", which was a single released from their 1989 album <i>Serious</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990:_To_Your_Soul">1990: <i>To Your Soul</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1990: To Your Soul">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jaz-O released his second album <i><a href="/wiki/To_Your_Soul" title="To Your Soul">To Your Soul</a></i> on July 16, 1990. Despite the album not reaching the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts" title="Billboard charts">Billboard charts</a>, its two singles, "The Originators" and "A Groove (This Is What U Rap 2)", peaked at No. 13 and No. 18, respectively, on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Songs</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Similar to his debut album, Jay-Z was the only guest, being featured on two songs.
</p>
<h3><span id="1991.E2.80.931996:_Production_work_behind_the_scenes"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1991–1996:_Production_work_behind_the_scenes">1991–1996: Production work behind the scenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1991–1996: Production work behind the scenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sometime after the album was released, Jaz-O was dropped from his record label and began to spend time working behind the scenes as a producer. At some point, Jaz-O connected with <a href="/wiki/The_Lox" title="The Lox">The Lox</a>, and produced a demo tape for the group, which would become instrumental to the start of their successful career.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Fellow <a href="/wiki/Yonkers" class="mw-redirect" title="Yonkers">Yonkers</a> native <a href="/wiki/Mary_J._Blige" title="Mary J. Blige">Mary J. Blige</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Larkin90_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Larkin90-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> would receive the tape from a cousin who did music with the trio. Becoming a supporter of the group, Blige would pass the demo tape on to <a href="/wiki/Bad_Boy_Records" title="Bad Boy Records">Bad Boy</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">Sean "Puffy" Combs</a> while on a tour with <a href="/wiki/Jodeci" title="Jodeci">Jodeci</a>. Liking what he heard, Puff shortly signed The Lox to a deal.<sup id="cite_ref-Larkin90_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Larkin90-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1996.E2.80.931998:_Involvement_with_Jay-Z.27s_early_success"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1996–1998:_Involvement_with_Jay-Z's_early_success">1996–1998: Involvement with Jay-Z's early success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1996–1998: Involvement with Jay-Z&#039;s early success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout the late 1990s, Jaz-O had quite a hand in Jay-Z's early solo success. Jaz-O produced, and was featured on "<a href="/wiki/Ain%27t_No_Nigga" title="Ain&#39;t No Nigga">Ain't No Nigga</a>," the second single from Jay-Z's 1996 debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Reasonable_Doubt_(album)" title="Reasonable Doubt (album)">Reasonable Doubt</a></i>. The song is cited as playing a significant role in securing Jay-Z's record with <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Recordings" title="Def Jam Recordings">Def Jam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hitquarters.com_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hitquarters.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jaz-O then produced "Rap Game / Crack Game," a track that appeared on Jay's second album <i><a href="/wiki/In_My_Lifetime,_Vol._1" title="In My Lifetime, Vol. 1">In My Lifetime, Vol. 1</a></i>, released in 1997.
</p><p>Jaz-O also produced two tracks for Jay-Z's <i><a href="/wiki/Streets_Is_Watching_(soundtrack)" title="Streets Is Watching (soundtrack)">Streets Is Watching</a></i>. The album fared well commercially reaching #3 on the Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums chart, and was released on May 12, 1998, by <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Recordings" title="Def Jam Recordings">Def Jam Recordings</a>.
</p><p>In 1999, Jaz-O appeared on Jay-Z's "<a href="/wiki/Nigga_What,_Nigga_Who_(Originator_99)" title="Nigga What, Nigga Who (Originator 99)">Nigga What, Nigga Who (Originator 99)</a>", a single that was released from Jay-Z's third album <i><a href="/wiki/Vol._2..._Hard_Knock_Life" title="Vol. 2... Hard Knock Life">Vol. 2... Hard Knock Life</a>.</i> The song became a big hit in Jaz-O's career at the time. However, this would be one of the last times the two would work together on music, for their friendship soured shortly afterwards.
</p>
<h3><span id="1999.E2.80.932016:_Feud_with_Jay-Z_and_forming_Kingz_Kounty"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1999–2016:_Feud_with_Jay-Z_and_forming_Kingz_Kounty">1999–2016: Feud with Jay-Z and forming Kingz Kounty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1999–2016: Feud with Jay-Z and forming Kingz Kounty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The long-standing feud between him and Jay-Z started when Jay-Z started <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a> and tried to convince Jaz-O and fellow rapper <a href="/wiki/Sauce_Money" title="Sauce Money">Sauce Money</a> to sign with the label. They both refused. It is rumored that they did not trust Roc-A-Fella Records CEO <a href="/wiki/Damon_Dash" title="Damon Dash">Damon Dash</a> and label co-founder Kareem "Biggs" Burke. In addition, they were not satisfied with what they were to receive had they signed the contract ($300,000 in Jaz-O's case). Jay-Z confirmed this event on the song "What We Talkin' About" from the <i><a href="/wiki/Blueprint_3" class="mw-redirect" title="Blueprint 3">Blueprint 3</a></i> album where he raps "Dame made millions, even Jaz made some scraps, he could've made more but he ain't sign his contract".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Instead of signing with Roc-A-Fella Records, Jaz-O inked a deal with Rancore Records, and formed the hip-hop group Immobilarie. With the group, he released a collaboration album titled <i><a href="/wiki/Jaz-O_%26_The_Immobilarie_Family_Present:_Kingz_Kounty" class="mw-redirect" title="Jaz-O &amp; The Immobilarie Family Present: Kingz Kounty">Jaz-O &amp; The Immobilarie Family Present: Kingz Kounty</a>.</i>
</p><p>Jay-Z went on to diss Jaz first on a track titled "Fuck Jaz-O AKA Jaz Ho", released by <a href="/wiki/DJ_Kayslay" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ Kayslay">DJ Kayslay</a> and featuring <a href="/wiki/Freeway_(rapper)" title="Freeway (rapper)">Freeway</a>, Geda K, <a href="/wiki/Young_Gunz" title="Young Gunz">Young Chris</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Bleek" title="Memphis Bleek">Memphis Bleek</a>. On the song, in which they rapped over <a href="/wiki/Styles_P" title="Styles P">Styles P</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Good_Times_(Styles_P_song)" title="Good Times (Styles P song)">Good Times</a>" instrumental, and later on his album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blueprint_2:_The_Gift_%26_The_Curse" title="The Blueprint 2: The Gift &amp; The Curse">The Blueprint 2: The Gift &amp; The Curse</a></i>, Jay-Z states: "I'ma let karma catch up to Jaz-O." Jaz then responded with a record titled  "Ova"  on a DJ Kayslay mixtape, and after the response from Jay-Z and the members of Roc-A-Fella, he released the diss record response known as "Ova Part 2" in which he raps over <a href="/wiki/N.O.R.E." title="N.O.R.E.">N.O.R.E.</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Nothin%27_(song)" title="Nothin&#39; (song)">Nothin'</a>" instrumental.
</p><p>During the long-standing feud between mentor and protégé, Jay-Z would still give Jaz-O credit for his success (while dissing him at the same time) as heard in the song "I Do It For Hip Hop" on <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Theater_of_the_Mind" title="Theater of the Mind">Theater of the Mind</a></i> album, where Jay-Z says "Shout out to <a href="/wiki/Grand_Master_Flash" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Master Flash">Grand Master Flash</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Caz" title="Grandmaster Caz">Caz</a> and even Jaz's bum ass".
</p><p>Soon after the "I Do It For Hip Hop" diss from Jay-Z, Jaz-O responded with his own diss record titled "Go Harder" which starts with Jaz-O rapping over his protege's "Brooklyn Go Hard" beat before the beat changes.
</p><p>In late August 2009, Jaz-O was featured on another song dissing Jay-Z, titled "Gangstas Ride" with West Coast rapper, <a href="/wiki/The_Game_(rapper)" title="The Game (rapper)">The Game</a>. Jaz references "Ether" with the line, "Jaz-O, stupid motherfucker, not Shawn, never been bashed on a <a href="/wiki/Tupac" class="mw-redirect" title="Tupac">Tupac</a> song". The song was fueled by beef between Game and Jay-Z at the time.
</p>
<h3><span id="2017.E2.80.932020:_Reunion_with_Jay-Z_and_signing_with_Roc_Nation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2017–2020:_Reunion_with_Jay-Z_and_signing_with_Roc_Nation">2017–2020: Reunion with Jay-Z and signing with Roc Nation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2017–2020: Reunion with Jay-Z and signing with Roc Nation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2017, Jaz-O and Jay-Z  reunited at Jay-Z's <a href="/wiki/4:44_(album)" class="mw-redirect" title="4:44 (album)"><i>4:44</i></a> concert, seemingly ending their feud.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In 2019, it was reported that the two had worked out a music deal for Jaz-O's Kingz Kounty Media Group to be distributed through <a href="/wiki/Roc_Nation" title="Roc Nation">Roc Nation</a>'s Equity Distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 7, 2020, Jaz-O released an extended play (EP) titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Warmup_EP" class="mw-redirect" title="The Warmup EP">The Warmup</a></i>, on Kingz Kounty Media Group. It was distributed by Roc Nation's Equity Distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2021:_Other_ventures_and_releases">2021: Other ventures and releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2021: Other ventures and releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jaz-O has since moved his 'Kingz Kounty Music Group" to Rival Distribution. He released "Lookin' Like", a collaboration single with <a href="/wiki/Nipsey_Hussle" title="Nipsey Hussle">Nipsey Hussle</a>, on July 2, 2021. The song's music video was directed by Da Inphamus Amadeuz.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_style">Musical style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Musical style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jaz-O cites <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Caz" title="Grandmaster Caz">Grandmaster Caz</a> as an early influence. Caz was the artist who made Jaz-O decide to become a rapper.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhyming_style">Rhyming style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Rhyming style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jaz-O was the rapper to pioneer the triplet flow, which allows the rapper to flow far beyond sixteen syllables within the 4/4 <a href="/wiki/Time_signature" title="Time signature">time signature</a>, being able to add extra syllables to the sixteen multiplies the syllables into twenty-fourths, which makes a triplet of an eighth. Since the 1990s, the triplet <a href="/wiki/Rhyme_technique" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhyme technique">rhyme technique</a> has become a common distinction among some of the most respected hip-hop lyricists. Several rappers who are well known for the technique include his former apprentice <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twista" title="Twista">Twista</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bone_Thugs-N-Harmony" class="mw-redirect" title="Bone Thugs-N-Harmony">Bone Thugs-N-Harmony</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Fu-Schnickens" title="Fu-Schnickens">Fu-Schnickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spice-1" class="mw-redirect" title="Spice-1">Spice-1</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tech_N9ne" title="Tech N9ne">Tech N9ne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaz-O&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jaz-O was known to be a visible figure in the <a href="/wiki/Nuwaubian_Nation" title="Nuwaubian Nation">Nuwaubian Nation</a> during the 1980s. Nuwaubian beliefs and symbolism were prominent in the earlier years of his music career, and Nuwaubian fashion influenced his wardrobe.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
