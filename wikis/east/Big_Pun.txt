<p><b>Christopher Lee Rios</b> (November 10, 1971 – February 7, 2000),<sup id="cite_ref-The_Source:_The_Magazine_of_Hip-hop_Music,_Culture_&amp;_Politics_2000_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Source:_The_Magazine_of_Hip-hop_Music,_Culture_&amp;_Politics_2000-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Biography.com_2017_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biography.com_2017-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> better known by his stage name <b>Big Pun</b> (short for <b>Big Punisher</b>), was an American rapper.  Emerging from the <a href="/wiki/Underground_hip_hop" title="Underground hip hop">underground hip hop</a> scene in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">the Bronx</a> borough of New York City in the early 1990s, he came to prominence upon being discovered by fellow Bronx rapper <a href="/wiki/Fat_Joe" title="Fat Joe">Fat Joe</a>, and made his earliest appearance on his 1995 album <i><a href="/wiki/Jealous_One%27s_Envy" title="Jealous One&#39;s Envy">Jealous One's Envy</a></i>.
</p><p>In 1997, Big Pun signed with Fat Joe's label, <a href="/wiki/Terror_Squad_(record_label)" class="mw-redirect" title="Terror Squad (record label)">Terror Squad Productions</a> in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Loud_Records" title="Loud Records">Loud Records</a> as a solo artist. The same year, he released his <a href="/wiki/41st_Annual_Grammy_Awards" title="41st Annual Grammy Awards">Grammy-nominated</a> debut studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Capital_Punishment_(Big_Pun_album)" title="Capital Punishment (Big Pun album)">Capital Punishment</a></i> in April 1998 to critical acclaim and commercial success, peaking at #5 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> and becoming the first solo hip hop record by a Latino artist to go <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">Platinum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Pun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rios was born in <a href="/wiki/The_South_Bronx" class="mw-redirect" title="The South Bronx">The South Bronx</a> in New York City to parents of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans" title="Puerto Ricans">Puerto Rican</a> descent. He grew up in the <a href="/wiki/Soundview,_Bronx" title="Soundview, Bronx">Soundview</a> neighborhood and had at least two sisters and one brother.<sup id="cite_ref-massappeal.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-massappeal.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vibe_1998_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vibe_1998-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_bio_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_bio-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He regularly played <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> and trained in <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">boxing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-massappeal.com_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-massappeal.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He moved out of his mother's house at age 15 and was homeless for a period of time in the late 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-uproxx.com_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uproxx.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Later, he received a large settlement from the city stemming from an incident in 1976, where Rios broke his leg while playing in a park.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Using his settlement money, Rios married his high school sweetheart, Liza, and the two moved into a home together.
</p><p>Rios struggled with <a href="/wiki/Clinical_depression" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinical depression">depression</a> stemming from his turbulent childhood, and he coped with it by <a href="/wiki/Emotional_eating" title="Emotional eating">overeating</a>. Between the ages of 18 and 21, Rios' weight rocketed from 180&#160;lb (82&#160;kg) to 300&#160;lb (140&#160;kg); he was consequently unable to tie his own shoes.<sup id="cite_ref-uproxx.com_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uproxx.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-massappeal.com_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-massappeal.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Pun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the late 1980s, he began writing rap lyrics. He later formed the underground group Full-A-Clips with <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Link" title="Cuban Link">Lyrical Assassin</a>, Joker Jamz and Toom. Rios made a number of recordings with the group in the 1990s, which have not been released. At this point Rios was operating under the alias Big Moon Dawg.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> After changing his stage name to Big Punisher, Rios met fellow Puerto Rican and Bronx rapper <a href="/wiki/Fat_Joe" title="Fat Joe">Fat Joe</a> in 1995 and made his commercial debut on Fat Joe's second album, <i>Jealous One's Envy</i>, in addition to appearing on the song, "Watch Out". He also appeared on <a href="/wiki/The_Beatnuts" title="The Beatnuts">The Beatnuts</a>' song "Off the Books".
</p>
<h3><span id="Capital_Punishment_.281997.E2.80.931998.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Capital_Punishment_(1997–1998)"><i>Capital Punishment</i> (1997–1998)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Pun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Capital Punishment (1997–1998)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1997, Big Pun began recording songs for his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Capital_Punishment_(Big_Pun_album)" title="Capital Punishment (Big Pun album)">Capital Punishment</a></i>. In 1997, producer <a href="/wiki/Knobody" title="Knobody">Knobody</a>'s production partner Sean C took advantage of his new role as A&amp;R at <a href="/wiki/Loud_Records" title="Loud Records">Loud Records</a> to play Knobody's tracks to Big Pun.<sup id="cite_ref-hitquarters.com_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hitquarters.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Suitably impressed, the rapper hired Knobody to remix "<a href="/wiki/I%27m_Not_a_Player" title="I&#39;m Not a Player">I'm Not a Player</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-hitquarters.com_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hitquarters.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The remixed song, featuring <a href="/wiki/Joe_(singer)" title="Joe (singer)">Joe</a> and titled "<a href="/wiki/Still_Not_a_Player" title="Still Not a Player">Still Not a Player</a>", became Big Pun's first major mainstream hit and major breakthrough for Knobody.<sup id="cite_ref-hitquarters.com_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hitquarters.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The full-length debut <i>Capital Punishment</i> followed in 1998, and became the first album by a solo Latino rapper to go <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">platinum</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> peaking at No. 5 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>. <i>Capital Punishment</i> was also nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Album">Grammy Award for Best Rap Album</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="The_Terror_Squad_collaboration_album_.281999.E2.80.932000.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Terror_Squad_collaboration_album_(1999–2000)">The Terror Squad collaboration album (1999–2000)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Pun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Terror Squad collaboration album (1999–2000)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Big Pun became a member of <a href="/wiki/Terror_Squad_(group)" title="Terror Squad (group)">Terror Squad</a>, a New York-based group of rappers founded by <a href="/wiki/Fat_Joe" title="Fat Joe">Fat Joe</a>, with most of the roster supplied by the now-defunct Full-A-Clips who released their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Album_(Terror_Squad_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Album (Terror Squad album)">The Album</a></i> in 1999. The album did not fare well commercially but it was well received critically and the album was meant to start the foundation for all other Terror Squad members to release their solo projects.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_problems_and_death">Health problems and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Pun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Health problems and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rios struggled with weight issues his entire adult life. He weighed 180 pounds (82&#160;kg) at age 18, which increased to 300 pounds (140&#160;kg) at 21.<sup id="cite_ref-700_pounds_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-700_pounds-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> His weight fluctuated in the early 1990s between <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obese</a> and morbidly obese.<sup id="cite_ref-700_pounds_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-700_pounds-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Rios enrolled in a weight-loss program at <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> in 1999, and shed 80 pounds (36&#160;kg), but he prematurely quit the program and eventually regained the weight.<sup id="cite_ref-700_pounds_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-700_pounds-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> His weight was a constant topic of argument among him and his friends, to the point that Rios would not eat around them.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_bio_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_bio-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-700_pounds_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-700_pounds-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 5, 2000, Rios withdrew from a planned <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> performance with <a href="/wiki/Fat_Joe" title="Fat Joe">Fat Joe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Lopez" title="Jennifer Lopez">Jennifer Lopez</a> due to illness. Two days later while staying at a hotel with his family in <a href="/wiki/White_Plains,_New_York" title="White Plains, New York">White Plains, New York</a>, he suffered a heart attack and respiratory failure and was taken to a hospital, where he died at the age of 28 after paramedics were unable to revive him. His weight had reached a peak of 698 pounds (317&#160;kg) at the time of his death.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Obituary_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Obituary-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Rios was survived by his wife, Liza, and their three children,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Star, Vanessa and <a href="/wiki/Chris_Rivers" title="Chris Rivers">Christopher Jr.</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_works_and_legacy">Posthumous works and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Pun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Posthumous works and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Big Pun's second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Yeeeah_Baby" title="Yeeeah Baby">Yeeeah Baby</a></i>, completed after his death, was released in April 2000. It peaked at No. 3 on the Billboard charts and earned <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">gold record</a> status within three months of its release. A <a href="/wiki/List_of_works_published_posthumously" title="List of works published posthumously">posthumous</a> compilation album, <i><a href="/wiki/Endangered_Species_(Big_Pun_album)" title="Endangered Species (Big Pun album)">Endangered Species</a></i>, was released in April 2001. <i>Endangered Species</i> collected some of Pun's <a href="/wiki/Greatest_hits_album" title="Greatest hits album">greatest hits</a>, previously unreleased material, numerous guest appearances, and remixed "greatest verses." As with his other albums, it also peaked in the top ten of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, reaching No. 7, but didn't sell as much as the previous Big Pun albums had. He collaborated with Fat Joe on <i>Duets: The Final Chapter</i>, an album of tracks featuring <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>, also deceased. The track "Get Your Grind On" begins with a Big Pun radio interview in which he said he would perform a duet with Biggie at the gates of <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">heaven</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Pun was also featured on a track from the revived <a href="/wiki/Terror_Squad_(group)" title="Terror Squad (group)">Terror Squad's</a> second album, <i><a href="/wiki/True_Story_(Terror_Squad_album)" title="True Story (Terror Squad album)">True Story</a>,</i> on the track "Bring 'Em Back" with <a href="/wiki/Big_L" title="Big L">Big L</a>, another deceased rapper.
</p><p>On May 2, 2001, the New York City Council stalled plans to rename a small portion of Rogers Place as a tribute, due to distaste over Big Pun's lyrics that "include[d] profanity and references to violence and drug dealing".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A second posthumous album was planned for release by Sony Music Entertainment in 2006 but was shelved due to a dispute with producer <a href="/wiki/John_Benitez" title="John Benitez">John "Jellybean" Benitez</a>, who owned the publishing rights to many of the intended album's tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In June 2005, Liza Rios put her husband's $100,000 custom Terror Squad medallion up for auction on <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>, citing financial difficulties due to receiving no royalties from Pun's album sales.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 22, 2021, the intersection of East Fordham Rd and Grand Concourse in his native Bronx was named "Big Pun Plaza" in Pun's honor. A ceremony including family, friends, and local politicians preceded the street naming.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
