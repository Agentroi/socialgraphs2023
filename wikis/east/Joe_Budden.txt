<p><b>Joseph Anthony Budden II</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (born August 31, 1980)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is an American media personality and retired rapper. He first gained recognition as a rapper with his 2003 top 40 single "<a href="/wiki/Pump_It_Up_(Joe_Budden_song)" title="Pump It Up (Joe Budden song)">Pump It Up</a>" and as a member of the hip hop <a href="/wiki/Supergroup_(music)" title="Supergroup (music)">supergroup</a> <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse_(group)" title="Slaughterhouse (group)">Slaughterhouse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-discogs.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discogs.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-garrett_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-garrett-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 2018, he retired from rap, and found success as a broadcaster, having a much publicized run as a co-host on <i>Everyday Struggle</i> for <a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)#Complex_TV" class="mw-redirect" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a>. He currently hosts <i><a href="/wiki/The_Joe_Budden_Podcast" title="The Joe Budden Podcast">The Joe Budden Podcast</a></i>, released twice a week on <a href="/wiki/Patreon" title="Patreon">Patreon</a> and <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/State_of_the_Culture" title="State of the Culture">State of the Culture</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Revolt_(TV_network)" title="Revolt (TV network)">Revolt</a>. He has been described as "the <a href="/wiki/Howard_Stern" title="Howard Stern">Howard Stern</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip-hop</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joe_Budden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Budden was born to Joseph Budden and Fay Southerland on August 31, 1980, in the <a href="/wiki/East_Harlem" title="East Harlem">East Harlem</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Manhattan,_New_York_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper Manhattan, New York City">Upper Manhattan, New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He moved to <a href="/wiki/Jersey_City,_New_Jersey" title="Jersey City, New Jersey">Jersey City, New Jersey</a>, at the age of thirteen with his mother and older brother, where he attended <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_High_School_(New_Jersey)" title="Lincoln High School (New Jersey)">Lincoln High School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Budden's father was absent from his life during his childhood, a subject he would later address in his music.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bottomley_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bottomley-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Budden was a troubled youth<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and sent to <a href="/wiki/Laurinburg_Institute" title="Laurinburg Institute">Laurinburg Institute</a>, a boarding school in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, where he began to hone his skills as a rapper. After returning to Jersey, he began using drugs, developing an addiction to <a href="/wiki/Phencyclidine" title="Phencyclidine">angel dust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-complex.com_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> After an emotional confrontation with his mother, Budden voluntarily went into rehab on July 3, 1997, in exchange for him being allowed to attend his senior prom.<sup id="cite_ref-Bottomley_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bottomley-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-complex.com_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Budden did not earn his diploma, and fathered a child with an older woman by the age of 20.<sup id="cite_ref-complex.com_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> With his son on the way, Budden began taking music more seriously. In 2001, he teamed up with producer Dub-B, also known as White Boy, and began releasing his first mixtapes and demos, one of which ended up in the hands of <a href="/wiki/Hot_97" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot 97">Hot 97</a> radio host and <a href="/wiki/Desert_Storm_Records" title="Desert Storm Records">Desert Storm Records</a> label head <a href="/wiki/DJ_Clue" title="DJ Clue">DJ Clue</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joe_Budden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Breakthrough.2C_self-titled_debut_.282002.E2.80.932003.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakthrough,_self-titled_debut_(2002–2003)">Breakthrough, self-titled debut (2002–2003)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joe_Budden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breakthrough, self-titled debut (2002–2003)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Budden quickly became a mixtape fixture,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and secured a major deal with <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Recordings" title="Def Jam Recordings">Def Jam Recordings</a> in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-discogs.com_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-discogs.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He first gained attention through the promotional single "Focus", which spent seventeen weeks on the US <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> chart, peaking at No. 43.<sup id="cite_ref-HotR&amp;B_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HotR&amp;B-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 8, 2003, Budden released "<a href="/wiki/Pump_It_Up_(Joe_Budden_song)" title="Pump It Up (Joe Budden song)">Pump It Up</a>" as the <a href="/wiki/Lead_single" title="Lead single">lead single</a> for his upcoming album. The song, produced by <a href="/wiki/Just_Blaze" title="Just Blaze">Just Blaze</a>, was a commercial success, charting at No. 16 on the US <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a>, as well as its <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Airplay" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Airplay">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Airplay</a> chart at No. 18.<sup id="cite_ref-HotR&amp;B_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HotR&amp;B-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Airplay_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Airplay-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The song peaked at No. 38 on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> chart,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> No. 10 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Songs</a> chart,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and No. 39 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_100_Airplay_(Radio_Songs)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot 100 Airplay (Radio Songs)">Radio Songs</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The song was also featured on soundtracks for hit movies like <i><a href="/wiki/2_Fast_2_Furious" title="2 Fast 2 Furious">2 Fast 2 Furious</a></i> (2003) and <i><a href="/wiki/You_Got_Served" title="You Got Served">You Got Served</a></i> (2004), as well as the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Madden_NFL_2004" title="Madden NFL 2004">Madden NFL 2004</a></i>. <i><a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Vendetta" title="Def Jam Vendetta">Def Jam Vendetta</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Fight_for_NY" class="mw-redirect" title="Def Jam Fight for NY">Def Jam Fight for NY</a></i>, &amp; <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Fight_for_NY:_The_Takeover" title="Def Jam Fight for NY: The Takeover">Def Jam Fight for NY: The Takeover</a> where Budden appeared as a playable character.<sup id="cite_ref-Bush_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The song received a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> nomination for the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Male_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Male Rap Solo Performance">Best Male Rap Solo Performance</a> at the <a href="/wiki/46th_Annual_Grammy_Awards" title="46th Annual Grammy Awards">46th Annual Grammy Awards</a>.
</p><p>On June 10, 2003, Budden released his eponymous debut studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Joe_Budden_(album)" title="Joe Budden (album)">Joe Budden</a></i>. It debuted at No. 8 on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a>, selling 95,000 units in its first week,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and going on to sell over 420,000 copies in the United States. Its second single, "<a href="/wiki/Fire_(Yes,_Yes_Y%27all)" title="Fire (Yes, Yes Y&#39;all)">Fire (Yes, Yes Y'all)</a>", featuring <a href="/wiki/Guest_appearance" title="Guest appearance">guest vocals</a> by American <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> peaked at No. 18 on the US <i>Billboard</i> <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Airplay" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Airplay">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Airplay</a> chart<sup id="cite_ref-Airplay_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Airplay-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and No. 48 on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-HotR&amp;B_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HotR&amp;B-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Budden made a remix featuring Paul Cain and <a href="/wiki/Fabolous" title="Fabolous">Fabolous</a>, which appeared on the latter's mixtape, titled <i><a href="/wiki/More_Street_Dreams,_Pt._2:_The_Mixtape" title="More Street Dreams, Pt. 2: The Mixtape">More Street Dreams, Pt. 2: The Mixtape</a></i>. In late 2003, Budden featured on the <a href="/wiki/R._Kelly" title="R. Kelly">R. Kelly</a> produced song, <i><a href="/wiki/Clubbin" class="mw-redirect" title="Clubbin">Clubbin</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Marques_Houston" title="Marques Houston">Marques Houston</a>, which peaked at No. 39 on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> and at No. 15 on the UK BPI charts.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Label_issues.2C_Mood_Muzik_series_.282003.E2.80.932008.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Label_issues,_Mood_Muzik_series_(2003–2008)">Label issues, <i>Mood Muzik</i> series (2003–2008)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joe_Budden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Label issues, Mood Muzik series (2003–2008)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While on Def Jam, Budden released two mixtapes, <i>Mood Muzik: The Worst of Joe Budden</i> on December 9, 2003, and <i><a href="/wiki/Mood_Muzik_2:_Can_It_Get_Any_Worse%3F" title="Mood Muzik 2: Can It Get Any Worse?">Mood Muzik 2: Can It Get Any Worse?</a></i> on December 26, 2005. Both were critically acclaimed<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Complex_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Complex Magazine">Complex</a> retrospectively naming <i>Mood Muzik 2</i> as one of the best mixtapes of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> During this time, his intended second album, <i>The Growth</i>, suffered continuous delays, due to disagreements between Joe and Def Jam executives over the direction of the album.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> On May 25, 2005, Joe released "<a href="/wiki/Gangsta_Party_(Joe_Budden_song)" title="Gangsta Party (Joe Budden song)">Gangsta Party</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a> as the album's first single. <i>The Growth</i> was eventually shelved and Budden was released from Def Jam.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2007, Budden signed a multi-album deal with <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_Digital" title="Amalgam Digital">Amalgam Digital</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> His first release on the label was <i><a href="/wiki/Mood_Muzik_3:_The_Album" title="Mood Muzik 3: The Album">Mood Muzik 3: The Album</a></i> on February 26, 2008, a retailed version of his mixtape <i><a href="/wiki/Mood_Muzik_3:_For_Better_or_for_Worse" title="Mood Muzik 3: For Better or for Worse">Mood Muzik 3: For Better or for Worse</a></i>, released previously on December 15, 2007. Although it sold poorly, the album received universal acclaim from critics, who praised Budden's willingness to discuss personal issues.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Halfway_House.2C_Slaughterhouse.2C_Padded_Room.2C_Escape_Route_.282008.E2.80.932012.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Halfway_House,_Slaughterhouse,_Padded_Room,_Escape_Route_(2008–2012)"><i>Halfway House</i>, Slaughterhouse, <i>Padded Room</i>, <i>Escape Route</i> (2008–2012)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joe_Budden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Halfway House, Slaughterhouse, Padded Room, Escape Route (2008–2012)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On October 28, 2008, Budden released his third studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Halfway_House_(album)" title="Halfway House (album)">Halfway House</a></i>, exclusively in <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">digital format</a>. It was the first in a series of connected albums.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Its release marked Budden's first return to the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> in five years, with approximately 3,000 downloads sold in its first week of release.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The album featured "Slaughterhouse", the first collaboration between Budden, <a href="/wiki/Crooked_I" title="Crooked I">Crooked I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royce_da_5%279%22" title="Royce da 5&#39;9&quot;">Royce da 5'9"</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joell_Ortiz" title="Joell Ortiz">Joell Ortiz</a>. The song's positive reception inspired the four to form a group, naming themselves <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse_(group)" title="Slaughterhouse (group)">Slaughterhouse</a> after the song.<sup id="cite_ref-xxlmag.com_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmag.com-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After an initial delay,<sup id="cite_ref-MVR_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MVR-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Budden's fourth album, <i><a href="/wiki/Padded_Room" class="mw-redirect" title="Padded Room">Padded Room</a></i> was released on February 24, 2009, debuting at No. 42 on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a> chart and No. 2 on the <a href="/wiki/Top_Independent_Albums" class="mw-redirect" title="Top Independent Albums">Top Independent Albums</a> chart, with 13,451 copies sold in the first week of release.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Budden's fifth album, <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_Route" title="Escape Route">Escape Route</a></i>, followed on August 11, 2009, and met with positive reception from critics.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The same day, Slaughterhouse released their self-titled debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse_(Slaughterhouse_album)" title="Slaughterhouse (Slaughterhouse album)">Slaughterhouse</a></i>, through <a href="/wiki/E1_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="E1 Entertainment">E1</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> On October 26, 2010, Budden released <i><a href="/wiki/Mood_Muzik_4:_A_Turn_4_the_Worst" title="Mood Muzik 4: A Turn 4 the Worst">Mood Muzik 4: A Turn 4 the Worst</a></i>.
</p><p>Slaughterhouse planned a second album for release in 2010, tentatively titled <i>No Muzzle</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Royce_da_5%279%22" title="Royce da 5&#39;9&quot;">Royce da 5'9"</a> confirmed talks between the group and <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Shady_Records" title="Shady Records">Shady Records</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and felt their second album should be released on a major label.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> After some complications with E1 and Amalgam,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Slaughterhouse officially signed a deal with <a href="/wiki/Shady_Records" title="Shady Records">Shady Records</a> on January 12, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> On February 8, 2011, Slaughterhouse released a self-titled <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse_(EP)" title="Slaughterhouse (EP)">extended play</a>.
</p><p>On August 28, 2012, Slaughterhouse released their second studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Welcome_to:_Our_House" title="Welcome to: Our House">Welcome to: Our House</a></i>, which debut at No. 2 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> and No. 1 on the <i>Billboard</i> Top Rap Albums, selling 52,000 copies its first week. It was preceded by the mixtape, <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_House_(album)" title="On the House (album)">On The House</a></i>, which was released on August 19, 2012.
</p>
<h3><span id="No_Love_Lost.2C_All_Love_Lost.2C_Rage_.26_The_Machine_.282013.E2.80.932016.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="No_Love_Lost,_All_Love_Lost,_Rage_&amp;_The_Machine_(2013–2016)"><i>No Love Lost</i>, <i>All Love Lost</i>, <i>Rage &amp; The Machine</i> (2013–2016)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joe_Budden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: No Love Lost, All Love Lost, Rage &amp; The Machine (2013–2016)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On October 16, 2012, Budden released "<a href="/wiki/She_Don%27t_Put_It_Down" title="She Don&#39;t Put It Down">She Don't Put It Down</a>", featuring <a href="/wiki/Lil_Wayne" title="Lil Wayne">Lil Wayne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tank_(American_singer)" title="Tank (American singer)">Tank</a>. The song was the lead single from his upcoming sixth studio album. It debut at No. 96 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, marking Budden's first appearance on the chart in nearly ten years since his debut single "<a href="/wiki/Pump_It_Up_(Joe_Budden_song)" title="Pump It Up (Joe Budden song)">Pump It Up</a>".
</p><p>In January 2013, Budden joined the cast of <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Love_%26_Hip_Hop:_New_York" title="Love &amp; Hip Hop: New York">Love &amp; Hip Hop: New York</a></i> in season three. He would return for its fourth season later that year.
</p><p>On February 5, 2013, Budden released <i><a href="/wiki/No_Love_Lost_(Joe_Budden_album)" title="No Love Lost (Joe Budden album)">No Love Lost</a></i>, which debut at No. 15 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> selling 30,000 copies in its first week. By March 20, 2013, the album had sold 60,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> It was preceded by the mixtape <i><a href="/wiki/A_Loose_Quarter" title="A Loose Quarter">A Loose Quarter</a></i>, which was released on November 20, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-www.hiphopdx.com_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.hiphopdx.com-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> On March 26, 2013, Budden released the album's second single, "N.B.A. (Never Broke Again)", featuring <a href="/wiki/Wiz_Khalifa" title="Wiz Khalifa">Wiz Khalifa</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Montana" title="French Montana">French Montana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 12, 2014, Budden participated in <i>Total Slaughter</i>, a rap battle event opposing <a href="/wiki/Hollow_Da_Don" class="mw-redirect" title="Hollow Da Don">Hollow Da Don</a>, a top rap battler and influencer in battle rap. Judges awarded victory to Hollow through unanimous decision.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2015, Budden began releasing a weekly podcast with Rory Farrell and Marisa Mendez, then known as <i>I'll Name This Podcast Later</i>. On October 16, 2015, Budden released his seventh studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/All_Love_Lost" title="All Love Lost">All Love Lost</a></i>, to universal acclaim from critics.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> It was preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">extended play</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Some_Love_Lost" title="Some Love Lost">Some Love Lost</a></i>, on November 4, 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> and the singles "Broke" and "Slaughtermouse".
</p><p>Budden announced his last solo tour dates on May 16, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-Budden_Last_Tour_Dates_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Budden_Last_Tour_Dates-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> On July 2, 2016, Budden released a diss track, "Making A Murderer Part I", primarily aimed at Canadian rapper <a href="/wiki/Drake_(entertainer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Drake (entertainer)">Drake</a>, although he also takes shots at <a href="/wiki/Meek_Mill" title="Meek Mill">Meek Mill</a> in the song. He later explained that the diss was not personal, done for the competition and sport, and that he has no gripe with either artist.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> In the aftermath of the feud, Mendez was replaced on Budden's podcast by Jamil "Mal" Clay, after which the show became known as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Joe_Budden_Podcast" title="The Joe Budden Podcast">The Joe Budden Podcast</a></i>.
</p><p>On October 21, 2016, Budden released his eighth and final solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Rage_%26_The_Machine" title="Rage &amp; The Machine">Rage &amp; The Machine</a></i>, produced entirely by <a href="/wiki/AraabMUZIK" class="mw-redirect" title="AraabMUZIK">AraabMUZIK</a>. The album debuted at No. 40 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, selling 11,341 copies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Broadcasting_career_.282017.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcasting_career_(2017–present)">Broadcasting career (2017–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joe_Budden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Broadcasting career (2017–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On April 17, 2017, Budden began co-hosting <i>Everyday Struggle</i>, a daily morning show for <a href="/wiki/Complex_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Complex Magazine">Complex</a>, with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Akademiks" title="DJ Akademiks">DJ Akademiks</a> and Nadeska Alexis.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> On June 25, 2017, during the <a href="/wiki/BET_Awards" title="BET Awards">BET Awards</a> pre-show, Budden and his co-hosts conducted an interview with <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">Atlanta rap group</a> <a href="/wiki/Migos" title="Migos">Migos</a>, where tensions rose between Budden and Migos member <a href="/wiki/Takeoff_(rapper)" title="Takeoff (rapper)">Takeoff</a> after DJ Akademiks questioned his absence on Migos' 2016 hit single <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_and_Boujee" title="Bad and Boujee">Bad and Boujee</a></i>. Budden walked off set and a momentary standoff occurred between himself and the group.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Migos" title="Migos">Migos</a> member <a href="/wiki/Quavo" title="Quavo">Quavo</a> referred to Joe Budden in the song "<a href="/wiki/Ice_Tray" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice Tray">Ice Tray</a>", saying "If a nigga hatin' call him Joe Budden".<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Budden left the show in December 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 14, 2018, Budden announced a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">Sean Combs</a> and his media company <a href="/wiki/Revolt_(TV_network)" title="Revolt (TV network)">Revolt</a>, creating and producing the talk show <i><a href="/wiki/State_of_the_Culture" title="State of the Culture">State of the Culture</a>,</i> which premiered on September 10, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Budden would co-host with rapper and fellow <i><a href="/wiki/Love_%26_Hip_Hop" title="Love &amp; Hip Hop">Love &amp; Hip Hop</a></i> co-star <a href="/wiki/Remy_Ma" title="Remy Ma">Remy Ma</a>. During the summer of 2018, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Joe_Budden_Podcast" title="The Joe Budden Podcast">The Joe Budden Podcast</a></i> began touring, with live performances through the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> During this time, he officially announced that he was retiring from rapping.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> In August 2018, Budden signed a deal to bring his podcast to <a href="/wiki/Spotify" title="Spotify">Spotify</a> and expand the show to a bi-weekly schedule, with new episodes every Wednesday and Saturday.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> On August 27, 2020, Budden announced he was leaving Spotify at the end of his contract over a financial disagreement with the streaming service.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2018, Budden returned to <i><a href="/wiki/Love_%26_Hip_Hop:_New_York" title="Love &amp; Hip Hop: New York">Love &amp; Hip Hop: New York</a></i> in season nine.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> The next year, he returned for the show's tenth anniversary season.
</p><p>On February 3, 2021, Budden announced that he is bringing exclusive content from his podcast to crowdfunding service <a href="/wiki/Patreon" title="Patreon">Patreon</a>. He also announced that he would be joining Patreon's board as Creator Equity Advisor with the goal to address "everything that's wrong with the monetization system for creators."<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joe_Budden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Budden has two children.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> His oldest son, Joseph Budden III (often referred to as Trey), was born in 2001. In 2010, a warrant for Budden was issued by the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_County,_New_Jersey" title="Hudson County, New Jersey">Hudson County, New Jersey</a> Sheriff's Office for unpaid child support.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> His second son, Lexington, was born on December 15, 2017, to Budden and model and <a href="/wiki/Video_vixen" title="Video vixen">video vixen</a> Cyn Santana. The couple became engaged in December 2018, after Budden proposed during a live episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Joe_Budden_Podcast" title="The Joe Budden Podcast">The Joe Budden Podcast</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> in New York.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The couple has since called off their engagement.
On March 30, 2012, Budden spent a night in jail and missed a <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse_(group)" title="Slaughterhouse (group)">Slaughterhouse</a> concert in his home town over a $75 parking ticket.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, Budden turned himself in to police following allegations he assaulted his girlfriend and stole her cell phone,<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> and appeared in Manhattan Criminal Court on charges of assault, grand larceny and robbery. A judge later dismissed all charges against Budden.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Budden has admitted to having an addiction to <a href="/wiki/Phencyclidine" title="Phencyclidine">PCP</a> and later <a href="/wiki/MDMA" title="MDMA">MDMA</a>, but has been off drugs and publicly spoken out against their use.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
