<p><b>David Blake</b> (born July 31, 1988),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> better known by his stage name <b>David Stones</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> <a href="/wiki/Recording_artist" class="mw-redirect" title="Recording artist">recording artist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Songwriter" title="Songwriter">songwriter</a> from <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn, New York">Brooklyn, New York</a>. He is the co-founder of District of Hip Hop also known as the DOHH Squad.
</p><p>David started receiving notoriety in 2010 while still known by the <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> Stones after <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remixing</a> <a href="/wiki/Diddy-Dirty_Money" class="mw-redirect" title="Diddy-Dirty Money">Diddy Dirty Money's</a> hit song <a href="/wiki/Hello_Good_Morning" title="Hello Good Morning">Hello Good Morning</a> and releasing a <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> to accompany it. Among the websites and <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a> to showcase the video was <a href="/wiki/Spike_(TV_channel)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spike (TV channel)">Spike.com</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He then followed up with a music video to his song "Steppin Out" which was featured on the <a href="/wiki/Rapper" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapper">rapper</a> <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent's</a> official video blog BooBooTV.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He was labeled a “HHL Co-Signed Artist” by HipHopLead.com<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and featured in their “Unsigned Spotlight” section that same year.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Stones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1988.E2.80.932011:_Early_life_and_musical_beginnings"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1988–2011:_Early_life_and_musical_beginnings">1988–2011: Early life and musical beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Stones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1988–2011: Early life and musical beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>David Stones was born in the <a href="/wiki/Brownsville,_Brooklyn" title="Brownsville, Brooklyn">Brownsville</a> section of Brooklyn, New York. An area known as one of the roughest neighborhoods in not only the borough of Brooklyn but also the entire state of New York.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">Rapping</a> was a hobby he picked up from watching his cousin Kenyatta Blake, better known as <a href="/wiki/Buckshot_(rapper)" title="Buckshot (rapper)">Buckshot</a> from the critically acclaimed 90's rap groups <a href="/wiki/Black_Moon_(group)" title="Black Moon (group)">Black Moon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boot_Camp_Clik" title="Boot Camp Clik">Boot Camp Clik</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> When asked about his cousins influence over his career in an interview David replied, "I use him as a mentor, so when I run into some type of jam in the game I can always hit him up and ask, 'what do you think about this?'".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While a <a href="/wiki/Freshman" title="Freshman">freshman</a> in high school David Stones formed a rap group with his childhood friend Dominick Bunch under the name Styxx ‘N Stones. The two participated in spontaneous <a href="/wiki/Rap_battle" class="mw-redirect" title="Rap battle">rap battles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_rap" title="Freestyle rap">cyphers</a> throughout their neighborhood in an attempt to build up their reputation as respectable <a href="/wiki/Emcee_(hip_hop)" class="mw-redirect" title="Emcee (hip hop)">emcees</a>. Although they recorded a lot of music together they never officially released any projects as a group and decided to go their separate ways with Styxx enlisting in the <a href="/wiki/US_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="US Navy">US Navy</a> while David chose to keep pursuing a career in music, but they remained friends. In 2012 Styxx died in a car accident.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006 David released his first <a href="/wiki/Mixtape" title="Mixtape">mixtape</a> entitled, “The New Stone Age” under the pseudonym Stones. The project didn't circulate too far outside of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_metropolitan_area" title="New York metropolitan area">Tri-State Area</a> due to lack of promotion, however it did get him recognized locally. His local buzz led to him getting noticed by hometown hero <a href="/wiki/Smooth_Da_Hustler" class="mw-redirect" title="Smooth Da Hustler">Smooth Da Hustler</a> who he later collaborated with on a revamped version to one of the mixtapes songs called, "Get Wit It".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 2007–008 David released various songs and <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_rap" title="Freestyle rap">freestyles</a> independently on his <a href="/wiki/Myspace" title="Myspace">Myspace</a> page.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The following year he caught the attention of Gallic Wars Mixtapes CEO who offered him a management deal in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He then went on to release his second mixtape, "The 2009 Collection" which featured a collection of songs, freestyles, and interviews recorded that year on <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year&#39;s Eve">New Year's Eve</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>David Stones took a hiatus from the <a href="/wiki/Music_industry" title="Music industry">music industry</a> for the second half of 2010 and the entire 2011 after switching <a href="/wiki/Talent_management" title="Talent management">management</a> and decided to change his stage name from Stones to David Stones, stating the name change symbolized "progression" and marked "a new chapter" in his career.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012:_The_Steppin_Stone:_All_Or_Nuthin">2012: <i>The Steppin Stone: All Or Nuthin</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Stones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2012: The Steppin Stone: All Or Nuthin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On November 22, 2012,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> David Stones released his third mixtape, <a href="/wiki/The_Steppin%27_Stone:_All_Or_Nuthin%27" class="mw-redirect" title="The Steppin&#39; Stone: All Or Nuthin&#39;">The Steppin' Stone: All Or Nuthin'</a> which features guest appearances from Buckshot, Mark Jonas, and Willie the Kid.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mixtape featured standout tracks such as, "Why I Love It"<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> which caught the attention of <a href="/wiki/No_Malice" title="No Malice">No Malice</a> from the critically acclaimed rap group <a href="/wiki/Clipse" title="Clipse">Clipse</a> who then tweeted David his appreciation for the song.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Hip Hop blogger Tony Grands described the song as a "piano-heavy anthem"<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> while Christopher Bryan of RapRise declared David's "trying to bring the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Hip_Hop" class="mw-redirect" title="East Coast Hip Hop">East Coast</a> back in a real way".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It also featured "Triumph 2012",<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> in which David remixes the classic <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a> song and features Willie The Kid who is the younger brother of Wu-Tang affiliate <a href="/wiki/La_the_Darkman" title="La the Darkman">La the Darkman</a>. While reviewing the track Hip Hop blogger Erin Ashley noted David's bravery stating it took "real guts" to remake a classic song such as Wu-Tang’s <a href="/wiki/Triumph_(song)" title="Triumph (song)">“Triumph”</a>, a song she stated, "dominates people’s lives to this day." Ashley then went on to shower David with praises for the bold move saying he "held his own" while describing it as "that 90s <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> [she's] been longing for."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The mixtape also featured the title track "All Or Nuthin"<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> where David talks about his struggles and reminisces on his fallen partner-in-rhyme, Styxx.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Who Need's A Genre? applauded David for the "raw emotion" and "knowledge" displayed on the song.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a review of the mixtape Joshua Hendricks applauded David Stones for putting together a project that's not just "a blabbering bunch of random words", stating his "flow is consistent", and he raps about real issues. When speaking on the mixtapes <a href="/wiki/Record_producer" title="Record producer">production</a> he called it "really good", noting there are "some ride in the whip worthy tracks" while labeling “The truth”,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> “Live @ The Ramada”,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and “Systm Bumpn"<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> as the projects standout tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2013.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2013–present">2013–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Stones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2013–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 2013–2014 Stones released only one song, a remix of <a href="/wiki/Drake_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Drake (rapper)">Drake's</a> <a href="/wiki/0_to_100_/_The_Catch_Up" title="0 to 100 / The Catch Up">0 to 100 / The Catch Up</a> single. Music journalist Skinny Friedman reviewed the track and gave it a 70 rating on the scale of 0 to 100.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artistry">Artistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Stones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Artistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Stones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an interview with former <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a> player, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Henry_(American_football)" title="Anthony Henry (American football)">Anthony Henry's</a> online music magazine David Stones cited his hometown as a key musical influence and stated it's "fast-paced" lifestyle is responsible for the "experiences and the insight" that shows in his music. He then went on to state, "I always try to present a picture of what’s going on in my mind in a genuine way that people can relate to" when asked to describe the best aspects of his style. While speaking on '90s hip hop a.k.a. <a href="/wiki/Golden_age_hip_hop" title="Golden age hip hop">"The Golden Age"</a> which he described as "super original" he specifically referenced <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/AZ_(rapper)" title="AZ (rapper)">AZ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boot_Camp_Clik" title="Boot Camp Clik">Boot Camp Clik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dungeon_Family" title="Dungeon Family">Dungeon Family</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> as some of his "all time favorites".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
