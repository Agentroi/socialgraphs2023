<p><b>Lamont Coleman</b> (May 30, 1974&#160;&#8211;&#32;February 15, 1999), known professionally as <b>Big L</b>, was an American rapper.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Emerging from <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a> in New York City in 1992, Coleman became known among <a href="/wiki/Underground_hip-hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Underground hip-hop">underground hip-hop</a> fans for his freestyling ability. He was eventually signed to <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>, where, in 1995, he released his debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Lifestylez_ov_da_Poor_%26_Dangerous" title="Lifestylez ov da Poor &amp; Dangerous">Lifestylez ov da Poor &amp; Dangerous</a></i>. On February 15, 1999, Coleman was shot nine times in his East Harlem neighborhood and later succumbed to his injuries.
</p><p>Coleman was noted for his use of wordplay, and writers at <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a>, <a href="/wiki/HipHopDX" title="HipHopDX">HipHopDX</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Source (magazine)"><i>The Source</i></a> have praised him for his lyrical ability.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Henry Adaso described him as "one of the most talented poets in hip-hop history."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Regarding Coleman's legacy in an interview with <a href="/wiki/Funkmaster_Flex" title="Funkmaster Flex">Funkmaster Flex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a> claimed "[Coleman] scared me to death. When I heard [his performance at the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Theater" title="Apollo Theater">Apollo Theater</a>] on tape, I was scared to death. I said, 'Yo, it's no way I can compete if this is what I gotta compete with.'"<sup id="cite_ref-mtv.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_L&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lamont Coleman was born on May 30, 1974, in the <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">Harlem</a> neighborhood of New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He was the third and youngest child of Gilda Terry (d. 2008)<sup id="cite_ref-mother&#39;s_death_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mother&#39;s_death-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and Charles Davis.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Davis left the family while Coleman was a child.<sup id="cite_ref-finesse-interview_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finesse-interview-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He had two older half siblings: Donald and Leroy Phinazee (d. 2002).<sup id="cite_ref-mother&#39;s_death_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mother&#39;s_death-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Coleman received the nicknames "Little L" and "Mont-Mont" as a child.<sup id="cite_ref-herald_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herald-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-don-phinazee-interview_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-don-phinazee-interview-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> His elder brother, Donald Phinazee, took Coleman to a <a href="/wiki/Run-DMC" title="Run-DMC">Run-DMC</a> concert at the <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Theatre_(New_York_City)" title="Beacon Theatre (New York City)">Beacon Theatre</a> when Coleman was about 7 years old. According to Phinazee, Coleman was awed by the performance which sparked his interest in rapping. By age 12, Coleman became a big hip hop fan and started <a href="/wiki/Freestyling" class="mw-redirect" title="Freestyling">freestyling</a> with other people in his neighborhood.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-don-phinazee-interview_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-don-phinazee-interview-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Coleman began writing rhymes in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He also founded a group known as Three the Hard Way in 1990, but it was quickly broken up due to a lack of enthusiasm among the members which  consisted of Coleman, Doc Reem, and Rodney.<sup id="cite_ref-Don_Q&amp;A_PT_2_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Don_Q&amp;A_PT_2-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dx-remember_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dx-remember-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> No projects were released, and after Rodney left, the group was renamed Two Hard Motherfuckers.<sup id="cite_ref-Don_Q&amp;A_PT_2_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Don_Q&amp;A_PT_2-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Around this time, people started to refer to Coleman as "Big L".<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In the summer of 1990, Coleman met <a href="/wiki/Lord_Finesse" title="Lord Finesse">Lord Finesse</a> at an autograph session in a record shop on 125th Street.<sup id="cite_ref-1995_Source_Interview_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1995_Source_Interview-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rawkus_Bio_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawkus_Bio-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> After he did a freestyle, Finesse and Coleman exchanged numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-Rawkus_Bio_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawkus_Bio-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Coleman attended <a href="/wiki/Julia_Richman_High_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Julia Richman High School">Julia Richman High School</a> and graduated in 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> While in high school, Coleman freestyle battled regularly; in his last interview, he stated, "in the beginning, all I ever saw me doing was battling everybody on the street corners, rhyming in the hallways, beating on the wall, rhyming to my friends. Every now and then, a house party, grab the mic, a block party, grab the mic."<sup id="cite_ref-Last-Interview_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Last-Interview-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_L&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1992.E2.80.931995:_First_recordings_and_record_deal"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1992–1995:_First_recordings_and_record_deal">1992–1995: First recordings and record deal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_L&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1992–1995: First recordings and record deal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1992, Coleman recorded various demos, some of which were featured on his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Lifestylez_ov_da_Poor_%26_Dangerous" title="Lifestylez ov da Poor &amp; Dangerous">Lifestylez ov da Poor &amp; Dangerous</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-dx-remember_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dx-remember-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hess2010-40_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess2010-40-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> On February 11, Coleman appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/Yo!_MTV_Raps" title="Yo! MTV Raps">Yo! MTV Raps</a></i> with Lord Finesse to help promote Finesse's studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Funky_Man" title="Return of the Funky Man">Return of the Funky Man</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Coleman's first professional appearance came on "Yes You May (Remix)", the <a href="/wiki/B-side" class="mw-redirect" title="B-side">B-side</a> of "Party Over Here" (1992) by Lord Finesse,<sup id="cite_ref-Hess2010-40_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess2010-40-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and his first album appearance was on "Represent" off of <a href="/wiki/Showbiz_%26_A.G." class="mw-redirect" title="Showbiz &amp; A.G.">Showbiz &amp; A.G.</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Runaway_Slave" title="Runaway Slave">Runaway Slave</a></i> (1992).<sup id="cite_ref-1995_Source_Interview_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1995_Source_Interview-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During this time, he won an amateur freestyle battle hosted by Nubian Productions which consisted of about 2,000 contestants.<sup id="cite_ref-Doc_Trailer_1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doc_Trailer_1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In 1992, Coleman signed to <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dx-remember_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dx-remember-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He then joined Lord Finesse's Bronx-based hip hop collective <a href="/wiki/Diggin%27_in_the_Crates_Crew" class="mw-redirect" title="Diggin&#39; in the Crates Crew">Diggin' in the Crates Crew</a> (DITC) which consisted of Lord Finesse, <a href="/wiki/Diamond_D" title="Diamond D">Diamond D</a>, <a href="/wiki/O.C._(rapper)" title="O.C. (rapper)">O.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat_Joe" title="Fat Joe">Fat Joe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buckwild_(music_producer)" title="Buckwild (music producer)">Buckwild</a>, Showbiz and A.G. In 1993, Coleman released his first promotional single, "Devil's Son", and later said it was one of the first <a href="/wiki/Horrorcore" title="Horrorcore">horrorcore</a> singles, influencing others. He said he wrote the song because "I've always been a fan of horror flicks. Plus the things I see in Harlem are very scary. So I just put it all together in a rhyme." However, he said he preferred other styles over horrorcore.<sup id="cite_ref-1995_Source_Interview_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1995_Source_Interview-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Coleman founded the Harlem rap group <a href="/wiki/Children_of_the_Corn_(group)" title="Children of the Corn (group)">Children of the Corn</a> (COC) with <a href="/wiki/Cam%27ron" title="Cam&#39;ron">Killa Cam</a> (Cam'ron), <a href="/wiki/Ma$e" class="mw-redirect" title="Ma$e">Murda Mase</a> (Ma$e), Bloodshed and <a href="/wiki/McGruff_(rapper)" title="McGruff (rapper)">McGruff</a> in 1993. On February 18, 1993, he performed live at the Uptown Lord Finesse Birthday Bash at the 2,000 Club, which included other performances from Fat Joe, <a href="/wiki/Nas_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nas (rapper)">Nas</a>, and Diamond D.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1994, he released his second promotional single "Clinic". On July 11, 1994, Coleman released the radio edit of "<a href="/wiki/Put_It_On" title="Put It On">Put It On</a>", followed up by the release of the music video three months later.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1995, the music video for the single "No Endz, No Skinz" debuted. It was directed by Brian Luvar.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His debut studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Lifestylez_ov_da_Poor_%26_Dangerous" title="Lifestylez ov da Poor &amp; Dangerous">Lifestylez ov da Poor &amp; Dangerous</a></i>, was released in March 1995. The album debuted at number 149 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and number 22 on <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <i>Lifestylez</i> would go on to sell over 200,000 copies as of 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-vh1-street_buzz_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vh1-street_buzz-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Three singles were released from the album; the first two, "Put It On" and "<a href="/wiki/M.V.P._(song)" title="M.V.P. (song)">M.V.P.</a>", reached the top 25 of <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Tracks" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot Rap Tracks">Hot Rap Tracks</a> and the third "<a href="/wiki/No_Endz,_No_Skinz" class="mw-redirect" title="No Endz, No Skinz">No Endz, No Skinz</a>" did not chart.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_singles_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic_singles-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1996.E2.80.931999:_independent_release"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1996–1999:_independent_release">1996–1999: independent release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_L&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1996–1999: independent release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1996, Coleman was dropped from Columbia mainly because of a dispute between Coleman and the label over artistic differences.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-Picture_Review_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Picture_Review-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> He stated, "I was there with a bunch of strangers that didn't really know my music."<sup id="cite_ref-crate_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crate-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1997, he started working on his second studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Picture_(Big_L_album)" title="The Big Picture (Big L album)">The Big Picture</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-allhiphop10_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allhiphop10-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> COC folded when Bloodshed died in a car accident in New York on March 2, 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Later that year, DITC appeared in the July issue of <i>On The Go Magazine</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Coleman then appeared on <a href="/wiki/O.C._(rapper)" title="O.C. (rapper)">O.C.</a>'s single "Dangerous" from O.C.'s second album <i><a href="/wiki/Jewelz" title="Jewelz">Jewelz</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> That November, he was the opening act for O.C.'s European Jewlez Tour.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sometime in 1998, Coleman formed his own <a href="/wiki/Independent_label" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent label">independent label</a>, Flamboyant Entertainment.<sup id="cite_ref-riverfront_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-riverfront-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, it "planned to distribute the kind of hip-hop that sold without top 40 samples or <a href="/wiki/R_%26_B" class="mw-redirect" title="R &amp; B">R &amp; B</a> hooks."<sup id="cite_ref-Jasper1999-2_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jasper1999-2-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> That same year, Coleman released the single "Ebonics".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The song, based on <a href="/wiki/African-American_Vernacular_English" title="African-American Vernacular English">African-American Vernacular English</a>, was called one of the top five independent singles of the year by <i>The Source</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rawkus_Bio_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawkus_Bio-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Also in 1998, DITC released their first single, "Dignified Soldiers".<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the release of  "Ebonics", Coleman caught the eye of <a href="/wiki/Damon_Dash" title="Damon Dash">Damon Dash</a>, the CEO of <a href="/wiki/Roc-A-Fella_Records" title="Roc-A-Fella Records">Roc-A-Fella Records</a>. Dash offered to sign him to Roc-A-Fella, but Coleman wanted his crew to sign as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Don_Tube_Interview_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Don_Tube_Interview-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hess2010-41_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess2010-41-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> On February 8, 1999, Coleman, <a href="/wiki/McGruff_(rapper)" title="McGruff (rapper)">Herb McGruff</a>, C-Town, and <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> started the process to sign with Roc-A-Fella as a group called "The Wolfpack".<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-McGruff_Video_Interview_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGruff_Video_Interview-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Murder">Murder</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_L&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Murder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At around 8:30 PM on February 15, 1999, Big L was killed at 45 West 139th Street in his native Harlem after being shot nine times in the face and chest in a <a href="/wiki/Drive-by_shooting" title="Drive-by shooting">drive-by shooting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Gerard Woodley, one of Big L's childhood friends, was arrested three months later for the crime.<sup id="cite_ref-Woodley_MTV_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woodley_MTV-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"It's a good possibility it was retaliation for something Big L's brother did, or Woodley believed he had done," said a spokesperson for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Police_Department" title="New York City Police Department">New York City Police Department</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Woodley was later released due to lack of evidence, and the murder case remains officially unsolved.<sup id="cite_ref-Time_Bio_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time_Bio-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a November 2010 interview with <i>Planet Ill</i>, Donald Phinazee, Big L's oldest brother, claimed Big L was sent to "do something" he was not supposed to by their brother, Leroy "Big Lee" Phinazee. Donald attributed Big L being seen committing the act as the cause for his murder.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 24, 2016, Woodley was shot in the head and later died at Harlem Hospital.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Woodley's family maintains his innocence in Coleman's killing.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Rapper <a href="/wiki/Cam%27ron" title="Cam&#39;ron">Cam'ron</a>, who was a close friend of Big L and Woodley, attended Woodley's funeral. Later, Cam'ron posted a video to <a href="/wiki/Instagram" title="Instagram">Instagram</a> claiming Big L had attempted to murder Woodley a week before his death.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, Lou Black, Gerard Woodley's cousin, published <i>Ethylene: The Rise and Fall of The 139th St. NFL Crew</i>. The book details Black's first hand interactions with the NFL crew and Big L. In the book, Black claims Leroy "Big Lee" Phinazee, Coleman's eldest half-brother and leader of the NFL crew, violated his probation when he was found to be in possession of an illegal weapon and was sentenced to prison. According to Black, while in prison, Phinazee met and contracted a hitman from <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> to murder three members of the NFL gang including Woodley. Phinazee had tasked Big L to identify the targets to the hitman. On the day when the murder was planned, Woodley noticed the hitman following him and successfully scared him off. As Big L had been seen multiple times with the alleged hitman days prior, Woodley assumed Big L had taken part in the attempted shooting. Approximately a week after the attempted shooting of Woodley, Big L was killed. Black did not specify if Woodley personally killed Big L.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Coleman is buried at <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_Memorial_Park_(Paramus,_New_Jersey)" title="George Washington Memorial Park (Paramus, New Jersey)">George Washington Memorial Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paramus,_New_Jersey" title="Paramus, New Jersey">Paramus, New Jersey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_releases">Posthumous releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_L&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Posthumous releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tracks "Get Yours", "Way of Life", and "Shyheim's Manchild" <a href="/wiki/B/w" class="mw-redirect" title="B/w">b/w</a> "Furious Anger" were released as singles in 1999 for DITC's <a href="/wiki/D.I.T.C._(album)" title="D.I.T.C. (album)">self-titled album</a> (2000) on <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Boy_Records" title="Tommy Boy Records">Tommy Boy Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DITC_album_review_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DITC_album_review-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The album peaked at number 31 on <a href="/wiki/R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" class="mw-redirect" title="R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> and number 141 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Coleman's first posthumous single was "<a href="/wiki/Flamboyant_(Big_L_song)" title="Flamboyant (Big L song)">Flamboyant</a>" b/w "On the Mic", which arrived on May 30, 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The single peaked at number 39 on the <i>Billboard</i> <a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> and topped the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Tracks" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot Rap Tracks">Hot Rap Tracks</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic_singles_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic_singles-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> making it Coleman's first and only number-one single.
</p><p>Coleman's second and final studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Picture_(Big_L_album)" title="The Big Picture (Big L album)">The Big Picture</a></i>, was released on August 1, 2000, and featured <a href="/wiki/Fat_Joe" title="Fat Joe">Fat Joe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guru_(rapper)" title="Guru (rapper)">Guru</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gang_Starr" title="Gang Starr">Gang Starr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Big_Daddy_Kane" title="Big Daddy Kane">Big Daddy Kane</a> among others. <i>The Big Picture</i> was put together by his manager and partner in Flamboyant Entertainment, Rich King. It contains songs that he had recorded and <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a> recordings that were never used, completed by producers and guest emcees that Coleman respected or had worked with previously.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>The Big Picture</i> debuted at number 13 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i></a> 200, number two on <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a>, and sold 72,549 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-vh1-street_buzz_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vh1-street_buzz-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The album was <a href="/wiki/RIAA_Certification" class="mw-redirect" title="RIAA Certification">certified gold</a> a month later for shipments of 500,000 copies by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">RIAA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-picture-cert_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-picture-cert-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Big Picture</i> was the only music by Big L to appear on a music chart outside of the United States, peaking at number 122 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Children_of_the_Corn:_The_Collector%27s_Edition" class="mw-redirect" title="Children of the Corn: The Collector&#39;s Edition">Children of the Corn: The Collector's Edition</a></i>, a  compilation album containing COC songs, was released in 2003. Big L's next posthumous album, <i><a href="/wiki/139_%26_Lenox" title="139 &amp; Lenox">139 &amp; Lenox</a></i>, was released on August 31, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Lenox_DX_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lenox_DX-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Issued by Rich King on Flamboyant Entertainment, it contained previously unreleased and rare tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-Lenox_DX_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lenox_DX-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-139_Overview_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-139_Overview-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> The follow-up album, <i><a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Devil%27s_Son" title="Return of the Devil&#39;s Son">Return of the Devil's Son</a></i> (2010), peaked at number 73 on R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Coleman's next releases were <i><a href="/wiki/The_Danger_Zone_(album)" title="The Danger Zone (album)">The Danger Zone</a></i> (2011),<sup id="cite_ref-Danger_DX_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Danger_DX-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> and an album called <i><a href="/wiki/L_Corleone" title="L Corleone">L Corleone</a></i>, which was released on February 14, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_influence">Legacy and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_L&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Henry Adaso, a music journalist for <a href="/wiki/About.com" class="mw-redirect" title="About.com">About.com</a>, called him the 23rd-best <a href="/wiki/Master_of_ceremonies" title="Master of ceremonies">MC</a> of 1987 to 2007, claiming "[he was] one of the most auspicious storytellers in hip hop history."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/HipHopDX" title="HipHopDX">HipHopDX</a></i> called Coleman "the most underrated lyricist ever".<sup id="cite_ref-dx-remember_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dx-remember-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
Many tributes have been given to Coleman. The first was by Lord Finesse and the other members of DITC on March 6, 1999, at the Tramps.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Source</i> has done multiple tributes to him: first in July 2000,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> and then in March 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
<i><a href="/wiki/XXL_(magazine)" title="XXL (magazine)">XXL</a></i> also did a tribute to Coleman in March 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-xxl_tribute_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxl_tribute-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> On February 16, 2005, at SOB's restaurant and nightclub in Manhattan, a commemoration was held for him.<sup id="cite_ref-SOBS_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOBS-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> It included special guests such as DITC, Herb McGruff, and Kid Capri.<sup id="cite_ref-SOBS_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOBS-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> All the money earned went to his estate.<sup id="cite_ref-SOBS_64-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOBS-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a> paid tribute to Coleman in the music video for his single "<a href="/wiki/Like_Toy_Soldiers" title="Like Toy Soldiers">Like Toy Soldiers</a>". In an interview with <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>, Jay-Z stated: "We were about to sign him right before he passed away. We were about to sign him to Roc-a-Fella. It was a done deal…I think he was very talented…I think he had the ability to write big records, and big choruses."<sup id="cite_ref-mtv.com_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Rapper <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a> also said on MTV, "He scared me to death. When I heard that on tape, I was scared to death. I said, 'Yo, it's no way I can compete if this is what I gotta compete with.'"<sup id="cite_ref-mtv.com_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, <a href="/wiki/Royce_da_5%279%22" title="Royce da 5&#39;9&quot;">Royce da 5'9"</a> said he believed Coleman would have been a "top 3" rapper all time if he had not been killed so prematurely.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> In 2019, <a href="/wiki/Funkmaster_Flex" title="Funkmaster Flex">Funkmaster Flex</a> said "People can get mad at me for saying this, but he was the best lyricist at the time. He was a better lyricist than <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">Biggie</a> and Jay-Z. He just didn't have the marketing and promotion. Let me go on the record and say that. It's the truth."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> In 2022, the 140th Street and Lennox Avenue intersection in Harlem was co-named Lamont "Big L" Coleman Way.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Coleman is often credited in helping to create the <a href="/wiki/Horrorcore" title="Horrorcore">horrorcore</a> genre of hip hop with his 1992 song "Devil's Son."<sup id="cite_ref-1995_Source_Interview_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1995_Source_Interview-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, not all his songs fall into this genre. For example, in the song "Street Struck," Coleman discusses the difficulties of growing up in the ghetto and describes the consequences of living a life of crime.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Idris Goodwin of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> wrote that "[Big L had an] impressive command of the English language", with his song "Ebonics" being the best example of this.<sup id="cite_ref-globe_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globe-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Coleman was notable for using a rap style called "<a href="/wiki/Multisyllabic_rhymes" title="Multisyllabic rhymes">compounding</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-McGruff-Interview-L-Online_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGruff-Interview-L-Online-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> He also used <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphors</a> in his rhymes.<sup id="cite_ref-Lifestylez_Review_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lifestylez_Review-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> M.F. DiBella of <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" class="mw-redirect" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> stated Coleman was "a master of the lyrical stickup undressing his competition with kinetic metaphors and a brash comedic repertoire".<sup id="cite_ref-Lifestylez_Review_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lifestylez_Review-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> On the review of <i>The Big Picture</i>, she adds, describing "the Harlem MC as a master of the punch line and a vicious storyteller with a razor blade-under-the-tongue flow."<sup id="cite_ref-Picture_Review_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Picture_Review-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Trent Fitzgerald of Allmusic said Coleman was "a lyrically ferocious MC with raps deadlier than a snakebite and mannerisms cooler than the uptown pimp he claimed to be on records."<sup id="cite_ref-DITC_biography_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DITC_biography-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Documentary">Documentary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_L&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Documentary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A documentary <i>Street Struck: The Big L Story</i> was set to be released in 2017. Directed by a childhood friend and independent film director, Jewlz,<sup id="cite_ref-Doc_Trailer_1_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doc_Trailer_1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> approximately nine hours of footage was brought in, and the film's planned runtime was said to be 90 to 120 minutes long.<sup id="cite_ref-Don_Tube_Interview_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Don_Tube_Interview-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Released on August 29, 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-Doc_Trailer_1_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doc_Trailer_1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> the first trailer detailed that <i>Street Struck</i> would contain interviews from his mother Gilda Terry; his brother Donald; childhood friends E-Cash, D.O.C., McGruff, and Stan Spit; artists Mysonne and <a href="/wiki/Doug_E._Fresh" title="Doug E. Fresh">Doug E. Fresh</a>; producers Showbiz and Premier; and recording DJs Cipha Sounds and Peter Rosenberg.<sup id="cite_ref-Doc_Trailer_1_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doc_Trailer_1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Put together by Coleman's brother Donald, a soundtrack was said to have been made for the documentary as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Don_Tube_Interview_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Don_Tube_Interview-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> As of 2023, both the documentary and soundtrack have yet to be released.
</p>
