<p><b>Coke La Rock</b> (aka <b>Coco La Rock</b>; born April 24, 1955) is an <a href="/wiki/Old-school_hip_hop" title="Old-school hip hop">old-school</a> <a href="/wiki/Rapper" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapper">rapper</a> from <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> who is sometimes credited as being the first <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Ceremonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Master of Ceremonies">MC</a> in the history of <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip-hop</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2010, Coke La Rock was inducted into the High Times <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_Hall_of_Fame" title="Counterculture Hall of Fame">Counterculture Hall of Fame</a><sup id="cite_ref-ccw_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccw-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> at the annual ceremonies at the <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_Cup" title="Cannabis Cup">Cannabis Cup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kool_Herc_and_Coke_La_Rock">Kool Herc and Coke La Rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coke_La_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Kool Herc and Coke La Rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>La Rock was born in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">The Bronx</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> on April 24, 1955, with family roots going back to <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.
</p><p>Coke La Rock was a friend and musical partner of <a href="/wiki/DJ" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ">DJ</a> <a href="/wiki/Kool_Herc" class="mw-redirect" title="Kool Herc">Kool Herc</a>, who himself is generally considered to have laid down the foundation for hip-hop music starting in 1973. La Rock was an original member of Herc's MC crew, the Herculoids. 
</p><p>According to Herc, Coke La Rock's MC name had various iterations, beginning as "A-1 Coke," and then moving on to "Nasty Coke" before it was finalized as "Coke La Rock".<sup id="cite_ref-DaveyD_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DaveyD-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TLaRock_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TLaRock-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Coke La Rock joined Kool Herc for his first party, in 1973, to celebrate Herc's sister Cindy's birthday. It wasn't until about the fifth or sixth party that he took the name Coke La Rock. The name came to him in a dream. Before that time, he had no name and did his rapping out of sight from the audience, so no one knew who was doing the rapping.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>His original raps were usually shout-outs to friends, but gradually the poetry emerged.<sup id="cite_ref-Chang_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chang-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He originated such phrases as "You rock and you don't stop," and "Hotel, motel, you don't tell, we won't tell"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (which was immortalized on the first Sugarhill Gang single "<a href="/wiki/Rapper%27s_Delight" title="Rapper&#39;s Delight">Rapper's Delight</a>", although La Rock received no credit).
</p><p>Coke La Rock's raps were always purely improvisational, unlike those of later 70s-era rap groups, —such as the <a href="/wiki/Furious_Five" class="mw-redirect" title="Furious Five">Furious Five</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Cold_Crush_Brothers" title="The Cold Crush Brothers">Cold Crush Brothers</a>, who wrote down and rehearsed their rhymes and created elaborate routines. According to La Rock, while rapping "at first I would just call out [my friends'] names. Then I pretended dudes had double-parked cars; that was to impress the girls. Truthfully, I wasn’t there to rap, I was just playing around."<sup id="cite_ref-NYMag_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYMag-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nonetheless, La Rock's raps would, as with much else at Kool Herc's parties in the mid-1970s, serve as a basic model for other hip-hop artists that would come onto the Bronx music scene by the end of the decade. La Rock himself has argued, in a reference to two pioneering New York City narcotics dealers, that "me and Herc were to hip-hop what <a href="/wiki/Nicky_Barnes" title="Nicky Barnes">Nicky Barnes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lucas_(drug_dealer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Frank Lucas (drug dealer)">Frank Lucas</a> were to drugs."<sup id="cite_ref-NYMag_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYMag-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_in_popularity_and_later_years">Decline in popularity and later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coke_La_Rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Decline in popularity and later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As other nascent hip-hop groups patterned themselves after Herc and La Rock and improved on their formula, the popularity of Herc and the Herculoids began to wane as early as 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After Kool Herc was stabbed at a party, La Rock went looking to kill the perpetrator, who was part of the Executive Playhouse crew. He found the man's friends in a Bronx pool hall, but they had already moved their friend down south to avoid a confrontation. After this incident, La Rock decided to step away from hip hop and let the younger generation move in.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Since he'd had a long run lasting several years of being on top of the Bronx hip hop scene, La Rock didn't want to continue if murder was going to continue to be part of the game. Also his son, Dante La Rock, had just been born, and he needed to spend more time at home.
</p><p>Gary Harris, an employee of the first hip-hop record label, <a href="/wiki/Sugar_Hill_Records_(rap)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sugar Hill Records (rap)">Sugar Hill</a>, noted that "people respected Herc and Coke, but by the early eighties those guys were like specters—they just weren’t visible on the scene anymore."<sup id="cite_ref-NYMag_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYMag-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In contrast to other early Bronx hip-hop artists like <a href="/wiki/Afrika_Bambaataa" title="Afrika Bambaataa">Afrika Bambaataa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Flash" title="Grandmaster Flash">Grandmaster Flash</a>, Coke La Rock—like his partner Kool Herc—never achieved any recording success (indeed he did not record at all). However, late 2008 saw the release of what was deemed the first ever recording featuring La Rock, a song titled "Hello – Merry Christmas Baby!" which was released by Sedgwick &amp; Cedar as part of a special holiday compilation to pay homage to the birthplace of hip-hop.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>La Rock's place in hip-hop history was arguably immortalized in the legendary 1986 <a href="/wiki/Boogie_Down_Productions" title="Boogie Down Productions">Boogie Down Productions</a> song "South Bronx" (the opening salvo of the so-called "<a href="/wiki/The_Bridge_Wars" title="The Bridge Wars">Bridge Wars</a>") wherein <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">KRS-One</a> raps this:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Now way back in the days when hip-hop began<br />
With Coke La Rock, Kool Herc, and then <a href="/wiki/Afrika_Bambaataa" title="Afrika Bambaataa">Bam</a>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Coke La Rock had no association with, nor relation to, KRS-One's DJ at the time, <a href="/wiki/Scott_La_Rock" title="Scott La Rock">Scott La Rock</a>. Nor did Coke La Rock and another influential old-school MC, <a href="/wiki/T_La_Rock" title="T La Rock">T La Rock</a>, know one another in the early days of hip-hop, and thus the name similarity is coincidental.<sup id="cite_ref-TLaRock_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TLaRock-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2021, after years of being absent from the hip hop scene, Coke La Rock returned to the microphone with a verse on the <a href="/wiki/DJ_Kay_Slay" title="DJ Kay Slay">DJ Kay Slay</a> posse track "Rolling 110 Deep".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
