<p><b>Mark D. Ross</b> (born April 4, 1966), better known by his stage name <b>Brother Marquis</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Rapper" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapper">rapper</a> and member of <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a>.  He appears on all of 2 Live Crew's albums, except <i><a href="/wiki/Back_at_Your_Ass_for_the_Nine-4" title="Back at Your Ass for the Nine-4">Back at Your Ass for the Nine-4</a></i> (1994). Ross was the last member to join the most well known line up of the group.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886046785">.mw-parser-output .toclimit-2 .toclevel-1 ul,.mw-parser-output .toclimit-3 .toclevel-2 ul,.mw-parser-output .toclimit-4 .toclevel-3 ul,.mw-parser-output .toclimit-5 .toclevel-4 ul,.mw-parser-output .toclimit-6 .toclevel-5 ul,.mw-parser-output .toclimit-7 .toclevel-6 ul{display:none}</style><div class="toclimit-2"><meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" /></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brother_Marquis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ross was born in <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_New_York" title="Rochester, New York">Rochester, New York</a> on April 4, 1966. He and his mother moved from New York to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a> when Ross was 14 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brother_Marquis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1983.E2.80.931986_Early_career.2C_joining_2_Live_Crew_and_the_group.27s_breakthrough"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1983–1986_Early_career,_joining_2_Live_Crew_and_the_group's_breakthrough">1983–1986 Early career, joining 2 Live Crew and the group's breakthrough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brother_Marquis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1983–1986 Early career, joining 2 Live Crew and the group&#039;s breakthrough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ross released two <a href="/wiki/Twelve-inch_single" title="Twelve-inch single">twelve-inch singles</a>, "Westside Storie" and "Rhythm Rock", during his <a href="/wiki/High_school" class="mw-redirect" title="High school">high school</a> years, as a member of the rap group, the Caution Crew, which also included the rappers <a href="/wiki/Rodney-O_%26_Joe_Cooley" title="Rodney-O &amp; Joe Cooley">Rodney-O</a> and C Funk. These two singles were among the first 12-inch <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">hip hop records from the West Coast</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While still residing in <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">Southern California</a>, Ross performed small shows with <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a>, which, at that time, consisted of rappers <a href="/wiki/Fresh_Kid_Ice" title="Fresh Kid Ice">Fresh Kid Ice</a> (Christopher Wong Won), Amazing Vee (Yuri Vielot), and their <a href="/wiki/Disk_jockey" class="mw-redirect" title="Disk jockey">DJ</a> <a href="/wiki/Mr._Mixx" title="Mr. Mixx">Mr. Mixx</a> (David Hobbs).  Hobbs noticed Ross' <a href="/wiki/Battle_rap" title="Battle rap">battle rap</a> skills, and told Ross that if 2 Live Crew ever achieved success in the music business, Hobbs would call Ross to be a part of the group.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 1985, after finding <a href="/wiki/Underground_hip_hop" title="Underground hip hop">underground</a> success in <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, 2 Live Crew relocated to the city at the behest of local <a href="/wiki/Tour_promoter" title="Tour promoter">concert promoter</a> <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" class="mw-redirect" title="Luther Campbell">Luther Campbell</a>, who would later join the group as producer, recording artist, and <a href="/wiki/Hype_man" title="Hype man">hype man</a>.
</p><p>Shortly after the move to Miami, the artistic direction of 2 Live Crew changed from West Coast hip hop style rap to comical, sexually explicit lyrics, and rapper Vielot left the group.  In April, 1985, Hobbs made good on his previous promise to a then 19-year old Ross, and invited Ross to join 2 Live Crew with Wong Won, Hobbs and Campbell. The first 2 Live Crew single released with Ross was "Word".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2 Live Crew's <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_certification" title="Music recording certification">Gold-certified</a> debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_2_Live_Crew_Is_What_We_Are" title="The 2 Live Crew Is What We Are">The 2 Live Crew Is What We Are</a></i> (1986), exploded on the local scene. The expanding national popularity of <i>The 2 Live Crew Is What We Are</i> made Ross and his band mates rap superstars.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1988.E2.80.931991:_Best_selling_2_Live_Crew_albums_and_attendant_controversy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1988–1991:_Best_selling_2_Live_Crew_albums_and_attendant_controversy">1988–1991: Best selling 2 Live Crew albums and attendant controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brother_Marquis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1988–1991: Best selling 2 Live Crew albums and attendant controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1988, <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> released their second <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" class="mw-redirect" title="Studio album">studio album</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Move_Somethin%27_(album)" title="Move Somethin&#39; (album)">Move Somethin'</a></i>. The album was also certified Gold and featured the singles "Move Somethin'" and "Do Wah Diddy Diddy".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <i>Move Somethin'</i> improved on the charts from the previous album, making into #68 on the <i>Billboard 200</i> and #20 on the <i>Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</i> charts.
</p><p>2 Live Crew's third studio album <i><a href="/wiki/As_Nasty_As_They_Wanna_Be" class="mw-redirect" title="As Nasty As They Wanna Be">As Nasty As They Wanna Be</a></i> (1989), became the group's biggest seller, certified as Double Platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>. In 1990, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_Florida" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of Florida">United States District Court for the Southern District of Florida</a> ruled that the album was legally obscene;<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> but this ruling was later overturned by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Eleventh_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit">Eleventh Circuit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <i>As Nasty As They Wanna Be</i> is the first album in history to be deemed legally obscene.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1990, Ross and the <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> released their fourth album <i><a href="/wiki/Banned_in_the_U.S.A." title="Banned in the U.S.A.">Banned in the U.S.A.</a></i>, which was originally credited as <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" class="mw-redirect" title="Luther Campbell">Campbell</a>'s solo album featuring 2 Live Crew and in later editions credited as a 2 Live Crew album. The album included the hits "<a href="/wiki/Do_the_Bart" title="Do the Bart">Do the Bart</a>" and the title track. It was also the very first release to bear the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">RIAA</a>-standard <a href="/wiki/Parental_Advisory" title="Parental Advisory">Parental Advisory</a> warning sticker.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponymous</a> title single was a reference to the obscenity decision from the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Florida. <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen" title="Bruce Springsteen">Bruce Springsteen</a> granted the group permission to <a href="/wiki/Interpolation_(popular_music)" title="Interpolation (popular music)">interpolate</a> his song "<a href="/wiki/Born_in_the_U.S.A._(song)" title="Born in the U.S.A. (song)">Born in the U.S.A.</a>" for the single.
</p><p>Also in 1990, 2 Live Crew released <i>Live in Concert</i>, the group’s only <a href="/wiki/Album#Live" title="Album">live album</a>, and their fifth album overall.  The album was released under the Effect label, a subsidiary label of <a href="/wiki/Luke_Records" title="Luke Records">Luke Records</a>. The album peaked at #46 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a></i> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Weekend:_As_Nasty_as_They_Wanna_Be,_Pt._2" class="mw-redirect" title="Sports Weekend: As Nasty as They Wanna Be, Pt. 2">Sports Weekend: As Nasty as They Wanna Be, Pt. 2</a></i>, the fifth studio album and sixth album overall by the <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> was released in 1991 as a sequel of <i><a href="/wiki/As_Nasty_As_They_Wanna_Be" class="mw-redirect" title="As Nasty As They Wanna Be">As Nasty As They Wanna Be</a></i>.  A clean version was also released entitled <i>Sports Weekend: As Clean As They Wanna Be Part II</i> and was the sequel of <i><a href="/wiki/As_Clean_As_They_Wanna_Be" class="mw-redirect" title="As Clean As They Wanna Be">As Clean As They Wanna Be</a></i>.  <i>Sports Weekend: As Nasty as They Wanna Be, Pt. 2</i> would be the final album released by the most well known line up 2 Live Crew members (Ross, Wong Won, Campbell and Hobbs).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1992.E2.80.932003:_Subsequent_2_Live_Crew_success.2C_featured_rapper_and_solo_project"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1992–2003:_Subsequent_2_Live_Crew_success,_featured_rapper_and_solo_project">1992–2003: Subsequent 2 Live Crew success, featured rapper and solo project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brother_Marquis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1992–2003: Subsequent 2 Live Crew success, featured rapper and solo project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1993, Ross and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Toomp" title="DJ Toomp">DJ Toomp</a> formed a duo group named 2 Nazty and released one studio album, <i>Indecent Exposure</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> That same year, Ross appeared as a featured rapper on <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Home_Invasion_(album)" title="Home Invasion (album)">Home Invasion</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a> on the original version of "<a href="/wiki/99_Problems" title="99 Problems">99 Problems</a>", which was later remade into a major hit by <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Ross filed a lawsuit against Jay-Z and Ice-T over the "99 Problems" remake. Ross claimed that he was paid just $10,000 in royalties, despite being prominently featured on the original version.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1994, <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" class="mw-redirect" title="Luther Campbell">Campbell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fresh_Kid_Ice" title="Fresh Kid Ice">Wong Won</a>, and a local <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> rapper named Verb made an album called <i><a href="/wiki/Back_at_Your_Ass_for_the_Nine-4" title="Back at Your Ass for the Nine-4">Back at Your Ass for the Nine-4</a></i>, as The New 2 Live Crew, without the involvement of Ross or <a href="/wiki/Mr._Mixx" title="Mr. Mixx">Hobbs</a>. <i>Back at Your Ass for the Nine-4</i> is the last 2 Live Crew-related project to feature <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" class="mw-redirect" title="Luther Campbell">Campbell</a>, and the only album released without Ross.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ross, Wong Won and Hobbs would reunite to release the single "Hoochie Mama" for the soundtrack to the 1995 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Friday_(1995_film)" title="Friday (1995 film)">Friday</a></i>. The soundtrack reached #1 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a></i> chart, where it held its position for two weeks, and on the <i><a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a></i> chart for six weeks.
</p><p>In 1996, Ross reunited with <a href="/wiki/Fresh_Kid_Ice" title="Fresh Kid Ice">Wong Won</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mr._Mixx" title="Mr. Mixx">Hobbs</a> under the <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> banner to release the group's seventh studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Shake_a_Lil%27_Somethin%27" title="Shake a Lil&#39; Somethin&#39;">Shake a Lil' Somethin'</a></i>, whose eponymous single peaked at #11 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Singles</a></i> chart,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The single "Do the Damn Thing" made it to #24 on the same chart,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and "Be My Private Dancer" peaked at #34.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It would be the last 2 Live Crew album to feature Hobbs.
</p><p>In 1998, Ross, along with Wong Won, released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Real_One" title="The Real One">The Real One</a></i> the final <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> album to date.  The album's single "2 Live Party" featuring <a href="/wiki/Harry_Wayne_Casey" title="Harry Wayne Casey">KC</a> of <a href="/wiki/KC_and_the_Sunshine_Band" title="KC and the Sunshine Band">KC and the Sunshine Band</a> and "<a href="/wiki/Freak_Nasty" title="Freak Nasty">Freak Nasty</a>" peaked at #52 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a></i> chart<sup id="cite_ref-:1_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and #9 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Songs</a></i> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The title single "The Real One", featuring <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a>, peaked at #60 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-:3_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and #9 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Songs</a></i> charts.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, Ross released <i>Bottom Boi Style</i> on the Playalistic Entertainment label, his first and only <a href="/wiki/Solo_album" class="mw-redirect" title="Solo album">solo album</a> to date.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2006.E2.80.932010:_Reforming_and_reuniting_with_2_Live_Crew"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2006–2010:_Reforming_and_reuniting_with_2_Live_Crew">2006–2010: Reforming and reuniting with 2 Live Crew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brother_Marquis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2006–2010: Reforming and reuniting with 2 Live Crew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During 2006–07 Ross and <a href="/wiki/Fresh_Kid_Ice" title="Fresh Kid Ice">Wong Won</a> met, discussed their differences, and ultimately decided to relaunch <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a>. The duo made offers to past members (notably, Hobbs and Campbell) to re-join the group, but the offers were declined. Prior to <a href="/wiki/Fresh_Kid_Ice#Death" title="Fresh Kid Ice">Wong Won's death in 2017</a>, the duo toured and released singles.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, Ross and Wong Won briefly reunited with former band mates <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" class="mw-redirect" title="Luther Campbell">Campbell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mr._Mixx" title="Mr. Mixx">Hobbs</a> as 2 Live Crew, and were honored at the 2010 <i><a href="/wiki/Hip_Hop_Honors#7th_VH1_Hip_Hop_Honors_(2010)" title="Hip Hop Honors">VH1 Hip-Hop Honors: The Dirty South Edition</a></i> awards show.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In late 2010, Ross and Wong Won released 2 Live Crew singles "I'm 2 Live" featuring rapper <a href="/wiki/Mannie_Fresh" title="Mannie Fresh">Mannie Fresh</a>, "Cougar," and "Boom" featuring rapper <a href="/wiki/E-40" title="E-40">E-40</a>. The duo announced the pending release of an album, <i>Just Wanna be Heard,</i> that remains unreleased to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2011.E2.80.93present:_Current_projects"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2011–present:_Current_projects">2011–present: Current projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brother_Marquis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2011–present: Current projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, Ross was a featured guest on the single "Steak &amp; Mash Potatoes" by Chain Swangaz. A <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> of the single featured Ross and Chain Swangaz as fast food employee who create havoc while their boss, played by <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Sheen" title="Charlie Sheen">Charlie Sheen</a>, is gone.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, Ross and <a href="/wiki/Fresh_Kid_Ice" title="Fresh Kid Ice">Wong Won</a> released the single "Take It Off," of which a music video was made with cameos by rappers <a href="/wiki/Flo_Rida" title="Flo Rida">Flo Rida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trick_Daddy" title="Trick Daddy">Trick Daddy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trina" title="Trina">Trina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mannie_Fresh" title="Mannie Fresh">Mannie Fresh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Flavor_Flav" title="Flavor Flav">Flavor Flav</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Later that year, Wong Won and Ross made cameo appearances in the <a href="/wiki/Flo_Rida" title="Flo Rida">Flo Rida</a> music video "<a href="/wiki/G.D.F.R._(song)" class="mw-redirect" title="G.D.F.R. (song)">G.D.F.R.</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  That same year, the duo also announced the pending release of a new 2 Live Crew studio album <i>Turn Me On</i>, which remains unreleased.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In late 2014, Ross and Wong Won reunited with <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" class="mw-redirect" title="Luther Campbell">Campbell</a> as the <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> for a series of shows through the following year, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, shortly after <a href="/wiki/Fresh_Kid_Ice" title="Fresh Kid Ice">Wong Won</a> left 2 Live Crew for the final time, Ross reunited with <a href="/wiki/Mr._Mixx" title="Mr. Mixx">Hobbs</a> to reform the group.  The duo released two singles <i>How Bout Dem Cowboys</i> and <i>One Horse Sleigh</i> in 2016 and continue to tour nationally as 2 Live Crew.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal">Personal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brother_Marquis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ross is an avowed <a href="/wiki/Born_again" title="Born again">born again Christian</a>, but feels no moral restrictions performing sexually explicit lyrics in <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> hits on tour or on recordings.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, Ross uploaded two videos to his <i><a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a></i> channel of him performing <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_comedy" title="Stand-up comedy">stand up comedy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In a 2015 <i>YouTube</i> interview, Ross stated that he was pursuing the goal of being a comic and that he has a daughter.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
