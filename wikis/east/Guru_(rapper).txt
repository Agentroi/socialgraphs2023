<p><b>Keith Edward Elam</b> (July 17, 1961<sup id="cite_ref-passport_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-passport-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>&#160;&#8211;&#32;April 19, 2010), better known by his stage name <b>Guru</b> (a <a href="/wiki/Backronym" title="Backronym">backronym</a> for <b>Gifted Unlimited Rhymes Universal</b>), was an American rapper, record producer and actor. He was a member of the hip hop duo <a href="/wiki/Gang_Starr" title="Gang Starr">Gang Starr</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>. He was born in <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/About.com" class="mw-redirect" title="About.com">About.com</a> placed him #49 on their list of Top 50 MCs of Our Time,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Source" title="The Source">The Source</a></i> ranked him #30 on their list of Top 50 Lyricists of All Time, saying "Guru dropped some of the most thoughtful rhymes on wax".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Guru died on April 19, 2010, from <a href="/wiki/Myeloma" class="mw-redirect" title="Myeloma">myeloma</a> at age 48.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guru_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Elam was born in the <a href="/wiki/Roxbury,_Boston" title="Roxbury, Boston">Roxbury</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. His father, Harry Justin Elam served both as a judge and a lawyer and he became the first African American judge appointed to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Municipal_Court" title="Boston Municipal Court">Boston Municipal Court</a> of Massachusetts and he later also became Chief Justice of the same court and finally as a judge he was appointed as an Associate Justice of the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Superior_Court" title="Massachusetts Superior Court">Massachusetts Superior Court</a>. Prior to his judgeship years, between 1971 and 1988, he was a prominent lawyer in the city of Boston from 1952 to 1971
and his mother, Barbara, was the co-director of libraries in the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Public_Schools" title="Boston Public Schools">Boston Public Schools</a> system. He attended the Advent School on <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Hill,_Boston" title="Beacon Hill, Boston">Beacon Hill</a> in Boston, <a href="/wiki/Noble_and_Greenough_School" title="Noble and Greenough School">Noble and Greenough School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dedham,_Massachusetts" title="Dedham, Massachusetts">Dedham, Massachusetts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cohasset_High_School" title="Cohasset High School">Cohasset High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cohasset,_Massachusetts" title="Cohasset, Massachusetts">Cohasset, Massachusetts</a> for high school.<sup id="cite_ref-phoenix_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phoenix-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Elam graduated with a degree in business administration from <a href="/wiki/Morehouse_College" title="Morehouse College">Morehouse College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a><sup id="cite_ref-guardian_obituary_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian_obituary-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and took graduate classes at the <a href="/wiki/Fashion_Institute_of_Technology" title="Fashion Institute of Technology">Fashion Institute of Technology</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guru_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Musical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Elam began his music career under the pseudonym <b>MC Keithy E</b> but later changed his stage name to <b>Guru</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-phoenix_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phoenix-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He founded <a href="/wiki/Gang_Starr" title="Gang Starr">Gang Starr</a> in 1987. The group initially released three records, produced by <a href="/wiki/The_45_King" title="The 45 King">The 45 King</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Wild_Pitch_Records" title="Wild Pitch Records">Wild Pitch Records</a> record label, but these records received little attention.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_obituary_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian_obituary-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45_king_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45_king-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> After a change in lineup, the group consisted of <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapper</a> Guru and producer <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>. Gang Starr released its first LP <i><a href="/wiki/No_More_Mr._Nice_Guy_(Gang_Starr_album)" title="No More Mr. Nice Guy (Gang Starr album)">No More Mr. Nice Guy</a></i> on Wild Pitch Records; the group achieved a sizable following and released six critically acclaimed and influential albums from 1989 to 2003. Two albums, <i><a href="/wiki/Moment_of_Truth_(Gang_Starr_album)" title="Moment of Truth (Gang Starr album)">Moment of Truth</a></i> (1998) and compilation <i><a href="/wiki/Full_Clip:_A_Decade_of_Gang_Starr" title="Full Clip: A Decade of Gang Starr">Full Clip: A Decade of Gang Starr</a></i> (1999) were certified gold in the United States by the <a href="/wiki/RIAA" class="mw-redirect" title="RIAA">RIAA</a>. Gang Starr made archetypal East Coast hip hop with Guru's rhyming described as sharp-eyed but anti-ostentatious.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_obituary_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian_obituary-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1993, Guru released the first in a series of four solo albums while still a member of Gang Starr. <i><a href="/wiki/Jazzmatazz,_Vol._1" class="mw-redirect" title="Jazzmatazz, Vol. 1">Jazzmatazz, Vol. 1</a></i> featured collaborations with <a href="/wiki/Donald_Byrd" title="Donald Byrd">Donald Byrd</a>, <a href="/wiki/N%27Dea_Davenport" title="N&#39;Dea Davenport">N'Dea Davenport</a>, <a href="/wiki/MC_Solaar" title="MC Solaar">MC Solaar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roy_Ayers" title="Roy Ayers">Roy Ayers</a> and received positive reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_review_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_review-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> His second solo LP, <i><a href="/wiki/Jazzmatazz,_Vol._2:_The_New_Reality" class="mw-redirect" title="Jazzmatazz, Vol. 2: The New Reality">Jazzmatazz, Vol. 2: The New Reality</a></i>, featured <a href="/wiki/Chaka_Khan" title="Chaka Khan">Chaka Khan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Lewis" title="Ramsey Lewis">Ramsey Lewis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Branford_Marsalis" title="Branford Marsalis">Branford Marsalis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jamiroquai" title="Jamiroquai">Jamiroquai</a>. The third installment, <i><a href="/wiki/Jazzmatazz,_Vol._3:_Streetsoul" class="mw-redirect" title="Jazzmatazz, Vol. 3: Streetsoul">Jazzmatazz, Vol. 3: Streetsoul</a></i>, was released in 2000, but it received less positive reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_review2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_review2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In reference to the Jazzmatazz project, Guru told Pete Lewis of <i><a href="/wiki/Blues_%26_Soul" title="Blues &amp; Soul">Blues &amp; Soul</a></i>: "Back around '93—when I first came up with the Jazzmatazz concept—I was noticing how a lot of cats were digging in the crates and sampling jazz breaks to make hip hop records. But while I thought that was cool, I wanted to take it to the next level and actually create a new genre by getting the actual dudes we were sampling into the studio to jam over hip hop beats with some of the top vocalists of the time. You know, the whole thing was experimental, but I knew it was an idea that would spawn some historic music."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1994, Guru appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hot_Organization" title="Red Hot Organization">Red Hot Organization</a>'s compilation album <i><a href="/wiki/Stolen_Moments:_Red_Hot_%2B_Cool" title="Stolen Moments: Red Hot + Cool">Stolen Moments: Red Hot + Cool</a></i>. The album, meant to raise awareness and funds in support of the AIDS epidemic in relation to the African American community, was heralded as the album of the year by <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>Time</i> magazine</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Gangstarr-03-mika.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Gangstarr-03-mika.jpg/220px-Gangstarr-03-mika.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Gangstarr-03-mika.jpg/330px-Gangstarr-03-mika.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Gangstarr-03-mika.jpg/440px-Gangstarr-03-mika.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1354" data-file-height="894" /></a><figcaption>Guru with Gang Starr, Germany, 1999</figcaption></figure>
<p>Guru's first solo album not a part the Jazzmatazz series, <i><a href="/wiki/Baldhead_Slick_%26_da_Click" title="Baldhead Slick &amp; da Click">Baldhead Slick &amp; da Click</a></i>, was released in 2001 to poor reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The album reached #22 on the Billboard R&amp;B/Hip Hop album charts. <i>Version 7.0: The Street Scriptures</i>, was released in 2005 on Guru's own record label, 7 Grand Records. The album was produced by labelmate Solar. It reached #54 on the Billboard R&amp;B albums charts and received mixed reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic3_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic3-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Guru's final releases were the fourth installment in the <i>Jazzmatazz</i> series, released in June 2007; and <i>Guru 8.0: Lost And Found</i>, released May 19, 2009 (also in collaboration with Solar). A final Gang Starr album, <i><a href="/wiki/One_of_the_Best_Yet" title="One of the Best Yet">One of the Best Yet</a></i>, was released in 2019.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guru_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On February 28, 2010, Guru went into <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a> and, following surgery, fell into a <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It was claimed that Guru had briefly awakened from his coma<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> but died on April 19, 2010, at the age of 48, from <a href="/wiki/Multiple_myeloma" title="Multiple myeloma">multiple myeloma</a>, a form of blood cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Guru was survived by his parents, three siblings, and a son named Keith Casim.<sup id="cite_ref-phoenix_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phoenix-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> His production partner, Solar, claimed that Guru had momentarily awakened from his coma to compose a letter to the public,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> although DJ Premier and members of Guru's family stated that he never regained consciousness.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Guru's family claimed that Solar had prevented them from having contact with Guru during his illness just before his death;<sup id="cite_ref-nme_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nme-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> the validity of the deathbed letter was consequently challenged by Guru's family.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In an interview on Conspiracy Worldwide Radio, Solar claimed that he was protective of Guru, and everything he had said was true. This interview was met by extreme emotion from the hip hop community and did little to clear the controversy surrounding his actions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>DJ Premier produced a tribute mix to Guru<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and has released a public letter<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> along with Guru's sister Patricia Elam.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Harry J. Elam, an older brother, wrote a personal memoir in remembrance published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> on April 23, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The Elam family had a Guru tribute website set up where visitors were able to view tributes and sign a memorial page.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Guru's nephew Justin Nicholas-Elam Ruff made a 16-minute documentary in which he narrated the story of his late uncle.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/2011_Grammy_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 Grammy Awards">2011 Grammy Awards</a>, Guru's name was not mentioned in the annual retrospective of musicians who had died since the 2010 awards. On April 21, 2011, Revive Da Live Big Band held a tribute show for Guru at Le Poisson Rouge in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The show paid homage to Guru's Jazzmatazz series and featured a full jazz band tribute, with all proceeds going towards the Elam family. During the concert, <a href="/wiki/Babygrande_Records" title="Babygrande Records">Babygrande Records</a> donated $5000 to Guru's son, K.C. Elam.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guru_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The French city of <a href="/wiki/Montpellier" title="Montpellier">Montpellier</a> named a small street "Allée Guru" after the rapper, citing his influence on both hip hop and jazz.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
