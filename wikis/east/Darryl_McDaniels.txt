<p><b>Darryl Matthews McDaniels</b> (born May 31, 1964), better known by his stage name <b>DMC</b>, is an American rapper. He is a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Run-DMC" title="Run-DMC">Run-DMC</a>, and is considered one of the pioneers of <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> culture.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darryl_McDaniels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McDaniels grew up in <a href="/wiki/Hollis,_Queens" title="Hollis, Queens">Hollis, Queens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EDM_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EDM-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VH1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VH1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was born to an unwed mother who surrendered him to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Foundling" title="New York Foundling">New York Foundling</a> home.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> He was a ward of the Foundling, in foster care, until placed with the McDaniels and eventually adopted by them. They raised him as a <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He attended <a href="/wiki/Rice_High_School_(Manhattan)" title="Rice High School (Manhattan)">Rice High School</a><sup id="cite_ref-HistoryMakers_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryMakers-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> in Manhattan and later enrolled in <a href="/wiki/Saint_John%27s_University,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint John&#39;s University, New York">St. John's University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Queens,_NY" class="mw-redirect" title="Queens, NY">Queens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-angel_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-angel-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>McDaniels first became interested in hip hop music after listening to recordings of <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Flash_and_the_Furious_Five" title="Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five">Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five</a>. In 1978, McDaniels taught himself to DJ in the basement of his parents' home, using <a href="/wiki/Turntablism" title="Turntablism">turntables</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Mixing_console" title="Mixing console">mixer</a> that he bought with his older brother, Alford, after having a comic book sale in their neighborhood.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> During this period he adopted the stage name "<b>Grandmaster Get High</b>".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darryl_McDaniels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Later that year, McDaniels sold his DJ equipment, after his friend <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Simmons" title="Joseph Simmons">Joseph "Run" Simmons</a> acquired his own turntables and mixer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> After <a href="/wiki/Jam-Master_Jay" class="mw-redirect" title="Jam-Master Jay">Jam-Master Jay</a> – who had a reputation as the best young DJ in <a href="/wiki/Hollis,_Queens" title="Hollis, Queens">Hollis</a> – joined the group, Run encouraged McDaniels to rap rather than DJ. Gradually, McDaniels came to prefer rapping to mixing records, and adopted the nickname of "Easy D". In 1981, he dropped the "Easy D" moniker in favor of "DMcD", the way he signed his work in school, and then to the shorter "D.M.C.". This new nickname alternately stood for "Devastating Mic Control"<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> or "Darryl Mac", his nickname since childhood as referenced in the lyrics of the song "King of Rock".
</p><p>In 1984, the trio released their <a href="/wiki/Run-D.M.C._(album)" title="Run-D.M.C. (album)">self-titled</a> debut album and became very successful in the hip-hop industry. The group's success continued to grow and reached its peak with their third album <i><a href="/wiki/Raising_Hell_(album)" title="Raising Hell (album)">Raising Hell</a></i>. The album went to No.&#160;6 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> and No.&#160;1 on the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart, making Run-D.M.C. the most popular hip-hop group at the time. During this time, McDaniels began to build a reputation as a heavy drinker. He was known to drink up to eight 40-ounce bottles of <a href="/wiki/Malt_liquor" title="Malt liquor">malt liquor</a> a day and was arrested twice for <a href="/wiki/Public_intoxication" title="Public intoxication">public intoxication</a> and <a href="/wiki/Driving_while_intoxicated" class="mw-redirect" title="Driving while intoxicated">driving while intoxicated</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VH1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VH1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1987 Run-D.M.C. wrote "<a href="/wiki/Christmas_in_Hollis" title="Christmas in Hollis">Christmas in Hollis</a>" for <i>A Very Special Christmas</i>. The music video for "Christmas in Hollis" was shot in Hollis, Queens. Run-D.M.C. filmed the video during their 1987 tour. DMC's mother made a guest appearance in the video.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1997, McDaniels began to develop a deep <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>. He became extremely unhappy with the rigorous routine of touring and performing, and with being away from his wife and newborn son. He began to rely heavily on <a href="/wiki/Prescription_drug" title="Prescription drug">prescription drugs</a> and alcohol to ease the pain. While on tour, McDaniels noticed his voice was giving out. He was later diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Spasmodic_dysphonia" title="Spasmodic dysphonia">spasmodic dysphonia</a>, a vocal disorder which causes involuntary spasms of the <a href="/wiki/Larynx" title="Larynx">larynx</a> muscles. He believes it was caused by the aggressive way in which he performs his lyrics compounded with the years of heavy drinking.<sup id="cite_ref-VH1_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VH1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<figure class="mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:D.M.C._2011-12-11_02.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/D.M.C._2011-12-11_02.jpg/250px-D.M.C._2011-12-11_02.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="188" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/D.M.C._2011-12-11_02.jpg/375px-D.M.C._2011-12-11_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/D.M.C._2011-12-11_02.jpg/500px-D.M.C._2011-12-11_02.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a><figcaption>McDaniels performing with his backing band in 2011</figcaption></figure>
<p>Meanwhile, McDaniels began to have creative differences with his bandmates in Run-D.M.C., which by then, was well past its prime as a commercially successful hip-hop group. A longtime fan of artists such as <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harry_Chapin" title="Harry Chapin">Harry Chapin</a>, McDaniels wanted to move towards a slower, softer sound which suited his now troubled voice. Run wanted to continue with the aggressive, <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a>-edged, sound that the group was known for. These disagreements caused McDaniels to sit out most of the recording of <i><a href="/wiki/Crown_Royal_(album)" title="Crown Royal (album)">Crown Royal</a></i> (2001).<sup id="cite_ref-VH1_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VH1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-angel_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-angel-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He appeared on only three songs.
</p><p>Feeling depressed and suicidal, McDaniels heard fellow adoptee <a href="/wiki/Sarah_McLachlan" title="Sarah McLachlan">Sarah McLachlan</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Angel_(Sarah_McLachlan_song)" title="Angel (Sarah McLachlan song)">Angel</a>" (1997) on the radio. The song touched McDaniels so deeply that it inspired him to reassess his life and career. He credits McLachlan and her album <i><a href="/wiki/Surfacing_(album)" title="Surfacing (album)">Surfacing</a></i> (on which "Angel" appeared) with saving his life.<sup id="cite_ref-VH1_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VH1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-angel_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-angel-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> With a new outlook on life, McDaniels decided to write his autobiography. While researching his early years, his mother, Bannah, revealed a shocking secret: Darryl had been placed for adoption when he was three months old. According to Bannah, his birth mother was a woman of <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican</a> descent named Bernada Lovelace. He also learned that he was born in <a href="/wiki/Harlem,_Manhattan" class="mw-redirect" title="Harlem, Manhattan">Harlem, Manhattan</a>, not <a href="/wiki/Hollis,_Queens" title="Hollis, Queens">Hollis, Queens</a>, as he had always believed. Even as a child, McDaniels knew he did not look like the rest of his family, and with the revelation, he finally understood why. The news inspired him to search for his birth mother. He began working with the <a href="/wiki/VH1" title="VH1">VH1</a> network on a documentary chronicling his quest.<sup id="cite_ref-VH1_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VH1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> His autobiography, <i>King of Rock: Respect, Responsibility, and My Life with Run-DMC</i>, was released in January 2001.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In February 2006, VH1 premiered the documentary titled <i>DMC: My Adoption Journey</i>. The program ends with McDaniels reuniting with his birth mother, who turned out to be named Berncenia and despite previous beliefs, was not, in fact, of Dominican descent. He thanks her for her choice because had he not been placed for adoption, <a href="/wiki/Run-D.M.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Run-D.M.C.">Run-D.M.C.</a> would have never existed. In March 2006, McDaniels released his solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Checks_Thugs_and_Rock_N_Roll" class="mw-redirect" title="Checks Thugs and Rock N Roll">Checks Thugs and Rock N Roll</a></i>. Produced and Music Directed by <a href="/wiki/Romeo_Antonio" title="Romeo Antonio">Romeo Antonio</a>. The first single, "Just Like Me",<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> features an interpolation of Harry Chapin's "<a href="/wiki/Cat%27s_in_the_Cradle" title="Cat&#39;s in the Cradle">Cat's in the Cradle</a>" (1974) performed by McDaniels' musical savior, Sarah McLachlan. During a recording session, McLachlan revealed to McDaniels that she, too, had been adopted.<sup id="cite_ref-VH1_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VH1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-angel_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-angel-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>McDaniels also collaborated with Adoptee Zara Philips on "I'm Legit."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He testified before the New Jersey State Legislature in support of legislation to restore adopted adults' access to their original birth certificates. The legislation McDaniels supported was signed by Governor Chris Christie and became effective on January 1, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> As a New York born adoptee, however, McDaniels did not have access to his own original birth record---he  hired a private investigator to help find his birth family in New York.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>McDaniels had written the first draft of his autobiography before learning he was adopted and was working on a second solo album, working titled <i>The Next Level</i>. Three tracks off the new album have been released<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2014)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> ("Next Level", "Hip Hop", and "Beef Eater") and can be heard on his <a href="/wiki/Myspace" title="Myspace">Myspace</a> page.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2007, McDaniels joined <a href="/wiki/Aerosmith" title="Aerosmith">Aerosmith</a> on stage at the Hard Rock Calling festival in London, England to perform "Walk This Way".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>McDaniels is featured in the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Guitar_Hero:_Aerosmith" title="Guitar Hero: Aerosmith">Guitar Hero: Aerosmith</a></i> (2008) singing Run-D.M.C.'s singles "<a href="/wiki/King_of_Rock" title="King of Rock">King of Rock</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Walk_This_Way" title="Walk This Way">Walk This Way</a>". He is also an unlockable guitarist in the game. In the game's trailer, it is revealed that McDaniels' son plays <i><a href="/wiki/Guitar_Hero_(series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Guitar Hero (series)">Guitar Hero</a></i> for hours each day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 2009, McDaniels performed in <i><a href="/wiki/The_People_Speak_(film)" title="The People Speak (film)">The People Speak</a></i>, a documentary feature film that uses dramatic and musical performances of the letters, diaries, and speeches of everyday Americans, based on historian <a href="/wiki/Howard_Zinn" title="Howard Zinn">Howard Zinn</a>'s nonfiction book <i><a href="/wiki/A_People%27s_History_of_the_United_States" title="A People&#39;s History of the United States">A People's History of the United States</a></i> (1980).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>

In December 2010, McDaniels appeared with <a href="/wiki/Talib_Kweli" title="Talib Kweli">Talib Kweli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mix_Master_Mike" title="Mix Master Mike">Mix Master Mike</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ahmet_Zappa" title="Ahmet Zappa">Ahmet Zappa</a> on a cover of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Zappa" title="Frank Zappa">Frank Zappa</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Willie_the_Pimp" title="Willie the Pimp">Willie the Pimp</a>" for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Frank_Zappa_AAAFNRAAAA_Birthday_Bundle_2010" title="The Frank Zappa AAAFNRAAAA Birthday Bundle 2010">The Frank Zappa AAAFNRAAAA Birthday Bundle 2010</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, McDaniels joined forces with producer Wade Martin to open the record label IME Records.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>

<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:11.6.14DarrylMcDanielsByLuigiNovi9.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/11.6.14DarrylMcDanielsByLuigiNovi9.jpg/220px-11.6.14DarrylMcDanielsByLuigiNovi9.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="149" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/11.6.14DarrylMcDanielsByLuigiNovi9.jpg/330px-11.6.14DarrylMcDanielsByLuigiNovi9.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/11.6.14DarrylMcDanielsByLuigiNovi9.jpg/440px-11.6.14DarrylMcDanielsByLuigiNovi9.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2209" /></a><figcaption>McDaniels signs copies of his comic book, <i>DMC</i> #1, at a November 6, 2014 appearance at <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Comics" title="Midtown Comics">Midtown Comics</a> in Manhattan.</figcaption></figure>
<p>In 2014, McDaniels ventured into the comics industry with his own publishing imprint, Darryl Makes Comics. McDaniels explains his lifelong love of the medium thus:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Growing up a mild-mannered, Catholic school kid, all I did was go to school and read comic books. I was strictly a <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> head; <a href="/wiki/The_Avengers_(comic_book)" title="The Avengers (comic book)">Avengers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man" title="Iron Man">Iron Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Captain_America" title="Captain America">Captain America</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Hulk" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hulk">The Hulk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron_Fist_(comics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron Fist (comics)">Iron Fist</a>, you name it. I loved Marvel because it was the city; it was all New York. The same backdrop I was living in in this universe was in the <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Universe" title="Marvel Universe">Marvel Universe</a>. Comics did for me what hip hop did for me as I got older; it empowered me, inspired me and educated me. I learned about Nazis, space exploration, everything from comics.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Darryl Makes Comics' first book is <i>DMC</i>, a 90-page anthology <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a> set in 1985 that features McDaniels as a superhero who confronts both criminals and other superheroes whose recklessness threatens innocent lives.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The comic's version of DMC wears McDaniels' signature <a href="/wiki/Adidas" title="Adidas">Adidas</a> sneakers, fedora and <a href="/wiki/Rope_chain" title="Rope chain">rope chain</a>, along with an elongated turtleneck that masks his face. The book is written by McDaniels and <a href="/wiki/Damion_Scott" title="Damion Scott">Damion Scott</a>, and edited by Darryl Makes Comics' Editor-in-Chief, <a href="/wiki/Edgardo_Miranda-Rodriguez" title="Edgardo Miranda-Rodriguez">Edgardo Miranda-Rodriguez</a> and Senior Editor <a href="/wiki/Rigo_%22Riggs%22_Morales" class="mw-redirect" title="Rigo &quot;Riggs&quot; Morales">Rigo "Riggs" Morales</a>. Each chapter in the anthology is illustrated by a different artist, because, as McDaniels explained, " If DMC was really running around and bumped into four different people, they'd have four different descriptions of what he was like. We wanted each artist's work to relate to each character's view of DMC. If you saw him, you might say, "He came out of a spaceship and had all these things flying around him!" But then another dude is like, "No! He had a sword and shield!" We thought, if so many people saw something different in DMC, we'd have to have different artwork to represent their opinions on him or their interpretation on him." In addition, graffiti writers such as MARE 139 were hired to give the shots of 1985 New York City graffiti a sense of authenticity. The book features an introduction by <a href="/wiki/Greg_Pak" title="Greg Pak">Greg Pak</a>, a cover by <a href="/wiki/Sal_Buscema" title="Sal Buscema">Sal Buscema</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Wiacek" title="Bob Wiacek">Bob Wiacek</a> and interior pinups by <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Pacheco" title="Carlos Pacheco">Carlos Pacheco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Burnham" title="Chris Burnham">Chris Burnham</a>, <a href="/wiki/ChrisCross" title="ChrisCross">ChrisCross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dexter_Vines" title="Dexter Vines">Dexter Vines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shelby_Robertson" title="Shelby Robertson">Shelby Robertson</a>, some of which are homages to iconic comics covers that influenced the creative staff as children. <i>DMC</i> debuted at the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Comic_Con" title="New York Comic Con">New York Comic Con</a> October 9–12, and was subsequently released in comics shops October 29.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The book received a four out of five stars rating by Tony Guerrero of Comic Vine, who lauded the charm and authenticity of the art.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of January 2015, McDaniels was working with the band <a href="/wiki/Generation_Kill_(band)" title="Generation Kill (band)">Generation Kill</a> on a project entitled <i>DMC Generation Kill</i>, to be produced by former <a href="/wiki/Guns_N%27_Roses" title="Guns N&#39; Roses">Guns N' Roses</a> guitarist <a href="/wiki/Ron_%22Bumblefoot%22_Thal" title="Ron &quot;Bumblefoot&quot; Thal">Ron "Bumblefoot" Thal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Charity_work">Charity work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darryl_McDaniels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Charity work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>The Felix Organization</b>
</p><p>In 2006, McDaniels and Sheila Jaffe, a fellow adoptee and Emmy award-winning casting director, co-founded The Felix Organization.
</p><p>Since its inception, The Felix Organization has served more than 10,000 children in the foster care system. Its flagship program, Camp Felix, is an annual sleepaway summer camp in Putnam Valley, New York. Additionally, The Felix Organization sponsors two teen camps on the East Coast. Camp Felix West is for Los Angeles-based youth in foster care.
</p><p><b>Other charity work</b>
</p><p>In September 2006, McDaniels received the Congressional <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Coalition_on_Adoption_Institute#Angels_in_Adoption" title="Congressional Coalition on Adoption Institute">Angels in Adoption</a> award for his work with children in foster care and promotion of adoption. He sits on the Board of Directors of Children's Rights, a national watchdog organization that reforms failing child welfare systems.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darryl_McDaniels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McDaniels has been a resident of <a href="/wiki/Wayne,_New_Jersey" title="Wayne, New Jersey">Wayne, New Jersey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> He has been very frank about his battles with depression, including an appearance on <i><a href="/wiki/Live_From_the_Barrage" title="Live From the Barrage">Live From the Barrage</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> speaking at length about it. He also has written pieces in <i><a href="/wiki/Men%27s_Health" title="Men&#39;s Health">Men's Health</a></i> and <i>BlackDoctor</i>, where he talked about his memoir, <i>Ten Ways Not to Commit Suicide</i> (Amistad, 2017).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
