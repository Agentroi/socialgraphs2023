<p><b>Seamus Padraic Ryan</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (born July 24, 1978), better known by his stage name <b>Esoteric</b>, is a Boston-based <a href="/wiki/Underground_hip-hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Underground hip-hop">underground hip-hop</a> artist. Esoteric is half of the hip hop duo <a href="/wiki/7L_%26_Esoteric" title="7L &amp; Esoteric">7L &amp; Esoteric</a>, a third of <a href="/wiki/Czarface" title="Czarface">Czarface</a> and is a member of underground hip-hop collectives <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Pharaohs" title="Army of the Pharaohs">Army of the Pharaohs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Demigodz" title="Demigodz">Demigodz</a>. He started his solo career in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esoteric_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1993-1996:_Early_career_and_.227L_.26_Esoteric.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1993-1996:_Early_career_and_&quot;7L_&amp;_Esoteric&quot;">1993-1996: Early career and "7L &amp; Esoteric"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esoteric_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1993-1996: Early career and &quot;7L &amp; Esoteric&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The duo formed in 1993 when Esoteric DJed a <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> show at a college radio station, WMWM 91.7FM north of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>. 7L, a DJ and producer who listened to the show, contacted Esoteric in the interest of collaborating. The two found they shared a common love of <a href="/wiki/The_golden_age_of_hip-hop" class="mw-redirect" title="The golden age of hip-hop">the golden age of hip-hop</a> and decided to form a group.
</p><p>After performing for some time in the Boston/Cambridge underground, the duo released their first single in 1996 as God Complex with MC Karma. The b-side "Secret Wars" gained a lot of attention as Esoteric paid tribute to the heroes of <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> in rhyme form. This led to the release of the <i>Rebel Alliance</i> LP, featuring acts such as Virtuoso, <a href="/wiki/Mr._Lif" title="Mr. Lif">Mr. Lif</a>, and Force Five. Also at this time the two dropped the God Complex moniker becoming simply 7L &amp; Esoteric.
</p>
<h3><span id="1997-1999:_Rebel_Alliance_.26_Army_of_the_Pharaohs"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1997-1999:_Rebel_Alliance_&amp;_Army_of_the_Pharaohs">1997-1999: <i>Rebel Alliance</i> &amp; <i>Army of the Pharaohs</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esoteric_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1997-1999: Rebel Alliance &amp; Army of the Pharaohs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soon after, 7L &amp; Esoteric put out their first 12" with re-mastered versions of their <i><a href="/wiki/Rebel_Alliance" title="Rebel Alliance">Rebel Alliance</a></i> songs "Protocol" and "Be Alert." "Be Alert", the B-side on the record, began to receive significant radio play, due to its unique sampling of the <a href="/wiki/Transformers" title="Transformers">Transformers</a>' TV theme song. The track, which was produced by Beyonder, became very popular in the underground scene, leading the duo to several European tours, major label interest, and becoming one of the first New England hip-hop acts to appear on the world-famous Stretch &amp; Bobbito WKCR radio show in NYC and get spins on the Wake Up Show with <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sway_%26_King_Tech" title="Sway &amp; King Tech">Sway &amp; King Tech</a>. <a href="/wiki/URB_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="URB Magazine">URB Magazine</a> had chosen 7L &amp; Esoteric for their "Next 100" issue in 1997.
</p><p>The duo then dropped the "Def Rhymes" single though Landspeed Distribution which earned them press in <a href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Source (magazine)">the Source</a>. "Def Rhymes" was also featured on the EP, <i>Speaking Real Words</i>, released in 1999. The album also featured <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a> member <a href="/wiki/Inspectah_Deck" title="Inspectah Deck">Inspectah Deck</a> on the title track. During promotion for the 12", Esoteric had spent time in Philadelphia and formed the supergroup <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Pharaohs" title="Army of the Pharaohs">Army of the Pharaohs</a> with <a href="/wiki/Jedi_Mind_Tricks" title="Jedi Mind Tricks">Jedi Mind Tricks</a> frontman <a href="/wiki/Vinnie_Paz" title="Vinnie Paz">Vinnie Paz</a>. Esoteric would appear three times on Jedi Mind Tricks breakthrough album <a href="/wiki/Violent_By_Design" class="mw-redirect" title="Violent By Design">Violent By Design</a> in 2000.
</p>
<h3><span id="2000-2005:_The_Soul_Purpose_.26_DC2:_Bars_of_Death"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000-2005:_The_Soul_Purpose_&amp;_DC2:_Bars_of_Death">2000-2005: <i>The Soul Purpose</i> &amp; <i>DC2: Bars of Death</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esoteric_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000-2005: The Soul Purpose &amp; DC2: Bars of Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>7L &amp; Esoteric released their debut LP <i><a href="/wiki/The_Soul_Purpose" title="The Soul Purpose">The Soul Purpose</a></i> in the summer of 2001. The single, "Call Me ES" charted at #83 on Billboard hip-hop singles.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The album received the Boston Music Award for "Best Hip-Hop Album,"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and led to the duo headlining tours of the United States and Europe. Upon their return, they recorded and released their second LP in the fall of 2002 titled <i><a href="/wiki/Dangerous_Connection" title="Dangerous Connection">Dangerous Connection</a></i> featuring Jedi Mind Tricks' Stoupe, Vinnie Paz, J-Live, Apathy, and others.
</p><p>After taking a break from recording the duo released their third LP <i><a href="/wiki/DC2:_Bars_of_Death" title="DC2: Bars of Death">DC2: Bars of Death</a></i> in the spring of 2004 with <a href="/wiki/Babygrande_Records" title="Babygrande Records">Babygrande Records</a>. The album received overall positive reviews and was lauded by long-time fans.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 2006, Esoteric compiled an album entitled "Moment of Rarities" which was also released with Babygrande. This CD was a collection of previously unreleased tracks. Esoteric featured on <a href="/wiki/John_Cena" title="John Cena">John Cena</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/You_Can%27t_See_Me" title="You Can&#39;t See Me">You Can't See Me</a></i> on the track "BeanTown".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006-2007:_Solo_career_start">2006-2007: Solo career start</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esoteric_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2006-2007: Solo career start">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/A_New_Dope" title="A New Dope">A New Dope</a> Esoteric embarked on a solo venture. In October 2007 he presented his debut <a href="/w/index.php?title=Egoclapper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Egoclapper (page does not exist)">Egoclapper</a> on his own label <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fly_Casual_Records,&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fly Casual Records, (page does not exist)">Fly Casual Records,</a> in which he took both the rhyming and production duties. A Limited Edition version was also available, packaged with bonus CD <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esoteric_Vs_Gary_Numan:_Pterodactyl_Tubeway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Esoteric Vs Gary Numan: Pterodactyl Tubeway (page does not exist)">Esoteric Vs Gary Numan: Pterodactyl Tubeway</a></i>. This featured Esoteric both rhyming and producing beats heavily sampling 1980s Electro icon <a href="/wiki/Gary_Numan" title="Gary Numan">Gary Numan</a>.
</p><p>He has also sparked some comedic video entertainment with his <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> series <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pterovision&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pterovision (page does not exist)">Pterovision</a> which served as a bi-weekly broadcast to the <a href="/wiki/7LES" class="mw-redirect" title="7LES">7LES</a> fan base. The series was initially released as a buildup to the release of Egoclapper.
</p>
<h3><span id="2008-2010:_Esoteric_vs._Japan.2C_Prison_Planet_and_Saving_Seamus_Ryan"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2008-2010:_Esoteric_vs._Japan,_Prison_Planet_and_Saving_Seamus_Ryan">2008-2010: <i>Esoteric vs. Japan</i>, <i>Prison Planet</i> and <i>Saving Seamus Ryan</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esoteric_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2008-2010: Esoteric vs. Japan, Prison Planet and Saving Seamus Ryan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 8, 2008, Esoteric released another solo venture, entitled <i>Esoteric vs. Japan - Pterodactyl Takes Tokyo</i>. This release featured Esoteric rhyming over beats sampled from Japanese music, TV and films, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Godzilla_(1954_film)" title="Godzilla (1954 film)">Godzilla</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gaiking" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaiking">Gaiking</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Force_Five" title="Force Five">Force Five</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Shogun_Warriors_(video_game)" title="Shogun Warriors (video game)">Shogun Warriors</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rodan_(film)" title="Rodan (film)">Rodan</a></i>.
</p><p>Esoteric's next collaboration was with rapper <a href="/wiki/Tha_Trademarc" title="Tha Trademarc">Tha Trademarc</a> and producer <a href="/w/index.php?title=DC_the_Midi_Alien&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="DC the Midi Alien (page does not exist)">DC the Midi Alien</a>. Together they form the group East Coast Avengers. Their debut album, <i>Prison Planet</i>, was released October 7, 2008, on underground rap label <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brick_Records&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brick Records (page does not exist)">Brick Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The album's lead single, "Kill Bill O'Reilly," quickly gained notoriety for its abrasive lyrics, and the metaphorical murder of the right wing American television/radio host, <a href="/wiki/Bill_O%27Reilly_(political_commentator)" title="Bill O&#39;Reilly (political commentator)">Bill O'Reilly</a>, and former guest commentator, <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Malkin" title="Michelle Malkin">Michelle Malkin</a>. Following responses by Malkin another single was released, "Dear Michelle". <a href="/wiki/MySpace" class="mw-redirect" title="MySpace">MySpace</a>, owned by O'Reilly's employers <a href="/wiki/Fox_Entertainment_Group" title="Fox Entertainment Group">Fox</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> has censored the songs and taken down related graphics as they were deemed too offensive. Keith Olbermann had featured their track on <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">MSNBC</a> during his show <i><a href="/wiki/Countdown_with_Keith_Olbermann" title="Countdown with Keith Olbermann">Countdown</a></i> as the presidential race was heating up.
</p><p>On February 10, 2009, Esoteric released his first solo instrumental album, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serve_or_Suffer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Serve or Suffer (page does not exist)">Serve or Suffer</a></i> which was dedicated to Marvel Comics' unsung artist <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a> and was featured in the Jack Kirby Museum and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Comic_Con" title="New York Comic Con">New York Comic Con</a>.
</p><p>Esoteric released <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saving_Seamus_Ryan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Saving Seamus Ryan (page does not exist)">Saving Seamus Ryan</a> on September 22, 2009, which featured dialogue created from sampling Harrison Ford's voice speaking to him throughout the entire album to tell a story. The CD came with a "short story" bundled in hardcover book with 50 pages. This is basically the lyrics with some extra content. The story goes from saying goodbye to his dying dog Max, in the first track (Studio Time/Goodbye) to Esoteric's character getting shot and robbed for an engagement ring. As the plot thickens, he joins up with Indie Solo, an odd character who speaks like Harrison Ford and guides Esoteric's career as the two hunt down the jewel thief, fictional character Benny Macko. The story introduces his real life dog Logan, who ultimately inspires him to see the good in the world, an example of art imitating life. The twist at the end of the book/album is that Indie Solo was indeed a figment of Esoteric's imagination.
</p>
<h3><span id="2010-2013:_Boston_Pharaoh.2C_The_Unholy_Terror_and_Killmatic"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2010-2013:_Boston_Pharaoh,_The_Unholy_Terror_and_Killmatic">2010-2013: <i>Boston Pharaoh</i>, <i>The Unholy Terror</i> and <i>Killmatic</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esoteric_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2010-2013: Boston Pharaoh, The Unholy Terror and Killmatic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, Esoteric released the album <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boston_Pharaoh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Boston Pharaoh (page does not exist)">Boston Pharaoh</a></i> to support his European tour with the <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Pharaohs" title="Army of the Pharaohs">Army of the Pharaohs</a>. Limited to 500 physical copies, and available only on the European tour, it is still highly sought after and considered very rare. The album was later released on <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a> and Amazon for digital release. The lone single from the album is called "Palin / Vick" and draws comparisons to <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Palin" title="Sarah Palin">Sarah Palin</a> and NFL star <a href="/wiki/Michael_Vick" title="Michael Vick">Michael Vick</a>, citing their affinity for animal cruelty. The song condemns dog-fighting and wolf-hunting, yet also suggests that racism is alive and well in America due to the media's varying portrayal of Palin and Vick, both animal murderers, yet one went to jail for killing dogs and the other repeatedly shot wolves from an airplane and still ran for office.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013-Present:_Czarface">2013-Present: <i>Czarface</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esoteric_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2013-Present: Czarface">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Czarface" title="Czarface">Czarface</a></b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> <a href="/wiki/Supergroup_(music)" title="Supergroup (music)">supergroup</a> formed in 2013 by <a href="/wiki/Underground_hip_hop" title="Underground hip hop">underground hip hop</a> duo <a href="/wiki/7L_%26_Esoteric" title="7L &amp; Esoteric">7L &amp; Esoteric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a> member <a href="/wiki/Inspectah_Deck" title="Inspectah Deck">Inspectah Deck</a>. They released their acclaimed debut studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Czarface_(album)" title="Czarface (album)">Czarface</a></i>, February 19, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-metacritic_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
The album was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Every_Hero_Needs_a_Villain" title="Every Hero Needs a Villain">Every Hero Needs a Villain</a></i> in 2015 and <i><a href="/wiki/A_Fistful_of_Peril" title="A Fistful of Peril">A Fistful of Peril</a></i> in 2016, both of which were also praised by critics.<sup id="cite_ref-metacritic1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-the_diamondback_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-the_diamondback-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 2018, Czarface released a collaborative album with <a href="/wiki/MF_Doom" title="MF Doom">MF DOOM</a> titled "Czarface vs Metalface".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
