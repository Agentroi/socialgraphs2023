<p><b>Anthony Wheaton</b>, professionally known by his stage name <b>Sir Jinx</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> producer and rapper from Los Angeles. He is a cousin of multi-platinum producer <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>. He began his career as a member of the <a href="/wiki/C.I.A._(band)" title="C.I.A. (band)">C.I.A.</a> in the mid-80s with <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a> and <a href="/wiki/K-Dee" title="K-Dee">Kid Disaster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He produced tracks for the likes of <a href="/wiki/Dazzie_Dee" title="Dazzie Dee">Dazzie Dee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westside_Connection" title="Westside Connection">Westside Connection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yo-Yo_(rapper)" title="Yo-Yo (rapper)">Yo-Yo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tone_Loc" title="Tone Loc">Tone Loc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a>, <a href="/wiki/CeCe_Peniston" title="CeCe Peniston">CeCe Peniston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xzibit" title="Xzibit">Xzibit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a> among others, and also remixed songs for <a href="/wiki/Rage_Against_the_Machine" title="Rage Against the Machine">Rage Against the Machine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy" title="Public Enemy">Public Enemy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toni_Braxton" title="Toni Braxton">Toni Braxton</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Jinx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Jinx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anthony "Sir Jinx" Wheaton began his career in the mid-80s as a part of hip hop duo the Stereo Crew with fellow rapper <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>. They were signed with <a href="/wiki/Epic_Records" title="Epic Records">Epic Records</a> and released their debut single "She's a Skag" in 1986, which was produced by Wheaton's cousin <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and Dre's <a href="/wiki/World_Class_Wreckin%27_Cru" title="World Class Wreckin&#39; Cru">World Class Wreckin' Cru</a> bandmate <a href="/wiki/Alonzo_Williams" title="Alonzo Williams">Alonzo Williams</a>. Poor single sales caused them to be soon dropped off of the label. Joined by fellow rapper <a href="/wiki/K-Dee" title="K-Dee">Kid Disaster</a>, they were picked by Kru-Cut Records and changed the group's name to <a href="/wiki/C.I.A._(band)" title="C.I.A. (band)">C.I.A.</a> In 1987 the trio released their single "<a href="/wiki/My_Posse" title="My Posse">My Posse</a>" and appeared on the cover of <i><a href="/wiki/N.W.A._and_the_Posse" title="N.W.A. and the Posse">N.W.A. and the Posse</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Cube and Dre have been focused on forming <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>, which led to disband the C.I.A. Wheaton produced fellow rapper <a href="/wiki/Dazzie_Dee" title="Dazzie Dee">Dazzie Dee</a>'s first extended play <i>Turn It Loose</i>, released in 1989.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Jinx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Cube left N.W.A and <a href="/wiki/Ruthless_Records" title="Ruthless Records">Ruthless Records</a> over a financial dispute, all the former C.I.A. members along with several other artists formed the Lench Mob posse on Cube's founded <a href="/wiki/Lench_Mob_Records" title="Lench Mob Records">Lench Mob Records</a> label. Wheaton and Cube in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Chilly_Chill" title="Chilly Chill">Chilly Chill</a> and New York-based <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_production" title="Hip hop production">hip hop production</a> team <a href="/wiki/The_Bomb_Squad" title="The Bomb Squad">The Bomb Squad</a> produced Ice Cube's debut solo studio album <i><a href="/wiki/AmeriKKKa%27s_Most_Wanted" title="AmeriKKKa&#39;s Most Wanted">AmeriKKKa's Most Wanted</a></i> and the follow-up <i><a href="/wiki/Kill_at_Will" title="Kill at Will">Kill at Will</a></i> EP, both released in 1990. Both these projects were certified Platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>. Same year Wheaton produced "Ain't Nothin' but a Word to Me", the only song from <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Short_Dog%27s_in_the_House" title="Short Dog&#39;s in the House">Short Dog's in the House</a> album that has a featured <a href="/wiki/Guest_appearance" title="Guest appearance">guest appearance</a> (by Ice Cube).
</p><p>In 1991, Wheaton produced a significant number of songs on <a href="/wiki/Yo-Yo_(rapper)" title="Yo-Yo (rapper)">Yo-Yo</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Make_Way_for_the_Motherlode" title="Make Way for the Motherlode">Make Way for the Motherlode</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/WC_and_the_Maad_Circle" title="WC and the Maad Circle">WC and the Maad Circle</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ain%27t_a_Damn_Thang_Changed" title="Ain&#39;t a Damn Thang Changed">Ain't a Damn Thang Changed</a></i>, and Ice Cube's <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Certificate_(album)" title="Death Certificate (album)">Death Certificate</a></i>, including NWA <a href="/wiki/Diss_(music)" title="Diss (music)">diss track</a> "<a href="/wiki/No_Vaseline" title="No Vaseline">No Vaseline</a>". He also produced "How to Survive in South Central", a Cube's song from <i><a href="/wiki/Boyz_n_the_Hood" title="Boyz n the Hood">Boyz n the Hood</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Boyz_n_the_Hood_(soundtrack)" title="Boyz n the Hood (soundtrack)">soundtrack</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tone_L%C5%8Dc" class="mw-redirect" title="Tone Lōc">Tone Lōc</a> "I Adore You". Continuing into 1992 with Cube's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Predator_(Ice_Cube_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Predator (Ice Cube album)">The Predator</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Yo-Yo_(rapper)" title="Yo-Yo (rapper)">Yo-Yo</a>'s 1992 <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Pearl_(Yo-Yo_album)" title="Black Pearl (Yo-Yo album)">Black Pearl</a></i>, Wheaton stepped onto <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_hip_hop" title="East Coast hip hop">East Coast hip hop</a> scene producing the majority of <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">Kool G Rap</a>'s final studio album with DJ Polo <i><a href="/wiki/Live_and_Let_Die_(album)" title="Live and Let Die (album)">Live and Let Die</a></i>, and also managed to provide additional production on two tracks for <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)">George Clinton</a>'s son Trey Lewd's <i>Drop the Line</i>, and to produce the title track for <i><a href="/wiki/Trespass_(soundtrack)" title="Trespass (soundtrack)">Music from the Motion Picture Trespass</a></i>. Sir Jinx created remixes for <a href="/wiki/Rap_rock" title="Rap rock">rap-rock</a> outfits such as <a href="/wiki/Rage_Against_the_Machine" title="Rage Against the Machine">Rage Against the Machine</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_Radio" title="Guerrilla Radio">Guerrilla Radio</a> (Sir Jinx Edit)", "<a href="/wiki/Bullet_in_the_Head_(song)" title="Bullet in the Head (song)">Bullet in the Head</a> (Sir Jinx Remix)", and the unreleased "<a href="/wiki/Bombtrack" title="Bombtrack">Bombtrack</a> (Remix)", all of which were recorded in 1992.
</p><p>After producing a couple of tracks on Ice Cube's 1993 album <i><a href="/wiki/Lethal_Injection_(album)" title="Lethal Injection (album)">Lethal Injection</a></i>, Wheaton stopped working with Cube and his group <a href="/wiki/Da_Lench_Mob" title="Da Lench Mob">Da Lench Mob</a> due to their controversial direction.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He moved on to producing for R&amp;B-oriented solo acts such as <a href="/wiki/CeCe_Peniston" title="CeCe Peniston">CeCe Peniston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Levert" title="Gerald Levert">Gerald Levert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Hayes" title="Isaac Hayes">Isaac Hayes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teena_Marie" title="Teena Marie">Teena Marie</a>, and former <a href="/wiki/Bell_Biv_DeVoe" title="Bell Biv DeVoe">Bell Biv DeVoe</a> member <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Bell_(singer)" title="Ricky Bell (singer)">Ricky Bell</a>.
</p><p>Following a low-profile independent solo album in 1995, Sir Jinx returned to hip hop production on Gooch's 1997 album <i>A Lot on It</i> and <a href="/wiki/Xzibit" title="Xzibit">Xzibit</a>'s 1998 album <i><a href="/wiki/40_Dayz_%26_40_Nightz" title="40 Dayz &amp; 40 Nightz">40 Dayz &amp; 40 Nightz</a></i>. He also helped to produce skits on <a href="/wiki/Tash_(rapper)" title="Tash (rapper)">Tash</a>'s 1999 <i><a href="/wiki/Rap_Life" title="Rap Life">Rap Life</a></i> and helped on Xzibit's 2000 <i><a href="/wiki/Restless_(Xzibit_album)" title="Restless (Xzibit album)">Restless</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Jinx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wheaton provided production work on a song from comedian <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Griffin" title="Eddie Griffin">Eddie Griffin</a>'s 2003 film <i><a href="/wiki/Dysfunktional_Family" title="Dysfunktional Family">Dysfunktional Family</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Dysfunktional_Family_(soundtrack)" title="Dysfunktional Family (soundtrack)">soundtrack</a>. Same year he produced a couple of tracks on <a href="/wiki/The_Comrads" title="The Comrads">The Comrads</a> member Gangsta album <i>Penitentiary Chances</i>, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Posse_cut" title="Posse cut">posse cut</a> on <a href="/wiki/Westside_Connection" title="Westside Connection">Westside Connection</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Terrorist_Threats" title="Terrorist Threats">Terrorist Threats</a></i>.
</p><p>Outside of some minor production on <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a>'s 2004 album <i>Originals</i> and 2005 album <i><a href="/wiki/Against_the_Grain_(Kurupt_album)" title="Against the Grain (Kurupt album)">Against the Grain</a></i>, Xzibit's 2004 album <i><a href="/wiki/Weapons_of_Mass_Destruction_(album)" title="Weapons of Mass Destruction (album)">Weapons of Mass Destruction</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Ras_Kass" title="Ras Kass">Ras Kass</a>'s 2009 project <i>Quarterly</i>, Sir Jinx was relatively low-key during this period.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Jinx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, Wheaton produced a song for <a href="/wiki/Sadat_X" title="Sadat X">Sadat X</a>'s <i>Wild Cowboys II</i> album, as well as the track "Life in California" from Ice Cube's <i><a href="/wiki/I_Am_the_West" title="I Am the West">I Am the West</a></i> album, which marked the first time Sir Jinx and Cube had worked together on any new material together in several years. In an October 2010 interview for <a href="/wiki/HipHopDX" title="HipHopDX">HipHopDX</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Wheaton stated that he was going to help produce cousin Dr. Dre's <a href="/wiki/Development_hell" title="Development hell">long-awaited</a> release <i><a href="/wiki/Detox_(Dr._Dre_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Detox (Dr. Dre album)">Detox</a></i>. In spite of his relation to Dr. Dre, this is the first album the two have worked on together.
</p><p>Sir Jinx collaborated with Tri Star and Dat Boi Hop to form General Population. The group released their album <i>Sir Jinx Presents General Population: Rime Scene</i> in 2011 featuring guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Butch_Cassidy_(singer)" title="Butch Cassidy (singer)">Butch Cassidy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roscoe_(rapper)" title="Roscoe (rapper)">Roscoe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devin_the_Dude" title="Devin the Dude">Devin the Dude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jayo_Felony" title="Jayo Felony">Jayo Felony</a>, Kurupt, Ras Kass. The following year, Jinx produced the entire Tri Star's project <i>Trilogy</i>. Together with <a href="/wiki/Mike_%26_Keys" title="Mike &amp; Keys">Mike &amp; Keys</a>, Jinx produced debut <a href="/wiki/Serial_Killers_(musical_group)" title="Serial Killers (musical group)">Serial Killers</a> single "First 48". In 2016, he and Dre produced T.I.'s non-album single "Dope", which featured vocals by <a href="/wiki/Marsha_Ambrosius" title="Marsha Ambrosius">Marsha Ambrosius</a>.
</p>
