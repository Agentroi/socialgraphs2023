<p><b>Edwin Maximilian "Eddie" Hayes, Jr.</b> (born September 30, 1970), better known by his stage name <b>Aceyalone</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapper</a> from <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, United States. He is a member of <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_Fellowship" title="Freestyle Fellowship">Freestyle Fellowship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haiku_D%27Etat" class="mw-redirect" title="Haiku D&#39;Etat">Haiku D'Etat</a> and The A-Team. He is also a co-founder of <a href="/wiki/Project_Blowed" title="Project Blowed">Project Blowed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Aceyalone is best known for his role in evolving <a href="/wiki/Alternative_hip_hop" title="Alternative hip hop">left-field hip-hop</a> on the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast</a> at a time when the West Coast was dominated by <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Birchmeier_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birchmeier-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aceyalone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_Blowed_and_Freestyle_Fellowship">Project Blowed and Freestyle Fellowship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aceyalone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Project Blowed and Freestyle Fellowship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aceyalone emerged from the <a href="/wiki/Project_Blowed" title="Project Blowed">Project Blowed</a> collective, considered to be the longest-running open mic hip-hop workshop.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He began rapping as part of the group <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_Fellowship" title="Freestyle Fellowship">Freestyle Fellowship</a>, which consisted of Aceyalone, <a href="/wiki/Myka_9" title="Myka 9">Myka 9</a>, and Self Jupiter. Later, P.E.A.C.E. Freestyle Fellowship developed a reputation for influencing a style of fast double-time rap used by rappers like <a href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes" title="Busta Rhymes">Busta Rhymes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone_Thugs-n-Harmony" title="Bone Thugs-n-Harmony">Bone Thugs-n-Harmony</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Migos" title="Migos">Migos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weiss_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiss-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Aceyalone was part of Freestyle Fellowship releases of <i><a href="/wiki/To_Whom_It_May_Concern..." title="To Whom It May Concern...">To Whom It May Concern...</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Innercity_Griots" title="Innercity Griots">Innercity Griots</a></i> and a <a href="/wiki/Project_Blowed" title="Project Blowed">Project Blowed</a> compilation in 1994. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_projects">Solo projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aceyalone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solo projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aceyalone signed as a solo artist to <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a> after the Freestyle Fellowship failed to break into mainstream radio with <a href="/wiki/Island_Records" title="Island Records">Island Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weiss_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiss-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Aceyalone released his debut solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/All_Balls_Don%27t_Bounce" title="All Balls Don&#39;t Bounce">All Balls Don't Bounce</a></i>, in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He returned three years later with the dark concept album <i><a href="/wiki/A_Book_of_Human_Language" title="A Book of Human Language">A Book of Human Language</a></i>, which was a collaboration with producer Mumbles.<sup id="cite_ref-Pecoraro_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pecoraro-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> His third solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Accepted_Eclectic" title="Accepted Eclectic">Accepted Eclectic</a></i>, was released in 2001 and featured <a href="/wiki/Abstract_Rude" title="Abstract Rude">Abstract Rude</a> with production from <a href="/wiki/Evidence_(musician)" title="Evidence (musician)">Evidence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He released <i><a href="/wiki/Hip_Hop_and_the_World_We_Live_In" title="Hip Hop and the World We Live In">Hip Hop and the World We Live In</a></i> in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Aceyalone's next offering came a year later, and was titled <i><a href="/wiki/Love_%26_Hate_(Aceyalone_album)" title="Love &amp; Hate (Aceyalone album)">Love &amp; Hate</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The track “Find Out” was featured on the soundtrack to <i><a href="/wiki/You_Got_Served" title="You Got Served">You Got Served</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 2006, Aceyalone released <i><a href="/wiki/Magnificent_City" title="Magnificent City">Magnificent City</a></i>, a collaborative album with producer <a href="/wiki/RJD2" title="RJD2">RJD2</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> followed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Imperial_(album)" title="Grand Imperial (album)">Grand Imperial</a></i> mixtape.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Aceyalone frequently collaborates with producer Bionik, including on the 2007 release <i><a href="/wiki/Lightning_Strikes_(Aceyalone_album)" title="Lightning Strikes (Aceyalone album)">Lightning Strikes</a></i> and the 2009 release <i><a href="/wiki/Aceyalone_%26_the_Lonely_Ones" title="Aceyalone &amp; the Lonely Ones">Aceyalone &amp; the Lonely Ones</a></i>. Both albums explored different genres – <a href="/wiki/Dancehall" title="Dancehall">dancehall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doo-wop" title="Doo-wop">doo-wop</a>, respectively – as part of Aceyalone's goal of “exploring the world of music through hip hop.”<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Phil_Spector" title="Phil Spector">Phil Spector</a>-inspired <i>Aceyalone &amp; the Lonely Ones</i> followed.<sup id="cite_ref-ign_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Inspired by Spector's <a href="/wiki/Wall_of_Sound" title="Wall of Sound">Wall of Sound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bo_Diddley" title="Bo Diddley">Bo Diddley</a>, Aceyalone said: “I'm not from that era, but this is my ode to it. I'm just putting myself into that character as a showman and bandleader.”<sup id="cite_ref-ign_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Leanin%27_on_Slick" title="Leanin&#39; on Slick">Leanin' on Slick</a></i>, released in 2013 with <a href="/wiki/Decon_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Decon Records">Decon Records</a>, continued the retro flow of the previous release, this time taking inspiration from <a href="/wiki/1960s_R%26B" class="mw-redirect" title="1960s R&amp;B">1960s style-R&amp;B</a> and hot buttered soul.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_influences">Style and influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aceyalone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Style and influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aceyalone has been noted particularly for his innovative lyrical style and content. Some attribute the double-time rap styles that emerged in the mid-1990s to Aceyalone and <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_Fellowship" title="Freestyle Fellowship">Freestyle Fellowship</a>, although this is disputed by others.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Aceyalone and Freestyle Fellowship were noted for their rejection of the West Coast trend of gangsta rap. Aceyalone developed strong critiques of rap music's commercialization and glorification of violence.<sup id="cite_ref-Pecoraro_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pecoraro-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
