<p><b>Christopher Wong Won</b> (May 29, 1964&#160;&#8211;&#32;July 13, 2017), better known by his stage name <b>Fresh Kid Ice</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Trinidadian_and_Tobagonian_Americans" title="Trinidadian and Tobagonian Americans">Trinidadian-American</a> <a href="/wiki/Rapper" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapper">rapper</a>. He was a co-founder of the rap group <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a>, appearing on all of the group's albums from 1985 to 1998. Wong Won was born and spent his early childhood in <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Spain" title="Port of Spain">Port of Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, before emigrating to the United States. In his early twenties, Wong Won was in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> and he co-founded <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> while he was stationed in California. Early 2 Live Crew singles gained so much traction in Florida that they relocated there. By 1986, the group released the single "Throw The 'D<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>"; it is now considered the blueprint of <a href="/wiki/Miami_bass" title="Miami bass">Miami bass</a>.
</p><p>Later in 1986, 2 Live Crew released their debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_2_Live_Crew_Is_What_We_Are" title="The 2 Live Crew Is What We Are">The 2 Live Crew Is What We Are</a>.</i> The album established the group's signature style of comical <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">sexually explicit</a> lyrics. After a slew of successful releases the group met with considerable controversy as a <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_Florida" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of Florida">U.S. district court</a> ruled the album legally obscene. They were prosecuted, but all later acquitted. Wong Won is the only 2 Live Crew member to appear in the all their subsequent albums.
</p><p>Wong Won was the first prominent <a href="/wiki/Asian_hip_hop" title="Asian hip hop">Asian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asian_American_hip_hop" title="Asian American hip hop">Asian American rapper</a>, releasing his first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chinaman_(album)" title="The Chinaman (album)">The Chinaman</a></i>, in 1992. In the 2000s, he continued touring and releasing singles with 2 Live Crew. Wong Won's last projects, before his death in 2017, were his autobiography <i>My Rise 2 Fame</i> (2015) and the compilation <i>Breaking Glass Ceilings Volume 1</i> (2017).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_Kid_Ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wong Won was born in <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Spain" title="Port of Spain">Port of Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a> on May 29, 1964. His family is of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Trinidadian_and_Tobagonian" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese Trinidadian and Tobagonian">Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afro-Trinidadians_and_Tobagonians" title="Afro-Trinidadians and Tobagonians">Afro-Trinidadian</a> ancestry, with his Asian heritage originating primarily in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>. Wong Won and his family were <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1976, at the age of 12, Wong Won and his family emigrated from Trinidad and Tobago to the United States, settling in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, New York City. In 1982, Wong Won graduated from <a href="/wiki/Samuel_J._Tilden_High_School" title="Samuel J. Tilden High School">Samuel J. Tilden High School</a> in Brooklyn.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_Kid_Ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1984:_Military_service.2C_founding_2_Live_Crew_and_Asian_Hip_hop_pioneer"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1984:_Military_service,_founding_2_Live_Crew_and_Asian_Hip_hop_pioneer">1984: Military service, founding 2 Live Crew and Asian Hip hop pioneer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_Kid_Ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1984: Military service, founding 2 Live Crew and Asian Hip hop pioneer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Asian_American_hip_hop" title="Asian American hip hop">Asian American hip hop</a></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Young_Fresh_Kid_Ice.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Young_Fresh_Kid_Ice.jpg/220px-Young_Fresh_Kid_Ice.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="230" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Young_Fresh_Kid_Ice.jpg/330px-Young_Fresh_Kid_Ice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d4/Young_Fresh_Kid_Ice.jpg 2x" data-file-width="403" data-file-height="422" /></a><figcaption>Fresh Kid Ice in the early 1980s</figcaption></figure>
<p>According to Wong Won, he joined the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> in 1982, while stationed in <a href="/wiki/March_Air_Reserve_Base" title="March Air Reserve Base">March Air Force Base</a> near <a href="/wiki/Riverside,_California" title="Riverside, California">Riverside, California</a>. Wong Won met fellow Airmen Yuri Vielot (Amazing Vee) and David Hobbs (<a href="/wiki/Mr._Mixx" title="Mr. Mixx">Mr. Mixx</a>), with whom he formed the rap group <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Wong Won recounted that the group would perform in small, local venues on weekends, unbeknownst to their superiors.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1985, 2 Live Crew released the single "The Revelation", which became popular in Florida, so much so that they relocated to <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, minus Vielot who left the group, at the behest of local <a href="/wiki/Tour_promoter" title="Tour promoter">concert promoter</a> <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" class="mw-redirect" title="Luther Campbell">Luther Campbell</a>, after Wong Won and Hobbs were discharged from the Air Force.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With the subsequent success of 2 Live Crew, Wong Won became the first rapper of note in <a href="/wiki/Asian_American_hip_hop" title="Asian American hip hop">Asian American hip hop</a>, and the first <a href="/wiki/Asian_hip_hop" title="Asian hip hop">rapper of Asian heritage</a> to gain notoriety.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1985.E2.80.931986:_Miami_Bass_pioneer_and_2_Live_Crew.27s_breakthrough"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1985–1986:_Miami_Bass_pioneer_and_2_Live_Crew's_breakthrough">1985–1986: Miami Bass pioneer and 2 Live Crew's breakthrough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_Kid_Ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1985–1986: Miami Bass pioneer and 2 Live Crew&#039;s breakthrough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Miami_bass" title="Miami bass">Miami bass</a></div>
<p>In 1985, <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> released their next single, "What I Like" on Fresh Beat Records, with Wong Won appearing as the only rapper on the track.  That same year, <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a> entered into a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>-based rap producer <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" class="mw-redirect" title="Luther Campbell">Luther Campbell</a> who formed <a href="/wiki/Luke_Records" title="Luke Records">Luke Skyywalker Records</a> with the group. Shortly after forming the record label, Campbell joined 2 Live Crew as producer, artist, and <a href="/wiki/Hype_man" title="Hype man">hype man</a>. In April of that same year, 1985, rapper <a href="/wiki/Brother_Marquis" title="Brother Marquis">Brother Marquis</a> (Mark D. Ross) joined 2 Live Crew, forming the most well known and recognized line up of the group (Wong Won, Ross, <a href="/wiki/Mr._Mixx" title="Mr. Mixx">Hobbs</a>, and Campbell).
</p><p>In January 1986, 2 Live Crew released the <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">single</a> "Throw The 'D<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>", the rap was performed and the lyrics were written by Wong Won.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The single became an influential blueprint as to how future <a href="/wiki/Miami_bass" title="Miami bass">Miami bass</a> songs were written and produced.<sup id="cite_ref-Bein2014_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bein2014-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 25, 1986, the reconfigured 2 Live Crew became popular locally and nationally with the release of their <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_certification" title="Music recording certification">Gold-certified</a> debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_2_Live_Crew_Is_What_We_Are" title="The 2 Live Crew Is What We Are">The 2 Live Crew Is What We Are</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  Notorious for <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">sexually explicit</a> lyrics, that made many DJs of the time uncomfortable.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1988.E2.80.931991:_Subsequent_fame_and_controversy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1988–1991:_Subsequent_fame_and_controversy">1988–1991: Subsequent fame and controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_Kid_Ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1988–1991: Subsequent fame and controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a></div>
<p>In 1988, 2 Live Crew released their second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Move_Somethin%27_(album)" title="Move Somethin&#39; (album)">Move Somethin'</a></i> It was also certified Gold and featured the singles "Move Somethin'" and "Do Wah Diddy Diddy".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Reached #68 on the <i>Billboard 200</i> and #20 on the <i>Top R&amp;B/Hip Hop Albums</i> charts.
</p><p>2 Live Crew's third album <i><a href="/wiki/As_Nasty_As_They_Wanna_Be" class="mw-redirect" title="As Nasty As They Wanna Be">As Nasty As They Wanna Be</a></i> (1989), became the group's most successful commercial studio album and was certified <a href="/wiki/Double_Platinum" class="mw-redirect" title="Double Platinum">double Platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>. The album's single "<a href="/wiki/Me_So_Horny" title="Me So Horny">Me So Horny</a>" peaked at 26 of the <i>Billboard Top 100</i> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  A clean version of the album, <i><a href="/wiki/As_Clean_As_They_Wanna_Be" class="mw-redirect" title="As Clean As They Wanna Be">As Clean As They Wanna Be</a></i> was released concurrently with the explicit version.
</p><p>In 1990, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_Florida" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of Florida">United States District Court for the Southern District of Florida</a> ruled  <i>As Nasty As They Wanna Be</i> to be legally <a href="/wiki/Obscene" class="mw-redirect" title="Obscene">obscene</a>, becoming the first album in history to be so declared by a <a href="/wiki/Federal_judiciary_of_the_United_States" title="Federal judiciary of the United States">federal court</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> this ruling was later overturned by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Eleventh_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit">U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> An obscenity trial followed, where all of the defendants, including Wong Won, were eventually acquitted. Later that year, 2 Live Crew released <i><a href="/wiki/Banned_in_the_U.S.A." title="Banned in the U.S.A.">Banned in the U.S.A.</a></i>. The album included the hits "<a href="/wiki/Do_the_Bart" title="Do the Bart">Do the Bart</a>" peaked at 20 on the <i>Top 100</i> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The eponymous title single referred to the earlier federal court obscenity ruling regarding the group's previous album <i><a href="/wiki/As_Nasty_As_They_Wanna_Be" class="mw-redirect" title="As Nasty As They Wanna Be">As Nasty As They Wanna Be</a>.</i> <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen" title="Bruce Springsteen">Bruce Springsteen</a> granted the group permission to <a href="/wiki/Interpolation_(popular_music)" title="Interpolation (popular music)">interpolate</a> his song "<a href="/wiki/Born_in_the_U.S.A._(song)" title="Born in the U.S.A. (song)">Born in the U.S.A.</a>" for the single. <i>Banned in the U.S.A.</i> was also the first album to bear the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">RIAA</a>-standard <a href="/wiki/Parental_Advisory" title="Parental Advisory">Parental Advisory</a> warning sticker.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Also that year 2 Live Crew's album <i><a href="/wiki/Live_in_Concert_(2_Live_Crew_album)" title="Live in Concert (2 Live Crew album)">Live in Concert</a></i>  peaked at number 46 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a></i> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Weekend:_As_Nasty_as_They_Wanna_Be,_Pt._2" class="mw-redirect" title="Sports Weekend: As Nasty as They Wanna Be, Pt. 2">Sports Weekend: As Nasty as They Wanna Be, Pt. 2</a></i>, featuring the single "<a href="/wiki/Pop_That_Coochie" title="Pop That Coochie">Pop That Coochie</a>", which reached 58 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_100" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot 100">Hot 100</a></i> chart, was released by 2 Live Crew in 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The group's sixth album was a sequel of sorts to <i><a href="/wiki/As_Nasty_As_They_Wanna_Be" class="mw-redirect" title="As Nasty As They Wanna Be">As Nasty As They Wanna Be</a></i>, and was also accompanied by a clean version, <i>Sports Weekend: As Clean As They Wanna Be, Pt. 2</i>. It is the last studio album to include all of the members of the group.  Wong Won would become the only member of the group to appear on every subsequent album.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1992.E2.80.932004:_Continued_success_and_solo_projects"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1992–2004:_Continued_success_and_solo_projects">1992–2004: Continued success and solo projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_Kid_Ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1992–2004: Continued success and solo projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1992, Wong Won released his debut solo, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chinaman_(rap_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Chinaman (rap album)">The Chinaman</a></i>.  It is the first American hip hop album to embrace having an Asian heritage.<sup id="cite_ref-Playboy_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Playboy-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> On the <i>Billboard</i> charts, the album peaked at No. 38 and stayed two weeks on the <i><a href="/wiki/Heatseekers_Albums" class="mw-redirect" title="Heatseekers Albums">Heatseekers Albums</a></i> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Chinaman</i> was also on the <i><a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a></i> chart for ten weeks, peaking at No. 56.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Deal_with_This" title="Deal with This">Deal with This</a></i> was released in 1993 as a Rock on Crew vs 2 Live Crew album, where Wong Won only teamed up with Hobbs.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1994, the album <i><a href="/wiki/Back_at_Your_Ass_for_the_Nine-4" title="Back at Your Ass for the Nine-4">Back at Your Ass for the Nine-4</a></i> by The New 2 Live Crew was released. On the album, Wong Won was joined by Campbell, and a new rapper named Verb.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The album peaked at No. 52 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a></i> chart and #9 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a></i> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1995, Wong Won reunited with Ross and Hobbs as the 2 Live Crew, and released the group's album <i><a href="/wiki/Shake_a_Lil%27_Somethin%27" title="Shake a Lil&#39; Somethin&#39;">Shake a Lil' Somethin'</a></i> .The single "Shake a lil' Somethin'", peaked at No. 72 on the <i>Top 100</i> chart and #11 on the <i>Hot Rap Songs|Hot Rap Singles</i> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Two of the album's singles charted: "Do the Damn Thing", which made it to #24 on the <i>Hot Rap Singles</i> chart, and "Be My Private Dancer", which peaked at No. 34.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1998, only Wong Won and Ross participated to the 2 Live Crew release <i><a href="/wiki/The_Real_One" title="The Real One">The Real One</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The album's singles "2 Live Party", peaked at No. 52 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Songs" title="Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs">Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</a></i> chart, #9 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Songs</a></i> chart, while "The Real One" peaked at No. 60 on the <i>Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs</i> and #9 on the <i>Hot Rap Songs</i> charts.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After 2 Live Crew, Wong Won said he started Chinaman Records and brought his own show on the road.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> With this endeavor he released three solo albums: <i>Still Nasty</i> (2000),<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <i>Stop Playin</i> (2003),<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> <i>and Freaky Chinese</i> (2004).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Rapper <a href="/wiki/Flo_Rida" title="Flo Rida">Flo Rida</a>, then unknown, with his underground group named GroudHoggz participated to Wong Won's 2004 release.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2006.E2.80.932017:_Reforming_2_Live_Crew_and_final_projects"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2006–2017:_Reforming_2_Live_Crew_and_final_projects">2006–2017: Reforming 2 Live Crew and final projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_Kid_Ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2006–2017: Reforming 2 Live Crew and final projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During 2006–07 Wong Won and Ross met, discussed their differences, and ultimately decided to relaunch <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">2 Live Crew</a>. They  made offers to past members to rejoin the group, but were declined. As the 2 Live Crew, Wong Won and Ross toured and released singles.<sup id="cite_ref-Wong_Won_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wong_Won-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, Wong Won and 2 Live Crew were honorees at the 2010 <i><a href="/wiki/Hip_Hop_Honors#7th_VH1_Hip_Hop_Honors_(2010)" title="Hip Hop Honors">7th VH1 Hip-Hop Honors</a></i>. In August, Wong Won and <a href="/wiki/Brother_Marquis" title="Brother Marquis">Ross</a> announced the pending release of an album named, <i>Just Wanna Be Heard</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, Wong Won and Ross released the single "Take It Off".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Also, Wong Won and Ross made cameo appearances in the <a href="/wiki/Flo_Rida" title="Flo Rida">Flo Rida</a> music video "<a href="/wiki/G.D.F.R._(song)" class="mw-redirect" title="G.D.F.R. (song)">G.D.F.R.</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>  That same year, the duo announced the pending release of a new 2 Live Crew studio album <i>Turn Me On</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Also that year, Wong Won and Ross reunited with <a href="/wiki/Luther_Campbell" class="mw-redirect" title="Luther Campbell">Campbell</a> for several performances.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, Wong Won published his <a href="/wiki/Autobiography" title="Autobiography">autobiography</a> <i>My Rise 2 Fame</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The book chronicled Wong Won's journey from <a href="/wiki/Airman#United_States_Air_Force" title="Airman">U.S. Air Force Airman</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip hop">Hip hop</a> scene.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 13, 2017, Wong Won released his final project, a <a href="/wiki/Compilation_album" title="Compilation album">compilation album</a> named <i>Breaking Glass Ceilings Volume 1</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_issues_and_death">Health issues and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_Kid_Ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Health issues and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1988, shortly before the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Move_Somethin%27_(album)" title="Move Somethin&#39; (album)">Move Somethin'</a></i>, Wong Won was involved in a near fatal car accident.  His injuries included damage to his <a href="/wiki/Brachial_plexus" title="Brachial plexus">brachial plexus</a>, which resulted in the loss of mobility in his left arm.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Wong Won had a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> in 2009 and 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 13, 2017, Wong Won died at the Miami <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Health_Administration" title="Veterans Health Administration">VA Hospital</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> His death was attributed to <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis" title="Cirrhosis">cirrhosis</a> of the liver.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_Kid_Ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While not the first to rap, Wong Won is noted for being the first <a href="/wiki/Asian_hip_hop" title="Asian hip hop">Asian rapper</a> to gain some notoriety.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> When 2 Live Crew started to gain traction, Wong Won said that many fans had no clue that he was Asian until the group's music videos were released.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> For a while Wong Won was the only noticeable Asian American rapper until others came along.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
