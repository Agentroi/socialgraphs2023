<p><b>Earl Stevens</b> (born November 15, 1967), better known by his stage name <b>E-40</b>, is an American rapper. He is a founding member of the rap group <a href="/wiki/The_Click" title="The Click">The Click</a> and the founder of Sick Wid It Records. He has released 26 studio albums to date, appeared on numerous movie soundtracks, and has also done guest appearances on a host of other rap albums. Initially an <a href="/wiki/Underground_music" title="Underground music">underground</a> artist, his 1995 solo album <i><a href="/wiki/In_a_Major_Way" title="In a Major Way">In a Major Way</a></i> opened him up to a wider audience. Beginning in 1998, he began collaborating with mainstream rappers outside the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a>. He rose to higher mainstream popularity in 2006 with his single "<a href="/wiki/Tell_Me_When_to_Go" title="Tell Me When to Go">Tell Me When to Go</a>", which was produced by <a href="/wiki/Lil_Jon" title="Lil Jon">Lil Jon</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Earl Stevens was born in <a href="/wiki/Vallejo,_California" title="Vallejo, California">Vallejo, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bling_n_wing_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bling_n_wing-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He grew up with his siblings raised by a divorced mother who worked three jobs, and he became interested in hip hop after hearing "<a href="/wiki/Rapper%27s_Delight" title="Rapper&#39;s Delight">Rapper's Delight</a>" by <a href="/wiki/The_Sugarhill_Gang" title="The Sugarhill Gang">the Sugarhill Gang</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-spotlight_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spotlight-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rap_empire_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rap_empire-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Beginning in fourth grade, Stevens played the <a href="/wiki/Snare_drum" title="Snare drum">snare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bass_drum" title="Bass drum">bass drum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-spotlight_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spotlight-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He graduated from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hogan_High_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hogan High School (page does not exist)">Hogan High School</a> in Vallejo in 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Stevens played baseball in high school, recorded music with his siblings, and sold their recordings from the back of a car.<sup id="cite_ref-hardest_working_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hardest_working-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> After high school, Stevens enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Grambling_State_University" title="Grambling State University">Grambling State University</a> in 1986 with his cousin Brandt Jones and attended the school for one year.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eternal_comeback_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eternal_comeback-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-spotlight_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spotlight-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1986.E2.80.931999"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1986–1999">1986–1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1986–1999">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stevens made his rap debut as E-40 in 1986 with Jones (performing as <a href="/wiki/B-Legit" title="B-Legit">B-Legit</a>), sister <a href="/wiki/Suga_T" class="mw-redirect" title="Suga T">Suga T</a>, and brother <a href="/wiki/D-Shot" title="D-Shot">D-Shot</a> in the group Most Valuable Players. After impressing fellow students with a rap remix of the school song and a Grambling State talent show, Most Valuable Players released a single, "The King's Men".<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_click_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_click-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-spotlight_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spotlight-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The group later became <a href="/wiki/The_Click" title="The Click">The Click</a> and released the <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> <i>Let's Side</i> in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The EP was co-produced by Mike Mosley and Al Eaton and was released on Sick Wid It Records, an independent label founded by E-40. In 1992 they released a second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Down_and_Dirty_(album)" title="Down and Dirty (album)">Down and Dirty</a></i>, and in 1993 E-40 made his solo album debut. <i>Federal</i>, a nine-track LP/14-track CD produced by Studio Ton and released by Sick Wid It Records in association with SMG (Solar Music Group), a regional distributor.
</p><p>In 1993 the Click had mainstream hit, "<a href="/wiki/Captain_Save_a_Hoe" title="Captain Save a Hoe">Captain Save a Hoe</a>" (radio edit "Captain Save Them Thoe"). They moved back to <a href="/wiki/Vallejo,_California" title="Vallejo, California">Vallejo</a> and teamed up with D-Shot, E-40's brother, to form the group MVP or Most Valuable Players. E-40's gospel singing uncle (Saint Charles) helped them put out the record.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Suga-T was then added to the group to form <a href="/wiki/The_Click" title="The Click">the Click</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Synonymous with Bay Area rap, E-40 garnered a regional following, and eventually a national one, with his flamboyant raps, while his entrepreneurial spirit, embodied by his homegrown record label, Sick Wid It Records, did much to cultivate a flourishing rap scene to the east of San Francisco Bay, in communities such as Oakland and his native Vallejo. Along with <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spice_1" title="Spice 1">Spice 1</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ant_Banks" title="Ant Banks">Ant Banks</a>, E-40 was among the first West Coast rappers to sign a major-label deal, penning a deal with Jive Records in 1994, after years of releasing music independently. Thus, six additional solo albums were to follow, beginning with <i><a href="/wiki/In_a_Major_Way" title="In a Major Way">In a Major Way</a></i> in 1995 as well as remastered versions of E-40's independent Sick Wid It recordings from previous years. <i>In a Major Way</i> was regionally well-received, with guest spots by such rappers as <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mac_Mall" title="Mac Mall">Mac Mall</a>, as well as his son <a href="/wiki/Droop-E" title="Droop-E">Droop-E</a>.
</p><p>Although having a large following on the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast</a>, E-40 did not have a large mainstream audience, so only two of his songs released under Jive Records, "<a href="/wiki/1-Luv" title="1-Luv">1-Luv</a>" featuring Levitti and "Things'll Never Change" featuring <a href="/wiki/Bo-Roc" class="mw-redirect" title="Bo-Roc">Bo-Roc</a>, charted on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He had been working nearly exclusively with rappers from the Bay Area until 1997, when he released the double disc compilation <i><a href="/wiki/Southwest_Riders" title="Southwest Riders">Southwest Riders</a></i> featuring exclusively rap acts from the Bay Area and the south. His collaboration with southern rappers continued in 1998, when he was given guest appearances on albums by <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">Southern</a> rappers, including <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_(Eightball_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lost (Eightball album)">Lost</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/8Ball_%26_MJG" title="8Ball &amp; MJG">Eightball</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/MP_Da_Last_Don" class="mw-redirect" title="MP Da Last Don">MP Da Last Don</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Master_P" title="Master P">Master P</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2000.E2.80.932010"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000–2010">2000–2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000–2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Lil_Jon_and_E-40.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Lil_Jon_and_E-40.jpg/250px-Lil_Jon_and_E-40.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="166" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Lil_Jon_and_E-40.jpg/375px-Lil_Jon_and_E-40.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Lil_Jon_and_E-40.jpg/500px-Lil_Jon_and_E-40.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a><figcaption>E-40 (right) with <a href="/wiki/Lil_Jon" title="Lil Jon">Lil Jon</a> in 2007</figcaption></figure>
<p>In 2003 E-40 began hosting <i>E-Feezy Radio</i>, a weekly program on San Francisco hip-hop radio station <a href="/wiki/KMEL" title="KMEL">KMEL</a> that showcased Bay Area hip hop.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> KMEL regularly broadcast the program until 2008.  After completing a deal with <a href="/wiki/Jive_Records" title="Jive Records">Jive Records</a> he signed with <a href="/wiki/Lil_Jon" title="Lil Jon">Lil Jon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/BME_Recordings" class="mw-redirect" title="BME Recordings">BME Recordings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros. Records</a>. After the signing, he appeared on Lil Jon's single "<a href="/wiki/Snap_Yo_Fingers" title="Snap Yo Fingers">Snap Yo Fingers</a>", also featuring Sean P of <a href="/wiki/YoungBloodz" class="mw-redirect" title="YoungBloodz">YoungBloodz</a>, which became a hit reaching the top 10 of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>.  Afterwards, his single "<a href="/wiki/Tell_Me_When_To_Go" class="mw-redirect" title="Tell Me When To Go">Tell Me When To Go</a>", featuring <a href="/wiki/Oakland" class="mw-redirect" title="Oakland">Oakland</a> rapper <a href="/wiki/Keak_Da_Sneak" class="mw-redirect" title="Keak Da Sneak">Keak Da Sneak</a>, became popular throughout the United States, and E-40 appeared on <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sucker_Free" title="Sucker Free">Direct Effect</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Entertainment_Television" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Entertainment Television">BET</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/106_%26_Park" title="106 &amp; Park">106 &amp; Park</a></i>. Publicity for E-40 was achieved through the MTV special <i>My Block: The Bay</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> He later released "<a href="/wiki/U_And_Dat" class="mw-redirect" title="U And Dat">U And Dat</a>" in April 2006, featuring <a href="/wiki/T-Pain" title="T-Pain">T-Pain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kandi_Burruss" title="Kandi Burruss">Kandi Girl</a> and produced by <a href="/wiki/Lil_Jon" title="Lil Jon">Lil Jon</a>. His album <i><a href="/wiki/My_Ghetto_Report_Card" title="My Ghetto Report Card">My Ghetto Report Card</a></i> debuted at #1 on the <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart and #3 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_200" class="mw-redirect" title="Billboard Hot 200">Billboard Hot 200</a> on March 14, 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Released through Sick Wid It/BME/Warner Bros. Records, the album was produced by <a href="/wiki/Lil_Jon" title="Lil Jon">Lil Jon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rock" title="Rick Rock">Rick Rock</a>, and E-40's son, <a href="/wiki/Droop-E" title="Droop-E">Droop-E</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ghetto_report_card_amg_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ghetto_report_card_amg-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> He was also featured on <a href="/wiki/DJ_Shadow" title="DJ Shadow">DJ Shadow</a>'s new album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Outsider_(DJ_Shadow_album)" title="The Outsider (DJ Shadow album)">The Outsider</a></i>, on a track called <i>Dat's My Part</i>. In 2006, he also appeared on <a href="/wiki/Tech_N9ne" title="Tech N9ne">Tech N9ne</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Everready:_The_Religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Everready: The Religion">Everready: The Religion</a></i> CD on a track titled <i>Jellysickle</i>. In that same year, he contributed a verse to the official remix of <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_Okay_(One_Blood)" title="It&#39;s Okay (One Blood)">It's Okay (One Blood)</a></i> by fellow West coast rapper <a href="/wiki/The_Game_(rapper)" title="The Game (rapper)">the Game</a> along with 24 other prominent MCs.
In 2008, E-40's new album <i>The Ball Street Journal</i> came out, with "Wake it Up" featuring <a href="/wiki/Akon" title="Akon">Akon</a> as the lead single. "Got Rich Twice" featuring <a href="/wiki/Turf_Talk" title="Turf Talk">Turf Talk</a> followed. The album peaked at #42 on the <i>Billboard</i> 200.
</p><p>In 2009, 40 was featured on the track "Santana DVX" on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Incredibad" title="Incredibad">Incredibad</a></i> from the comedy group <a href="/wiki/The_Lonely_Island" title="The Lonely Island">the Lonely Island</a>. E-40 is listed in the credits as one of the writers. In the song, E-40 assumes the identity of <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Santana" title="Carlos Santana">Carlos Santana</a> and raps about his personalized brand of sparkling white wine. E-40 was featured on <a href="/wiki/BrokeNCYDE" class="mw-redirect" title="BrokeNCYDE">BrokeNCYDE</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/I%27m_Not_a_Fan,_But_the_Kids_Like_It!" title="I&#39;m Not a Fan, But the Kids Like It!">I'm Not a Fan, But the Kids Like It!</a></i> on the song <i>Booty Call</i>. E-40 was also featured on the song "Kush Is My Cologne" (along with <a href="/wiki/Bun_B" title="Bun B">Bun B</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Devin_the_Dude" title="Devin the Dude">Devin the Dude</a>) on <a href="/wiki/Gucci_Mane" title="Gucci Mane">Gucci Mane</a>'s 2009 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_State_vs._Radric_Davis" title="The State vs. Radric Davis">The State vs. Radric Davis</a></i>.
</p><p>On March 30, 2010, E-40 released two albums called <i><a href="/wiki/Revenue_Retrievin%27:_Day_Shift" title="Revenue Retrievin&#39;: Day Shift">Revenue Retrievin': Day Shift</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Revenue_Retrievin%27:_Night_Shift" title="Revenue Retrievin&#39;: Night Shift">Revenue Retrievin': Night Shift</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Both include guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, Gucci Mane, <a href="/wiki/Bobby_V" title="Bobby V">Bobby V</a>, and more. They debuted at numbers 47 &amp; 49 respectively on the 200. The first single from the <i>Day Shift</i> album is "<a href="/wiki/Bitch_(E-40_song)" title="Bitch (E-40 song)">Bitch</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a>. On March 29, 2011, E-40 released two albums called <i><a href="/wiki/Revenue_Retrievin%27:_Overtime_Shift" title="Revenue Retrievin&#39;: Overtime Shift">Revenue Retrievin': Overtime Shift</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Revenue_Retrievin%27:_Graveyard_Shift" title="Revenue Retrievin&#39;: Graveyard Shift">Revenue Retrievin': Graveyard Shift</a></i>. They include guest spots from Lil Jon, <a href="/wiki/Bun_B" title="Bun B">Bun B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slim_Thug" title="Slim Thug">Slim Thug</a>, Tech N9ne &amp; more. They both entered on the 200 chart at #'s 42 &amp; 40. In November 2010, E-40 and <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a> announced that they would release two collaborative album in Summer 2012 entitled <i><a href="/wiki/History:_Mob_Music" title="History: Mob Music">History: Mob Music</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/History:_Function_Music" title="History: Function Music">History: Function Music</a></i>. E-40 also performed at the <a href="/wiki/Gathering_of_the_Juggalos" title="Gathering of the Juggalos">Gathering of the Juggalos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2011.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2011–present">2011–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2011–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, E-40 released three solo albums: <i>The Block Brochure: Welcome to the Soil</i> series <a href="/wiki/The_Block_Brochure:_Welcome_to_the_Soil_1" title="The Block Brochure: Welcome to the Soil 1">1</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Block_Brochure:_Welcome_to_the_Soil_2" title="The Block Brochure: Welcome to the Soil 2">2</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Block_Brochure:_Welcome_to_the_Soil_3" title="The Block Brochure: Welcome to the Soil 3">3</a>, and released a collaboration album with Too Short.<sup id="cite_ref-facebook.com_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-facebook.com-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The first single from the second <i>Block Brochure</i> album is "<a href="/wiki/Function_(song)" title="Function (song)">Function</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/YG_(rapper)" title="YG (rapper)">YG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iamsu!" title="Iamsu!">Iamsu!</a> &amp; Problem. It peaked at #21 on the <a href="/wiki/Bubbling_Under_Hot_100_Singles" class="mw-redirect" title="Bubbling Under Hot 100 Singles">Bubbling Under Hot 100 Singles</a> chart, #62 on the Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs chart, and #22 on the Rap Songs chart, becoming his most successful single in years. The volumes include Snoop Dogg,  Too Short, <a href="/wiki/Kendrick_Lamar" title="Kendrick Lamar">Kendrick Lamar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juicy_J" title="Juicy J">Juicy J</a>,  Tech N9ne, <a href="/wiki/Twista" title="Twista">Twista</a>,   Brotha Lynch, Andre Nickatina, <a href="/wiki/2_Chainz" title="2 Chainz">2 Chainz</a>,  &amp; T-Pain as guests. They each debuted at #'s 58, 59 &amp; 72 on the <i>Billboard</i> 200, respectively, and the triple album box set that contains all three <i>Block Brochure</i> volumes debuted at #44. <i>History: Function Music</i> debuted at #63 on the 200, while <i>Mob</i> debuted at #72. He made a cameo appearance in the music video for <a href="/wiki/Young_Jeezy" class="mw-redirect" title="Young Jeezy">Young Jeezy</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/R.I.P._(Young_Jeezy_song)" title="R.I.P. (Young Jeezy song)">R.I.P.</a>" featuring <a href="/wiki/2_Chainz" title="2 Chainz">2 Chainz</a> in March 2013, and was also featured on the song's official "G-Mix" also featuring Snoop Dogg and Too Short. It was revealed that the 4th, 5th and 6th editions of <i>The Block Brochure</i> would be released exactly one year after the release date of the first three volumes. However, there is no confirmed release date for the three albums, but the artwork for all three volumes and the triple album box set was released on June 12.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> On May 21, he released the first single from the three albums, "Ripped" featuring Lil Jon.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> On July 18, he premiered the second single "All My Niggaz" featuring <a href="/wiki/Danny_Brown_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Danny Brown (rapper)">Danny Brown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schoolboy_Q" title="Schoolboy Q">Schoolboy Q</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The song was released to iTunes on August 20, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> On August 26, the video for "Off the Block" featuring Stressmatic and J. Banks was released and confirmed to be on the album.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> On Facebook E-40 announced that parts 4, 5 and 6 would be released on December 10, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 9, 2014, E-40 released <i><a href="/wiki/Sharp_On_All_4_Corners:_Corner_1" title="Sharp On All 4 Corners: Corner 1">Sharp On All 4 Corners: Corner 1</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sharp_On_All_4_Corners:_Corner_2" title="Sharp On All 4 Corners: Corner 2">Sharp On All 4 Corners: Corner 2</a></i>. The single "<a href="/wiki/Choices_(Yup)" title="Choices (Yup)">Choices (Yup)</a>" charted at number 125 on Billboard and number 43 on r&amp;b/hip-hop chart late summer 2015 and was certified Gold by the <a href="/wiki/RIAA" class="mw-redirect" title="RIAA">RIAA</a> May 9, 2016. E-40 collaborated with <a href="/wiki/G-Eazy" title="G-Eazy">G-Eazy</a> on the song "Nothing to Me", released in December 2015 on G-Eazy's album <i>When It's Dark Out</i>. On <a href="/wiki/Lecrae" title="Lecrae">Lecrae</a>'s album, <i><a href="/wiki/Church_Clothes_3" title="Church Clothes 3">Church Clothes 3</a></i>, E-40 is featured on the track "Can't Do You". On November 18, 2016, E-40 released <i>The D-Boy Diary</i> Parts 1 and 2.
</p><p>In 2018, E-40 announced the "Definitions" album trilogy.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> He released his twenty-fifth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gift_of_Gab_(album)" title="The Gift of Gab (album)">The Gift of Gab</a></i> on August 23, which was followed by his twenty-sixth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Practice_Makes_Paper" title="Practice Makes Paper">Practice Makes Paper</a></i> on July 26, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The third album, <i>The Rule of Thumb</i>, is yet to be released. In 2019, he made a guest appearance in the music video of "West Coast", performed by <a href="/wiki/G-Eazy" title="G-Eazy">G-Eazy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blueface" title="Blueface">Blueface</a>, ALLBLACK and <a href="/wiki/YG_(rapper)" title="YG (rapper)">YG</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_career">Business career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Business career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Along with former <a href="/wiki/NFL" class="mw-redirect" title="NFL">NFL</a> player <a href="/wiki/Chester_McGlockton" title="Chester McGlockton">Chester McGlockton</a>, E-40 opened a <a href="/wiki/Fatburger" title="Fatburger">Fatburger</a> franchise in <a href="/wiki/Pleasant_Hill,_California" title="Pleasant Hill, California">Pleasant Hill, California</a>, which has now been shut down.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> E-40 has promoted a forthcoming publication, <i>E-40's Book of Slang</i>, since 1998,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> but as of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=E-40&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> the volume has not yet been released.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> He is also a spokesperson for Landy Cognac, and he opened the now-defunct Ambassador's Lounge, a <a href="/wiki/Nightclub" title="Nightclub">nightclub</a> in Downtown <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> On November 16, 2007, it was announced that Stevens signed a franchise agreement with Wingstop Restaurants, Inc. and will open one in Southampton shopping center located in <a href="/wiki/Benicia,_California" title="Benicia, California">Benicia, California</a>. "I was introduced to Wingstop in Dallas and as soon as I tasted the wings I was hooked. I love the food," said Stevens. "I chose to open in the Bay Area because this is the soil where I was born and raised, and I still live here to this day. People here are going to love these things."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In late 2007, E-40 announced a new line of energy drinks called "40 Water."<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> He also has a number of other ventures in the beverage industry, including the wine industry, where he has released three wines including a red blend called "Function," a moscato and high-alcohol fortified wine called "Mangoscato."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The wines are branded using the rapper's real name, Earl Stevens. In December 2014, he released a premixed cocktail beverage called Sluricane Hurricane, inspired by the 1995 hit song "Hurricane" from his group the Click.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2015, E-40 released a line of malt liquor called "E-40", available in 24 oz. cans and 40 oz. glass bottles.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>E-40 is also an investor and has his own investment company. He was an early investor in social media apps <a href="/wiki/Clubhouse_(app)" title="Clubhouse (app)">Clubhouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Convoz" title="Convoz">Convoz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=E-40&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>E-40 lives in <a href="/wiki/Danville,_California" title="Danville, California">Danville, California</a>, with his wife Tracey.<sup id="cite_ref-eternal_comeback_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eternal_comeback-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> They married in 1991 and have two children who grew up to become rappers: Earl Jr., who performs as <a href="/wiki/Droop-E" title="Droop-E">Droop-E</a>, and Emari (Issue).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>E-40 is a longtime <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_49ers" title="San Francisco 49ers">San Francisco 49ers</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">San Francisco Giants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_State_Warriors" title="Golden State Warriors">Golden State Warriors</a> fan,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> and the team gave away 15,000 bobblehead figures of himself to visitors at a Giants game on June 25, 2022,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> where he also made the ceremonial first pitch of the game.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
