<p><b>Eric Lynn Wright</b> (September 7, 1964<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWesthoff2017284_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWesthoff2017284-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> &#8211; March 26, 1995), known professionally as <b>Eazy-E</b>, was an American rapper who propelled <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast rap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a> by leading the group <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a> and its label, <a href="/wiki/Ruthless_Records" title="Ruthless Records">Ruthless Records</a>. He is often referred to as the "Godfather of Gangsta Rap".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Born and raised in <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton, California</a>, Wright had several legal troubles before founding Ruthless in 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> After a short solo career with frequent collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, they joined, forming N.W.A, later that year. N.W.A's debut studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton" title="Straight Outta Compton">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>, was released in 1988. Controversial upon release, it is now ranked among <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone%27s_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="Rolling Stone&#39;s 500 Greatest Albums of All Time">the greatest</a> and most influential albums ever. The group released its second and final studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Niggaz4Life" title="Niggaz4Life">Niggaz4Life</a></i>, in 1991, and soon after disbanded.
</p><p>During N.W.A's splintering, largely by disputes over money, Eazy-E became embroiled in bitter rivalries with Ice Cube and Dr. Dre, who had departed for solo careers in 1989 and 1991, respectively. Resuming his solo career Eazy-E released two EPs,<sup id="cite_ref-amg_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amg-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> yet he remained more significant behind the scenes, signing and nationally debuting the rap group <a href="/wiki/Bone_Thugs-n-Harmony" title="Bone Thugs-n-Harmony">Bone Thugs-n-Harmony</a> from 1993 to 1994. He died from <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>-related illness in 1995.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_Ruthless_Records_investment">Early life and Ruthless Records investment</span></h2>
<p>Eric Wright was born to Richard and Kathie Wright on September 7, 1964, in <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton, California</a>, a Los Angeles suburb once noted for high crime rates and gang culture.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> His father was a postal worker and his mother was a grade-school administrator.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Wright dropped out of high school in the tenth grade,<sup id="cite_ref-dallas_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dallas-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> but later received a <a href="/wiki/General_Educational_Development" title="General Educational Development">general equivalency diploma</a> (GED).<sup id="cite_ref-Put_Gangsta_Rap_on_Charts_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Put_Gangsta_Rap_on_Charts-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1062260506">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:.5em 1.4em .8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:.5em 0 .8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{overflow:hidden;position:relative;margin:.5em auto .8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft span,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright span{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox>blockquote{margin:0;padding:0;border-left:0;font-family:inherit;font-size:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:110%;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote>:first-child{margin-top:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote:last-child>:last-child{margin-bottom:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quote-title,.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quotebox-quote{display:block}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:640px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{width:100%!important;margin:0 0 .8em!important;float:none!important}}</style><div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:30%; ; background-color: #FFFFF0;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>No one survived on the streets without a protective mask. No one survived naked. You had to have a role. You had to be "thug," "playa," "athlete," "gangsta," or "dope man." Otherwise, there was only one role left to you: "victim."
</p>
</blockquote>
<p><cite class="center-aligned" style="">Jerry Heller on Eazy-E<sup id="cite_ref-Ruthless_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruthless-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>Wright supported himself mainly by selling drugs, and introduced his cousin to the illicit occupation.<sup id="cite_ref-dallas_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dallas-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Wright's music manager <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Heller" title="Jerry Heller">Jerry Heller</a> recalls seeing Wright selling <a href="/wiki/Marijuana" class="mw-redirect" title="Marijuana">marijuana</a>, but not <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a>. Heller would claim that Wright's "dope dealer" label was part of his "self-forged armor".<sup id="cite_ref-Ruthless_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruthless-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Wright was also labeled as a "thug". Heller explains: "The hood where he grew up was a dangerous place. He was a small guy. 'Thug' was a role that was widely understood on the street; it gave you a certain level of protection in the sense that people hesitated to fuck with you. Likewise, 'dope dealer' was a role that accorded you certain privileges and respect."<sup id="cite_ref-Ruthless_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruthless-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1986, at age 22, Wright had allegedly earned as much as US$250,000 from dealing drugs. However, after his cousin was shot and killed, he decided that he could make a better living in the Los Angeles hip hop scene, which was growing rapidly in popularity.<sup id="cite_ref-Chang_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chang-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He started recording songs during the mid-1980s in his parents' garage, thus starting Ruthless Records.<sup id="cite_ref-Put_Gangsta_Rap_on_Charts_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Put_Gangsta_Rap_on_Charts-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-allrapnews.com_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allrapnews.com-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The original idea for Ruthless Records came when Wright asked Heller to go into business with him. Wright suggested a half-ownership company, but it was later decided that Wright would get eighty percent of the company's income and Heller would only get twenty percent. According to Heller, he told Wright, "Every dollar comes into Ruthless, I take twenty cents. That's industry standard for a manager of my caliber. I take twenty, you take eighty percent. I am responsible for my expenses and you're responsible for yours. You own the company. I work for you."<sup id="cite_ref-Ruthless_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruthless-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Along with Heller, Wright invested much of his money into <a href="/wiki/Ruthless_Records" title="Ruthless Records">Ruthless Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Heller claims that he invested the first $250,000 and would eventually put up to $1,000,000 into the company.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruthless_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruthless-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span></h2>
<h3><span id="N.W.A_and_Eazy-Duz-It_.281986.E2.80.931991.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="N.W.A_and_Eazy-Duz-It_(1986–1991)">N.W.A and <i>Eazy-Duz-It</i> (1986–1991)</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>'s (also named as "the world's most dangerous group"<sup id="cite_ref-allrapnews.com_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allrapnews.com-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>) original lineup consisted of <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Prince" title="Arabian Prince">Arabian Prince</a>, Dr. Dre, Eazy-E, and Ice Cube.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DJ_Yella" title="DJ Yella">DJ Yella</a> and <a href="/wiki/MC_Ren" title="MC Ren">MC Ren</a> joined later.<sup id="cite_ref-amgnwa_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amgnwa-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Compilation_album" title="Compilation album">compilation album</a> <i><a href="/wiki/N.W.A._and_the_Posse" title="N.W.A. and the Posse">N.W.A. and the Posse</a></i> was released on November 6, 1987, and would go on to be certified Gold in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The album featured material previously released as singles on the Macola Records label, which was responsible for distributing the releases by N.W.A and other artists like the <a href="/wiki/Fila_Fresh_Crew" title="Fila Fresh Crew">Fila Fresh Crew</a>, a <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast rap</a> group originally based in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, Texas.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eazy-E's debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Eazy-Duz-It" title="Eazy-Duz-It">Eazy-Duz-It</a></i>, was released in 1988, and featured twelve tracks. It was labeled as West Coast hip hop, <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a> and, later, as <a href="/wiki/Golden_age_hip_hop" title="Golden age hip hop">golden age hip hop</a>. It has sold over 2.5&#160;million copies in the United States and reached number forty-one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Put_Gangsta_Rap_on_Charts_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Put_Gangsta_Rap_on_Charts-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The album was produced by Dr. Dre and DJ Yella and largely written by MC Ren, Ice Cube, and <a href="/wiki/The_D.O.C." title="The D.O.C.">The D.O.C.</a><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Both Glen Boyd from the <i><a href="/wiki/Seattle_Post-Intelligencer" title="Seattle Post-Intelligencer">Seattle Post-Intelligencer</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>'s Jon Wiederhorn claimed that <i>Eazy-Duz-It</i> "paved the way" for N.W.A's most controversial album, <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton" title="Straight Outta Compton">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Seattle_Post-Intelligencer_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seattle_Post-Intelligencer-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Wright's only solo in the album was a remix of the song "8 Ball", which originally appeared on <i>N.W.A. and the Posse</i>. The album featured Wright's writing and performing; he performed on seven songs and helped write four songs.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ice Cube left N.W.A in 1989 because of internal disputes and the group continued as a four-piece ensemble.<sup id="cite_ref-amgnwa_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amgnwa-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> N.W.A released <i><a href="/wiki/100_Miles_and_Runnin%27" title="100 Miles and Runnin&#39;">100 Miles and Runnin'</a></i> in 1990 and <i><a href="/wiki/Niggaz4Life" title="Niggaz4Life">Niggaz4Life</a></i> in 1991. A diss war started between N.W.A and Ice Cube when "100 Miles and Runnin'" and "Real Niggaz" were released. Ice Cube responded with "<a href="/wiki/No_Vaseline" title="No Vaseline">No Vaseline</a>" on <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Certificate_(album)" title="Death Certificate (album)">Death Certificate</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Wright performed on seven of the eighteen songs on <i>Niggaz4Life</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In March 1991, Wright accepted an invitation to a lunch benefiting the Republican Senatorial Inner Circle, hosted by then-U.S. President <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> A spokesman for the rapper said that Eazy-E supported Bush because of his performance in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="End_of_N.W.A_and_feud_with_Dr._Dre_.281991.E2.80.931994.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_N.W.A_and_feud_with_Dr._Dre_(1991–1994)">End of N.W.A and feud with Dr. Dre (1991–1994)</span></h3>
<p>N.W.A began to split up after Jerry Heller became the band's manager. Dr. Dre recalls, "The split came when Jerry Heller got involved. He played the divide and conquer game. Instead of taking care of everybody, he picked one nigga to take care of and that was Eazy. And Eazy was like, 'I'm taken care of, so fuck it'." Dr. Dre and The D.O.C. sent <a href="/wiki/Suge_Knight" title="Suge Knight">Suge Knight</a> to look into Eazy-E's financial situation as they began to grow suspicious of Eazy-E and Jerry Heller. Dr. Dre and The D.O.C. asked Eazy-E to release them from Ruthless, but Eazy-E refused. The impasse led to what reportedly transpired between Suge Knight and Eazy-E at the recording studio where <i>Niggaz4life</i> was recorded. After he refused to release Dr. Dre and The D.O.C., Suge Knight told Eazy-E that he had kidnapped Jerry Heller and was holding him prisoner in a van. This did not convince Eazy-E to release Dr. Dre and The D.O.C. from Ruthless, and Suge Knight threatened Eazy-E's family: Suge Knight gave Eazy-E a piece of paper that contained Eazy's mother's address, telling him, "I know where your mama stays." Eazy-E finally signed Dr. Dre and The D.O.C.'s releases, officially ending N.W.A.<sup id="cite_ref-Dre_bio_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dre_bio-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1993, Eazy E regularly attended court in the prosecution of the officers involved in the <a href="/wiki/Beating_of_Rodney_King" class="mw-redirect" title="Beating of Rodney King">beating of Rodney King</a>, often standing alongside Theodore J. Briseno, who he had met through sharing the same lawyer.  Eazy E said, "The difference is that most people look at the video and say four white officers were responsible for the beating of this helpless, innocent black man. I say, 'Wrong.' Three white officers were responsible for beating Rodney King. The other officer (Theodore J. Briseno) happens to be Mexican-American, and he tried to stop them."  This caused controversy in the hip-hop community, with <a href="/wiki/Willie_D" title="Willie D">Willie D</a> calling Eazy E a "sellout".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The feud with Dr. Dre continued after a track on Dre's debut album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i>, "<a href="/wiki/Fuck_wit_Dre_Day_(And_Everybody%27s_Celebratin%27)" title="Fuck wit Dre Day (And Everybody&#39;s Celebratin&#39;)">Fuck wit Dre Day (And Everybody's Celebratin')</a>", contained lyrics that insulted Eazy-E. Eazy responded with the <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_On_(Dr._Dre)_187um_Killa" title="It&#39;s On (Dr. Dre) 187um Killa">It's On (Dr. Dre) 187um Killa</a></i>, featuring the tracks "<a href="/wiki/Real_Muthaphuckkin_G%27s" title="Real Muthaphuckkin G&#39;s">Real Muthaphuckkin G's</a>" and "It's On". The album, which was released on October 25, 1993, contains pictures of Dre wearing "lacy outfits and makeup" when he was a member of the Electro-hop <a href="/wiki/World_Class_Wreckin%27_Cru" title="World Class Wreckin&#39; Cru">World Class Wreckin' Cru</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dre_bio_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dre_bio-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span></h2>
<p>Wright had a son, Eric Darnell Wright (known as <a href="/wiki/Lil_Eazy-E" title="Lil Eazy-E">Lil Eazy-E</a>), in 1984. He also had a daughter named Erin,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> who changed her name to Ebie.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In October 2016, Ebie launched a crowd-funding campaign to produce a film called <i>Ruthless Scandal: No More Lies</i> to investigate her father's death.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> It ended unsuccessfully in December 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wright met Tomica Woods at a Los Angeles nightclub in 1991 and they married in 1995, twelve days before his death.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> They had a son named Dominick and a daughter named Daijah (born six months after Wright's death).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> After Wright's death, Ruthless was taken over by his wife. According to Jerry Heller, Wright had eleven children with eight women.<sup id="cite_ref-Soraya_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soraya-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Illness_and_death">Illness and death</span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1062260506"><div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:25em; ;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>Now, I'm in the biggest fight of my life and it ain't easy. But I want to say much love to those who have been down with me and thanks for all your support. Just remember: It's <i>your</i> real time and <i>your</i> real life.
</p>
</blockquote>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">--Statement from Eazy-E's camp on his behalf, March 16.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWesthoff2017278–279_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWesthoff2017278–279-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>On February 24, 1995, Wright was admitted to the <a href="/wiki/Cedars-Sinai_Medical_Center" title="Cedars-Sinai Medical Center">Cedars-Sinai Medical Center</a> in Los Angeles with a violent cough.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> He was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> He announced his illness in a public statement on March 16, 1995. It is believed Wright contracted the infection from a sexual partner.<sup id="cite_ref-Chang_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chang-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
During the week of March 20, having already made amends with <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>, he drafted a final message to his fans.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> On March 26, 1995, Eazy-E died from AIDS-induced <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>, one month after his diagnosis.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Soraya_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soraya-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> He was 30 years old (most reports at the time said he was 31 due to the falsification of his date of birth by one year).<sup id="cite_ref-Put_Gangsta_Rap_on_Charts_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Put_Gangsta_Rap_on_Charts-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> He was buried on April 7, 1995, at <a href="/wiki/Rose_Hills_Memorial_Park" title="Rose Hills Memorial Park">Rose Hills Memorial Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Whittier,_California" title="Whittier, California">Whittier, California</a>. Over 3,000 people attended his funeral,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> including Ice Cube and DJ Yella.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> He was buried in a gold casket, and was dressed in a flannel shirt, jeans, and his Compton hat. His final album, <i><a href="/wiki/Str8_off_tha_Streetz_of_Muthaphukkin_Compton" title="Str8 off tha Streetz of Muthaphukkin Compton">Str8 off tha Streetz of Muthaphukkin Compton</a>,</i> was released ten months after his death on January 30, 1996.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_influences_and_style">Musical influences and style</span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Eazy-E_logo.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Eazy-E_logo.svg/220px-Eazy-E_logo.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="73" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Eazy-E_logo.svg/330px-Eazy-E_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Eazy-E_logo.svg/440px-Eazy-E_logo.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="335" data-file-height="111" /></a><figcaption>Logo used by Eazy-E</figcaption></figure>
<p>Allmusic cites Eazy-E's influences as <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a>, <a href="/wiki/Redd_Foxx" title="Redd Foxx">Redd Foxx</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_Tee" class="mw-redirect" title="King Tee">King Tee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bootsy_Collins" title="Bootsy Collins">Bootsy Collins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Run%E2%80%93D.M.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Run–D.M.C.">Run–D.M.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Pryor" title="Richard Pryor">Richard Pryor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Lover" title="Egyptian Lover">Egyptian Lover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schoolly_D" title="Schoolly D">Schoolly D</a>, <a href="/wiki/Too_$hort" class="mw-redirect" title="Too $hort">Too $hort</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Sugarhill_Gang" title="The Sugarhill Gang">the Sugarhill Gang</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)">George Clinton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_main_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_main-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In the documentary <i>The Life and Timez of Eric Wright</i>, Eazy-E mentions collaborating with many of his influences.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When reviewing <i>Str8 off tha Streetz of Muthaphukkin Compton</i>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> noted "...&#160;Eazy-E sounds revitalized, but the music simply isn't imaginative. Instead of pushing forward and creating a distinctive style, it treads over familiar gangsta territory, complete with bottomless bass, whining synthesizers, and meaningless boasts."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> When reviewing <i>Eazy-Duz-It</i>, Jason Birchmeier of Allmusic said, "In terms of production, Dr. Dre and Yella meld together P-Funk, Def Jam-style hip-hop and the leftover electro sounds of mid-'80s Los Angeles, creating a dense, funky, and thoroughly unique style of their own." Birchmeier described Eazy-E's style as "dense, unique and funky", and said that it sounded "absolutely revolutionary in 1988".<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_main_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_main-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several members of N.W.A wrote lyrics for <i>Eazy-Duz-It</i>: Ice Cube, The D.O.C. and MC Ren.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The EP <i><a href="/wiki/5150:_Home_4_tha_Sick" title="5150: Home 4 tha Sick">5150: Home 4 tha Sick</a></i> features a song written by <a href="/wiki/Naughty_by_Nature" title="Naughty by Nature">Naughty by Nature</a>. The track "Merry Muthaphuckkin' Xmas" features <a href="/wiki/Menajahtwa" title="Menajahtwa">Menajahtwa</a>, Buckwheat, and <a href="/wiki/Atban_Klann" title="Atban Klann">Atban Klann</a> as guest vocalists, and "Neighborhood Sniper" features <a href="/wiki/Kokane" title="Kokane">Kokane</a> as a guest vocalist.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> <i>It's On (Dr. Dre) 187um Killa</i> features several guest vocalists, including <a href="/wiki/Dresta" title="Dresta">Gangsta Dresta</a>, <a href="/wiki/B.G._Knocc_Out" title="B.G. Knocc Out">B.G. Knocc Out</a>. Kokane, <a href="/wiki/Cold_187um" title="Cold 187um">Cold 187um</a>, Rhythum D, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dirty_Red&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dirty Red (page does not exist)">Dirty Red</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> <i>Str8 off tha Streetz of Muthaphukkin Compton</i> featured several guest vocalists, including B.G. Knocc Out, Gangsta Dresta, <a href="/wiki/Sylk-E._Fyne" title="Sylk-E. Fyne">Sylk-E. Fyne</a>, Dirty Red, Menajahtwa, Roger Troutman, and ex-N.W.A members MC Ren and DJ Yella.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h3>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Memorial_Eazy-E_made_by_streetartist_LJvanT_@_Leeuwarden_the_Netherlands.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Memorial_Eazy-E_made_by_streetartist_LJvanT_%40_Leeuwarden_the_Netherlands.jpg/280px-Memorial_Eazy-E_made_by_streetartist_LJvanT_%40_Leeuwarden_the_Netherlands.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="180" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Memorial_Eazy-E_made_by_streetartist_LJvanT_%40_Leeuwarden_the_Netherlands.jpg/420px-Memorial_Eazy-E_made_by_streetartist_LJvanT_%40_Leeuwarden_the_Netherlands.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Memorial_Eazy-E_made_by_streetartist_LJvanT_%40_Leeuwarden_the_Netherlands.jpg/560px-Memorial_Eazy-E_made_by_streetartist_LJvanT_%40_Leeuwarden_the_Netherlands.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="660" /></a><figcaption>Graffiti of Eazy-E in <a href="/wiki/Leeuwarden" title="Leeuwarden">Leeuwarden</a>, Netherlands</figcaption></figure>
<p>Eazy-E has been called the "godfather of gangsta rap".<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MTV_lil_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV_lil-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> MTV's Reid Shaheem said that Eazy was a "rap-pioneer",<sup id="cite_ref-MTV_lil_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV_lil-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> and he is sometimes cited by critics as a legend.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Steve Huey of <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> said that he was "one of the most controversial figures in gangsta rap".<sup id="cite_ref-amg_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amg-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Since his 1995 death, many book and video biographies have been produced, including 2002's <i>The Day Eazy-E Died</i> and <i>Dead and Gone</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When Eazy was diagnosed with AIDS, many magazines like <i><a href="/wiki/Jet_(magazine)" title="Jet (magazine)">Jet</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> <i>Billboard</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crisis_(newspaper)" title="The Crisis (newspaper)">The Crisis</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> covered the story and released information on the topic.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> All of his studio albums and EPs charted on the <i>Billboard</i> 200,<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_singles_&amp;_EPs_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_singles_&amp;_EPs-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> and many of his singles—"<a href="/wiki/Eazy-Duz-It_(song)" title="Eazy-Duz-It (song)">Eazy-Duz-It</a>", "<a href="/wiki/We_Want_Eazy" title="We Want Eazy">We Want Eazy</a>", "Real Muthaphuckkin G's", and "<a href="/wiki/Just_tah_Let_U_Know" title="Just tah Let U Know">Just tah Let U Know</a>"—also charted in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_singles_&amp;_EPs_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_singles_&amp;_EPs-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012 an Eazy-E documentary was released by Ruthless Propaganda, called <i>Ruthless Memories</i>. The documentary featured interviews with Heller, MC Ren, and B.G. Knocc Out.<sup id="cite_ref-www.hiphopdx.com_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.hiphopdx.com-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 2015 film <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton_(film)" title="Straight Outta Compton (film)">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>, Eazy-E is played by <a href="/wiki/Jason_Mitchell" title="Jason Mitchell">Jason Mitchell</a> and the film is dedicated in his memory.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eazy-E was then portrayed by Omari Wallace in the 2016 film <i><a href="/wiki/Surviving_Compton:_Dre,_Suge_%26_Michel%27le" title="Surviving Compton: Dre, Suge &amp; Michel&#39;le">Surviving Compton: Dre, Suge &amp; Michel'le</a></i>. The film portrays Eazy-E sympathetically as having been a friend to <a href="/wiki/Michel%27le" title="Michel&#39;le">Michel'le</a>, in one scene warning her to get out of the house because he fears that a drunken <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> is returning home to beat her.
</p>
