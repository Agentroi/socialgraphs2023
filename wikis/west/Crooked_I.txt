<p><b>Dominick Antron Wickliffe</b> (born September 23, 1976), better known by his stage names <b>Crooked I</b> and <b>Kxng Crooked</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> (stylised as <b>KXNG Crooked</b> and pronounced "King Crooked") is an American <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">rapper</a> from <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach</a>, California. He is best known as a former member of the hip hop <a href="/wiki/Supergroup_(music)" title="Supergroup (music)">supergroup</a> <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse_(group)" title="Slaughterhouse (group)">Slaughterhouse</a> with other members <a href="/wiki/Joe_Budden" title="Joe Budden">Joe Budden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joell_Ortiz" title="Joell Ortiz">Joell Ortiz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Royce_da_5%279%22" title="Royce da 5&#39;9&quot;">Royce da 5'9"</a>. He is CEO of his own record labels, Dynasty Entertainment and C.O.B. Digital<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> as well as Senior Vice President of Treacherous Records.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Before starting his own label, he was also signed to <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Records" title="Virgin Records">Virgin Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a>. He is currently one half of the rap duo Crook and Joell with his former Slaughterhouse bandmate, Joell Ortiz. Together, they released three studio albums.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crooked_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virgin_Records_Era">Virgin Records Era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crooked_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Virgin Records Era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Around the age of seventeen, Crooked I started an independent record company called Muscle Records with professional football players also from Long Beach, Chucky Miller and <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Russell_(American_football)" title="Leonard Russell (American football)">Leonard Russell</a>. It's not known how many songs were recorded, and the music recorded during this period still remains unreleased. This is when he caught the attention of Noo Trybe/<a href="/wiki/Virgin_Records" title="Virgin Records">Virgin Records</a>, and landed his first record deal in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Crooked I originally got his start appearing on compilations released by Big C-Style's record label, 19th Street Records, such as <i>19th Street LBC Compilation</i> and <i>Straight Outta Cali</i>. He also appeared on the soundtracks to movies such as <i>Ride</i> and <i>Caught Up</i>, where he collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Doggy Dogg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tha_Eastsidaz" title="Tha Eastsidaz">Tha Eastsidaz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Luniz" class="mw-redirect" title="The Luniz">The Luniz</a>. At the time, Crooked I was still signed under Noo Trybe/<a href="/wiki/Virgin_Records" title="Virgin Records">Virgin Records</a>, who handled distribution for 19th Street Records. Business was going well, but for unknown reasons, <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Records" title="Virgin Records">Virgin Records</a> decided to get rid of their urban division, otherwise known as Noo Trybe, and Crooked I was out of a record deal.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wickliffe was later contacted by Big C-Style and <a href="/wiki/Daz_Dillinger" title="Daz Dillinger">Daz Dillinger</a> to form <a href="/wiki/DPG_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="DPG Records">DPG Records</a>, which was originally going to be a sub-label under <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> at the time. Crooked was going to be 20% owner of the label, and the first act released as well.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Daz left <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> to run <a href="/wiki/DPG_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="DPG Records">DPG Records</a> independently, but rather than go with Daz, Crooked was trying to figure out his options.
</p><p>Crooked I also had talks of signing to <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>'s Aftermath imprint, but Dre wanted to release <i><a href="/wiki/Chronic_2001" class="mw-redirect" title="Chronic 2001">Chronic 2001</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Marshall_Mathers_LP" class="mw-redirect" title="Marshall Mathers LP">Marshall Mathers LP</a></i> before releasing Crooked.<sup id="cite_ref-DC_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He also had talks with other major record labels who wanted to sign him, but they too were waiting for <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Chronic_2001" class="mw-redirect" title="Chronic 2001">Chronic 2001</a></i> album to be released before they would consider signing another West Coast MC.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It was around this time that Crooked I re-negotiated the original deal he had with <a href="/wiki/DPG_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="DPG Records">DPG Records</a>/<a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> as a solo artist deal with just <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Death_Row.27s_second_dynasty"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_Row's_second_dynasty">Death Row's second dynasty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crooked_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death Row&#039;s second dynasty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While on Death Row, Crooked I appeared on a number of compilations, such as <i>Dysfunktional Family</i>, and appeared on various releases, such as <a href="/wiki/Ja_Rule" title="Ja Rule">Ja Rule</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Temptation_(Ja_Rule_album)" title="The Last Temptation (Ja Rule album)">The Last Temptation</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/2Pac" class="mw-redirect" title="2Pac">2Pac</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Nu-Mixx_Klazzics" title="Nu-Mixx Klazzics">Nu-Mixx Klazzics</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-DC_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He also recorded two albums, <i>Untouchable</i> and <i>Say Hi To The Bad Guy</i>, both of which were unreleased due to various label issues and industry politics. He did manage to release a mixtape along with Death Row's then in-house producer, Darren Vegas, called <i>Westcoasanostra Vol. 1</i> in the Spring of 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-DC_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Start_of_a_new_Dynasty.2C_and_Digital_Innovation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Start_of_a_new_Dynasty,_and_Digital_Innovation">Start of a new Dynasty, and Digital Innovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crooked_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Start of a new Dynasty, and Digital Innovation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 2004, Crooked I's album had not been released, <a href="/wiki/Suge_Knight" title="Suge Knight">Suge Knight</a> was constantly in and out of jail, and Crooked I's contract with Death Row Records had expired by November 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-wc2k_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wc2k-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DC05_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC05-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He left Death Row in early 2004 to start his own label, Dynasty Entertainment.<sup id="cite_ref-DC05_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC05-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wc2k-05_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wc2k-05-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It was not long after that when Crooked I signed a distribution deal with Treacherous Records/Universal,<sup id="cite_ref-DC05_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC05-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> but more legal trouble from Death Row surfaced so Crooked I had to stop production for his then titled debut album, <i>Mama's Boy</i>, which was actually going to be the name of Crooked's second release under <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a>. Later on the title of the album changed to <i>Mama's Boy Got a Loaded Gun</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-DC05_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC05-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the meantime, Crooked I began to build a substantial following online, with a fan community/discussion forum that garnered over 15,000 subscribed members by the end of its first year, often referred to as the Dynasty Online Community on his Dynasty Entertainment website that was located at westcoastdynasty.com. Crooked I's <a href="/wiki/Digital_marketing" title="Digital marketing">digital marketing</a> innovation would see him launch Dynasty TV, the Internet's first ever artist based video streaming platform that showcased music videos, interviews and concert footage, before YouTube was in existence. He released the online mixtape, <i>Young Boss Vol. 1</i> through the website, it was downloaded over 20,000 times directly from his own website, which crashed the Web server in its first hour of being available for download. The mixtape received critical acclaim throughout the underground Hip-Hop circuit, garnering an impressive "XL" rating by the Hip-Hop publication, <a href="/wiki/XXL_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="XXL Magazine">XXL Magazine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DC05_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC05-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the legal matters settled, Crooked began recording again, and began filming his own documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Life_After_Death_Row" title="Life After Death Row">Life After Death Row</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-wc2k-05_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wc2k-05-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> His internet presence and buzz would continue to grow, while he appeared on compilations such as Sway &amp; King Tech's <i>Back 2 Basics</i>, and on fellow Treacherous Records label mate K. Young's debut album, <i>Learn How To Love</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-wc2k_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wc2k-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, the name of Crooked's debut album changed to Boss Music.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hip-Hop_Weekly_era">Hip-Hop Weekly era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crooked_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hip-Hop Weekly era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Crooked I started the Hip-Hop Weekly series via the internet, he was the first artist in history to do such a series.<sup id="cite_ref-BS_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BS-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Every seven days he released a new track over various hip-hop beats of old and new instrumentals. The tracks were available each week on his official forum, website and an Imeem profile set-up solely for the Hip-Hop Weekly series, this would become the No. 1 most viewed Imeem profile on the social media site's rap music section. His ability to personally connect with his fans through his Hip-Hop Weekly series is evident since he would fulfill beat selection requests as well as give shout-outs over any given track to fans who requested it via his MySpace page.<sup id="cite_ref-BS_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BS-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The Hip-Hop Weekly series began on April 4, 2007 and ended on April 3, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BS_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BS-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wickliffe generated so much of a buzz with the Hip-Hop Weekly series that he was featured on the cover of the December/2007 issue of <a href="/wiki/XXL_(magazine)" title="XXL (magazine)">XXL</a> for a second time, this time as part of XXL's Freshmen 10.<sup id="cite_ref-xxlmag.com_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmag.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Also Vibe magazine called Crooked I an "Internet Gorilla" on the cover of their August 2008 edition, when they interviewed him about his approach to marketing online.
</p><p>Crooked I, along with DJ Felli Fel and DJ Nik Bean, released <i>St. Valentine's Day Bossacre</i> on February 14, 2008. Although it's called a mixtape, it consists of all new tracks by Crooked I, much like <i>Young Boss Vol. 2</i>, and boasts guest appearances by <a href="/wiki/Royce_da_5%279%22" title="Royce da 5&#39;9&quot;">Royce da 5'9"</a>, and Roscoe Umali, with production from <a href="/wiki/DJ_Felli_Fel" title="DJ Felli Fel">DJ Felli Fel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rock" title="Rick Rock">Rick Rock</a>, Komplex, and MG.  The February 2008 issue of Ozone West reviewed <i>St. Valentine's Day Bossacre</i> and rated it 4 slaps out of 5.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another mixtape and an EP was released in 2008 by Crooked I, <i>The Block Obama: Hood Politics</i><sup id="cite_ref-2DB_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2DB-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and <i>Block Obama II</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-hhdx_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hhdx-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Block Obama: Hood Politics</i> was originally going to be hosted by <a href="/wiki/DJ_Whoo_Kid" title="DJ Whoo Kid">DJ Whoo Kid</a> and DJ Strong, but ended up being released un-tagged without a DJ, and for free download on the internet.<sup id="cite_ref-2DB_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2DB-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <i>Block Obama II</i> was released on the day of the 2008 Presidential election, originally as a digital album for sale through <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a>, Amazon, <a href="/wiki/Napster" title="Napster">Napster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhapsody_(online_music_service)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhapsody (online music service)">Rhapsody</a>, and other online retailers.<sup id="cite_ref-hhdx_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hhdx-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Physical CD's of <i>Block Obama II</i> were later made, and sold through <a href="/wiki/CDBaby" class="mw-redirect" title="CDBaby">CDBaby</a>. The EP also features guest appearances from Dynasty Entertainment artists Horse Shoe G.A.N.G. and Sauce The Boss, in addition to <a href="/wiki/Knoc-turn%27al" title="Knoc-turn&#39;al">Knoc-turn'al</a> and production by Jim Gittum, Komplex, and <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rock" title="Rick Rock">Rick Rock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hhdx_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hhdx-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slaughterhouse_era">Slaughterhouse era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crooked_I&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Slaughterhouse era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joe_Budden" title="Joe Budden">Joe Budden</a> reached out to Crooked I, <a href="/wiki/Royce_da_5%279%22" title="Royce da 5&#39;9&quot;">Royce da 5'9"</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joell_Ortiz" title="Joell Ortiz">Joell Ortiz</a>, and Nino Bless for a track titled "Slaughterhouse" on his digital release, <i><a href="/wiki/Halfway_House_(album)" title="Halfway House (album)">Halfway House</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-xxlmag.com_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmag.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Based on the reception of the track, they decided to form a super-group, minus Nino Bless, and named it after this song.<sup id="cite_ref-xxlmag.com_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xxlmag.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> They released numerous songs throughout early 2009, building a buzz for their <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse_(Slaughterhouse_album)" title="Slaughterhouse (Slaughterhouse album)">self-titled album</a>, which was released through <a href="/wiki/E1_Music" class="mw-redirect" title="E1 Music">E1</a> on August 11, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The album features production from <a href="/wiki/The_Alchemist_(musician)" title="The Alchemist (musician)">Alchemist</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Khalil" title="DJ Khalil">DJ Khalil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mr._Porter" class="mw-redirect" title="Mr. Porter">Mr. Porter</a>, Streetrunner, and guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Pharoahe_Monch" title="Pharoahe Monch">Pharoahe Monch</a>, K. Young, and The New Royales.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 27, 2009 in Long Beach, California, Crooked I was allegedly shot at during a conversation with a fan. The conflicting early media reports on the incident in the following hours initially left people worried and uncertain on his condition, but he confirmed to MTV News and other media that he Was alive and, while mentally distracted, physically well. Crooked I has declined to further elaborate on the incident, citing where he is from it is against the code of the streets.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 10, 2009, Crooked I released a digital-only solo EP, <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Pig_Face_Weapon_Waist" class="mw-redirect" title="Mr. Pig Face Weapon Waist">Mr. Pig Face Weapon Waist</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopwired.com_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopwired.com-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> "It’s a spin-off of my Slaughterhouse alter ego," he said of the odd title, "cause I come out on stage with a pig mask and a Dickie suit on."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The EP also boasts guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, K. Young, <a href="/wiki/M.O.P." title="M.O.P.">M.O.P.</a>, The Horseshoe G.A.N.G., and of course fellow Slaughterhouse members, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Budden" title="Joe Budden">Joe Budden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joell_Ortiz" title="Joell Ortiz">Joell Ortiz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Royce_da_5%279%22" title="Royce da 5&#39;9&quot;">Royce da 5'9"</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopwired.com_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopwired.com-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In addition to production from Komplex, <a href="/wiki/Scram_Jones" title="Scram Jones">Scram Jones</a>, Streetrunner, Frequency, and P. Silva.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopwired.com_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopwired.com-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After briefly considering plans to release a full-length <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Pig_Face_Weapon_Waist" class="mw-redirect" title="Mr. Pig Face Weapon Waist">Mr. Pig Face Weapon Waist</a></i> LP in January 2010, Crooked I revised his priorities and announced <i>Million Dollar Story</i>, to be released in 2010. Production for the album is to include contributions from <a href="/wiki/DJ_Khalil" title="DJ Khalil">DJ Khalil</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Alchemist_(producer)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Alchemist (producer)">The Alchemist</a>, and newcomer Quest. In addition, Crooked I stated his intentions to get <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a> beats for the album.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The new owners of Death Row Records, WIDEawake Entertainment, released an album called <i>Hood Star</i> on June 15, 2010, comprising recordings Crooked I made while signed to the label in the early 2000s. It has 15 songs Wickliffe recorded while signed to the label, with guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Juvenile_(rapper)" title="Juvenile (rapper)">Juvenile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danny_Boy_(singer)" title="Danny Boy (singer)">Danny Boy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ray_J" title="Ray J">Ray J</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sisqo" class="mw-redirect" title="Sisqo">Sisqo</a>, and others.<sup id="cite_ref-ballerstatus.com_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ballerstatus.com-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 17, 2010, Crooked I released yet another EP, titled <i>Planet C.O.B. Vol. 1</i>, which features production from <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rock" title="Rick Rock">Rick Rock</a>, Komplex, and more.<sup id="cite_ref-planetcob.com_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-planetcob.com-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 1, 2010, the first single for Crooked I's official debut solo album, <i>Million Dollar Story</i> was released on iTunes titled "Villain", which features Gracie Productions recording artist Sally Anthony.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 12, 2011, after months of speculation, Slaughterhouse officially signed to <a href="/wiki/Shady_Records" title="Shady Records">Shady Records</a>, along with artist <a href="/wiki/Yelawolf" title="Yelawolf">Yelawolf</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 21, 2011, Crooked I released the <i>Million Dollar Story</i> EP, available for purchase through <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a>, to tie fans over until the album is released.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> It features two brand new songs and two previously released tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 12, 2011, the compilation <i>Planet C.O.B. Vol. 2</i> was released digitally, and for a limited time was available on CD through Crooked I's official website.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Unlike the previous installment, which was an EP by Crooked I, this album features songs by all of the C.O.B. affiliates, such as Horseshoe G.A.N.G., Sauce Tha Boss, Coniyac, and more. To keep the momentum up for the C.O.B. movement, Crooked I brought his official clothing line to the internet.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> On November 17, 2011, Crooked I announced a new digital EP titled <i>In None We Trust - The Prelude</i>, is set to be released on December 13 and will include guest appearances from <a href="/wiki/Jay_Rock" title="Jay Rock">Jay Rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasses_Malone" title="Glasses Malone">Glasses Malone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mistah_F.A.B." title="Mistah F.A.B.">Mistah F.A.B.</a>, Horseshoe G.A.N.G., Coniyac and K. Young.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> On November 22, 2011, the first single from the EP titled "No Competition" was released to the internet.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 16, 2014, Crooked I aka Kxng Crooked released <i><a href="/wiki/Sex,_Money_and_Hip-Hop" title="Sex, Money and Hip-Hop">Sex, Money and Hip-Hop</a></i> with guest appearances including <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse_(group)" title="Slaughterhouse (group)">Slaughterhouse</a>, Emanny, Horseshoe Gang, Shalé, Bo-Roc from <a href="/wiki/The_Dove_Shack" title="The Dove Shack">The Dove Shack</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kokane" title="Kokane">Kokane</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Novel_(musician)" title="Novel (musician)">Novel</a>. <a href="/wiki/J.U.S.T.I.C.E._League" title="J.U.S.T.I.C.E. League">J.U.S.T.I.C.E. League</a>, Jonathan Hay, Mike Smith, Sarah J, DJ King Tech, Aktive, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rock" title="Rick Rock">Rick Rock</a> and Tabu handled the production for the album.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In May 2015, Crooked appeared on four songs "I Didn't Mean To Treat You Bad", "Ashamed", "Another Mirror" and "Groupie" from <i><a href="/wiki/When_Music_Worlds_Collide_(Jonathan_Hay_and_Mike_Smith_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="When Music Worlds Collide (Jonathan Hay and Mike Smith album)">When Music Worlds Collide</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2016, Crooked I and <a href="/wiki/Lazarus_(rapper)" title="Lazarus (rapper)">Lazarus</a> collaborated on the single "Fearless" in which they dissed politician <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> and called him a <a href="/wiki/Racist" class="mw-redirect" title="Racist">racist</a>. The single included the verse: "Bunch of cowards scared to tell a racist like Donald to step on top of the <a href="/wiki/Trump_Tower_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Trump Tower (disambiguation)">Trump Towers</a> and take the fast way down".<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In March 2016, Crooked appeared on three songs "Paradise" with <a href="/wiki/Royce_da_5%279%22" title="Royce da 5&#39;9&quot;">Royce da 5'9"</a>, "Johnny College" and "Don't Close Your Eyes" from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Urban_Hitchcock_LP" title="The Urban Hitchcock LP">The Urban Hitchcock LP</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> He also appeared on <i>Mountain Biking Through Louisville</i> album by <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Hay_(Producer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jonathan Hay (Producer)">Jonathan Hay</a> and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Whoo_Kid" title="DJ Whoo Kid">DJ Whoo Kid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>KXNG Crooked features on the Intro track<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> of his brother's "Horseshoe Gang"'s upcoming album Anti-Trap Music" which will be released on the 29th April. The track was posted and uploaded to SoundCloud. In November 2016, he released the album <i><a href="/wiki/Good_vs._Evil_(album)" title="Good vs. Evil (album)">Good Vs. Evil</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> His most recent album, <i><a href="/wiki/Good_vs._Evil_II:_The_Red_Empire" title="Good vs. Evil II: The Red Empire">Good vs. Evil II: The Red Empire</a></i>, was released on December 8, 2017. It serves as a direct sequel to <i>Good vs. Evil</i>, expanding upon the themes of his previous work.
</p><p>In April 2018, Crooked I announced his departure from Slaughterhouse in order to pursue his solo career.<sup id="cite_ref-hnhh_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hnhh-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the start of 2019, Crooked I released a song called 96 GS, and in the song announced that his weekly hip-hop singles were to return. He followed that with singles titled Halfway Me, Pistol Grip, Once Upon A Time in the LBC, and many more. In March 2019, KXNG Crooked was featured in the song "Revenge" (along with fellow <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse_(group)" title="Slaughterhouse (group)">Slaughterhouse</a> member <a href="/wiki/Joell_Ortiz" title="Joell Ortiz">Joell Ortiz</a>) on Cryptik Soul's album <i>Killer's Blood</i>.
</p><p>Throughout 2019, Crooked I released a series of mixtapes titled <i>The Weeklys</i>, which feature compilations of songs from his "Hip Hop Weekly" series in which he releases a new freestyle song every week of the year.
</p><p>In January 2020, Crooked I appeared on Eminem's eleventh studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Music_to_Be_Murdered_By" title="Music to Be Murdered By">Music to Be Murdered By</a></i>, on the track "I Will" along with former Slaughterhouse groupmates <a href="/wiki/Joell_Ortiz" title="Joell Ortiz">Joell Ortiz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Royce_da_5%279%22" title="Royce da 5&#39;9&quot;">Royce da 5'9"</a>, as well as on the track "Stockholm Syndrome", on <a href="/wiki/Russ_(rapper)" title="Russ (rapper)">Russ</a>' EP titled <i>CHOMP</i>.
</p>
