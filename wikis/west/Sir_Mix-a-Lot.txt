<p><b>Anthony L. Ray</b> (born August 12, 1963), better known by his stage name <b>Sir Mix-a-Lot</b> or his <a href="/wiki/Citizens_band_radio" title="Citizens band radio">CB</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_CB_slang" title="List of CB slang">handle</a> <b>Prime Minista</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Rapper" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapper">rapper</a>, songwriter, and record producer. He is best known for his 1992 hit song "<a href="/wiki/Baby_Got_Back" title="Baby Got Back">Baby Got Back</a>", which peaked at number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Mix-a-Lot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anthony Ray was born on August 12, 1963, in <a href="/wiki/Auburn,_Washington" title="Auburn, Washington">Auburn, Washington</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, and grew up in Seattle's Central District. In Ray's youth, his mother, Ella Kingsland, worked as a licensed practical nurse at the King County Jail making six or seven dollars an hour. Ray was a fan of <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> and started rapping in the early 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While living in the Bryant Manor apartments on 19th Ave and East Yesler Way, Anthony Ray started school at <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_High_School_(Seattle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Roosevelt High School (Seattle)">Roosevelt High School</a>, near the University District, when the Seattle Public School District was in the throes of what would be a 21-year experiment to integrate the school system; <a href="/wiki/Guns_N%27_Roses" title="Guns N&#39; Roses">Guns N' Roses</a> member <a href="/wiki/Duff_McKagan" title="Duff McKagan">Duff McKagan</a> and actress <a href="/wiki/Rose_McGowan" title="Rose McGowan">Rose McGowan</a> also went there. Students were bused from their neighborhoods to schools at the other end of the city. From 1978 to 1999, when the busing program was in operation, minorities carried the burden of busing, going from the South End and the Central Area to predominantly white schools in the North End.<sup id="cite_ref-Busing_Blues_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Busing_Blues-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ray said he knew that some North End residents did not want black children bused into their neighborhoods. But for him, the experience offered respite from the projects. "I’ve heard things like, 'Forced integration is not good,' 'I want my kid to be able to go to school in our community; that’s why we moved here' – all those things I totally understand," he said. "But from my perspective, I didn’t have the luxury of living in a neighborhood where a good school was. We didn’t make that kind of money. So from my perspective, it was the best thing that could have happened to me."<sup id="cite_ref-Busing_Blues_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Busing_Blues-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> A music teacher at Eckstein Middle School introduced Ray to the possibility of a music career. 
</p><p>Ray was interested in electronics and CB radio from a young age. One of his early jobs was working at a pinball arcade servicing machines, and during that time he started to fix keyboards and other musical equipment. He still works with electronics as a hobby.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Mix-a-Lot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Mix-a-Lot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soon after high school, he began DJing parties at local community centers.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> By 1983, Sir Mix-A-Lot had begun playing weekends regularly at the Rainier Vista Boys and Girls Club in South Seattle. Soon he moved locations and started hosting his parties at the Rotary Boys and Girls Club in the Central District.  It was there that he met 'Nasty' Nes Rodriguez, a local radio DJ and host of Fresh Tracks, the West Coast's first rap radio show on Seattle station <a href="/wiki/KKDZ" title="KKDZ">KKFX</a> (K-Fox).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sir Mix-a-Lot partnered with Nasty Nes and local businessman Ed Locke to found the Nastymix record label in 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The first song to gain popularity outside of Seattle was "Square Dance Rap" in 1986. Mix-a-Lot had originally decided to rap the entire song slowly, then speed it up and increase its pitch in post production, Mix later told <i>Seattle Refined</i> in 2018 that "I didn't want to rap, that's why I use this weird Smurf voice".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> After the song was picked up by DJs in clubs nationwide, he toured Florida, New York, and other states. While in Arizona, he noticed a street named Broadway with a restaurant named Dick's, just like Seattle. This gave him the idea to write his next hit, "<a href="/wiki/Posse_on_Broadway" title="Posse on Broadway">Posse on Broadway</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The title referred to <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Seattle)" title="Broadway (Seattle)">Broadway</a> in Seattle's <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Hill,_Seattle,_Washington" class="mw-redirect" title="Capitol Hill, Seattle, Washington">Capitol Hill</a> district.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Released in 1987, the single made the Top 100 but quickly disappeared, although it remains popular in the Seattle area for its references to many local landmarks.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Swass" title="Swass">Swass</a></i>, Sir Mix-a-Lot's debut album, was released in 1988 with two other singles: "Square-Dance Rap"<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and "Iron Man", a <a href="/wiki/Rap_metal" title="Rap metal">rap metal</a> track sampling from the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_(song)" title="Iron Man (song)">song of the same name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Black_Sabbath" title="Black Sabbath">Black Sabbath</a>; it was backed by the band <a href="/wiki/Metal_Church" title="Metal Church">Metal Church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 1990, the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> certified <i>Swass</i> <a href="/wiki/Platinum_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Platinum record">platinum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Seminar_(album)" title="Seminar (album)">Seminar</a></i>, released in 1989, featured "My Hooptie", "Beepers", "Gortex" and "I Got Game".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Mix-a-Lot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1991, Sir Mix-A-Lot signed to the <a href="/wiki/Def_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Def American">Def American</a> label, which also bought the rights to his first two albums, and released his third album <i><a href="/wiki/Mack_Daddy" title="Mack Daddy">Mack Daddy</a></i> in 1992. The single "<a href="/wiki/Baby_Got_Back" title="Baby Got Back">Baby Got Back</a>" was a number one hit that went double platinum<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and won the 1993 <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Rap_Solo_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance">Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> aired the <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for the song only after 9 PM because of "many, many, complaints."<sup id="cite_ref-Billboard_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1993, Sir Mix-a-Lot collaborated with Seattle-based grunge band <a href="/wiki/Mudhoney" title="Mudhoney">Mudhoney</a> for the song "Freak Momma" on the <i><a href="/wiki/Judgment_Night_(film)" title="Judgment Night (film)">Judgment Night</a></i> soundtrack.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1994, he released the album <i><a href="/wiki/Chief_Boot_Knocka" title="Chief Boot Knocka">Chief Boot Knocka</a></i>, which reached #69 on the Billboard 200 and #28 on the Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Charts. The album featured the hit single "Put 'Em On The Glass". "Just Da Pimpin' in Me" was nominated for the Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance but lost to "Let Me Ride" by Dr. Dre.
</p><p>When his 1996 album <i><a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Bumpasaurus" title="Return of the Bumpasaurus">Return of the Bumpasaurus</a></i> was only given a low label promotion, leading to lackluster sales, Sir Mix-a-Lot left Def American. During the time off, he worked closely with the band <a href="/wiki/The_Presidents_of_the_United_States_of_America_(band)" title="The Presidents of the United States of America (band)">The Presidents of the United States of America</a> under the group name "Subset" with a combination of rock and rap music, but nothing was ever officially released.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Mix-a-Lot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sir Mix-a-Lot signed with the <a href="/wiki/Independent_label" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent label">independent label</a> Artist Direct for his 2003 album <i><a href="/wiki/Daddy%27s_Home_(Sir_Mix-a-Lot_album)" title="Daddy&#39;s Home (Sir Mix-a-Lot album)">Daddy's Home</a></i> with "Big Johnson" as its lead single.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The next year, he released a DVD entitled "Shhhh... Don't Tell 'Em That" to promote the album.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Mix-a-Lot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, Sir Mix-a-Lot announced his next album, <i>Dun 4got About Mix</i>. The lead single "Carz" was released to YouTube on 23 Nov 2010. By June 2011, the video had had over a million views, although no release date for the album has been set as of 2023.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the same year, his <i>F.U.B.A.R. Remix</i> for the song <i>Conditions Of My Parole</i> appeared on <a href="/wiki/Puscifer" title="Puscifer">Puscifer</a>'s remix album <i><a href="/wiki/All_Re-Mixed_Up" title="All Re-Mixed Up">All Re-Mixed Up</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-spin_puscifer_remix_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spin_puscifer_remix-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, Sir Mix-a-Lot produced the album <i>Dream</i> for the urban rock band <a href="/wiki/Ayron_Jones_and_The_Way" class="mw-redirect" title="Ayron Jones and The Way">Ayron Jones and The Way</a>. He opened their album release party at Neumos on November 2. That Christmas season, he promoted the <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_Lottery" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington State Lottery">Washington State Lottery</a> over the Christmas season, with advertisements featuring his music appearing on <a href="/wiki/Spotify" title="Spotify">Spotify</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 6, 2014, Sir Mix-a-Lot collaborated and performed with the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Symphony" title="Seattle Symphony">Seattle Symphony</a> on a new composition by <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Prokofiev" title="Gabriel Prokofiev">Gabriel Prokofiev</a> as part of the symphony's Sonic Evolution series of new orchestral pieces inspired by Seattle's music icons.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, rapper <a href="/wiki/Nicki_Minaj" title="Nicki Minaj">Nicki Minaj</a> released the single "<a href="/wiki/Anaconda_(Nicki_Minaj_song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anaconda (Nicki Minaj song)">Anaconda</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> which prominently featured samples from "Baby Got Back". Sir Mix-a-Lot praised both the artist and the song, calling it the "new and improved version" of "Baby Got Back".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2016, Sir Mix-a-Lot collaborated with TNT and LK on the track and video, "Streets Don't Love Me".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 2017 to 2019, Sir Mix-a-Lot hosted a morning radio show based in Seattle on Hot 103.7 FM. 
</p><p>He owns multiple residences, including one in Auburn, Washington, where he has continued to maintain a strong presence performing at small festivals across the country.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_work">Other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sir_Mix-a-Lot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1995, Sir Mix-A-Lot was the regular host of the short-lived UPN anthology drama series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Watcher_(1995_TV_series)" title="The Watcher (1995 TV series)">The Watcher</a></i>, in which, in 2000, he was quoted in Vibe magazine as saying "in retrospect, I wish I hadn't done (the show)"<sup id="cite_ref-VIBE_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VIBE-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, Sir Mix-a-Lot appeared on <a href="/wiki/Adult_Swim" title="Adult Swim">Adult Swim</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_Goes_to_the_Mayor" title="Tom Goes to the Mayor">Tom Goes to the Mayor</a></i>. In 2008, he appeared on Adult Swim's <i><a href="/wiki/Robot_Chicken" title="Robot Chicken">Robot Chicken</a></i> singing a song entitled "Table Be Round". It was sung in the style of "Baby Got Back", but was about to King Arthur's creation of the Round Table. He also voiced politician <a href="/wiki/Hans_Blix" title="Hans Blix">Hans Blix</a> and singer <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a> in the season 3 finale. He appeared in an episode of Season 4 of <i><a href="/wiki/BoJack_Horseman" title="BoJack Horseman">BoJack Horseman</a></i> playing himself.
</p><p>Sir Mix-A-Lot provides narration and commentary in <i>Wheedle's Groove</i>, a 2009 documentary about the Seattle 1960s and 70s funk and soul scene.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2018, the <a href="/wiki/DIY_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="DIY Network">DIY Network</a> aired a special called <i>Sir Mix-A-Lot's House Remix</i>, which involved Sir Mix-A-Lot buying and flipping a house in Seattle.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2019, he became the spokesperson for <a href="/wiki/Cards_Against_Humanity" title="Cards Against Humanity">Cards Against Humanity</a>'s "Ass Pack".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
