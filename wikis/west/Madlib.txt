<p><b>Otis Jackson Jr.</b> (born October 24, 1973), known professionally as <b>Madlib</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">DJ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Record_producer" title="Record producer">music producer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multi-instrumentalist" title="Multi-instrumentalist">multi-instrumentalist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">rapper</a>. He is widely known for his collaborations with <a href="/wiki/MF_Doom" title="MF Doom">MF Doom</a> (as <a href="/wiki/Madvillain" title="Madvillain">Madvillain</a>), <a href="/wiki/J_Dilla" title="J Dilla">J Dilla</a> (as <a href="/wiki/Jaylib" class="mw-redirect" title="Jaylib">Jaylib</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Gibbs" title="Freddie Gibbs">Freddie Gibbs</a> (as <a href="/wiki/MadGibbs" class="mw-redirect" title="MadGibbs">MadGibbs</a>). Madlib has described himself as a "DJ first, producer second, and MC last."<sup id="cite_ref-Aziri2002_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aziri2002-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> His stage name is an acronym for "Mind Altering Demented Lessons In Beats".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madlib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Otis Jackson Jr. was born on October 24, 1973,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Oxnard,_California" title="Oxnard, California">Oxnard, California</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> to musician parents Otis Jackson, Sr. and Dora Sinesca Jackson. He sampled his first song at 11 years old, sourced from his father's collection. His younger brother is the producer and rapper <a href="/wiki/Oh_No_(musician)" title="Oh No (musician)">Michael "Oh No" Jackson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> His uncle is the jazz trumpeter <a href="/wiki/Jon_Faddis" title="Jon Faddis">Jon Faddis</a>. He was raised in Oxnard, where he began his music career.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madlib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1993.E2.80.931998:_Early_career"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1993–1998:_Early_career">1993–1998: Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madlib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1993–1998: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1990s, Madlib formed a loose-knit collective composed of rappers who worked with him in his Oxnard-based "Crate Diggas Palace" studio.<sup id="cite_ref-cdp_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cdp-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This collective was composed primarily of his friends, and became known as CDP. The crew included affiliated artists such as Madlib's younger brother <a href="/wiki/Oh_No_(musician)" title="Oh No (musician)">Oh No</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kan_Kick" title="Kan Kick">Kankick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Perkins_(rapper)" title="Dudley Perkins (rapper)">Dudley Perkins</a> aka Declaime, <a href="/wiki/M.E.D._(rapper)" title="M.E.D. (rapper)">M.E.D.</a> aka Medaphor, and others. Madlib's first commercially released music was production for the rap group <a href="/wiki/Tha_Alkaholiks" title="Tha Alkaholiks">Tha Alkaholiks</a> in 1993. He went on to record music of his own with the group <a href="/wiki/Lootpack" title="Lootpack">Lootpack</a>. Their 12-inch EP <i>Psyche Move</i> was released by Madlib's father in 1995 on a label also called Crate Diggas Palace. This record caught the attention of <a href="/wiki/Peanut_Butter_Wolf" title="Peanut Butter Wolf">Peanut Butter Wolf</a>, founder of the <a href="/wiki/Stones_Throw_Records" title="Stones Throw Records">Stones Throw Records</a> label, who signed the group in 1998.
</p>

<h3><span id="1999.E2.80.932009:_Stones_Throw_Records"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1999–2009:_Stones_Throw_Records">1999–2009: Stones Throw Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madlib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1999–2009: Stones Throw Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lootpack's 1999 debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Soundpieces:_Da_Antidote" title="Soundpieces: Da Antidote">Soundpieces: Da Antidote</a></i> ushered in a string of releases on Stones Throw centering on Madlib's production work which would continue for a decade. His first solo work, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Unseen_(album)" title="The Unseen (album)">The Unseen</a></i>, under the guise of <a href="/wiki/Quasimoto" title="Quasimoto">Quasimoto</a>, came in 2000. The album was met with critical acclaim and named by <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Spin Magazine">Spin</a></i> as one of the top 20 albums of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2001, Madlib moved away from hip hop music and began a series of releases from Yesterdays New Quintet, a <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>-based, hip hop and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic</a>-influenced quintet made up of alter-egos or fictional musicians played by Madlib. Over the next several years, through several record releases on Stones Throw and other labels, the growing number of pseudonyms and fictional players came to be known as Yesterdays Universe.<sup id="cite_ref-ynq_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ynq-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Madlib was later invited to remix tracks from the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Note_Records" title="Blue Note Records">Blue Note Records</a> archive in 2003, which he released as <i><a href="/wiki/Shades_of_Blue_(Madlib_album)" title="Shades of Blue (Madlib album)">Shades of Blue</a></i>. In addition to the remixes, the album contained newly recorded interpretations of Blue Note originals, many of which were credited to members of Yesterdays New Quintet. Beginning with the 2007 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Funky_Side_of_Life" title="The Funky Side of Life">The Funky Side of Life</a></i> by Yesterdays New Quintet spin-off group Sound Directions, the Yesterdays Universe also began incorporating additional session musicians who were not pseudonyms of Madlib.
</p><p>Returning to hip hop music in 2003, Madlib announced two collaborative projects. He joined hip hop producer <a href="/wiki/J_Dilla" title="J Dilla">J Dilla</a> in a duo known as <a href="/wiki/Jaylib" class="mw-redirect" title="Jaylib">Jaylib</a>, which released <i><a href="/wiki/Champion_Sound" title="Champion Sound">Champion Sound</a></i>. Madlib then collaborated with rapper <a href="/wiki/MF_Doom" title="MF Doom">MF Doom</a>, known together as <a href="/wiki/Madvillain" title="Madvillain">Madvillain</a>. Produced by using a <a href="/wiki/Boss_SP-303" title="Boss SP-303">Boss SP-303</a> and a turntable, their 2004 album <i><a href="/wiki/Madvillainy" title="Madvillainy">Madvillainy</a></i> was highly anticipated and well-received, topping many critics' year-end lists.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2005 Quasimoto album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Further_Adventures_of_Lord_Quas" title="The Further Adventures of Lord Quas">The Further Adventures of Lord Quas</a></i> met with warm reception<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and continued the Quasimoto tradition of using vocal samples from <a href="/wiki/Melvin_Van_Peebles" title="Melvin Van Peebles">Melvin Van Peebles</a>, who is credited on the album liner notes as a collaborator. Throughout the rest of the decade Madlib continued to release jazz material simultaneously with his hip hop work: <i><a href="/wiki/Perseverance_(Percee_P_album)" title="Perseverance (Percee P album)">Perseverance</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Percee_P" title="Percee P">Percee P</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Liberation_(Talib_Kweli_and_Madlib_album)" title="Liberation (Talib Kweli and Madlib album)">Liberation</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Talib_Kweli" title="Talib Kweli">Talib Kweli</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sujinho" title="Sujinho">Sujinho</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Conti" title="Ivan Conti">Ivan Conti</a> of <a href="/wiki/Azymuth" title="Azymuth">Azymuth</a>, his own instrumental hip hop series <a href="/wiki/Beat_Konducta" title="Beat Konducta">Beat Konducta</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/In_Search_of_Stoney_Jackson" title="In Search of Stoney Jackson">In Search of Stoney Jackson</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Strong_Arm_Steady" title="Strong Arm Steady">Strong Arm Steady</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/O._J._Simpson_(album)" title="O. J. Simpson (album)">O. J. Simpson</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Guilty_Simpson" title="Guilty Simpson">Guilty Simpson</a>, and production work for artists such as <a href="/wiki/Erykah_Badu" title="Erykah Badu">Erykah Badu</a> and <a href="/wiki/De_La_Soul" title="De La Soul">De La Soul</a>.
</p>

<h3><span id="2010.E2.80.93present:_Madlib_Invazion.2C_Madlib_Medicine_Show"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2010–present:_Madlib_Invazion,_Madlib_Medicine_Show">2010–present: Madlib Invazion, <i>Madlib Medicine Show</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madlib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010–present: Madlib Invazion, Madlib Medicine Show">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Maclib" redirects here. For macro library, see <a href="/wiki/Macro_(computer_science)" title="Macro (computer science)">Macro (computer science)</a>.</div>
<p>In 2010, Madlib announced his own <a href="/wiki/Imprint_label" class="mw-redirect" title="Imprint label">imprint</a> called <a href="/wiki/Madlib_Invazion_(label)" class="mw-redirect" title="Madlib Invazion (label)">Madlib Invazion</a>, formed to release a music series called <a href="/wiki/Madlib_Medicine_Show" title="Madlib Medicine Show">Madlib Medicine Show</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mms_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mms-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The series would ultimately take over two years to complete, culminating with 13 album releases and several vinyl-only EPs spanning hip hop, jazz, remixes, and multi-genre DJ mixtapes. The label has continued to release records outside of the original series. In 2011, Madlib composed the <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">film score</a> for the <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">A Tribe Called Quest</a> documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/Beats,_Rhymes_%26_Life:_The_Travels_of_A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="Beats, Rhymes &amp; Life: The Travels of A Tribe Called Quest">Beats, Rhymes &amp; Life: The Travels of A Tribe Called Quest</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-beats_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beats-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Madlib also produced "Cadillacs" with <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a> for his mixtape <i><a href="/wiki/That%27s_My_Work_Volume_3" class="mw-redirect" title="That&#39;s My Work Volume 3">That's My Work Volume 3</a></i>, released on February 27, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-complex-madlibsnoop_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex-madlibsnoop-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Freddie_Gibbs" title="Freddie Gibbs">Freddie Gibbs</a> and Madlib announced plans for a collaboration album late in 2011 with the release of an EP titled <i><a href="/wiki/Thuggin%27_(EP)" title="Thuggin&#39; (EP)">Thuggin</a></i>, which was followed by a second EP titled <i><a href="/wiki/Shame_(EP)" title="Shame (EP)">Shame</a></i> on June 22, 2012, and a third EP titled <i><a href="/wiki/Deeper_(EP)" title="Deeper (EP)">Deeper</a></i> on September 24, 2013. The duo's full-length collaboration album <i><a href="/wiki/Pi%C3%B1ata_(Freddie_Gibbs_and_Madlib_album)" title="Piñata (Freddie Gibbs and Madlib album)">Piñata</a></i> was released on March 18, 2014, to widespread critical acclaim. The pair, later known as <a href="/wiki/MadGibbs" class="mw-redirect" title="MadGibbs">MadGibbs</a>, released a follow-up album titled <i><a href="/wiki/Bandana_(album)" title="Bandana (album)">Bandana</a></i> on June 28, 2019.
</p><p>In a 2010 interview with <a href="/wiki/LA_Weekly" title="LA Weekly">LA Weekly</a>, Madlib stated that <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> put five of his beats on hold for the album he was working on at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-laweekly-madlib_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laweekly-madlib-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> While none of the beats were used, Madlib did take part in the recording sessions for the album, which evolved from <i>Good Ass Job</i> to <i><a href="/wiki/My_Beautiful_Dark_Twisted_Fantasy" title="My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy">My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx-madlibwest_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx-madlibwest-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> He was also rumored to be involved in West's collaboration album with <a href="/wiki/Jay-Z" title="Jay-Z">Jay-Z</a> entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Watch_the_Throne" title="Watch the Throne">Watch the Throne</a></i>, but ultimately was not.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx-madlibthrone_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx-madlibthrone-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> West was interviewed as part of the 2014 Stones Throw documentary film <i>Our Vinyl Weighs A Ton</i>, in which he opens up about working with Madlib and wanting more of his beats for future projects.<sup id="cite_ref-rs-madlibwest_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-madlibwest-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 18, 2016, West released the Madlib-produced "No More Parties in L.A." featuring <a href="/wiki/Kendrick_Lamar" title="Kendrick Lamar">Kendrick Lamar</a> on <a href="/wiki/SoundCloud" title="SoundCloud">SoundCloud</a> as part of his <a href="/wiki/GOOD_Fridays" title="GOOD Fridays">GOOD Fridays</a> series.<sup id="cite_ref-st-madlibwestla_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-st-madlibwestla-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> According to reports, the track originated from the recording sessions for <i>My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy</i> in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-complex-madlibwestla_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex-madlibwestla-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> West also recited a few lines from the track in the <i>Our Vinyl Weighs A Ton</i> interview.<sup id="cite_ref-rs-madlibwest_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-madlibwest-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> "No More Parties in L.A." appears on his seventh album, which underwent several name changes: <i>So Help Me God</i>, <i>SWISH</i>, and <i>Waves</i>, before finally being released as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_of_Pablo" title="The Life of Pablo">The Life of Pablo</a></i> on February 12, 2016. West also hinted at the possibility of future collaborations with Madlib via <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> thanking him for sending over six beat CDs.<sup id="cite_ref-fader-madlibwest_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fader-madlibwest-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following <a href="/wiki/Mac_Miller" title="Mac Miller">Mac Miller</a>'s death, Chicago producer Thelonious Martin claimed on February 20, 2019, that Mac Miller and Madlib were reportedly working on an album together, called MacLib.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Madlib addressed this statement on March 19, 2019, stating that he had recorded an EP with Mac Miller between 2015 and 2017, but that there were no plans of releasing the EP.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> However, Madlib added during an interview on June 3, 2019, that if Mac Miller's estate gives him the right to, he will release the EP.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> On February 8, 2020, a MacLib song was leaked.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> On March 28, 2023, Madlib revealed in an interview on <i>Sway in the Morning</i> that he was "finishing up" his collaborative effort with the late Miller, and that the rapper's estate was on board with a release.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2021, in an interview with The Guardian, Madlib revealed he missed the opportunity to join forces with Kendrick Lamar on his 2015 album, <i><a href="/wiki/To_Pimp_a_Butterfly" title="To Pimp a Butterfly">To Pimp a Butterfly</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 23, 2021, fellow rapper <a href="/wiki/Logic_(rapper)" title="Logic (rapper)">Logic</a> released "Mars Only pt. 3," a collaboration between him and Madlib, on his YouTube channel under the name Madgic.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_style_and_influences">Musical style and influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madlib&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Musical style and influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During an interview on <a href="/wiki/Chrome_Children" title="Chrome Children">Chrome Children</a>, Madlib stated that his most significant musical influences include <a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">Miles Davis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Ra" title="Sun Ra">Sun Ra</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Axelrod_(musician)" title="David Axelrod (musician)">David Axelrod</a>. Some of his hip hop influences are <a href="/wiki/Large_Professor" title="Large Professor">Large Professor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marley_Marl" title="Marley Marl">Marley Marl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_C" title="Paul C">Paul C</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Pooh" title="DJ Pooh">DJ Pooh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
