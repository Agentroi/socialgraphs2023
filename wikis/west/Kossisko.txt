<p><b>Kossisko Konan</b> (1991/1992–present), known <a href="/wiki/Mononymous_person" class="mw-redirect" title="Mononymous person">mononymously</a> as <b>Kossisko</b> and formerly known as <b>100s</b>, is an American singer/rapper and songwriter from <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a>. In 2012, Kossisko, as rapper 100s, released his debut mixtape <i>Ice Cold Perm</i>. He was signed to <a href="/wiki/Fool%27s_Gold_Records" title="Fool&#39;s Gold Records">Fool's Gold Records</a> in June 2013. Following the release of the single "Ten Freaky Hoes", it was announced that he would be releasing his second mixtape, an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> titled <i>Ivry</i>, which was released on March 7, 2014, via Fool's Gold. It featured a greater <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">g-funk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disco_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Disco music">disco</a> influence than his debut mixtape. He is also well known for his song "Life of a Mack", which appeared on the <a href="/wiki/The_Music_of_Grand_Theft_Auto_V" class="mw-redirect" title="The Music of Grand Theft Auto V">soundtrack</a> of the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Theft_Auto_V" title="Grand Theft Auto V">Grand Theft Auto V</a></i>. At the start of 2015, he retired the 100s persona and now performs under the name Kossisko (which is his birth name). As Kossisko, he released his first single "<a href="/wiki/This_May_Be_Me_(single)" class="mw-redirect" title="This May Be Me (single)">This May Be Me</a>" on February 17, 2015. The track was premiered by <a href="/wiki/The_Fader" title="The Fader">The Fader</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Later that year, Kossisko released his debut EP, <i><a href="/wiki/Red_White_N_Cruel" title="Red White N Cruel">Red White N Cruel</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 2016, Kossisko launched a crowdfunding campaign to help fund his work on a short horror film titled '2037.'<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kossisko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kossisko was born to a Jewish mother and African father.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He was raised in <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a>. At age 16, due to disciplinary issues, he was sent by his father to a remote boarding school in <a href="/wiki/Abidjan" title="Abidjan">Abidjan</a>, inside the <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>. Kossisko was told that the Africa trip was a vacation, but he was left at the school indefinitely. At the boarding school, he lived in a three-bedroom house with 15 people. He contracted <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> five times. At one point, Kossisko ran away from the boarding school and fled to the local American embassy. Despite his pleas, his parents refused to allow him to return to the U.S., and because he was under the age of 18, he had to remain in <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>. After his attempt at escape, he was sent to the small town of <a href="/wiki/Bouafl%C3%A9" title="Bouaflé">Bouaflé</a> where he lived with his uncle. Not long after, he and his uncle's family moved to <a href="/wiki/Yamoussoukro" title="Yamoussoukro">Yamoussoukro</a>. While in <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>, he learned to speak <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Kossisko returned to the United States in late 2010.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_influence">Cultural influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kossisko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cultural influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> honored the artistry of Kossisko as 100s by remixing the cover of <i>IVRY</i> as a marketing tool for the comic book studios latest project.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kossisko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Musical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kossisko wrote his first song in 2008 and recorded for the first time in early 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He began to take rapping seriously in 2011 at the age of 19.<sup id="cite_ref-uproxx1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uproxx1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Following his stay in the Ivory Coast, Kossisko moved back to Berkeley where he began recording his debut mixtape as 100s titled, <i>Ice Cold Perm</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> On September 5, 2012, 100s released <i>Ice Cold Perm</i>, which was produced largely by Joe Wax. The cover artwork was inspired by the cover of <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Doggfather" title="Tha Doggfather">Tha Doggfather</a></i>, which he called a classic album.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> On the mixtape he also collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Main_Attrakionz" title="Main Attrakionz">Main Attrakionz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Hemsworth" title="Ryan Hemsworth">Ryan Hemsworth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-uproxx1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uproxx1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Brandon Soderberg of <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> commented on it saying, "This is snarling, no-nonsense pimp-rap, celebrating exploitation and preemptively lashing out at everyone trying to get anything over on 100s, but it finds a way to complicate those regressive, retro sensibilities".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> On October 18, 2012, the Noisey subsidiary of <i><a href="/wiki/Vice_(magazine)" title="Vice (magazine)">Vice</a></i> premiered the music video for "Brick Sell Phone".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In an interview with <i>Vice</i> later that month, Kossisko said his next project would be titled <i>Sex Symbols</i>, which he had already started recording. He described it as different saying, "It's more influenced by R&amp;B. I've got some singers on there. It's going to be more funky, too. What I wanted to do, as best as I could, was have a fusion between [19]90s mack-rap and late [19]80s pop and soul. Something like <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Brown" title="Bobby Brown">Bobby Brown</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rick_James" title="Rick James">Rick James</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> On January 23, 2013, <i>Ice Cold Perm</i> was released for <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">digital download</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In early 2013, he went on the Group Hug Tour with <a href="/wiki/Kreayshawn" title="Kreayshawn">Kreayshawn</a>, then performed five shows at 2013's <a href="/wiki/SXSW" class="mw-redirect" title="SXSW">SXSW</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-uproxx1_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uproxx1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 4, 2013, 100s released the music video for "1999". This was followed by the announcement of his signing to <a href="/wiki/A-Trak" title="A-Trak">A-Trak</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Fool%27s_Gold_Records" title="Fool&#39;s Gold Records">Fool's Gold Records</a> later that week.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The following month <i><a href="/wiki/Complex_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Complex (magazine)">Complex</a></i> named him a new rapper to watch out for.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> His song "Life of a Mack" was featured on the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Theft_Auto_V" title="Grand Theft Auto V">Grand Theft Auto V</a></i>, on the station Radio Los Santos.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Off the popularity it gained from appearing in the game, 100s released it on September 17, 2013, as his debut single.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Around the same time he was featured in an <a href="/wiki/IPhone_5c" title="IPhone 5c">iPhone 5c</a> commercial.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Then from October to December 11, 2013, 100s toured with <a href="/wiki/ASAP_Ferg" title="ASAP Ferg">ASAP Ferg</a> on the Turnt x Burnt <a href="/wiki/Concert_tour" title="Concert tour">concert tour</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> While on tour he released "Keep a Bitch Broke", a new collaboration with Aston Matthews, <a href="/wiki/Joey_Fatts" title="Joey Fatts">Joey Fatts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Da$h" class="mw-redirect" title="Da$h">Da$h</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He was named one of the ten <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast</a> rappers to watch for during 2014 by The BoomBox.<sup id="cite_ref-theboombox1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theboombox1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 26, 2014, 100s released the song "Ten Freaky Hoes" and revealed that he would be releasing a free <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> titled <i>Ivry</i>, replacing the previously announced <i>Sex Symbols</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> "Ten Freaky Hoes" is a "melodic G-funk jam that adds a dose of Californian psychedelia to his formerly frozen atmospherics."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <i>Ivry</i> was subsequently set for a March 2014 release. "Ten Freaky Hoes", is also most notably the song that ended the existence of 100s, with a short letter from the artist placed at the end of the music video saying, "To the 100s fans, I appreciate each and everyone of you but its now time for me to count my journey. So this is goodbye. -Kossisko".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The EP was then released on March 6, 2014, by Fool's Gold, being premiered by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fader" title="The Fader">The Fader</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It was met with positive reviews from <a href="/wiki/Music_critic" class="mw-redirect" title="Music critic">music critics</a> including writers for <i>The Fader</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Stereogum" title="Stereogum">Stereogum</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Fact_(UK_magazine)" title="Fact (UK magazine)">Fact</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Now_(newspaper)" title="Now (newspaper)">Now</a></i>, Julia Leconte said "It will make you smile till your face hurts as you press repeat for the 16th time, but to steal one of his song titles, 100s isn't "fuckin around." He presents himself with sophistication, and you take him seriously as a legit mack rapper rather than a farcical <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a> type wearing ridiculous oversize velvet or fur."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Nick Catucci of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> credited him for bring back <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">g-funk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 2016 interview, Kossisko described feeling "reborn" after he retired the 100s character and reinvented himself as Kossisko.<sup id="cite_ref-Culture_Creature_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_Creature-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> He also explained that his work as 100s was partially related to anger towards his mother after the traumatic experience of being sent to Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Culture_Creature_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_Creature-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
