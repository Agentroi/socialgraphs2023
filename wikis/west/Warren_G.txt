<p><b>Warren Griffin III</b> (born November 10, 1970) is an American rapper, DJ, and producer known for his role in <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast rap</a>'s 1990s ascent.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A pioneer of <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a>, he attained mainstream success with the 1994 single "<a href="/wiki/Regulate_(song)" title="Regulate (song)">Regulate</a>", a duet with Nate Dogg. He significantly helped <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>'s career during the latter's beginnings, also introducing him to <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, who later signed Snoop Dogg.
</p><p>His debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Regulate..._G_Funk_Era" title="Regulate... G Funk Era">Regulate... G Funk Era</a></i>, debuted at #2 on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a> chart, selling 176,000 in its opening week. The album later went on the sell over 3 million copies in the US and was certified <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_certification" title="Music recording certification">3x multi-platinum</a>. The single "<a href="/wiki/Regulate_(song)" title="Regulate (song)">Regulate</a>" spent 18 weeks in the Top 40 of the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a>, with three weeks at No. 2, while "<a href="/wiki/This_D.J." title="This D.J.">This D.J.</a>", reached No. 9. Both songs earned <a href="/wiki/1995_Grammy" class="mw-redirect" title="1995 Grammy">Grammy</a> nominations.
</p><p>Three songs from his second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Take_a_Look_Over_Your_Shoulder" title="Take a Look Over Your Shoulder">Take a Look Over Your Shoulder</a></i>, reached the Top 40,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> as did his 1998 duet with Nate Dogg, "<a href="/wiki/Nobody_Does_It_Better_(Nate_Dogg_song)" title="Nobody Does It Better (Nate Dogg song)">Nobody Does It Better</a>". Both <i>Take a Look Over Your Shoulder</i> and his next album, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Want_It_All_(album)" title="I Want It All (album)">I Want It All</a></i>, were certified gold, the last of his albums to attain certification. His 2001 comeback attempt, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_Regulator" title="The Return of the Regulator">The Return of the Regulator</a></i>, failed to reach his earlier commercial heights. Along with longtime collaborators <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>, he formed the hip-hop trio <a href="/wiki/213_(group)" title="213 (group)">213</a>, named for <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach</a>'s area code, releasing in 2004 an album titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hard_Way_(213_album)" title="The Hard Way (213 album)">The Hard Way</a></i>. 
</p><p>His next two albums, 2005's <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Mid-Nite_Hour" title="In the Mid-Nite Hour">In the Mid-Nite Hour</a></i> and then 2009's <i><a href="/wiki/The_G_Files" title="The G Files">The G Files</a></i>, released independently, were self-produced. In the 2010s, he experienced a resurgence in popularity amid the <a href="/wiki/Information_Age" title="Information Age">digital age</a>. In 2015, he released <i><a href="/wiki/Regulate..._G_Funk_Era,_Part_II" title="Regulate... G Funk Era, Part II">Regulate... G Funk Era, Part II</a></i>, an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> featuring archived recordings of Nate Dogg, who died in 2011. In 2017, "Regulate", certified platinum in 1994, went multi-platinum, propelled by digital downloads.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Warren Griffin III was born on November 10, 1970, and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He had three sisters and was the only son of Warren Griffin Jr., an airplane mechanic, and Ola, a <a href="/wiki/Dietitian" title="Dietitian">dietician</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They divorced when Warren was 4 and he lived with his mother and three sisters in <a href="/wiki/East_Long_Beach" class="mw-redirect" title="East Long Beach">East Long Beach</a> until he was just about to start middle school.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1982, Warren went to live with his father in <a href="/wiki/North_Long_Beach,_Long_Beach,_California" title="North Long Beach, Long Beach, California">North Long Beach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> His new wife, Verna, had three children from a prior marriage,<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> one of whom was Andre Young, the soon-to-become <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> who in 1984 joined a leading DJ crew, the <a href="/wiki/World_Class_Wreckin%27_Cru" title="World Class Wreckin&#39; Cru">World Class Wreckin' Cru</a>, which by 1985 doubled as an electro rap group, which in 1987 put out the Los Angeles area's first rap recording under a major label.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:29_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:29-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> By then, a <a href="/wiki/Jordan_High_School_(Long_Beach,_California)" title="Jordan High School (Long Beach, California)">Jordan High School</a> student, Warren was playing football and running with friends.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1988, age 17, Warren was jailed for gun possession.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> While incarcerated, he took the nickname Warren G.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> By this time Dr. Dre was already beginning to experience success as the writer and <a href="/wiki/Record_producer" title="Record producer">record producer</a> for <a href="/wiki/Ruthless_Records" title="Ruthless Records">Ruthless Records</a>, as well as, as being a member of <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a> with Ruthless Records founder <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>. N.W.A’s landmark album, <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton" title="Straight Outta Compton">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>, was driving the Los Angeles area's rap scene to swiftly drop electro for gangsta.,<sup id="cite_ref-:29_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:29-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Once out of jail, Warren worked at the Long Beach shipyards<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>and began focusing on music after Dr. Dre taught him how to use a drum machine.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>By 1990, Warren G had formed the trio <a href="/wiki/213_(group)" title="213 (group)">213</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:13_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> with two longtime running mates, Nathaniel "<a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>" Hale and Calvin "<a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>" Broadus. 213 was a contributor to the <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a> sound to soon emerge in rap.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The trio dissolved after Warren G connected them to Dr. Dre.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> At that point, two solo careers were launched: Dr. Dre's and Snoop Dogg's, upon G-funk.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Nate, too, signed to Dr. Dre's <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Warren G initially helped there,<sup id="cite_ref-:13_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but, not desiring a career in his mentor and stepbrother's shadow, signed to <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Recordings" title="Def Jam Recordings">Def Jam Recordings</a>, in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:23_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:23-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Start_with_213_.28circa_1990.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Start_with_213_(circa_1990)">Start with 213 (circa 1990)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Start with 213 (circa 1990)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 1990, in his hometown <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach</a>, as record producer and rapper, Warren formed a music trio with two of his longtime running mates, Nathaniel "<a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>" Hale, a rapperlike singer, and Calvin "<a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Rock</a>" Broadus,<sup id="cite_ref-:24_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:24-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> a singerlike rapper.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Long Beach trio, fond of <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a> rap group <a href="/wiki/415_(group)" title="415 (group)">415</a>, named for the <a href="/wiki/Bay_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Bay area">Bay area</a>'s area code, took the name <a href="/wiki/213_(group)" title="213 (group)">213</a>, the Los Angeles area's.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Practicing and recording in the modest studio in Long Beach record store V.I.P.,<sup id="cite_ref-:24_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:24-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> they cut a demo tape.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, already a celebrity, rebuffed his younger stepbrother Warren's requests for him to listen.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before long, homemade copies of 213's songs spread in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_county" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles county">Los Angeles county</a>, particularly the cities <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pomona,_California" title="Pomona, California">Pomona</a>, and Los Angeles city's sections <a href="/wiki/Watts,_Los_Angeles" title="Watts, Los Angeles">Watts</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Los_Angeles" title="South Los Angeles">South Central</a>, but no label picked them up.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> One day, Warren phoned Dre to catch up, and found him at a bachelor party—thrown for Dre's friend Andre "LA Dre" Bolton, another record producer—whereupon Warren found himself invited to join it.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> There, once the songs began to repeat, Warren offered LA Dre the 213 tape.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Liking it, he summoned Dr. Dre, who, hearing the Snoop rap "Super Duper Snooper", immediately welcomed the trio.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Days later, 213 moved into Dre's lavish <a href="/wiki/Troubadour_style#Architecture" title="Troubadour style">troubadour-style</a> house in <a href="/wiki/Calabasas,_California" title="Calabasas, California">Calabasas</a>, home to both his wife and his recording studio.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 1992, Dr. Dre's debut solo single "<a href="/wiki/Deep_Cover_(song)" title="Deep Cover (song)">Deep Cover</a>" introduced America to Snoop Doggy Dogg, the track's guest but instantly star rapper.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:17_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:17-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Warren helped Dre find sounds for Dre's debut solo album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-:6_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:13_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> further debuting Snoop, whereby superstardom chased Snoop into 1993 and, via Snoop's own debut solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">Doggystyle</a></i>, captured him by 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> By then, also solo, Nate, too, had joined Dre's label, <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Warren, returning to Long Beach, aimed to find his own way.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:18_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:18-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 2004, a 213 album finally arrived: <a href="/wiki/The_Hard_Way_(213_album)" title="The Hard Way (213 album)"><i>The Hard Way</i></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:17_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:17-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Solo_stardom_.281993.E2.80.931996.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_stardom_(1993–1996)">Solo stardom (1993–1996)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solo stardom (1993–1996)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During 1993, at Dr. Dre's studio, Warren met <a href="/wiki/John_Singleton" title="John Singleton">John Singleton</a>, director of <i><a href="/wiki/Boyz_n_the_Hood" title="Boyz n the Hood">Boyz n the Hood</a></i>, the seminal film named for <a href="/wiki/Boyz-n-the-Hood" title="Boyz-n-the-Hood">Eazy-E's debut single</a>, produced by Dre.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Singleton asked Warren to produce a song for <a href="/wiki/Poetic_Justice_(soundtrack)" title="Poetic Justice (soundtrack)">the soundtrack</a> of his forthcoming film <i><a href="/wiki/Poetic_Justice_(film)" title="Poetic Justice (film)">Poetic Justice</a></i>. Warren thus produced <a href="/wiki/Mista_Grimm" title="Mista Grimm">Mista Grimm</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Indo_Smoke" title="Indo Smoke">Indo Smoke</a>", featuring Warren G and <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg" title="Nate Dogg">Nate Dogg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The single's success led to Warren's invitation to <a href="/wiki/Russell_Simmons" title="Russell Simmons">Russell Simmons</a>'s label <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam_Recordings" title="Def Jam Recordings">Def Jam Recordings</a>, where Warren G signed a record deal.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Also that year, Warren and Nate, along with <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a>—whom the 213 trio had brought to Dre to help on his album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-:4_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>—feature on "Ain't No Fun", a huge underground hit, too risque to be a single, on Snoop's <i><a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">Doggystyle</a></i> album, released in November.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the <i><a href="/wiki/Above_The_Rim" class="mw-redirect" title="Above The Rim">Above The Rim</a></i> soundtrack, from <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> in April 1994, the single "<a href="/wiki/Regulate_(song)" title="Regulate (song)">Regulate</a>" was a duet cowritten and performed by Warren G and Nate Dogg. Spending 20 weeks on the popular songs chart, the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a>, with 18 of them in the Top 40, including three weeks at No. 2 in May,<sup id="cite_ref-:8_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> it was the summer's top rap hit.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Certified gold, half a million copies sold, since June, it attained platinum, a million copies, in August.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In January 2017, via digital downloading, it was certified 2x multi-platinum.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Back in the American summer of 1994, it stood at No. 1 on the <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a> charts.<sup id="cite_ref-Vernallis_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vernallis-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Performing in Japan, he would discover fans who apparently understood no English, but knew all the lyrics. Into the 21st century, it remained Def Jam's biggest hit single.<sup id="cite_ref-:19_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:19-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Russell Simmons, a Def Jam founder, explains, "Warren's music was worldwide because the melody plays no matter what the language."<sup id="cite_ref-:23_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:23-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yet further, unlike other G-funk, short for <i>gangsta</i> funk, Warren G, even called "a romantic" at heart,<sup id="cite_ref-:32_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:32-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> voiced simpler concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-:31_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:31-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> And his modest rap styling maximized, by heeding, his modest lyricism.<sup id="cite_ref-:25_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:25-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> "Regulate" doubled as the lead single Warren G's debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Regulate..._G_Funk_Era" title="Regulate... G Funk Era">Regulate... G Funk Era</a></i>, arriving in June 1994. Selling a million copies in three days, it debuted at No. 2 on the popular albums chart, the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">Billboard 200</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_3-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In August, it was certified 2x multi-platinum, two million copies sold.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Its second single, "<a href="/wiki/This_D.J." title="This D.J.">This D.J.</a>", went gold, half a million copies, in September,<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> while peaking in July at No. 9.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/1995_Grammy_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="1995 Grammy Awards">1995 Grammy Awards</a>, in March, both singles were nominated.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> And in January, the album's other single, "<a href="/wiki/Do_You_See" title="Do You See">Do You See</a>", had peaked at No. 42.<sup id="cite_ref-:14_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:14-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In August, the album was certified 3x multi-platinum.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> That month also brought some Warren G collaborations on two albums from his Long Beach associates, <a href="/wiki/Twinz" title="Twinz">Twinz</a> only album<i><a href="/wiki/Conversation_(album)" title="Conversation (album)">Conversation (album)</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/The_Dove_Shack" title="The Dove Shack">The Dove Shack</a> trio's <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_the_Shack" title="This Is the Shack">This Is the Shack</a></i>. And 1996 saw Warren G on the "Groupie" track of Snoop's second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Doggfather" title="Tha Doggfather">Tha Doggfather</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Follow-up_albums_.281997.E2.80.932001.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Follow-up_albums_(1997–2001)">Follow-up albums (1997–2001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Follow-up albums (1997–2001)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Warren G's second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Take_a_Look_Over_Your_Shoulder" title="Take a Look Over Your Shoulder">Take a Look Over Your Shoulder</a></i>, released in March 1997, was certified gold, half a million copies sold, in May.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Sharing with the <a href="/wiki/Supercop_(soundtrack)" title="Supercop (soundtrack)"><i>Supercop</i> soundtrack</a> the single "<a href="/wiki/What%27s_Love_Got_to_Do_with_It_(Warren_G_song)" class="mw-redirect" title="What&#39;s Love Got to Do with It (Warren G song)">What's Love Got To Do with It</a>", featuring singer <a href="/wiki/Adina_Howard" title="Adina Howard">Adina Howard</a>, a spin on the <a href="/wiki/What%27s_Love_Got_to_Do_with_It_(song)" title="What&#39;s Love Got to Do with It (song)">1984 single</a> by <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>, reached No. 2 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and peaked in the US at No. 32 on the Billboard Hot 100.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Smokin%27_Me_Out" title="Smokin&#39; Me Out">Smokin' Me Out</a>", featuring <a href="/wiki/Ron_isley" class="mw-redirect" title="Ron isley">Ron Isley</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Isley_Brothers" title="The Isley Brothers">the classic soul group</a>, reaching No. 35, was big on the Los Angeles area's radio play.<sup id="cite_ref-:25_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:25-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> "I Shot the Sheriff", a lyrical spin on the <a href="/wiki/I_Shot_the_Sheriff" title="I Shot the Sheriff">1973 single</a> by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley_%26_The_Wailers" class="mw-redirect" title="Bob Marley &amp; The Wailers">Bob Marley &amp; the Wailers</a>, yet an instrumental borrow from rap group <a href="/wiki/EPMD" title="EPMD">EPMD</a>'s 1988 single "<a href="/wiki/Strictly_Business_(EPMD_song)" title="Strictly Business (EPMD song)">Strictly Business</a>", which itself samples that Wailers classic, reached No. 20.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Yet a letdown overall, the album missed his debut's superstar potential.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 1998, Warren G's sixth appearance in the Billboard Hot 100's upper tier Top 40 became Nate Dogg's single "<a href="/wiki/Nobody_Does_It_Better_(Nate_Dogg_song)" title="Nobody Does It Better (Nate Dogg song)">Nobody Does it Better</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-:8_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>—on <a href="/wiki/G-Funk_Classics,_Vol._1_%26_2" title="G-Funk Classics, Vol. 1 &amp; 2">Nate's repeatedly delayed debut album</a>—featuring Warren G, in another duet, which peaked at No. 18 on the Billboard Hot 100.<sup id="cite_ref-:14_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:14-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Here, incidentally, Warren raps a <a href="/wiki/Bar_(music)" title="Bar (music)">bar</a> indicating his transition to family life.<sup id="cite_ref-:30_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:30-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Warren's third album, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Want_It_All_(album)" title="I Want It All (album)">I Want It All</a></i>, released in October 1999, has Warren mainly producing—where, perhaps, his greater comparative strength among musical peers abides—while vocals go largely to guest artists, including Nate Dogg, Snoop Dogg, <a href="/wiki/RBX" title="RBX">RBX</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eve_(rapper)" title="Eve (rapper)">Eve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slick_Rick" title="Slick Rick">Slick Rick</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jermaine_Dupri" title="Jermaine Dupri">Jermaine Dupri</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Certified gold in November 1999,<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> it bears the single "<a href="/wiki/I_Want_It_All_(Warren_G_song)" title="I Want It All (Warren G song)">I Want It All</a>", featuring <a href="/wiki/Mack_10" title="Mack 10">Mack 10</a>, which, becoming Warren's most recent Top 40 appearance, peaked on the Hot 100 at No. 23.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Over 20 years later, his 1997 and 1999 albums remain at gold certification, which none of his subsequent albums have achieved.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Released in December 2001, Warren's fourth album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_Regulator" title="The Return of the Regulator">The Return of the Regulator</a></i>, with a litany of collaborators, including the <a href="/wiki/P-Funk" class="mw-redirect" title="P-Funk">P-Funk</a> father and G-funk godfather <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)">George Clinton</a> and, elsewhere, Dr. Dre producing a track, is allegedly overdone, a comeback undone by Warren's reaching beyond his strengths and being outdone by his guests.<sup id="cite_ref-:20_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:20-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:12_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> He "wastes a hot, Dre-produced beat", in the single "<a href="/wiki/Lookin%27_at_You_(song)" title="Lookin&#39; at You (song)">Lookin' at You</a>", alleges a <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> writer, who finds G-funk on its deathbed and Warren G "administering the fatal shot".<sup id="cite_ref-:12_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The album peaked at number 83 the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, and became his final album under a <a href="/wiki/Major_record_label" class="mw-redirect" title="Major record label">major record label</a>, here <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal Music Group</a>, before returned on an independent label.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Indie_career_.282005.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Indie_career_(2005–present)">Indie career (2005–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Indie career (2005–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Mid-Nite_Hour" title="In the Mid-Nite Hour">In the Mid-Nite Hour</a></i>, released in October 2005, Warren G's fifth album, his first without a major label involved,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> was on Hawino Records.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Heavily featuring his native, 213 groupmates Nate and Snoop, it is devotedly Warren's own project, homemade on a low budget.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Music critics assess it to better carry Warren G's own virtues as G-funk's <a href="/wiki/Everyman" title="Everyman">everyman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_36-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:10_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Yet by that very virtue, as expected, it saw scarce exposure beyond Warren G's fans.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_36-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:10_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His sixth album, in September 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/The_G_Files" title="The G Files">The G Files</a></i>, "still the same basic G-funk sound", adds to "that classic soul vibe", Warren explains, "a taste of that modern electro sound".<sup id="cite_ref-:11_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Disliking what he put as the rap standard of "some drums and one synth sound", he titled "The West is Back" for return to "that great soulful sound".<sup id="cite_ref-:11_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> "100 Miles and Running" features Nate Dogg—recorded before Nate's strokes in 2007 and 2008—and the <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">Raekwon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From June to September 2013, Warren toured in the West Coast Fest, "an OG affair" with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Quik" title="DJ Quik">DJ Quik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mack_10" title="Mack 10">Mack 10</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dogg_Pound" class="mw-redirect" title="Dogg Pound">Dogg Pound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone_Thugs_N_Harmony" class="mw-redirect" title="Bone Thugs N Harmony">Bone Thugs N Harmony</a>, and others.<sup id="cite_ref-:21_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:21-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Meanwhile, in a guest role, Warren played OG Hemingway in the sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Newsreaders" title="Newsreaders">Newsreaders</a></i> on the <a href="/wiki/Cartoon_Network" title="Cartoon Network">Cartoon Network</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Adult_Swim" title="Adult Swim">Adult Swim</a> programming.<sup id="cite_ref-:27_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:27-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> And in August 2014, on the <a href="/wiki/Mnet_(TV_channel)" title="Mnet (TV channel)">Mnet</a> channel's reality series <i>American Hustle Life</i>, he directed an alternate music video for "Boy In Luv", by South Korean boy band <a href="/wiki/BTS" title="BTS">BTS</a>.
</p><p>Nostalgic fans would ask Warren for more of classic <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a>, and even ask for more from Nate Dogg, who had died in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:26_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:26-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The single "<a href="/wiki/My_House_(Warren_G_song)" title="My House (Warren G song)">My House</a>", leading Warren G's first <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a>, arrived on July 13, 2015. With four songs, the EP, premised as a sequel to the 1994 original, is titled <i><a href="/wiki/Regulate..._G_Funk_Era,_Part_II" title="Regulate... G Funk Era, Part II">Regulate... G Funk Era, Part II</a></i>. Released on August 6, it features <a href="/wiki/E-40" title="E-40">E-40</a>, <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeezy" title="Jeezy">Jeezy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bun_B" title="Bun B">Bun B</a>, and, in all four songs, Nate Dogg. With his unique knack for intuiting Warren's production cues, Nate leaves behind some of his 213 partner's favorite recordings.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Warren has four children with his wife, Tenille Griffin. Getting older, increasingly identifying with his father, fond of cooking and storytelling, Warren G embraces "his morals and good family fun".<sup id="cite_ref-:28_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:28-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>His oldest son, <a href="/wiki/Olaijah_Griffin" title="Olaijah Griffin">Olaijah</a>, played college football for the <a href="/wiki/USC_Trojans_football" title="USC Trojans football">USC Trojans</a> at the cornerback position from 2018 to 2020 and was recognized with all-conference honors in 2019 and 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-:16_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:16-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In April 2021, Olaijah was signed by the NFL's <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bills" title="Buffalo Bills">Buffalo Bills</a> as an undrafted free agent. 
</p><p>In 2019, Warren G launched a line of barbecue sauces and rubs, Sniffin Griffin's BBQ, for retail and restaurant supply. This was inspired by his father, a cook in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">US Navy</a> and avid barbecue chef.<sup id="cite_ref-:28_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:28-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:22_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
