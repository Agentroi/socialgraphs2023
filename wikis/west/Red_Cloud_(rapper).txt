<p><b>Henry Andrade</b> (born November 18, 1978), known professionally as <b>RedCloud</b>, is an American rapper based in Los Angeles, California<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> who creates hip hop tracks recognizably influenced by his spiritual and Indigenous heritage.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Cloud_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Henry Andrade<sup id="cite_ref-TPTAONE_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TPTAONE-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CBI_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBI-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> was born on November 18,<sup id="cite_ref-TWEET_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TWEET-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 1978<sup id="cite_ref-CBI_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBI-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Hawthorne,_California" title="Hawthorne, California">Hawthorne, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CBI_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBI-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He is of Indigenous <a href="/wiki/Mexican_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican people">Mexican</a> heritage (<i><a href="/wiki/Huichol" title="Huichol">Wixáritari</a>)</i>. He became a confessed, or "born again"  believer in Jesus Christ in 1991 while in the eighth grade and at that time repented from being a gang member.<sup id="cite_ref-CBNSTORY_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBNSTORY-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Cloud_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moving from gangs to hip hop and gospel in the early 90s, RedCloud signed with Syntax Records in 2000<sup id="cite_ref-JFHP_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFHP-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMP_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMP-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and recorded his first studio album <i>Is This Thing On?</i> which was released in April 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-JFHAONE_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFHAONE-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMAONE_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMAONE-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This album received two four and a half star out of five reviews by <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a>'s Jo-Ann Greene and Scott Fryberger of Jesus Freak Hideout,<sup id="cite_ref-JFHAONE_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFHAONE-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMAONE_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMAONE-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> yet <i>The Phantom Tollbooth</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s Brian A. Smith rated it three clocks out of a possible five.<sup id="cite_ref-TPTAONE_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TPTAONE-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His second release, <i>Traveling Circus</i>, was released on December 16, 2003 with Syntax.<sup id="cite_ref-JFHATWO_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFHATWO-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMATWO_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMATWO-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The album received a five star review from Jesus Freak Hideout's Scott Fryberger,<sup id="cite_ref-JFHATWO_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFHATWO-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> while attaining a four star rating from Jo-Ann Greene at AllMusic.<sup id="cite_ref-AMATWO_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMATWO-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/Cross_Rhythms" title="Cross Rhythms">Cross Rhythms</a>' Tony Cummings rated it a nine out of ten,<sup id="cite_ref-CRATWO_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRATWO-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rapzilla" title="Rapzilla">Rapzilla</a> rated it three and a half R's.<sup id="cite_ref-RAPZILLA_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RAPZILLA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Tony LaFlanza, indicating for <i>The Phantom Tollbooth</i>, rated the album a four out of five clock release.<sup id="cite_ref-TPTATWO_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TPTATWO-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> His most recent album, <i>Hawthorne's Most Wanted</i>, was his third studio album with Syntax Records that came out on May 22, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-JFHATHREE_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFHATHREE-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AMATHREE_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMATHREE-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Scott Fryberger rated the album four stars,<sup id="cite_ref-JFHATHREE_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFHATHREE-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> while Jo-Ann Greene rated it four and a half stars.<sup id="cite_ref-AMATHREE_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMATHREE-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Wayne Gough, rated the album nine out of ten for Cross Rhythms,<sup id="cite_ref-CRATHREE_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRATHREE-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and <i>The Phantom Tollbooth'</i>s Bert Saraco rated it four clocks.<sup id="cite_ref-TPTATHREE_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TPTATHREE-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> RedCloud released a <a href="/wiki/Mix_tape" class="mw-redirect" title="Mix tape">mix tape</a> in 2006, <i>The Warriors Society</i>, again with Syntax Records.<sup id="cite_ref-JFHMIX_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFHMIX-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> RedCloud moved more than 50,000 units of his releases in his decade with the label. He toured with <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">KRS-One</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immortal_Technique" title="Immortal Technique">Immortal Technique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evidence_(musician)" title="Evidence (musician)">Evidence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tech_N9ne" title="Tech N9ne">Tech N9ne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Murs_(rapper)" title="Murs (rapper)">Murs</a>, among others.
</p><p>After splitting with Syntax Records in 2010, he formed the independent 1491 Nations record label and released his underground mix tape, <i>1491 Nation Presents: MC RedCloud</i> (2011). The following year, RedCloud subsequently redefined his music with the help of collaborators <a href="/wiki/Crystle_Lightning" title="Crystle Lightning">Crystle Lightning</a> and DJ Hydroe, creating a performance crew known for its electro-house/hip hop fusion featuring RedCloud’s underground spit. Called LightningCloud, Sam Slovik of the L.A. Weekly called the crew, "a near-earth object inventing new realms of the Electro-House-Hip-Hop revolution on the planet. Urban futurist[s], MC RedCloud and Crystle Lightning are L.A.’s subterranean Bonnie and Clyde." Their self-titled debut (2012) is an eclectic mix of straight hip hop (“Burn It Down”), the digital groove of <a href="/wiki/Electro_house" title="Electro house">electro house</a> dance styles (e.g. “Zoom,” or “Gravitron”), hip hop inflected French <a href="/wiki/Y%C3%A9-y%C3%A9" title="Yé-yé">yé-yé</a> (“Hang It Up Daddy”), with a twist of B-movie slasher flick <a href="/wiki/Tango_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tango (music)">tango</a> (“Zombie Love”). He travels North America as a performance artist and is known for songs that travel the globe in geography, message, and experience. His collaborative LightningCloud album (2012) features the work of time-honored collaborators, including <a href="/wiki/Pigeon_John" title="Pigeon John">Pigeon John</a>, DJ Wise, and producer Greg “DJ Ei8ht” Leonti. The <i>LightningCloud</i> album received recognition from the <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Peoples_Choice_Music_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Aboriginal Peoples Choice Music Awards">Aboriginal Peoples Choice Music Awards</a> (APCMA) for Best Hip Hop Album in November 2012.
</p><p>In 2013, RedCloud battled against MCs across Southern California to win the “Who’s Next: Battle for the Best” contest on the number one hip hop station in the United States, <a href="/wiki/Power_106" class="mw-redirect" title="Power 106">Power 106</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> As winners, his LightningCloud crew received a cash prize, a performance with <a href="/wiki/Kendrick_Lamar" title="Kendrick Lamar">Kendrick Lamar</a>, and represented west coast hip hop against <a href="/wiki/WQHT" title="WQHT">Hot 97’s</a> Brooklyn-based, east coast representative <a href="/wiki/Radamiz" title="Radamiz">Radamiz</a> in a freestyle battle in Austin, Texas on March 15, 2013. After winning that MC battle,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> LightningCloud received the opportunity to use a beat produced with <a href="/wiki/Timbaland" title="Timbaland">Timbaland</a> for a subsequent track called <i>Sake Bombs.</i>
</p><p>RedCloud’s rhyme connects to <a href="/wiki/Native_American_hip_hop" title="Native American hip hop">Native American hip hop</a>, a musical genre popular with American and Canadian Indigenous peoples. Like comparable MCs old and new (including <a href="/wiki/Litefoot" title="Litefoot">Litefoot</a>, <a href="/wiki/RezOfficial" class="mw-redirect" title="RezOfficial">RezOfficial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joey_Stylez" title="Joey Stylez">Joey Stylez</a>, and <a href="/wiki/War_Party_(band)" title="War Party (band)">War Party</a>, among many others), RedCloud raps about Native issues, politics, and social consciousness. He also fuses traditional Native musics to contemporary hip hop style and provides workshops and performances to raise the Indigenous consciousness of Tribal youth.
</p><p>In 2014, RedCloud broke the <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Record</a> for freestyle rapping that was previously set at 17 hours, and he went 18 hours one minute and 14 seconds.<sup id="cite_ref-HIPHOPDX_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HIPHOPDX-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
