<p><b>Tracy Lynn Curry</b> (born June 10, 1968), better known as <b>The D.O.C.</b>, is an American rapper, songwriter, and record producer. In addition to a solo career, he was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_hip_hop" title="Southern hip hop">Southern hip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Fila_Fresh_Crew" title="Fila Fresh Crew">Fila Fresh Crew</a> and later collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_rap" class="mw-redirect" title="Hardcore rap">hardcore rap</a> group <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>–where he co-wrote many of their releases–as well as <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>'s solo debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Eazy-Duz-It" title="Eazy-Duz-It">Eazy-Duz-It</a></i>. He has also worked with <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, co-writing his <a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">solo debut album</a>, while Dre produced Curry's <a href="/wiki/No_One_Can_Do_It_Better" title="No One Can Do It Better">solo debut album</a>, released by <a href="/wiki/Ruthless_Records" title="Ruthless Records">Ruthless Records</a>. He was one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suge_Knight" title="Suge Knight">Suge Knight</a>.
</p><p>After Fila Fresh Crew split up in 1988, the D.O.C. went on to pursue a successful solo career. In 1989, he released his debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/No_One_Can_Do_It_Better" title="No One Can Do It Better">No One Can Do It Better</a></i>, which reached number-one on the US <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart for two weeks and spawned two number one hits on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Rap_Songs" title="Hot Rap Songs">Hot Rap Songs</a> chart: "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_Funky_Enough" title="It&#39;s Funky Enough">It's Funky Enough</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/The_D.O.C._%26_The_Doctor" title="The D.O.C. &amp; The Doctor">The D.O.C. &amp; The Doctor</a>". The album went platinum five years after its release. In late 1989, months after the release of <i>No One Can Do It Better</i>, the D.O.C. suffered a serious car crash that permanently changed his voice. Since his accident, he has released two more albums, <i><a href="/wiki/Helter_Skelter_(The_D.O.C._album)" title="Helter Skelter (The D.O.C. album)">Helter Skelter</a></i> in 1996 and <i><a href="/wiki/Deuce_(The_D.O.C._album)" title="Deuce (The D.O.C. album)">Deuce</a></i> in 2003. In 2015, he said his voice was fully recovered.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_D.O.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tracy Lynn Curry was born on June 10, 1968, in Dallas, Texas. As a teenager, Curry began his career as a member of <a href="/wiki/Fila_Fresh_Crew" title="Fila Fresh Crew">Fila Fresh Crew</a>, a hip hop trio that originated in Dallas, Texas. While in the group, Curry was known as <b>Doc-T.</b>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_D.O.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fila_Fresh_Crew">Fila Fresh Crew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_D.O.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fila Fresh Crew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1987, Fila Fresh Crew had four songs featured on the compilation album <i><a href="/wiki/N.W.A_and_the_Posse" class="mw-redirect" title="N.W.A and the Posse">N.W.A and the Posse</a></i> which featured various other artists; the same four tracks would later appear on the group's album <i><a href="/wiki/Tuffest_Man_Alive" title="Tuffest Man Alive">Tuffest Man Alive</a></i>, which was released in 1988. Though the album would produce three singles, the group disbanded not long after its release. By this point, Curry had moved to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and become acquainted with members of <i>N.W.A</i> and <a href="/wiki/Ruthless_Records" title="Ruthless Records">Ruthless Records</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ruthless_Records_and_No_One_Can_Do_It_Better">Ruthless Records and <i>No One Can Do It Better</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_D.O.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ruthless Records and No One Can Do It Better">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Curry would begin using the name the D.O.C. after he was signed to <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ruthless_Records" title="Ruthless Records">Ruthless Records</a>. The D.O.C. contributed lyrics to <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>'s debut studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton" title="Straight Outta Compton">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-whoz_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whoz-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and performed the opening verse on "Parental Discretion Iz Advised". Curry also wrote for Eazy-E's debut studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Eazy-Duz-It" title="Eazy-Duz-It">Eazy-Duz-It</a></i> and co-wrote "Keep Watchin'" from <a href="/wiki/Michel%27le" title="Michel&#39;le">Michel'le</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Michel%27le_(album)" title="Michel&#39;le (album)">self-titled debut album</a>.
</p><p>In 1989, the D.O.C. released his solo debut, the <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>-produced <i><a href="/wiki/No_One_Can_Do_It_Better" title="No One Can Do It Better">No One Can Do It Better</a></i>. The album was very well received by critics, and sold well, peaking at no. 20 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> for two consecutive weeks; by 1994, the album reached Platinum status.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" class="mw-redirect" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> gives the album a five-star rating and describes it as "an early landmark of West Coast Rap" as well as "an undeniable masterpiece". <i>No One Can Do It Better</i> produced five singles and five music videos.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Automobile_crash_and_move_to_Death_Row">Automobile crash and move to Death Row</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_D.O.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Automobile crash and move to Death Row">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 1989, five months after the release of <i>No One Can Do It Better</i>, Curry was involved in a near-fatal car crash. Driving home from a party, he <a href="/wiki/Sleep-deprived_driving" class="mw-redirect" title="Sleep-deprived driving">fell asleep at the wheel</a> and his car veered off the freeway. Curry, who was not wearing a <a href="/wiki/Seat_belt" title="Seat belt">seat belt</a>, was thrown out the rear window, slamming face-first into a tree. His injuries required 21 hours of plastic surgery, and he spent 2½ weeks in the hospital. He could not speak for about a month, and he was left with a different, raspier voice.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In a 2015 interview with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Vlad" title="DJ Vlad">DJ Vlad</a>, Curry stated that he was under the influence of alcohol and cannabis and was actually pulled over before the accident but the police let him go.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The D.O.C. continued to write for N.W.A and contributed lyrics and minor vocals to their 1990 EP <i><a href="/wiki/100_Miles_and_Runnin%27" title="100 Miles and Runnin&#39;">100 Miles and Runnin'</a></i>, where he co-wrote all the songs except for "Just Don't Bite It" and "Kamurshol", and their final album <i><a href="/wiki/Niggaz4Life" title="Niggaz4Life">Niggaz4Life</a></i>.
</p>

<p>In 1991, the D.O.C. left Ruthless Records along with Dr. Dre and Michel'le to sign with newly founded <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a>. The D.O.C. also used his talents as one of the writers for Dr. Dre's debut solo album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i>, contributing to the tracks "<a href="/wiki/Nuthin%27_but_a_G_Thang" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuthin&#39; but a G Thang">Nuthin' but a G Thang</a>", "Lil' Ghetto Boy", "A Nigga Witta Gun", "Lyrical Gangbang" and "<a href="/wiki/Bitches_Ain%27t_Shit" title="Bitches Ain&#39;t Shit">Bitches Ain't Shit</a>".  He also appeared on the skit track "The $20 Sack Pyramid".  He is referenced by name in "<a href="/wiki/Nuthin%27_but_a_G_Thang" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuthin&#39; but a G Thang">Nuthin' but a G Thang</a>", and appears in the song's video as well.  The liner notes to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i> say, "I want to give a special shout out to The D.O.C. for talking me into doin' this album."  His name is mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a> in the <a href="/wiki/The_Chronic_Intro" class="mw-redirect" title="The Chronic Intro">intro</a> of the album. ("Peace to da D.O.C., still makin' it funky enough").
</p><p>In addition to <i>The Chronic</i>, the D.O.C. worked on Snoop Dogg's debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">Doggystyle</a></i>, and added some vocals on the song "Serial Killa". The D.O.C. continued to be a <a href="/wiki/Ghostwriter" title="Ghostwriter">ghostwriter</a> for various songs on Dr. Dre and Snoop Dogg albums. "<a href="/wiki/Real_Muthaphuckkin_G%27s" title="Real Muthaphuckkin G&#39;s">Real Muthaphuckkin G's</a>", from <a href="/wiki/Eazy-E" title="Eazy-E">Eazy-E</a>'s 1993 extended play <i><a href="/wiki/It%27s_On_(Dr._Dre)_187um_Killa" title="It&#39;s On (Dr. Dre) 187um Killa">It's On (Dr. Dre) 187um Killa</a></i>, includes lyrics sampled from "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_Funky_Enough" title="It&#39;s Funky Enough">It's Funky Enough</a>" in the song's hook.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feud_with_Dr._Dre_and_Helter_Skelter">Feud with Dr. Dre and <i>Helter Skelter</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_D.O.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feud with Dr. Dre and Helter Skelter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1996, the D.O.C. attempted a comeback following the car crash which severely damaged his vocal cords. The album, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Helter_Skelter_(The_D.O.C._album)" title="Helter Skelter (The D.O.C. album)">Helter Skelter</a></i>, produced two singles with music videos. <i>Helter Skelter</i> was widely ignored and even somewhat discredited by the D.O.C. himself.<sup id="cite_ref-ruthless_to_death_row_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ruthless_to_death_row-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The name of the album is a reference to <a href="/wiki/Charles_Manson" title="Charles Manson">Charles Manson</a>'s idea of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Helter_Skelter_(song)" title="Helter Skelter (song)">Helter Skelter</a>" prophesying the end of the world.
</p><p>The title and concept behind this album were originally developed by Dr. Dre as a collaborative effort between him and <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>, titled <i>Heltah Skeltah</i>. At that time, however, the D.O.C. had become disillusioned with <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a> and Dre, having received no payment for his work <a href="/wiki/Ghostwriter" title="Ghostwriter">ghostwriting</a> at Death Row.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> So in late 1994, D.O.C. decided to leave Death Row and headed to <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>. Taking lyrics he had already written for <i>Heltah Skeltah</i>, he recorded <i>Helter Skelter</i>, keeping the name to spite Dre.<sup id="cite_ref-ruthless_to_death_row_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ruthless_to_death_row-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> His lyrics were inspired by the writings of <a href="/wiki/Milton_William_Cooper" title="Milton William Cooper">Milton William Cooper</a>. Especially noticeable in songs <i>Secret Plan</i> and <i>Welcome to the New World</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Silverback_Records_and_Deuce">Silverback Records and <i>Deuce</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_D.O.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Silverback Records and Deuce">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1997, the D.O.C. founded his own Dallas-based record label, <b>Silverback Records</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The D.O.C. introduced Dallas rapper 6Two to Dre, who featured him on his 1999 comeback album <i><a href="/wiki/2001_(Dr._Dre_album)" title="2001 (Dr. Dre album)">2001</a></i>; Curry also provided lyrics for the album.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> On July 20, 2000, the D.O.C. appeared on stage with Dr. Dre and Snoop Dogg at <a href="/wiki/The_Centrum" class="mw-redirect" title="The Centrum">The Centrum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Worcester,_Massachusetts" title="Worcester, Massachusetts">Worcester, Massachusetts</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Up_in_Smoke_Tour" title="Up in Smoke Tour">Up in Smoke Tour</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, the D.O.C. released his third album entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Deuce_(The_D.O.C._album)" title="Deuce (The D.O.C. album)">Deuce</a></i> on Silverback Records. The album was originally meant to be a 6Two album, completely produced by Dr. Dre and released through <a href="/wiki/Aftermath_Entertainment" title="Aftermath Entertainment">Aftermath Entertainment</a>. However, D.O.C. and Dre argued over whether D.O.C. should be rapping on the album.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The D.O.C.'s presence on this album is minimal however, making an appearance to introduce tracks or perform in skits such as "My Prayer" and "Souliloquy". The only single released from <i>Deuce</i> was "<a href="/wiki/Deuce_(The_D.O.C._album)#Singles" title="Deuce (The D.O.C. album)">The Shit</a>", which features former-<a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a> members <a href="/wiki/MC_Ren" title="MC Ren">MC Ren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>, along with Snoop Dogg and 6Two. <i>Deuce</i> focuses primarily on showcasing other artists on D.O.C.'s Silverback Records label, including U.P.-T.I.G.H.T., El Dorado, and in particular, 6Two.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_D.O.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The D.O.C. wrote lyrics for Snoop Dogg's album <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Blue_Carpet_Treatment" title="Tha Blue Carpet Treatment">Tha Blue Carpet Treatment</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In December 2006, the D.O.C. revealed that he was working on his fourth album, entitled <i>Voices</i>, and stated that it would be released after Dr. Dre's upcoming fourth album <i><a href="/wiki/Detox_(Dr._Dre_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Detox (Dr. Dre album)">Detox</a></i>, which was scrapped in favor of 2015's <i><a href="/wiki/Compton_(Dr._Dre_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Compton (Dr. Dre album)">Compton</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In a May 2008 interview, the D.O.C. stated that he and Dre were working on the album, explaining "There is an album, and you got the title, but that's also because that's the title Dre likes. Dre and I decided to do another D.O.C. album after this <i>Detox</i> record. We decided to do one more together and end our story the right way."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dubcnn.com_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dubcnn.com-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the release of the biopic <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton_(film)" title="Straight Outta Compton (film)">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>, the D.O.C. revealed that his natural voice had returned if he had concentrated, and that he recorded new music, although he was not ready to release anything yet. Although he is not mentioned in the songwriting credits, the D.O.C. claimed that he helped write Dr. Dre's third album <i><a href="/wiki/Compton_(album)" title="Compton (album)">Compton</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2022, he collaborated with Codefendants, a new group formed by <a href="/wiki/Ceschi" title="Ceschi">Ceschi</a> and Sam King from <a href="/wiki/Get_Dead" title="Get Dead">Get Dead</a>, on a track called "The Fast Ones". This marked the first new recording featuring D.O.C. in 19 years.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_appearances">Media appearances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_D.O.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Media appearances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>He made a guest appearance in <a href="/wiki/Shyne" title="Shyne">Shyne</a>'s music-video for the song "That's Gangsta", which <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">samples</a> the same beat D.O.C. uses for his first hit "It's Funky Enough", <a href="/wiki/Foster_Sylvers" title="Foster Sylvers">Foster Sylvers</a>'s "Misdemeanor". The song "Lend Me An Ear" was featured on Lakai skate shoe's video "<a href="/wiki/Fully_Flared" title="Fully Flared">Fully Flared</a>".</li>
<li>He made an appearance in the documentary <i>We From Dallas</i> (2014), a film dedicated to telling the history of hip hop from the Dallas perspective.</li>
<li>The D.O.C. is portrayed by actor Marlon Yates Jr. in the 2015 <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a> biopic <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton_(2015_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Straight Outta Compton (2015 film)">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>.</li>
<li>He made an appearance in the series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Defiant_Ones_(documentary)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Defiant Ones (documentary)">The Defiant Ones</a></i> (2017), a four-episode series that analyzes the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Iovine" title="Jimmy Iovine">Jimmy Iovine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, alongside a number of those involved in their partnership.</li>
<li>He made an appearance in episode five of the series <i><a href="/wiki/Winning_Time:_The_Rise_of_the_Lakers_Dynasty" title="Winning Time: The Rise of the Lakers Dynasty">Winning Time: The Rise of the Lakers Dynasty</a></i> (2022).</li></ul>
