<p><b>Roger McBride</b> (born December 14, 1968),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> better known by his stage name <b>King T</b> (formerly known as <b>King Tee</b>), is an American <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast Hip hop rapper</a> from <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton, California</a>. Emerging as one of Compton's earliest hip hop artists, he was signed to <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a>, where he released his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Act_a_Fool_(album)" title="Act a Fool (album)">Act a Fool</a></i> in 1988 with the hit singles "Act a Fool," "Payback's A Mutha," "The Coolest," and "Bass” [Remix], all of which were considered hip-hop classics. In the late 1980s and early 1990s, he worked primarily with producer <a href="/wiki/DJ_Pooh" title="DJ Pooh">DJ Pooh</a>, and was responsible for the rise of <a href="/wiki/Tha_Alkaholiks" title="Tha Alkaholiks">Tha Alkaholiks</a>, whom he helped guide into the rap game.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> King T is also the <a href="/wiki/CEO" class="mw-redirect" title="CEO">CEO</a> of his own record label, King T Inc.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Roger McBride was born in <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton, California</a> on December 14, 1968 and has attended high school. He had been around the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> hip-hop scene for many years alongside <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kid_Frost" class="mw-redirect" title="Kid Frost">Kid Frost</a> and is widely considered a pioneer in the genre. In 1988, he made his debut with <i>Act a Fool</i>, which is considered a classic of <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip-hop" class="mw-redirect" title="West Coast hip-hop">West Coast hip-hop</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tha_Alkaholiks">Tha Alkaholiks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tha Alkaholiks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During his time with Capitol, T began mentoring a young trio of rappers called <a href="/wiki/Tha_Alkaholiks" title="Tha Alkaholiks">Tha Alkaholiks</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-LarkinDM_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LarkinDM-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> consisting of <a href="/wiki/Tash_(rapper)" title="Tash (rapper)">Tash</a>, <a href="/wiki/J-Ro" class="mw-redirect" title="J-Ro">J-Ro</a> and <a href="/wiki/E-Swift" class="mw-redirect" title="E-Swift">E-Swift</a>. He also helped mentor their loosely affiliated collective, the <a href="/wiki/Likwit_Crew" title="Likwit Crew">Likwit Crew</a>. The Likwit Crew also includes others, such as <a href="/wiki/Xzibit" title="Xzibit">Xzibit</a>, who would later rise to fame and bring King T into the orbit of Compton producer <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>. Tha Alkaholiks put out their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/21_%26_Over_(album)" title="21 &amp; Over (album)">21 &amp; Over</a></i> under King T's guidance on <a href="/wiki/Loud_Records" title="Loud Records">Loud Records</a> in 1993.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Notorious_B.I.G._influence">The Notorious B.I.G. influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Notorious B.I.G. influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>T greatly influenced <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> with his deep voice, flow and rhyme style, which Biggie would at times imitate on his 1994 album <i><a href="/wiki/Ready_to_Die" title="Ready to Die">Ready to Die</a></i>. Tee later paid homage to Biggie on the track "6 In'a Moe'nin" on his album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kingdom_Come" title="The Kingdom Come">The Kingdom Come</a></i>, using a similar setup to and vocal samples of Biggie's track “Somebody Gotta Die". In 2010, <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a> confirmed in an interview that King T was one of Biggie's favorite rappers.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Record_deals">Record deals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Record deals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After 1993's <i><a href="/wiki/Tha_Triflin%27_Album" title="Tha Triflin&#39; Album">Tha Triflin' Album</a></i>, on which T worked with <a href="/wiki/Marley_Marl" title="Marley Marl">Marley Marl</a>, he left Capitol for <a href="/wiki/MCA_Records" title="MCA Records">MCA</a> and put out <i><a href="/wiki/IV_Life" title="IV Life">IV Life</a></i> in 1994. After leaving MCA as well, he built with <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> and ended up signing to the producer's label, <a href="/wiki/Aftermath_Entertainment" title="Aftermath Entertainment">Aftermath Entertainment</a>. However, T only released three songs while on the label: "Str8 Gone" and "Fame”, which appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre_Presents_the_Aftermath" class="mw-redirect" title="Dr. Dre Presents the Aftermath">Dr. Dre Presents the Aftermath</a></i>, and "Some L.A. Niggaz", which appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/2001_(Dr._Dre_album)" title="2001 (Dr. Dre album)">2001</a></i>. The two began working on King T's Aftermath album, but the project was subject to repeated push-backs. Eventually, King T requested a release from the label.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_with_Ant_Banks">Work with Ant Banks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Work with Ant Banks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>King T has also worked extensively with <a href="/wiki/Oakland" class="mw-redirect" title="Oakland">Oakland</a> rapper and producer <a href="/wiki/Ant_Banks" title="Ant Banks">Ant Banks</a>. He first appeared on Banks' 1997 compilation <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Thangs" title="Big Thangs">Big Thangs</a></i>, on the song "West Riden". In 2000 he was featured on <i><a href="/wiki/Lead_the_Way_(album)" title="Lead the Way (album)">Lead the Way</a></i>, an album by Banks' supergroup T.W.D.Y., on the song "No Win Situation".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="San_Andreas:_The_Original_Mixtape">San Andreas: The Original Mixtape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: San Andreas: The Original Mixtape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/San_Andreas:_The_Original_Mixtape" title="San Andreas: The Original Mixtape">San Andreas: The Original Mixtape</a></i> is the debut album by American West Coast rapper Young Maylay, released July 5, 2005. King T co-wrote and produced most of the songs on the album. He made appearances and some productions on songs "Liq Hittaz", "That’s Real", “Inna Ghetto", “Boss Up Freestyle", “Twist A Corner", “Salute'n G'z", and "Speak On It". The album was released by Maylay's record label, Maylaynium Musiq.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Releases">Releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, King T independently released his Aftermath album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kingdom_Come" title="The Kingdom Come">The Kingdom Come</a></i>, produced by Dr. Dre and <a href="/wiki/Battlecat_(producer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Battlecat (producer)">DJ Battlecat</a>. He also released <i>The Ruthless Chronicles</i> in 2004, which had songwriting by T's protégé <a href="/wiki/Young_Maylay" title="Young Maylay">Young Maylay</a> and featured some of the songs heard on <i>The Kingdom Come</i>, as well as others produced by <a href="/wiki/DJ_Quik" title="DJ Quik">DJ Quik</a>. King T also guested on <a href="/wiki/DJ_Muggs" title="DJ Muggs">DJ Muggs</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Soul_Assassins_II" title="Soul Assassins II">Soul Assassins II</a></i> album.
</p><p>King T was mentioned on <a href="/wiki/Nas_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nas (rapper)">Nas</a>' song “Where Are They Now?"  in 2006, and in 2007 appeared on the song's West Coast remix, along with <a href="/wiki/Kam_(rapper)" title="Kam (rapper)">Kam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a>, <a href="/wiki/Candyman_(rapper)" title="Candyman (rapper)">Candyman</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Threat_(rapper)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Threat (rapper) (page does not exist)">Threat</a>,  and <a href="/wiki/The_Conscious_Daughters" title="The Conscious Daughters">The Conscious Daughters</a>, MC Breeze of LA Posse, and the <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>-based artist <a href="/wiki/Sir_Mix-A-Lot" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Mix-A-Lot">Sir Mix-A-Lot</a>. After this appearance, King T put out a mixtape called <i>Boss Up, Vol. 1</i> with music by <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Game_(rapper)" title="The Game (rapper)">The Game</a>, J-Ro, and several other West Coast artists. In 2006, he made a guest appearance on the song "Poppin' Off" from Xzibit's album <i><a href="/wiki/Full_Circle_(Xzibit_album)" title="Full Circle (Xzibit album)">Full Circle</a></i>. He has also started his own label, though no artists are yet signed to it.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A remix of King T's song "Money" appeared on Dr. Dre's son Hood Surgeon's <i>The Autopsy Mixtape</i>. The song was renamed "Fast Money" and features King T and Dr. Dre. The original song was on <i>The Kingdom Come</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His daughter, Heaven, died at the age of 16 after a car accident on May 19, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_events">Current events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Current events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>King T is currently signed to Table Records Music Distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In early 2013, King T announced he wants to record his final album. He stated that working on Xzibit's song "Louis XIII" motivated him to record his last album.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>King Tee (later known as King T), one of the West Coast's pioneering rappers, released a few major-label albums in the late 1980s and early 1990s. Though the Compton rapper enjoyed little commercial success, he is often cited<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2021)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as an influence upon the <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rappers</a> of the early to mid-1990s. He notably collaborated with a pair of premier West Coast rap producers, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Pooh" title="DJ Pooh">DJ Pooh</a> and <a href="/wiki/E-Swift" class="mw-redirect" title="E-Swift">E-Swift</a>, who were largely unknown at the time. In later years, King T resurfaced from time to time; for instance, he was featured on <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>'s as <i><a href="/wiki/2001_(Dr._Dre_album)" title="2001 (Dr. Dre album)">2001</a></i> and released some independent albums of his own, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kingdom_Come" title="The Kingdom Come">The Kingdom Come</a></i>.
</p>
