<p><b>Nissim Baruch Black</b> (born <b>Damian Jamohl Black</b>; December 9, 1986) is an American-Israeli rapper, songwriter, and record producer.
</p><p>Under the stage name <b>D. Black</b>, he released the albums <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cause_%26_Effect" title="The Cause &amp; Effect">The Cause &amp; Effect</a></i> (2006) and <i><a href="/wiki/Ali%27yah_(album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ali&#39;yah (album)">Ali'yah</a></i> (2009) and was featured on producer <a href="/wiki/Jake_One" title="Jake One">Jake One</a>'s debut album <i><a href="/wiki/White_Van_Music" title="White Van Music">White Van Music</a></i> (2008). He retired in 2011 to focus on his conversion to <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox Judaism</a>, but soon returned under his new legal name <b>Nissim Black</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> and began focusing on <a href="/wiki/Jewish_hip_hop" title="Jewish hip hop">Jewish hip hop</a>. He appeared on <a href="/wiki/Shtar" title="Shtar">Shtar</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Boss_EP" title="Boss EP">Rabbit Hole</a>" (2012) and has since released the mixtapes <i>Miracle Music</i> (2013) and <i>Love Notes</i> (2020), as well as the studio albums <i>Nissim</i> (2013), <i>Lemala</i> (2017), and <i>Gibor</i> (2019).
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nissim_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Black was born Damian Jamohl Black in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> on December 9, 1986, the son of rappers Mia Black and James "Captain Crunch" Croone, members of the pioneering Seattle hip hop groups the Emerald Street Boys and Emerald Street Girls.<sup id="cite_ref-tablet_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tablet-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amg_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amg-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> His grandparents had also been musicians, playing alongside <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quincy_Jones" title="Quincy Jones">Quincy Jones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-aish_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aish-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He grew up in Seattle's <a href="/wiki/Seward_Park,_Seattle" title="Seward Park, Seattle">Seward Park</a> neighborhood and was raised in his grandfather's <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a> faith, but was non-practicing and converted to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> at age 14 after attending an <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">evangelical</a> summer camp.<sup id="cite_ref-tablet_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tablet-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aish_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aish-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Prior to this conversion, he had been a member of the <a href="/wiki/Gangster_Disciples" title="Gangster Disciples">Gangster Disciples</a> gang, which he would later reference on the song "Mothaland Bounce".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> His parents divorced when he was two years old, and his mother took him with her and remarried shortly thereafter. Both his biological parents and stepfather used and sold drugs from home, prompting the <a href="/wiki/FBI" class="mw-redirect" title="FBI">FBI</a> to raid the house in 1995, leading to his mother's arrest; she later died from an overdose at the age of 37.<sup id="cite_ref-aish_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aish-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nissim_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="First_Sportn.27_Life_recordings:_Behind_the_Dirt_and_The_Cause_.26_Effect_.282002.E2.80.932007.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Sportn'_Life_recordings:_Behind_the_Dirt_and_The_Cause_&amp;_Effect_(2002–2007)">First Sportn' Life recordings: <i>Behind the Dirt</i> and <i>The Cause &amp; Effect</i> (2002–2007)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nissim_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First Sportn&#039; Life recordings: Behind the Dirt and The Cause &amp; Effect (2002–2007)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Cause_and_Effect" class="mw-redirect" title="The Cause and Effect">The Cause and Effect</a></div>
<p>Black began rapping in his preteens, recording under the name Danger. When he was 13, producer Vitamin D moved his studio into Black's stepfather's basement and began mentoring the boy, producing some of his first official recordings.<sup id="cite_ref-amg_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amg-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Black also recorded with producer Bean One of Dyme Def.<sup id="cite_ref-st1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-st1-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, Sportn' Life Records published its first release, a <a href="/wiki/Split_album" title="Split album">split</a> <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Vinyl record">vinyl</a> single between Black and the group Last Men Standing. The following year, Black appeared on a Sportn' Life <a href="/wiki/Compilation_album" title="Compilation album">compilation album</a>, alongside several members of <a href="/wiki/Oldominion" title="Oldominion">Oldominion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 2004, the 18-year-old Black was selected by his stepfather to replace him as co-<a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEO</a> of Sportn' Life, together with his father's partner, DeVon Manier.<sup id="cite_ref-amg_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amg-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-st1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-st1-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In addition to running the label, Black began releasing singles like "You Need a Thug" and "This Is Why" (the latter produced by <a href="/wiki/Jake_One" title="Jake One">Jake One</a>, whom he had associated with early on).<sup id="cite_ref-amg_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amg-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> On May 24, 2005, Black released his first Sportn' Life <a href="/wiki/Mixtape" title="Mixtape">mixtape</a> <i>Behind the Dirt</i>, which featured appearances from <a href="/wiki/J_Smooth" title="J Smooth">J Smooth</a> and Darrius Willrich of <a href="/wiki/Maktub" title="Maktub">Maktub</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007, he released his debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cause_%26_Effect" title="The Cause &amp; Effect">The Cause &amp; Effect</a></i>. Jake One, Bean One, and Vitamin D contributed to the production,<sup id="cite_ref-amg_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amg-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> though the album did not get much attention outside of the Pacific Northwest scene.<sup id="cite_ref-stranger_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stranger-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Despite this, the album sold over 4,500 copies regionally, and Black was invited to perform on the <a href="/wiki/The_Vera_Project" title="The Vera Project">Vera Project</a> stage at the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Hill_Block_Party" title="Capitol Hill Block Party">Capitol Hill Block Party</a> alongside <a href="/wiki/Blue_Scholars" title="Blue Scholars">Blue Scholars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Ali.27yah.2C_retirement.2C_and_conversion_to_Orthodox_Judaism_.282008.E2.80.932011.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ali'yah,_retirement,_and_conversion_to_Orthodox_Judaism_(2008–2011)"><i>Ali'yah</i>, retirement, and conversion to Orthodox Judaism (2008–2011)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nissim_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ali&#039;yah, retirement, and conversion to Orthodox Judaism (2008–2011)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ali%27yah_(album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ali&#39;yah (album)">Ali'yah (album)</a></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Nissim_at_Sas.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Nissim_at_Sas.jpg/220px-Nissim_at_Sas.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Nissim_at_Sas.jpg/330px-Nissim_at_Sas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Nissim_at_Sas.jpg/440px-Nissim_at_Sas.jpg 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="720" /></a><figcaption>Black performing in May 2013</figcaption></figure>
<p>After <i>The Cause and Effect</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> release, Black, now a husband and father, began questioning his Christian beliefs, turning to <a href="/wiki/Messianic_Judaism" title="Messianic Judaism">Messianic Judaism</a> and convincing his wife to follow suit.<sup id="cite_ref-tablet_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tablet-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aish_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aish-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He also started listening to artists like <a href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)" title="Common (rapper)">Common</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lauryn_Hill" title="Lauryn Hill">Lauryn Hill</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-stranger_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stranger-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> both developments began to strongly influence his music. In 2008, he contributed to Jake One's debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/White_Van_Music" title="White Van Music">White Van Music</a></i>, with the song "God Like", which received play on <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">MTV</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, Black released his second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Ali%27yah_(album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ali&#39;yah (album)">Ali'yah</a></i>. The album spent five weeks at number 4 on <a href="/wiki/CMJ" title="CMJ">CMJ</a>'s hip hop charts, while the video for its lead single, "Yesterday", also gained regular rotation on MTV.<sup id="cite_ref-aish_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aish-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> That same year, he performed at <a href="/wiki/Bumbershoot" title="Bumbershoot">Bumbershoot</a><sup id="cite_ref-st2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-st2-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and returned to Capitol Hill Block Party.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He was also one of the candidates to portray rapper <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a> in the 2009 biopic <i><a href="/wiki/Notorious_(2009_film)" title="Notorious (2009 film)">Notorious</a></i>, a role that ultimately went to <a href="/wiki/Jamal_Woolard" title="Jamal Woolard">Jamal Woolard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tablet_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tablet-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Six months after the album's release, Black renounced his belief in <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> and Christianity. No longer supporting the album's message but unable to quit his contract, he agreed to promote it, but refused to accept money outside of touring expenses and refused to perform on <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">Shabbat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-aish_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aish-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He officially retired in 2011 after releasing <i>The Blackest Brown</i> EP with friend and fellow rapper Bradley "B." Brown. He subsequently moved to Seward Park's Jewish community and began studying for conversion with Rabbi Simon Benzaquen at the <a href="/wiki/Sephardic_Bikur_Holim_Congregation" title="Sephardic Bikur Holim Congregation">Sephardic Bikur Holim Congregation</a>. During this time, he cut off all connections to his previous career, including abandoning his position at Sportn' Life and even getting rid of his own music collection.<sup id="cite_ref-tablet_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tablet-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Return_to_rapping.2C_Nissim.2C_and_Lemala_.282012.E2.80.932017.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_rapping,_Nissim,_and_Lemala_(2012–2017)">Return to rapping, <i>Nissim</i>, and <i>Lemala</i> (2012–2017)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nissim_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Return to rapping, Nissim, and Lemala (2012–2017)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Rapper-Nissim-Black.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Rapper-Nissim-Black.jpg/180px-Rapper-Nissim-Black.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="188" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Rapper-Nissim-Black.jpg/270px-Rapper-Nissim-Black.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Rapper-Nissim-Black.jpg/360px-Rapper-Nissim-Black.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3024" data-file-height="3155" /></a><figcaption>Black in September 2016</figcaption></figure>
<p>Near the end of his conversion process, Black's son developed <a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">meningitis</a> and had to be hospitalized, a blow to the financially struggling family. While praying over the situation in his study, Black discovered that a long-broken microphone had begun working again, and took this as a sign to return to music.<sup id="cite_ref-tablet_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tablet-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aish_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aish-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He officially announced his return to music in September 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-tablet_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tablet-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The following December, he appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Shtar" title="Shtar">Shtar</a> song "Rabbit Hole" from their album <i><a href="/wiki/Boss_EP" title="Boss EP">Boss EP</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 26, 2013, he released the mixtape <i>Miracle Music</i>, his first official recording under his new stage name, Nissim.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The following May, he appeared at the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Sasquatch!_Music_Festival" title="Sasquatch! Music Festival">Sasquatch! Music Festival</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tablet_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tablet-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Following a recording session in <a href="/wiki/London_Bridge_Studio" title="London Bridge Studio">London Bridge Studio</a>, he released his first new album, the self-titled <i>Nissim</i>, on September 17.<sup id="cite_ref-tablet_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tablet-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He closed out the year with "The Black Miracle", a <a href="/wiki/Hanukkah" title="Hanukkah">Hanukkah</a> single produced by <a href="/wiki/Aish_HaTorah" title="Aish HaTorah">Aish.com</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, after relocating to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, Nissim collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Gad_Elbaz" title="Gad Elbaz">Gad Elbaz</a> on the song "Hashem Melech 2.0" and with <a href="/wiki/Lipa_Schmeltzer" title="Lipa Schmeltzer">Lipa Schmeltzer</a> on  "Bar Mitzvah Time." The following year, on March 10, 2017, he released his fourth album, <i>Lemala</i>, which featured collaborations with Elbaz, <a href="/wiki/Shneur_Hasofer" title="Shneur Hasofer">DeScribe</a>, Netanel Israel, and Yisroel Laub.
</p>
<h3><span id="Gibor_and_Love_Notes_.282018.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Gibor_and_Love_Notes_(2018–present)"><i>Gibor</i> and <i>Love Notes</i> (2018–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nissim_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gibor and Love Notes (2018–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Black returned in July 2018 with the single "King of the World", announced as the lead single of an upcoming fifth album entitled <i>Gibor</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> That December, he performed at a charity <a href="/wiki/Hanukkah" title="Hanukkah">Hanukkah</a> concert in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> alongside Shea Rubenstein, <a href="/wiki/Mendy_Pellin" title="Mendy Pellin">Mendy Pellin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Six13" title="Six13">Six13</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <i>Gibor</i> was released on December 9, 2019 through Marom Entertainment, with guest features including Menachem Weinstein, Avi Kraus, and <a href="/wiki/Alex_Clare" title="Alex Clare">Alex Clare</a>. This was also the first official album to credit Black as Nissim Black rather than simply Nissim (he had been using both intermittently since 2016). 
</p><p>In February 2020, Black released a new standalone single, "Mothaland Bounce". The song, released with a <i><a href="/wiki/Coming_to_America" title="Coming to America">Coming to America</a></i>-inspired music video directed by Leon Robinson and Joel Kipnis, addresses the relationship between the rapper's Black and Jewish identities, such as Black describing himself as "<i><a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Hitler</a>'s worst nightmare</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Following two more singles, "Win" and "Best Friend", Black released a new mixtape entitled <i>Love Notes</i> on August 16, 2020.
</p><p>Since <i>Love Notes</i>, Black has released a string of singles and collaborated with artists including Yosef Daniel, <a href="/wiki/Kosha_Dillz" title="Kosha Dillz">Kosha Dillz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shlomo_Katz" title="Shlomo Katz">Shlomo Katz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Levi_Robin" title="Levi Robin">Levi Robin</a>. "Mothaland Bounce" peaked at No. 4 on the Israeli charts, while "The Hanukkah Song 2.0" and "Adored" peaked at No. 5 and No. 21 respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_work">Other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nissim_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 2020, Black was a guest on conservative commentator <a href="/wiki/Ben_Shapiro" title="Ben Shapiro">Ben Shapiro</a>'s podcast <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ben_Shapiro_Show" title="The Ben Shapiro Show">The Ben Shapiro Show</a></i>, where he defended hip hop against Shapiro's previous critiques of the genre.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Black started his own <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">podcast</a>, <i>The Deal with Nissim Black</i>, in May 2021 through SoulShop Studios; notable guests have included <a href="/wiki/Rudy_Rochman" title="Rudy Rochman">Rudy Rochman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanessa_Zoltan" title="Vanessa Zoltan">Vanessa Zoltan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatman_Scoop" title="Fatman Scoop">Fatman Scoop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meir_Kay" title="Meir Kay">Meir Kay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amar%27e_Stoudemire" title="Amar&#39;e Stoudemire">Amar'e Stoudemire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brandon_Tatum" title="Brandon Tatum">Brandon Tatum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamir_Goodman" title="Tamir Goodman">Tamir Goodman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coleman_Hughes" title="Coleman Hughes">Coleman Hughes</a>, and Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Tovia_Singer" title="Tovia Singer">Tovia Singer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> That same month, Black launched his own <a href="/wiki/Whisky" title="Whisky">whisky</a> brand, the Israeli-based Hava Whisky.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In April 2022, <i><a href="/wiki/Deadline_Hollywood" title="Deadline Hollywood">Deadline Hollywood</a></i> reported that <a href="/wiki/HBO_Max" class="mw-redirect" title="HBO Max">HBO Max</a> was developing <i>Motherland Bounce</i>, a comedy series based on Black's life, with comedian <a href="/wiki/Moshe_Kasher" title="Moshe Kasher">Moshe Kasher</a> announced as showrunner, Black as story writer, <a href="/wiki/Salli_Richardson" title="Salli Richardson">Salli Richardson</a> as director, and all three as executive producers.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nissim_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Black and his wife Adina, with whom he has seven children, were initially married in 2008; they participated in an <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox Jewish</a> marriage ceremony in 2013 at the <a href="/wiki/Sephardic_Bikur_Holim_Congregation" title="Sephardic Bikur Holim Congregation">Sephardic Bikur Holim Congregation</a> after both had converted. The marriage was a double ceremony shared with his childhood friend Yosef Brown and Yosef's wife Chana, both fellow converts.<sup id="cite_ref-jta_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jta-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Black and his family continued to live in <a href="/wiki/Seward_Park,_Seattle" title="Seward Park, Seattle">Seward Park</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> neighborhood where he grew up, until making <i><a href="/wiki/Aliyah" title="Aliyah">aliyah</a></i> to Israel in 2016 and settling in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> He self-identifies as a follower of <a href="/wiki/Breslov_(Hasidic_group)" class="mw-redirect" title="Breslov (Hasidic group)">Breslov Hasidism</a><sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and makes pilgrimage to <a href="/wiki/Uman" title="Uman">Uman</a> every <a href="/wiki/Rosh_Hashanah" title="Rosh Hashanah">Rosh Hashanah</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In an August 2018 interview with Menachem Toker of <a href="/wiki/Kol_Chai" title="Kol Chai">Radio Kol Chai</a>, Black revealed that his children had been subjected to <a href="/wiki/Racial_discrimination" title="Racial discrimination">racial discrimination</a> when they were denied admission to several local <a href="/wiki/Haredi_Judaism" title="Haredi Judaism">Haredi</a> <a href="/wiki/Yeshiva" title="Yeshiva">yeshivas</a> explicitly because they were black. He also said that he had sought advice on the matter from Rav <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Kanievsky" title="Chaim Kanievsky">Chaim Kanievsky</a> during a meeting and was told, "Being black is your <i>mayla</i> (advantage) and not a <i>chesaron</i> (disadvantage)."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Due to discrimination they experienced in the <a href="/wiki/Meah_Shearim" class="mw-redirect" title="Meah Shearim">Meah Shearim</a> neighborhood of Jerusalem, Black and his family relocated to the nearby city of <a href="/wiki/Beit_Shemesh" title="Beit Shemesh">Beit Shemesh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Black contracted <a href="/wiki/COVID-19" title="COVID-19">COVID-19</a> in July 2020 and was hospitalized at <a href="/wiki/Shaare_Zedek_Medical_Center" title="Shaare Zedek Medical Center">Shaare Zedek Medical Center</a> in Jerusalem; he and his family remained in quarantine until <a href="/wiki/Tisha_B%27Av" title="Tisha B&#39;Av">Tisha B'Av</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> After recovering from the disease, he reduced his coffee intake and adopted a <a href="/wiki/Ketogenic_diet" title="Ketogenic diet">ketogenic diet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Speaking to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jerusalem_Post" title="The Jerusalem Post">The Jerusalem Post</a></i> in 2021, he expressed concern with the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Israel" title="COVID-19 pandemic in Israel">Israeli government's COVID-19 pandemic response</a>: "I understand the government wanted to make things safe but I think they went a little overboard. I think people were almost ready for a rebellion."<sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Black has been critical of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Lives_Matter" title="Black Lives Matter">Black Lives Matter</a> movement, accusing them of promoting division<sup id="cite_ref-:4_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and denouncing their characterization of police officers as racists,<sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> their calls for <a href="/wiki/Police_abolition_movement" title="Police abolition movement">abolishing the police</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and what he called their "support [of] <a href="/wiki/Israeli%E2%80%93Palestinian_conflict" title="Israeli–Palestinian conflict">Palestinians over the Jews</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-:4_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Despite this, he has said he prefers not to be involved in political or religious disputes and has encouraged unity in the Jewish community.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
