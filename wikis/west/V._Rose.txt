<p><b>Vienna Rose Dare</b> (born January 9, 1989), is better known by her <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage name</a> <b>V. Rose</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Christian_electronic_dance_music" title="Christian electronic dance music">Christian electronic dance music</a> singer. Formerly signed to Clear Sight Music, she released two studio albums under that label, <i><a href="/wiki/V._Rose_(album)" title="V. Rose (album)">V. Rose</a></i> in 2011 and <i><a href="/wiki/Forever_After" title="Forever After">Forever After</a></i> in 2014, the latter of which charted at No. 28 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Christian_Albums" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Albums">Top Christian</a> chart, and No. 16 on the <a href="/wiki/Top_Heatseekers" class="mw-redirect" title="Top Heatseekers">Heatseekers</a>. <i>V. Rose</i> was re-released with a bonus disc in 2013 and titled <i>The Electro-Pop Deluxe</i>. She currently is signed to <a href="/wiki/Inpop_Records" title="Inpop Records">Inpop Records</a>, where they released, <i><a href="/wiki/Young_Dangerous_Heart_(V._Rose_album)" title="Young Dangerous Heart (V. Rose album)">Young Dangerous Heart</a></i>, on April 22, 2016.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V._Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>V. Rose was born Vienna Rose Da Re on January 9, 1989, in <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ascap_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ascap-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cbi_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fb_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fb-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (September 2018)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> At age six, V. Rose started singing, but by age eight asked God to help her compose music.<sup id="cite_ref-about_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In her teenage years, V. Rose and a childhood friend started a group called Harmony. When V. Rose was 15, producer SPEC signed the group to Spechouse Media, but Harmony soon folded in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-fb_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fb-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-about_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the disbandment of Harmony, V. Rose signed to Spechouse as a solo artist, and she eventually appeared on two songs by <a href="/wiki/Flame_(rapper)" title="Flame (rapper)">FLAME</a> on his album <i>Captured</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-fb_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fb-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jfhone_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfhone-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> She was subsequently signed to FLAME's Clear Sight label, releasing her <a href="/wiki/V._Rose_(album)" title="V. Rose (album)">self-titled</a> debut album on August 16, 2011; however, the album did not garner any commercial charting successes.<sup id="cite_ref-about_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It was well received by music critics, with <i><a href="/wiki/CCM_Magazine" title="CCM Magazine">CCM Magazine</a></i> calling the album an "upbeat pop production set to spiritually-grounded lyrics."<sup id="cite_ref-ccm_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccm-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (May 2021)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> On the other hand, V. Rose's sophomore studio album with Clear Sight Music, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Forever_After" title="Forever After">Forever After</a></i> and released on May 20, 2014, met with greater commercial success, charting on both the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Albums" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Albums">Christian Albums</a> and <a href="/wiki/Top_Heatseekers" class="mw-redirect" title="Top Heatseekers">Heatseekers Albums</a> charts at Nos. 28 and 16 respectively, for the charting week of June 7, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-chr_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chr-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-heat_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heat-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> On February 18, 2015, the website <a href="/wiki/Rapzilla" title="Rapzilla">Rapzilla</a> reported that V. Rose has signed to the Nashville-based <a href="/wiki/Inpop_Records" title="Inpop Records">Inpop Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_style">Musical style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V._Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Musical style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>V. Rose's musical style has been labeled as a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">R&amp;B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">electronic dance music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nrtone_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrtone-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lttm_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lttm-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Jesus Freak Hideout in a review of <i>V. Rose</i> stated that the pop, R&amp;B, and rock sound on the album's opening track is used much throughout the entire release, and said that V. Rose "should soon find herself among the elite in that great radio-play-type of girl-power-pop sound."<sup id="cite_ref-jfhone_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfhone-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> New Release Tuesday considered <i>V. Rose</i> to range from "rock to pop to R&amp;B to rap to straight-up, old-school <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">Gospel</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-nrtone_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrtone-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Rich Smith of Louder Than the Music reviewed a re-release of <i>V. Rose</i>, entitled <i>The Electro-Pop Deluxe</i>, that featured six extra tracks. In keeping with the title, the extra tracks feature an <a href="/wiki/Synthpop" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthpop">electro pop</a> sound, and three of these were in the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">EDM</a> vein.<sup id="cite_ref-lttm_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lttm-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Smith found the original part of <i>V.Rose</i> heavily pop-oriented, slightly <a href="/wiki/Teen_pop" title="Teen pop">teen pop</a> in places, but with some dance and more soulful elements.<sup id="cite_ref-lttm_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lttm-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
