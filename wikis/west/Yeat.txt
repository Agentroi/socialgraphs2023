<p><b>Noah Olivier Smith</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (born February 26, 2000), known professionally as <b>Yeat</b> (occasionally stylized as YEAT <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/j/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;yes&#39;">j</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">YEET</span></i></a>),<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is an American rapper, singer, songwriter, and record producer known for his experimental sound,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> unconventional fashion featuring a mix of designer brands and <a href="/wiki/Turban" title="Turban">turbans</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and his own unique lingo, frequently using made-up words like "twizzy" and "bonka".
</p><p>Yeat rose to prominence in mid-2021 after the release of his <a href="/wiki/Mixtape" title="Mixtape">mixtape</a> <i>4L</i> and debut studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Up_2_Me" title="Up 2 Me">Up 2 Me</a></i>, with tracks from the latter, including "<a href="/wiki/Money_So_Big" title="Money So Big">Monëy so big</a>" and "Gët Busy" which gained considerable popularity on <a href="/wiki/TikTok" title="TikTok">TikTok</a>. In 2022, he released his second studio album <i><a href="/wiki/2_Alive" title="2 Alive">2 Alivë</a></i>, and the EP <i><a href="/wiki/Lyfe_(EP)" title="Lyfe (EP)">Lyfë</a></i>; both projects debuted in the top ten on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>. In 2023, he released his third studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Afterlyfe" title="Afterlyfe">Aftërlyfe</a></i>, which debuted at number four on the US <i>Billboard</i> 200.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Noah Olivier Smith was born on February 26, 2000, in <a href="/wiki/Irvine,_California" title="Irvine, California">Irvine, California</a> to a Romanian mother and a Mexican and British-American father. His paternal grandmother is Mexican and from the city of <a href="/wiki/Tijuana" title="Tijuana">Tijuana</a> while his grandfather is an American of British descent.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Yeat has two brothers, Luca and Ethan. Yeat spent his early childhood in <a href="/wiki/Fullerton,_California" title="Fullerton, California">Fullerton, California</a> before he and his family moved to <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a>, he attended <a href="/wiki/Lakeridge_High_School" title="Lakeridge High School">Lakeridge High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Oswego,_Oregon" title="Lake Oswego, Oregon">Lake Oswego, Oregon</a>. After finishing high school, Yeat briefly moved to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> before eventually returning to the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> area to further pursue his music career.<sup id="cite_ref-OGM2021_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OGM2021-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="2015.E2.80.932021:_Career_beginnings"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2015–2021:_Career_beginnings">2015–2021: Career beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2015–2021: Career beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yeat began his career in 2015, originally making music under the name Lil Yeat, but these releases have since been deleted from the Internet.<sup id="cite_ref-McKinney2022_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinney2022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> On June 30, 2018, Yeat made his first public appearance under his current moniker, premiering a track called "Brink" on Elevator, a YouTube channel.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Yeat has stated that he created the name Yeat while <a href="/wiki/Substance_intoxication" title="Substance intoxication">high</a> and trying to come up with one word that sounds familiar to people.<sup id="cite_ref-McKinney2022_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinney2022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OGM2021_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OGM2021-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> His stage name has also been described as a combination of "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/yeet" class="extiw" title="wikt:yeet">yeet</a>" and "heat".<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Yeat has mentioned how his use of <a href="/wiki/LSD" title="LSD">LSD</a> was able to help him pursue his music career.<sup id="cite_ref-McKinney2022_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinney2022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He released his first mixtape, <i>Deep Blue Strips</i> on September 20, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> On February 21, 2019, he premiered the music video for his track "Stay Up" on Elevator.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2021.E2.80.932022:_Viral_success.2C_Up_2_M.C3.AB.2C_and_2_Aliv.C3.AB"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2021–2022:_Viral_success,_Up_2_Më,_and_2_Alivë">2021–2022: Viral success, <i>Up 2 Më</i>, and <i>2 Alivë</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2021–2022: Viral success, Up 2 Më, and 2 Alivë">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Up_2_Me" title="Up 2 Me">Up 2 Me</a> and <a href="/wiki/2_Alive" title="2 Alive">2 Alive</a></div>
<p>Yeat achieved viral success online through platforms such as <a href="/wiki/TikTok" title="TikTok">TikTok</a> in 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-Myers2021_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers2021-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brake2021_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brake2021-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_(website)" title="Pitchfork (website)">Pitchfork</a></i>, Mano Sundaresan commented "Cutting his teeth in the influential online rap collective Slayworld over the last few years, Yeat was always a little stranger than his peers, and consequently cast as a minor figure. But in 2021, his surrealist bent became his superpower."<sup id="cite_ref-Pitchfork100_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork100-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Yeat's music began to gain traction online following his <i>4L</i> mixtape, which was released June 11, 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-Myers2021_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers2021-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pierre2021b_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierre2021b-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The <i>4L</i> project notably included "Sorry Bout That" and "Money Twërk".<sup id="cite_ref-Pierre2021b_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierre2021b-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August, he released the EP <i>Trëndi</i> which had increased success with "Mad Bout That" and "Fukit".<sup id="cite_ref-Myers2021_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers2021-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Also in August, a snippet of his song "Gët Busy" went viral online, attracting considerable media and fan attention upon its release.<sup id="cite_ref-Pierre2021a_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierre2021a-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The song was particularly cited by media outlets for its line "this song already was turnt but here's a bell", which was immediately followed by the ringing of church bells (which are often incorporated into his songs).<sup id="cite_ref-Pierre2021a_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierre2021a-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richards2021_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards2021-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Fellow rappers <a href="/wiki/Drake_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Drake (rapper)">Drake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lil_Yachty" title="Lil Yachty">Lil Yachty</a> also referenced the line.<sup id="cite_ref-Pierre2021b_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierre2021b-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 10, Yeat released his debut studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Up_2_Me" title="Up 2 Me">Up 2 Më</a></i> through an initial one-album deal with <a href="/wiki/Interscope_Records" title="Interscope Records">Interscope Records</a> and Foundation Media.<sup id="cite_ref-Cole2021_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cole2021-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The album received generally positive reception from critics.<sup id="cite_ref-Pierre2021b_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierre2021b-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brake2021_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brake2021-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Richards2021_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards2021-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> After the limited Interscope and Foundation deal had ended, Yeat fulfilled a promise he made to <a href="/wiki/Zack_Bia" title="Zack Bia">Zack Bia</a> by signing with the latter's <a href="/wiki/Zack_Bia#Field_Trip_Recordings" title="Zack Bia">Field Trip Recordings</a>, in addition to Conor Ambrose's Listen to the Kids, in a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Geffen_Records" title="Geffen Records">Geffen Records</a> and Interscope Records.<sup id="cite_ref-Skelton2022_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Skelton2022-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 22, 2022, <i>Up 2 Me</i> made its debut on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, initially reaching number 183 and ultimately peaking at number 58.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby2022_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby2022-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Later that same month, Yeat teased a mid-February release date for his next album, <i><a href="/wiki/2_Alive" title="2 Alive">2 Alivë</a></i>. His song "U Could Tëll" was featured in the <i><a href="/wiki/Euphoria_(American_TV_series)" title="Euphoria (American TV series)">Euphoria</a></i> episode "You Who Cannot See, Think of Those Who Can", which premiered on January 30.<sup id="cite_ref-Knight2022_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight2022-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 11, the single "Still Countin" was released alongside a <a href="/wiki/Cole_Bennett" title="Cole Bennett">Cole Bennett</a>-directed music video.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> On February 18, Yeat released his second studio album, <i>2 Alivë</i>, through Geffen Records, Field Trip Recordings, Listen to the Kids, and Twizzy Rich.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It debuted and peaked at number six on the <i>Billboard</i> 200, selling around 36,000 units and becoming his highest charting project.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> On April 1, the deluxe version of <i>2 Alivë</i>, titled <i>2 Alivë (Geëk Pack)</i>, was released.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> On April 29, he released "No Handoutz", a collaborative single with <a href="/wiki/Internet_Money_Records" title="Internet Money Records">Internet Money Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 28, Yeat released "<a href="/wiki/Rich_Minion" title="Rich Minion">Rich Minion</a>", a single he was commissioned to create for a <a href="/wiki/Lyrical_Lemonade" class="mw-redirect" title="Lyrical Lemonade">Lyrical Lemonade</a>-produced trailer promoting the film <i><a href="/wiki/Minions:_The_Rise_of_Gru" title="Minions: The Rise of Gru">Minions: The Rise of Gru</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> After its release, the song became associated with "<a href="/wiki/Minions:_The_Rise_of_Gru##Gentleminions_TikTok_trend" title="Minions: The Rise of Gru">GentleMinions</a>", a meme involving people who would dress in formal attire and attend screenings of the film while it was playing in movie theatres during the summer of 2022.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Diaz2022_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diaz2022-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="2022.E2.80.93present:_Lyf.C3.AB_and_Aft.C3.ABrlyfe"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2022–present:_Lyfë_and_Aftërlyfe">2022–present: <i>Lyfë</i> and <i>Aftërlyfe</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2022–present: Lyfë and Aftërlyfe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Lyfe_(EP)" title="Lyfe (EP)">Lyfe (EP)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afterlyfe" title="Afterlyfe">Afterlyfe</a></div>
<p>On September 2, 2022, Yeat released "<a href="/wiki/Talk_(Yeat_song)" title="Talk (Yeat song)">Talk</a>", a single from his EP <i><a href="/wiki/Lyfe_(EP)" title="Lyfe (EP)">Lyfë</a></i>. The EP itself was released a week later on September 9, debuting and peaking at number ten on the <i>Billboard</i> 200.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowen2022_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowen2022-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> On October 21, he was featured on rapper <a href="/wiki/YoungBoy_Never_Broke_Again" title="YoungBoy Never Broke Again">YoungBoy Never Broke Again</a>'s song "I Don't Text Back", a track included on the latter's mixtape <i><a href="/wiki/Ma%27_I_Got_a_Family" title="Ma&#39; I Got a Family">Ma' I Got a Family</a></i>.
</p><p>On February 24, 2023, Yeat released his third studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Afterlyfe" title="Afterlyfe">Aftërlyfe</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The album includes a feature from YoungBoy Never Broke Again, in addition to Yeat's alter egos Kranky Kranky and Luh Geeky.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> It peaked at number four on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 and number one on the <i>Billboard</i> Rap Albums chart.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> On May 3, Yeat released the single "Already Rich"; the song, often referred to by the unofficial name "ard up", was originally leaked in 2021 and saw minor success on social media apps such as TikTok.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> On May 26, the single "<a href="/wiki/My_Wrist" title="My Wrist">My Wrist</a>" featuring Atlanta rapper <a href="/wiki/Young_Thug" title="Young Thug">Young Thug</a> was released; this song, notably, features production by producer <a href="/wiki/Pi%27erre_Bourne" title="Pi&#39;erre Bourne">Pi'erre Bourne</a> who is known for his work with rappers like <a href="/wiki/Playboi_Carti" title="Playboi Carti">Playboi Carti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drake_(musician)" title="Drake (musician)">Drake</a>, and others.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> On August 10, Yeat released the single "<a href="/wiki/Bigger_Then_Everything" title="Bigger Then Everything">Bigger Then Everything</a>" alongside a music video directed by <a href="/wiki/Cole_Bennett" title="Cole Bennett">Cole Bennett</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_style">Musical style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Musical style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yeat began making music that had <a href="/wiki/Auto-Tune" title="Auto-Tune">Auto-Tune</a>-infused vocals.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In 2021, he adopted a more aggressive and synth-based sound, joining a growing group of rappers that used "<a href="/wiki/Rage_rap" title="Rage rap">rage beats</a>", a sound that became a <a href="/wiki/SoundCloud" title="SoundCloud">SoundCloud</a> staple influenced by the lively vocal deliveries and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">EDM</a>-influenced beat selections of artists such as <a href="/wiki/Playboi_Carti" title="Playboi Carti">Playboi Carti</a>, specifically on <i><a href="/wiki/Whole_Lotta_Red" title="Whole Lotta Red">Whole Lotta Red</a></i> (2020).<sup id="cite_ref-Pierre2021b_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierre2021b-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> His vocal style has drawn comparisons to <a href="/wiki/Playboi_Carti" title="Playboi Carti">Playboi Carti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Future_(rapper)" title="Future (rapper)">Future</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Young_Thug" title="Young Thug">Young Thug</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pierre2021b_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pierre2021b-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Yeat has stated the latter two are some of his biggest inspirations. He also cited American rapper <a href="/wiki/T-Pain" title="T-Pain">T-Pain</a> as one of his biggest influences growing up, calling him "The GOAT of Auto-Tune".<sup id="cite_ref-OGM2021_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OGM2021-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Yeat's signature vocal preset is based on a vocal chain given to him by frequent collaborator and fellow musician <a href="/wiki/Weiland_(musician)" title="Weiland (musician)">Weiland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McKinney2022_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinney2022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yeat has also been noted to employ a <a href="/wiki/Ad_libitum" title="Ad libitum">unique lingo</a> in his music, coming up with <a href="/wiki/Ad_libitum" title="Ad libitum">ad-libs</a> and phrases such as "twizzy", "krank", and "luh geeky", and often referring to luxury SUVs as "<a href="/wiki/Tonka" title="Tonka">Tonka</a> trucks" or "Tonkas" in his lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-McKinney2022_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinney2022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> His father was one of the inspirations for him creating these unique words, as he himself would make up his own words when Yeat was a child.<sup id="cite_ref-McKinney2022_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McKinney2022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Certain aspects of Yeat's music have led him to be associated with various <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">Internet memes</a> and trends, especially the frequent use of bell sounds in his music.<sup id="cite_ref-Diaz2022_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diaz2022-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
