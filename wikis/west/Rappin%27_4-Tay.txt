<p><b>Anthony H. Forté</b> (born March 2, 1968), better known by his <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage name</a> <b>Rappin' 4-Tay</b>, is an American rapper from <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rappin%27_4-Tay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anthony Forte was born March 2, 1968, and grew up in San Francisco's <a href="/wiki/Fillmore_District,_San_Francisco" title="Fillmore District, San Francisco">Fillmore District</a>. He has a twin sister. He has a son as well as three daughters.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rappin%27_4-Tay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Right after high school, 4-Tay made his debut on the <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Life_Is...Too_Short" title="Life Is...Too Short">Life Is...Too Short</a></i>. He was later convicted on drug charges and served ten months in prison.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Upon his release from prison he released his debut album <i>Rappin' 4-Tay Is Back</i> in 1991, and followed up in 1994 with <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Fight_the_Feelin%27" title="Don&#39;t Fight the Feelin&#39;">Don't Fight the Feelin'</a></i>, which included the hits "<a href="/wiki/Playaz_Club" title="Playaz Club">Playaz Club</a>" (which sampled the song "<a href="/wiki/Private_Number_(Judy_Clay_and_William_Bell_song)" title="Private Number (Judy Clay and William Bell song)">Private Number</a>" by William Bell and Judy Clay and hit number 36 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>), the "Dank Season" featuring Seff Tha Gaffla, and "<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Be_Around_(Rappin%27_4-Tay_song)" title="I&#39;ll Be Around (Rappin&#39; 4-Tay song)">I'll Be Around</a>" (which hit number 39 on the US Hot 100, number 59 in Australia,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and number one in New Zealand<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> ).
</p><p>In 1995, two Rappin' 4-Tay songs—"Problems" and "A Message for Your Mind"—were featured on the <a href="/wiki/Dangerous_Minds_(soundtrack)" title="Dangerous Minds (soundtrack)"><i>Dangerous Minds</i> soundtrack</a>. "A Message For Your Mind" sampled "<a href="/wiki/I_Want_You_Back" title="I Want You Back">I Want You Back</a>" by <a href="/wiki/The_Jackson_5" title="The Jackson 5">The Jackson 5</a>.
</p><p>Rappin' 4-Tay's mainstream success has been scarce since then, but he was featured on <a href="/wiki/2Pac" class="mw-redirect" title="2Pac">2Pac</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/All_Eyez_on_Me" title="All Eyez on Me">All Eyez on Me</a></i> album on the track "<a href="/wiki/Only_God_Can_Judge_Me_(song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Only God Can Judge Me (song)">Only God Can Judge Me</a>" in 1996 and <a href="/wiki/Master_P" title="Master P">Master P</a>'s <i>West Coast Bad Boyz II</i> compilation in 1997. 4-Tay was also an original member of <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">Bay Area</a> <a href="/wiki/Supergroup_(music)" title="Supergroup (music)">supergroup</a> T.W.D.Y. in 1999.
</p><p>In 2003 Rappin' 4-Tay released the album <i>Gangsta Gumbo</i> with the single "Burning, Burning", followed up by the album <i>That's What You Thought</i> in 2007. In 2010 He was featured on R&amp;B Artist E. Broussard's song titled <a href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Game_Droppin%27&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Big Game Droppin&#39; (page does not exist)">Big Game Droppin'</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was featured on the song "My Alphabets" on fellow Bay Area rap veteran <a href="/wiki/Mac_Dre" title="Mac Dre">Mac Dre</a>'s 2004 album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Genie_of_the_Lamp" title="The Genie of the Lamp">The Genie of the Lamp</a></i>. 
Dec 06 2011 Rappin' 4-Tay, E-40 and <a href="/wiki/Playalitical" title="Playalitical">Playalitical</a> collaborated on a song entitled "Bounce It Like a Bad Check" which was featured on the <i>Political Playboy Music</i> album released by <a href="/wiki/Playalitical" title="Playalitical">Playalitical</a>. In 2012 Rappin' 4-Tay collaborated on the song "Picture a Nigga" with Lil' Gang$ta,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> as well as helping with his debut album.
</p><p>In 2013, he headlined the main stage of Seattle Hempfest with artists Ditch, Tony Tag, Brian Meyers and more.
</p><p>In May 2014, he appeared with E Bone415 in a music video about <a href="/wiki/Alcatraz_Island" title="Alcatraz Island">Alcatraz Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id=".22Playaz_Club.22_Controversy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Playaz_Club&quot;_Controversy">"Playaz Club" Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rappin%27_4-Tay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &quot;Playaz Club&quot; Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2014, 4-Tay spoke out against <a href="/wiki/Drake_(musician)" title="Drake (musician)">Drake</a> for using lyrics from 4-Tay's 1994 song "Playaz Club" in the collaboration "<a href="/wiki/Who_Do_You_Love%3F_(YG_song)" title="Who Do You Love? (YG song)">Who Do You Love?</a>" with <a href="/wiki/YG_(rapper)" title="YG (rapper)">YG</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The artists settled out of court with 4-Tay being promised $100,000<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and future credits for the song. However, it was later revealed that Drake had not actually paid 4-Tay the $100,000.<sup id="cite_ref-NeverPaid_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NeverPaid-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>4-Tay eventually received royalties for his contributions to "Who Do You Love?". In 2018, the royalties to both the YG single and "Playaz Club" were auctioned on Royalty Exchange and at one time had bids of upward of $38,250 with the seller reportedly being 4-Tay.<sup id="cite_ref-NeverPaid_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NeverPaid-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
