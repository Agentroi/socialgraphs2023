<p><b>Nathaniel Dwayne Hale</b> (August 19, 1969 – March 15, 2011), known professionally as <b>Nate Dogg</b>, was an American singer and rapper. He gained recognition for providing guest vocals for a multitude of hit rap songs between 1992 and 2007, earning the nickname "<a href="/wiki/Honorific_nicknames_in_popular_music" title="Honorific nicknames in popular music">King of Hooks</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hale began his career in the early 1990s as a member of <a href="/wiki/213_(group)" title="213 (group)">213</a>, a trio he formed in 1990 with his cousin <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a> and friend <a href="/wiki/Warren_G" title="Warren G">Warren G</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1994, he was featured on the latter's hit single "<a href="/wiki/Regulate_(song)" title="Regulate (song)">Regulate</a>", which reached number two on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> and served as a breakout success for both artists.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Nate Dogg would soon become a fixture in the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast hip hop</a> genre, regularly working with <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, Snoop Dogg, and <a href="/wiki/Xzibit" title="Xzibit">Xzibit</a> in the 1990s; his deep vocals became sought after for <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a>, and he would expand to work with a larger variety of artists in the 2000s, such as <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>, <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fabolous" title="Fabolous">Fabolous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mos_Def" title="Mos Def">Mos Def</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>. As a featured artist, Nate charted 16 times on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100, and in 2003 reached number one via 50 Cent's "<a href="/wiki/21_Questions" title="21 Questions">21 Questions</a>". Nate Dogg also was notably featured on Dr. Dre's "<a href="/wiki/The_Next_Episode" title="The Next Episode">The Next Episode</a>" and Eminem's "<a href="/wiki/%27Till_I_Collapse" title="&#39;Till I Collapse">'Till I Collapse</a>". In addition to his guest work, Nate Dogg released three studio albums, as well as a string of moderately successful singles of his own in the 1990s.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nate_Dogg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nathaniel Dwayne Hale was born on August 19, 1969, in <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-latimes_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt-obit_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-obit-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx_2011-03-16_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx_2011-03-16-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Press-Telegram_2011-03-15_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Press-Telegram_2011-03-15-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> Hale met <a href="/wiki/Warren_G" title="Warren G">Warren G</a> at <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach_Polytechnic_High_School" title="Long Beach Polytechnic High School">Long Beach Polytechnic High School</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> As a youth, he sang at Long Beach's New Hope Baptist Church, where his father was a pastor.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx_2011-03-16_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx_2011-03-16-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He also sang at Life Line Baptist Church in <a href="/wiki/Clarksdale,_Mississippi" title="Clarksdale, Mississippi">Clarksdale, Mississippi</a>.
</p><p>At age 17, Hale dropped out of high school, left home, and 30 days later enlisted in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-marine_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marine-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He was stationed at <a href="/wiki/Camp_Schwab" title="Camp Schwab">Camp Schwab</a> in <a href="/wiki/Okinawa_Prefecture" title="Okinawa Prefecture">Okinawa Prefecture</a>, Japan, in the Materiel Readiness Battalion of the <a href="/wiki/3rd_Marine_Logistics_Group#3rd_Force_Service_Support_Group_(3rd_FSSG)" title="3rd Marine Logistics Group">3rd Force Service Support Group</a>, which supplied ammunition to most of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>. After three years as an ammunition specialist, he was <a href="/wiki/Military_discharge" title="Military discharge">discharged</a> in 1989. Hale would recall that he joined the military because he "wanted to see if he was a man".<sup id="cite_ref-Arnold_2012_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold_2012-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_career">Musical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nate_Dogg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Musical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="213">213</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nate_Dogg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 213">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990,<sup id="cite_ref-About_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Nate Dogg, <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and Warren G formed a rap trio called <a href="/wiki/213_(group)" title="213 (group)">213</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-About_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> They recorded their first <a href="/wiki/Demo_(music)" title="Demo (music)">demo tape</a> in the back of record store V.I.P. in Long Beach. The demo was later heard by <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a> at a bachelor party.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solo_career">Solo career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nate_Dogg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solo career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nate Dogg debuted on Dr. Dre's first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i>, in 1992. Nate's trademark singing, complementing the new <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a> sound <a href="/wiki/G-funk" title="G-funk">G-funk</a>, was well received by critics. He signed to Dr. Dre's label, <a href="/wiki/Death_Row_Records" title="Death Row Records">Death Row Records</a>, in 1993. Nate Dogg also featured on Snoop Dogg's debut solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">Doggystyle</a></i>, in 1993, his singing prominent on the track "Ain't No Fun (If the Homies Can't Have None)".
</p><p>In 1994, Nate Dogg co-wrote his duet with Warren G, the single "<a href="/wiki/Regulate_(song)" title="Regulate (song)">Regulate</a>". That same year, Nate also featured on "How Long Will They Mourn Me?" from <a href="/wiki/Thug_Life" title="Thug Life">Thug Life</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Thug_Life,_Volume_I" title="Thug Life, Volume I">Thug Life, Volume I</a></i>. In July 1998, amid his departure from Death Row, the label released his double album, delayed about two years, <i><a href="/wiki/G-Funk_Classics,_Vol._1_%26_2" title="G-Funk Classics, Vol. 1 &amp; 2">G-Funk Classics, Vol. 1 &amp; 2</a></i>. In 2001, his <a href="/wiki/Elektra_Records" title="Elektra Records">Elektra Records</a> follow-up, <i><a href="/wiki/Music_%26_Me_(Nate_Dogg_album)" title="Music &amp; Me (Nate Dogg album)">Music &amp; Me</a></i>, peaked at No. 3 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a> chart.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> He also had an <a href="/wiki/Nate_Dogg_(album)" title="Nate Dogg (album)">eponymous album</a> that saw unauthorized release in 2003.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Nate Dogg was often sought to sing on other artists' tracks, usually to sing the <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hook</a>. As a featured artist, he charted 16 times on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, and in 2003 reached No. 1 via <a href="/wiki/50_Cent" title="50 Cent">50 Cent</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/21_Questions" title="21 Questions">21 Questions</a>".
</p><p>Otherwise, his successful collaborations are numerous, including <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">2Pac</a>'s "All Bout U", Dr. Dre's "<a href="/wiki/The_Next_Episode" title="The Next Episode">The Next Episode</a>", <a href="/wiki/Westside_Connection" title="Westside Connection">Westside Connection</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Gangsta_Nation" title="Gangsta Nation">Gangsta Nation</a>", <a href="/wiki/Mos_Def" title="Mos Def">Mos Def</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Oh_No_(Mos_Def_and_Pharoahe_Monch_song)" title="Oh No (Mos Def and Pharoahe Monch song)">Oh No</a>", <a href="/wiki/Fabolous" title="Fabolous">Fabolous</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Can%27t_Deny_It" title="Can&#39;t Deny It">Can't Deny It</a>", <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Area_Codes_(Ludacris_song)" title="Area Codes (Ludacris song)">Area Codes</a>", <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a>'s "Behind the Walls", <a href="/wiki/Mark_Ronson" title="Mark Ronson">Mark Ronson</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Ooh_Wee_(song)" title="Ooh Wee (song)">Ooh Wee</a>", <a href="/wiki/Houston_(singer)" title="Houston (singer)">Houston</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/I_Like_That_(Houston_song)" title="I Like That (Houston song)">I Like That</a>", <a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/%27Till_I_Collapse" title="&#39;Till I Collapse">'Till I Collapse</a>", "Never Enough", and "<a href="/wiki/Shake_That" title="Shake That">Shake That</a>", and <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Have_a_Party" title="Have a Party">Have a Party</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Top_Collaborations_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Top_Collaborations-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Further, in 2002, appearing on television, Nate Dogg was on a celebrity episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Weakest_Link_(American_game_show)" title="Weakest Link (American game show)">Weakest Link</a></i>, where, finally eliminated by <a href="/wiki/Xzibit" title="Xzibit">Xzibit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Young_MC" title="Young MC">Young MC</a>, he was among the final three.<sup id="cite_ref-mtvweakestlink_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtvweakestlink-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artistry">Artistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nate_Dogg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Artistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hale was known for his deep, melodic vocals, with his music often described as a mix between <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>, and his vocal range between <a href="/wiki/Tenor" title="Tenor">tenor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baritone" title="Baritone">baritone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Hale himself considered his voice and style to be mostly influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel music</a> he performed in the church choir as a child, though he also grew up listening to <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> and cited <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Gaye" title="Marvin Gaye">Marvin Gaye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_White" title="Maurice White">Maurice White</a> of <a href="/wiki/Earth,_Wind_%26_Fire" title="Earth, Wind &amp; Fire">Earth, Wind &amp; Fire</a> as some of his biggest musical influences.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He's considered to be the inventor of "gangsta singing", a singing style that consisted in the blend of R&amp;B and soul vocals with <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a> lyrics. The style was heavily influential to urban culture, with major R&amp;B artists like <a href="/wiki/R._Kelly" title="R. Kelly">R. Kelly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chris_Brown" title="Chris Brown">Chris Brown</a> later using it.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nate_Dogg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hale was charged for a 1991 robbery of a Check Changers shop and for a 1994 robbery of <a href="/wiki/Taco_Bell" title="Taco Bell">Taco Bell</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Pedro,_Los_Angeles" title="San Pedro, Los Angeles">San Pedro</a>, but was acquitted.<sup id="cite_ref-cases_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cases-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-robbery_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robbery-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1996, he was convicted of a drug offence in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County,_California" title="Los Angeles County, California">Los Angeles County</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-charges_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charges-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 17, 2000, for allegedly assaulting his former girlfriend and setting her mother's car on fire in <a href="/wiki/Lakewood,_California" title="Lakewood, California">Lakewood</a>, Hale was charged with kidnapping, domestic violence, terrorist threats, and arson.<sup id="cite_ref-arrest_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arrest-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Dr. Dre posted a $1 million bond.<sup id="cite_ref-bail_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bail-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The charges were dismissed while he pleaded <a href="/wiki/Nolo_contendere" title="Nolo contendere">no contest</a> to illegal gun possession by a felon,<sup id="cite_ref-charges_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charges-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and received a $1,000 fine and three years' <a href="/wiki/Probation" title="Probation">probation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-probation_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-probation-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 12, 2002, a tour bus carrying Hale, while outside of <a href="/wiki/Kingman,_Arizona" title="Kingman, Arizona">Kingman, Arizona</a>, was found with two pistols and four ounces of <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a>, whereby he was booked and then released on $3,500 bond.<sup id="cite_ref-mohave_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mohave-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The next month, the weapon charges were dropped for his guilty plea on a drug charge, and he was sentenced to probation, community service, and drug counseling.<sup id="cite_ref-mtv_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2006, Hale was charged with misdemeanor aggravated trespassing, telephone harassment, battery assault, dissuading a witness from reporting a crime, and breaking a <a href="/wiki/Restraining_order" title="Restraining order">restraining order</a>. On March 20, 2008, pleading guilty to trespassing and battery, he lost gun-ownership rights for ten years, received three years' probation, and was ordered to a domestic-violence intervention program.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx_2008-03-27_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx_2008-03-27-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 23, 2008, after allegedly threatening his estranged wife by emails and chasing her on <a href="/wiki/Interstate_405_(California)" title="Interstate 405 (California)">Interstate 405</a>, Hale was charged with two felony counts of criminal threats and one count of <a href="/wiki/Stalking" title="Stalking">stalking</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dropped_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dropped-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dui_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dui-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> He pleaded not guilty.<sup id="cite_ref-dui_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dui-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In April 2009, as the alleged victim had failed to contact prosecutors, the charges were dropped.<sup id="cite_ref-dropped_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dropped-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Incidentally, he was also convicted of <a href="/wiki/Drug%E2%80%93impaired_driving" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug–impaired driving">driving under the influence of drugs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dui_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dui-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>On December 19, 2007, Hale suffered a stroke.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> After a week in Pomona Valley Hospital Medical Center, he entered a <a href="/wiki/Physical_medicine_and_rehabilitation" title="Physical medicine and rehabilitation">rehabilitation</a> facility.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Although his body's left side was <a href="/wiki/Hemiparesis" title="Hemiparesis">weakened</a>, neither his cognition nor voice were affected and a full recovery was expected.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hale suffered another stroke on September 12, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-MTV_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MTV-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> On March 15, 2011, Hale died at age 41 in Long Beach of complications of multiple strokes<sup id="cite_ref-:4_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> or by congestive heart failure.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He was interred in Long Beach at <a href="/wiki/Forest_Lawn_Memorial_Park_(Long_Beach)" title="Forest Lawn Memorial Park (Long Beach)">Forest Lawn Memorial Park</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nate_Dogg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013, Nate Dogg's son Naijiel Hale was committed to play football at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> A couple of years later, in 2015, Nate's other son, Nathaniel Jr., having adopted the stage name Lil Nate Dogg, released his own album, <i>Son of a G</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Naijiel would also begin to create music, adopting the stage name NHale, and released his debut studio album, <i>Young OG</i>, in 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was reported a posthumous and final studio album entitled <i>Nate Dogg: It's a Wonderful Life</i> was announced in 2012, with a late spring or early summer 2013 release from Seven Arts Music and United Media &amp; Music Group. As of 2023, the album has not been released—with no further announcements given—and it was quietly shelved.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
