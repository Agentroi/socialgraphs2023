					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">American performance artist, author, hip hop musician, poet, and  activist</div>
<p><b>Timothy Terrell West</b> (born July 6, 1972), better known as <b>Tim'm T. West</b>, is an American educator and multi-discipline <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">performance artist</a>, author, <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> recording artist, <a href="/wiki/Poet" title="Poet">poet</a>, activist, and youth advocate.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_State_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Humboldt State University">Humboldt State University</a> notes West as a <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">Renaissance man</a> who has achieved success as a performer, activist, author, teacher, and poet.<sup id="cite_ref-calstate_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-calstate-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He has been featured in many documentaries about hip hop culture, in addition to his books, he is widely <a href="/wiki/Anthologized" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthologized">anthologized</a>, and has produced nine hip hop albums including with <a href="/wiki/Deep_Dickollective" title="Deep Dickollective">Deep Dickollective</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-calstate_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-calstate-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> West has been interviewed by an array of media outlets from <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> to the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a>.</i> He was awarded a "2013 Esteem Award", and in 2015 was named an LGBTQ icon.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tim%27m_T._West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>West was born with a <a href="/wiki/Speech_impediment" class="mw-redirect" title="Speech impediment">speech impediment</a> that caused him to <a href="/wiki/Stutter" class="mw-redirect" title="Stutter">stutter</a> and repeat the 'M' on his first name.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> His family began calling him "Tim'm" which has remained his moniker.<sup id="cite_ref-prideindex_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prideindex-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He is the second son of nine children, born into a musical family in <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a> to Charles Edward, a minister, and his mother, Irma Pearl Stinson, who served at various times as an administrative assistant and nurse.<sup id="cite_ref-thebody_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thebody-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He counts his mother as one of the biggest influences on his life, showing him how to be thankful, loving, forgiving, and <a href="/wiki/Gracious" class="mw-redirect" title="Gracious">gracious</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-thebody_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thebody-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> His early influences besides his family were <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rap" class="mw-redirect" title="Rap">rap</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_music" title="House music">house music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-clatl_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clatl-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He says his dad was more of a storefront preacher with mixed success, but it did offer the first times West recalls being a poet, and performing.<sup id="cite_ref-prideindex_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prideindex-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> West grew up primarily in <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">Little Rock, Arkansas</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Taylor,_Arkansas" title="Taylor, Arkansas">Taylor, Arkansas</a> in extreme poverty, where he attended Taylor High School.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thebody_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thebody-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He served as captain of his high school <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> team and president of his school's chapter of <a href="/wiki/Future_Farmers_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Future Farmers of America">Future Farmers of America</a>.
</p><p>He participated in <a href="/wiki/Upward_Bound" title="Upward Bound">Upward Bound</a> at <a href="/wiki/Southern_Arkansas_University" title="Southern Arkansas University">Southern Arkansas University</a> and competed in the National Science Competitions where he was twice selected to represent his state at the National Junior Science and Humanities Symposium. He met then-Governor <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> and traveled to <a href="/wiki/The_United_States_Military_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="The United States Military Academy">The United States Military Academy</a> at <a href="/wiki/West_Point" class="mw-redirect" title="West Point">West Point</a> to compete. He was interviewed by West Point recruiters, but after revealing he had <a href="/wiki/Men_who_have_sex_with_men" title="Men who have sex with men">same-sex</a> interests, he was dropped as a potential recruit. West is now openly <a href="/wiki/Queer" title="Queer">queer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His activity as a <a href="/wiki/Boy_Scout" class="mw-redirect" title="Boy Scout">Boy Scout</a> led him to join <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS Church) which sponsored his troop. He began attending LDS Church functions at the age of twelve. While being considered for a Mormon mission, he <a href="/wiki/Came_out" class="mw-redirect" title="Came out">came out</a> to his <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">Bishop</a>, which heightened his existing feelings of rejection and <a href="/wiki/Shame" title="Shame">shame</a>. This was a difficult for him, and he contemplated his future, as well as <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>. He struggled with depression and <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a>. He decided to move on to attend college, accept himself, even if uncertain about what his future held. His tireless work as a high school student enabled many opportunities for college, Cornell University and Duke University among others.
</p><p>West enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Durham,_North_Carolina" title="Durham, North Carolina">Durham, North Carolina</a>, and used an opportunity while at Duke, to attend <a href="/wiki/Howard_University" title="Howard University">Howard University</a> in his senior year to experience and study at a <a href="/wiki/Historically_black_colleges_and_universities" title="Historically black colleges and universities">historical black college</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He returned to complete his studies at Duke, and graduated in 1994, earning a Bachelor of Arts degree in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, with a concentration in <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_studies" title="Women&#39;s studies">women's studies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pre-law" title="Pre-law">pre-law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> After a few years working as a college admissions officer at Duke, he moved to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> to attend <a href="/wiki/The_New_School_for_Social_Research" title="The New School for Social Research">The New School for Social Research</a> where he earned a Masters of Arts degree in <a href="/wiki/Liberal_studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal studies">liberal studies</a> and philosophy in 1998, while immersing himself in the <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word" title="Spoken word">spoken word</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a> scene in Brooklyn and Manhattan.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chiread_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chiread-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He later earned a master's in modern thought and literature from <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Deep_Dickollective.2Fhip_hop"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep_Dickollective/hip_hop">Deep Dickollective/hip hop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tim%27m_T._West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Deep Dickollective/hip hop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>West traveled to the west coast, enrolling in a Ph.D. program in interdisciplinary modern thought and literature at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Shortly thereafter in 1999, he discovered that he had AIDS,<sup id="cite_ref-Chiread_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chiread-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> so he took an academic break to focus on his health and options for his future.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-clatl_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clatl-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It was during this time that he started youth advocacy work and joined forces in early 2000 with <a href="/wiki/Juba_Kalamka" title="Juba Kalamka">Juba Kalamka</a>, and Phillip Atiba Goff to form the black and <a href="/wiki/Homohop" class="mw-redirect" title="Homohop">queer hip-hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/Deep_Dickollective" title="Deep Dickollective">Deep Dickollective</a> (DDC).<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> West believes the <a href="/wiki/Bay_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Bay Area">Bay Area</a>, with San Francisco's AIDS <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activism</a>, and Oakland's <a href="/wiki/Black_Panthers" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Panthers">Black Panthers</a>, was one of the few settings where DDC could emerge.<sup id="cite_ref-Chiread_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chiread-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The "idea of pan-Africanism and black nationalism is also kind of working against progay [causes]. It's one of the few places were those two identities converge."<sup id="cite_ref-Chiread_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chiread-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> DDC was one of the first groups of their kind.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chiread_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chiread-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> They were formed, in part, as a response to the pandemic of HIV/AIDS among queer black men.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Curator William Jones booked them as part of his "Black Gay Male Radical Performances" series at the Black Dot Caf√© in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a>. Their debut recording "BourgieBohoPostPomoAfroHomo" surprised hip
hop and spoken word communities, with a fresh approach to the political and social issues, from a perspective of an increasingly vocal black and <a href="/wiki/Queer" title="Queer">queer</a> community.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> What started as a <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parodic</a> exercise became an underground, and critical success.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> West continues to educate on <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">homophobia</a> in hip hop.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> West coined the term <i>homohop</i> but feels that the term should be replaced.<sup id="cite_ref-dot429_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dot429-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> At first it helped organize <a href="/wiki/LGBTQI" class="mw-redirect" title="LGBTQI">LGBTQI</a> hip hop artists, but he feels now it serves to separate artists who are fundamentally just hip hop artists.<sup id="cite_ref-dot429_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dot429-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>West is featured prominently in the 2005 hip hop documentary, <i><a href="/wiki/Pick_Up_the_Mic" title="Pick Up the Mic">Pick Up the Mic</a></i> and appears in Byron Hurt's documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Beyond_Beats_and_Rhymes" class="mw-redirect" title="Beyond Beats and Rhymes">Beyond Beats and Rhymes</a></i>. Most recently he appeared in a set of documentaries directed by <a href="/wiki/Mario_Van_Peebles" title="Mario Van Peebles">Mario Van Peebles</a> about black manhood and responsibility; <i>Bring Your 'A' Game</i> and its complement, <i>Fair Game?</i> In Spring of 2007, DDC released its final, full-length studio project, <i>On Some Other</i>, on Sugartruck Recordings. The group disbanded in 2007. DDC was a part of <i>The Anthology of Rap</i> book from <a href="/wiki/Yale_University_Press" title="Yale University Press">Yale University Press</a> in 2010. West credits DDC for breaking through the media as queer and black artists, and enabling him to perform as an out LGBT black soloist, "We had pretty much remained invisible in the media prior to Deep Dickollective, so the affect was far-reaching."<sup id="cite_ref-prideindex_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prideindex-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As a solo artist, West released <i>Songs from Red Dirt</i> on Cellular Records in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The debut provided musical complement to his first book, <i>Red Dirt Revival: a poetic memoir in 6 Breaths</i> (2003).<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> West says the book and CD are testimonial, they are "literary and musical projections of my rites of passage from shame and silence into self-love."<sup id="cite_ref-thebody_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thebody-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005 he released a <a href="/wiki/Chapbook" title="Chapbook">chapbook</a>, <i>BARE: Notes From a Porchdweller</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <i>Flirting</i> (2007), was his second full-length poetic memoir book.<sup id="cite_ref-clatl_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clatl-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Also in 2007 he completed his second solo album, <i>Blakkboy Blue(s)</i>, which was met with positive critical response.<sup id="cite_ref-clatl_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clatl-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In the Fall of 2008, and in support of the campaign against California's <a href="/wiki/Proposition_8" class="mw-redirect" title="Proposition 8">Proposition 8</a>, West created a limited edition chapbook <i>Love In Full Color</i>. In January 2009, he released a third solo rap project, <i>In Security: The Golden Error</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In July 2011 he released <i>Fly Brotha</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-prideindex_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prideindex-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> which also received critical acclaim. His fifth solo release, <i>Snapshots: The He-Art and Experience of Tim'm T. West</i>, was released in April 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Chiread_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chiread-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>West continues to host "Front Porch,"<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> a spoken word/hip-hop/<a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> showcase that has appeared at various colleges and universities nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-revolution_11-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revolution-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-clatl_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clatl-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He continues to teach and perform nationally, promoting a collection of poetry released in April 2015, "pre|dispositions: affirmations on loving" and a new Hip-Hop/Soul/House project, "ICONography," which was released in October 2015.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Educator">Educator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tim%27m_T._West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Educator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>West worked in HIV/AIDS advocacy awareness and mobilization, specializing his work for young black <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bisexual" class="mw-redirect" title="Bisexual">bisexual</a>, <a href="/wiki/Same_gender_loving" title="Same gender loving">same gender loving</a> men at both AID Atlanta and Saint Hope Foundation's FUSION Center in <a href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> He has been recognized for his work over multiple years with the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_People_with_AIDS" class="mw-redirect" title="National Association of People with AIDS">National Association of People with AIDS</a>'s "Positive Youth Institute," and his graduation as a distinguished fellow of the Black AIDS Institute's "African-American HIV University's Community Mobilization College."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>West served as the Department Chair of English and Creative Writing at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oakland_School_of_the_Arts&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Oakland School of the Arts (page does not exist)">Oakland School of the Arts</a> from 2002 to 2004 before relocating to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a> where he taught in the English Department of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cesar_Chavez_Public_Charter_High_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cesar Chavez Public Charter High School (page does not exist)">Cesar Chavez Public Charter High School</a>. In various capacities, since 1995, West has also worked for <a href="/wiki/College_Summit" class="mw-redirect" title="College Summit">College Summit</a>, notably as a high school coordinator in DC in 2006 and 2007, where he helped to the build capacity of high
schools to get more of their students into college. He is known for his engaging teaching methods, and has taught on the post-secondary level as an instructor of Writing Pedagogy classes at <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Lang_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugene Lang College">Eugene Lang College</a> of The New School in New York City, and as an instructor in Stanford University's first-year Writing and Critical Thinking Program. In the 2008 to 2009 Academic Year, West taught in the Department of World Languages and Cultures as a Visiting
Lecturer in Ethnic Studies at California's <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_State_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Humboldt State University">Humboldt State University</a>. More recently, West served as an adjunct professor of English and Philosophy at <a href="/wiki/Houston_Community_College" title="Houston Community College">Houston Community College</a>, and in the summer of 2011, joined distinguished faculty with the <a href="/wiki/Washington_National_Cathedral" title="Washington National Cathedral">Washington National Cathedral</a> Scholars Program where he taught a 6-week blog-based <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>/activism course.
</p><p>In May 2011, West relocated to <a href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a>, where his daughter, Shannon Rose Matesky, a recent <a href="/wiki/DePaul_University" title="DePaul University">DePaul University</a> graduate and well known poet, spoken word artist and performer, lives. He started MyWritingProfessor.com where he serves as the owner and principal consultant, and remains a longstanding advocate for youth.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In December 2012, he accepted a position at the <a href="/wiki/Center_on_Halsted" title="Center on Halsted">Center on Halsted</a> where he was swiftly promoted to serve as the director of youth services through May 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chiread_16-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chiread-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Tim'm's desire to have a broader impact on LGBTQ youth and young adults led his interest in Teach For America, who'd just launched its <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.teachforamerica.org/LGBTQ">LGBTQ Initiative</a> and was seeking a managing director. Given the trajectory of his own struggles as a queer black male in the South and even a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/timm-west/lgbt-teachers_b_5985490.html">suicide attempt at sixteen</a>, Tim'm proudly accepted this position with Teach For America in July 2014; a position he feels bridges his work as an educator with his long-standing commitment to LGBTQ youth advocacy. He relocated from Washington, DC to Atlanta, GA where he serves on the Atlanta-based Programming Board for the LGBT Institute. Tim'm was promoted to Senior Managing Director of Teach For America's LGBTQ Community Initiative and continues to do work nationally to advance safer and braver classrooms for LGBTQ kids and educators.
</p>
