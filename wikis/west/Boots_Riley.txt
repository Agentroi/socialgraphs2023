<p><b>Raymond Lawrence</b> "<b>Boots</b>" <b>Riley</b> (born April 1, 1971), is an American <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">film director</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_production" title="Hip hop production">producer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">screenwriter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rapper" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapper">rapper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Socialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist">socialist</a> <a href="/wiki/Activist" class="mw-redirect" title="Activist">activist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He is the lead vocalist of <a href="/wiki/The_Coup" title="The Coup">The Coup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Street_Sweeper_Social_Club" title="Street Sweeper Social Club">Street Sweeper Social Club</a>. He made his feature-film directorial debut with <i><a href="/wiki/Sorry_to_Bother_You" title="Sorry to Bother You">Sorry to Bother You</a></i> (released July 2018), which he also wrote.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boots_Riley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Riley was born in 1971 in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> into a family of social justice organizers.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He is the son of Walter Riley, an African-American attorney, and Anitra Patterson, whose father was African-American and whose mother (Boots' maternal grandmother) was a <a href="/wiki/Jewish_refugees_from_Nazi_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish refugees from Nazi Germany">Jewish refugee</a> from <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="Königsberg">Königsberg</a> who fled Europe with her parents as a teenager in 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He is the second youngest of five siblings.
</p><p>By the time Boots was one, his family had moved to <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>, and when he was six they moved to <a href="/wiki/Oakland" class="mw-redirect" title="Oakland">Oakland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Hollywood_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Hollywood-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> where he later attended <a href="/wiki/Oakland_High_School_(Oakland,_California)" title="Oakland High School (Oakland, California)">Oakland High School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EBX_2006_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EBX_2006-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  When the school faced cutbacks in the 1980s, 2000 of Oakland High's 2200 students protested by participating in a walkout organized by Riley and friends.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Interested in politics at a young age, Riley joined the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_Against_Racism" title="International Committee Against Racism">International Committee Against Racism</a> at age 14<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and the radical <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Labor_Party_(United_States)" title="Progressive Labor Party (United States)">Progressive Labor Party</a> at age 15.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Hollywood_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Hollywood-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In 1991, Riley founded the <a href="/wiki/Political_hip_hop" title="Political hip hop">political hip hop</a> group <a href="/wiki/The_Coup" title="The Coup">The Coup</a> with <a href="/wiki/The_Coup#E-roc" title="The Coup">E-roc</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Alongside rappers <a href="/wiki/Spice_1" title="Spice 1">Spice 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mopreme_Shakur" title="Mopreme Shakur">Mopreme Shakur</a> (then known as Mocedes), they released a song on a 1991 compilation album called <i>Dope Like a Pound or a Key</i>, released by Wax That Azz Records.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Group DJ <a href="/wiki/Pam_the_Funkstress" class="mw-redirect" title="Pam the Funkstress">Pam the Funkstress</a> joined the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Riley was both chief lyric writer and music producer of The Coup's albums.
</p><p>In 1992, The Coup signed to <a href="/wiki/Wild_Pitch_Records" title="Wild Pitch Records">Wild Pitch Records</a>/<a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and released their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Kill_My_Landlord" title="Kill My Landlord">Kill My Landlord</a></i> in 1993. Two of the album's singles, "Dig It" and "Not Yet Free" received play on national Black radio, <a href="/wiki/Black_Entertainment_Television" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Entertainment Television">BET</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Yo!_MTV_Raps" title="Yo! MTV Raps">Yo! MTV Raps</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1993, <a href="/wiki/E-40" title="E-40">E-40</a> released the video for "Practice Lookin' Hard", a song based around Riley's lyric, "I got a mirror in my pocket and I practice lookin' hard", from the song "Not Yet Free".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The video featured Riley singing the chorus while he, E-40 and <a href="/wiki/Tupac_Shakur" title="Tupac Shakur">Tupac Shakur</a> reflected light into the camera from a handheld mirror while dancing around.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1994, The Coup released their second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Genocide_%26_Juice" title="Genocide &amp; Juice">Genocide &amp; Juice</a></i>, featuring guest appearances by E-40 and Spice 1. Fueled by video play and some radioplay for the single "Fat Cats and Bigga Fish", the album shot up the charts, but stalled when EMI absorbed Wild Pitch.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> At this point, E-roc left The Coup on amicable terms.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>1998's <i><a href="/wiki/Steal_This_Album" title="Steal This Album">Steal This Album</a></i>, released on indie label Dogday Records, was called "a masterpiece of slow-rolling West Coast funk" by <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The single, "Me and Jesus the Pimp in a '79 Granada Last Night", was an eight-minute song about the grown-up son of a prostitute driving his mother's killer to a secluded place in which to murder him.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> A novel, <i>Too Beautiful for Words</i> by Monique W. Morris, based on the story characters and descriptions in the song, was published by <a href="/wiki/HarperCollins" title="HarperCollins">HarperCollins</a> in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Del_the_Funky_Homosapien" title="Del the Funky Homosapien">Del the Funky Homosapien</a> guests on the track "The Repo Man Sings for You".
</p><p>The group's fourth album, <i><a href="/wiki/Party_Music" title="Party Music">Party Music</a></i>, was released on 75 Ark Records in 2001. It was re-released in 2005 by <a href="/wiki/Epitaph_Records" title="Epitaph Records">Epitaph Records</a>. The original cover art depicted group members standing in front of the Twin Towers of the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973%E2%80%932001)" title="World Trade Center (1973–2001)">World Trade Center</a> as they explode.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Riley is depicted pushing a button on a bass guitar tuner and DJ Pam the Funkstress is shown holding conductor's wands. The photo was taken in May 2001, with the album scheduled to be released just after the <a href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a>. In response to the uncanny similarity of the artwork with the attacks, the release was delayed until an alternative cover could be prepared. The album hit No. 8 in the 2001 <i><a href="/wiki/Village_Voice" class="mw-redirect" title="Village Voice">Village Voice</a></i> Pazz and Jop Poll- the most important year-end critics' list, was named "Pop Album of the Year" by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, and "Hip-Hop Album of the Year" by <i>Rolling Stone</i>. The album included a guest appearance by <a href="/wiki/Dead_prez" class="mw-redirect" title="Dead prez">dead prez</a> on the song "Get Up".
</p><p>Riley released a controversial press release on September 18, 2001, later published in the book, <i>Another World Is Possible</i>. The press release stated that "last week's events were symptomatic of a larger backlash against U.S. corporate imperialism". The controversy surrounding the cover art, press release and the lyrics from <i>Party Music</i> (specifically the song "5 Million Ways to Kill a CEO") led to Riley appearing on local network news affiliates all over the U.S. He appeared on <a href="/wiki/Fox_News" title="Fox News">Fox News</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Hannity_and_Colmes" class="mw-redirect" title="Hannity and Colmes">Hannity and Colmes</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Politically_Incorrect" title="Politically Incorrect">Politically Incorrect</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Bill_Maher" title="Bill Maher">Bill Maher</a>. During this time, conservative commentator <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Malkin" title="Michelle Malkin">Michelle Malkin</a> called Riley's lyrics "a stomach-turning example of anti-Americanism disguised as highbrow intellectual expression".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Independent</i> concluded it was "protest album of the year, by a million-man march".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, The Coup released <i><a href="/wiki/Pick_a_Bigger_Weapon" title="Pick a Bigger Weapon">Pick a Bigger Weapon</a></i> on Epitaph Records, featuring guest appearances by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Morello" title="Tom Morello">Tom Morello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talib_Kweli" title="Talib Kweli">Talib Kweli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Thought" title="Black Thought">Black Thought</a> from <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jello_Biafra" title="Jello Biafra">Jello Biafra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_with_Tom_Morello">Work with Tom Morello</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boots_Riley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Work with Tom Morello">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2003, guitarist <a href="/wiki/Tom_Morello" title="Tom Morello">Tom Morello</a> invited Riley to be part of the "Tell Us the Truth Tour", which was meant to shed light on the <a href="/wiki/Monopolization" title="Monopolization">monopolization</a> of the media and the coming <a href="/wiki/FTAA" class="mw-redirect" title="FTAA">FTAA</a> agreements.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The tour, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Janeane_Garofalo" title="Janeane Garofalo">Janeane Garofalo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Klein" title="Naomi Klein">Naomi Klein</a>, featured acoustic performances by Riley, Morello, <a href="/wiki/Billy_Bragg" title="Billy Bragg">Billy Bragg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Earle" title="Steve Earle">Steve Earle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Mills" title="Mike Mills">Mike Mills</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jill_Sobule" title="Jill Sobule">Jill Sobule</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, Morello approached Riley to form a band together under the name Street Sweeper. The duo, who later changed their name to <a href="/wiki/Street_Sweeper_Social_Club" title="Street Sweeper Social Club">Street Sweeper Social Club</a>, releasing their self-titled debut album in 2009. They toured in support of it along with <a href="/wiki/Nine_Inch_Nails" title="Nine Inch Nails">Nine Inch Nails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jane%27s_Addiction" title="Jane&#39;s Addiction">Jane's Addiction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> On May 24, a press release went out announcing Street Sweeper Social Club as one of the headliners of the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Rock_the_Bells" title="Rock the Bells">Rock the Bells</a> tour. Street Sweeper Social Club released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ghetto_Blaster_EP" title="The Ghetto Blaster EP">The Ghetto Blaster EP</a></i> in late July 2010.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_work">Independent work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boots_Riley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Independent work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1991, the same year Riley co-founded The Coup, he and other activists and hip hop artists created the Mau Mau Rhythm Collective.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The Collective put on "Hip-Hop <a href="/wiki/Edutainment" class="mw-redirect" title="Edutainment">Edutainment</a> Concerts", which allied with and promoted the campaigns of community-based organizations like Women's Economic Agenda Project (WEAP), <a href="/wiki/Copwatch" title="Copwatch">Copwatch</a>, International Campaign To Free <a href="/wiki/Geronimo_Pratt" title="Geronimo Pratt">Geronimo Pratt</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Black_Panther_Party" title="Black Panther Party">Black Panther</a> Alumni Association, and various anti-police brutality projects.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The Collective would use the growing popularity of their concerts to bring a large number of youth to take over a closed Oakland city council meeting and hold a public meeting.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, Riley produced the score for an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i> entitled "<a href="/wiki/Pranksta_Rap" title="Pranksta Rap">Pranksta Rap</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007 and 2008, Riley toured heavily with New Orleans-based band <a href="/wiki/Galactic" title="Galactic">Galactic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The band performed The Coup songs behind Riley's vocals and they also performed their collaboration, "Hustle Up". In 2008, while performing with Galactic, police interrupted the concert and Riley was charged with using "abusive language"—a charge that had not been laid in 26 years, and never before against a performer.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010 and 2011, Riley recorded with Ursus Minor on the album <i>I Will Not Take "But" for an Answer</i>, and toured with the group in France.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_television">Film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boots_Riley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, Riley finished a screenplay for "an <a href="/wiki/Absurdism" title="Absurdism">absurdist</a> dark comedy with aspects of <a href="/wiki/Magical_realism" class="mw-redirect" title="Magical realism">magical realism</a> and science fiction," inspired by his own time working as a telemarketer.<sup id="cite_ref-npr_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npr-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In 2017, he was able to begin production on his screenplay for <i><a href="/wiki/Sorry_to_Bother_You" title="Sorry to Bother You">Sorry to Bother You</a></i>, directing it himself, with stars <a href="/wiki/Lakeith_Stanfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Lakeith Stanfield">Lakeith Stanfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tessa_Thompson" title="Tessa Thompson">Tessa Thompson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steven_Yeun" title="Steven Yeun">Steven Yeun</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The film premiered at the <a href="/wiki/2018_Sundance_Film_Festival" title="2018 Sundance Film Festival">Sundance Film Festival</a> on January 20, 2018, and was theatrically released in the United States on July 6, 2018, by <a href="/wiki/Annapurna_Pictures" title="Annapurna Pictures">Annapurna Pictures</a>. The film received acclaim for its cast and concept, as well as Riley's screenplay and direction.
</p><p>In July 2018, Riley signed a TV script deal with Media Res.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In June 2020, he announced plans for a new seven-episode series entitled <i><a href="/wiki/I%27m_a_Virgo" title="I&#39;m a Virgo">I'm a Virgo</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Jharrel_Jerome" title="Jharrel Jerome">Jharrel Jerome</a>. The show premiered at <a href="/wiki/South_by_Southwest" title="South by Southwest">South by Southwest</a> on March 11, 2023, and was released on <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Prime_Video" title="Amazon Prime Video">Amazon Prime Video</a> on June 23rd 2023.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2021, Riley signed a two-year overall television deal with Media Res.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</p><p>When E-Roc left The Coup in 1994, Riley decided to stop making music in favor of forming an organization called <b>The Young Comrades</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> with a few other radical, black community organizers including journalist and activist JR Valrey. The organization mounted a few important campaigns in Oakland which yielded some minor victories, such as the campaign against Oakland's "no cruising" ordinance.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2000, Riley, through his workshop on Art and Organizing at <a href="/wiki/La_Pe%C3%B1a_Cultural_Center" title="La Peña Cultural Center">La Peña Cultural Center</a>, led a group of young artists to create "Guerilla Hip-Hop Concerts" on a flatbed truck which traveled throughout Oakland to protest California's <a href="/wiki/Proposition_21" class="mw-redirect" title="Proposition 21">Proposition 21</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The workshop also distributed tens of thousands free cassettes of "The Rumble", which he called "newspapers on tape.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, Riley taught a daily high school class, "Culture and Resistance: Persuasive Lyric Writing", at the School of Social Justice and Community Development in East Oakland.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the fall of 2011, Riley became heavily involved with the <a href="/wiki/Occupy_Oakland" title="Occupy Oakland">Occupy Oakland</a> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> In 2018, he spoke at the Socialism 2018 conference.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/34th_Independent_Spirit_Awards" title="34th Independent Spirit Awards">34th Independent Spirit Awards</a> in 2019, Riley criticized U.S. involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_presidential_crisis" title="Venezuelan presidential crisis">2019 Venezuelan presidential crisis</a> during his acceptance speech for the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Spirit_Award_for_Best_First_Feature" title="Independent Spirit Award for Best First Feature">Best First Feature</a> award for <i>Sorry To Bother You</i>. His speech, which was cut short, was delivered to the press.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2020, Boots announced his support for <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_senators_from_Vermont" title="List of United States senators from Vermont">Vermont Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2020" class="mw-redirect" title="United States presidential election, 2020">2020 United States presidential election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bernie_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bernie-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
