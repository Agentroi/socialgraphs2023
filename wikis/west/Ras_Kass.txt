<p><b>John R. Austin II</b> (born September 26, 1973),<sup id="cite_ref-Yeung_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yeung-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> better known by his stage name <b>Ras Kass</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Rapper" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapper">rapper</a>. He is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> <a href="/wiki/Supergroup_(music)" title="Supergroup (music)">supergroup</a> <a href="/wiki/The_HRSMN" class="mw-redirect" title="The HRSMN">The HRSMN</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Canibus" title="Canibus">Canibus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Killah_Priest" title="Killah Priest">Killah Priest</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a> in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-ambrosiaforheads_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ambrosiaforheads-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He is also a member of the group Golden State Warriors with <a href="/wiki/Xzibit" title="Xzibit">Xzibit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saafir" title="Saafir">Saafir</a>. <a href="/wiki/About.com" class="mw-redirect" title="About.com">About.com</a> ranked him No. 30 on their list of the <i>Top 50 MCs of Our Time (1987–2007)</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_Media" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitchfork Media">Pitchfork Media</a> called him "one of the best rappers of all time."<sup id="cite_ref-pitchfork_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitchfork-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ras_Kass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Austin was born on September 26, 1973<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Carson,_California" title="Carson, California">Carson, California</a>. In 1996 he and <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Teedra_Moses" title="Teedra Moses">Teedra Moses</a> had twin sons, Ras and Taj, both of whom are founding members of the rap group <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coast_Contra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Coast Contra (page does not exist)">Coast Contra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Black_Mafia_Family" title="Black Mafia Family">BMF</a> Entertainment artist Barima "Bleu DaVinci" McKnight is Austin's cousin.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ras_Kass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ras Kass took his stage name from the legendary <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethiopian">Ethiopian</a> Emperor <a href="/wiki/Yohannes_IV" title="Yohannes IV">Yohannes IV</a> whose name was Ras Kassa Mircha before he was crowned as an emperor. In 1991, he made his debut appearance on the single "Trick or Treat" by Wild West Corral alongside B.O.X., Ganjah K, Torche and produced by <a href="/wiki/Battlecat_(record_producer)" title="Battlecat (record producer)">DJ Battlecat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Ras Kass sent waves through the hip-hop world with his debut independent single release "Remain Anonymous," earning him a Hip-Hop Quotable in <i>The Source Magazine</i>. Before his signing with Priority/EMI Records, Ras Kass began making guest appearances on several records and freestyles on numerous radio shows, further solidified the emerging lyricist's notoriety. Recorded guest appearances include Sway &amp; King Tech's "Come Widdit" (feat. Ras Kass, Ahamad and Saafir) (<a href="/wiki/Priority_Records" title="Priority Records">Priority Records</a>) and their "Wake Up Show Anthem '94" (feat. Ras Kass, <a href="/wiki/Nas_(rapper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nas (rapper)">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauryn_Hill" title="Lauryn Hill">Lauryn Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chino_XL" title="Chino XL">Chino XL</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organized_Konfusion" title="Organized Konfusion">Organized Konfusion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saafir" title="Saafir">Saafir</a>), as well as Chino XL's "Riiiot" (<a href="/wiki/American_Recordings_(record_label)" title="American Recordings (record label)">American Recordings</a>), and <a href="/wiki/KeyKool" class="mw-redirect" title="KeyKool">KeyKool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhettmatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhettmatic">Rhettmatic</a>'s "E=MC5" (feat. Ras Kass, LMNO, and Meen Green) <a href="/wiki/Up_Above_Records" title="Up Above Records">Up Above Records</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soul_on_Ice_and_Rasassination"><i>Soul on Ice</i> and <i>Rasassination</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ras_Kass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Soul on Ice and Rasassination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From a young age, Austin was influenced by hip hop music and inspired by a variety of emcees including <a href="/wiki/Ice_Cube" title="Ice Cube">Ice Cube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rakim" title="Rakim">Rakim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scarface_(rapper)" title="Scarface (rapper)">Scarface</a>, and <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">KRS-One</a>. His first album, <i><a href="/wiki/Soul_on_Ice_(album)" title="Soul on Ice (album)">Soul on Ice</a></i>, was released in 1996. Taking its name from a book by <a href="/wiki/Eldridge_Cleaver" title="Eldridge Cleaver">Eldridge Cleaver</a>, Ras addressed racial relations in the same manner, most notably with "Ordo Abchao" and "Nature of the Threat." The album was released on <a href="/wiki/Priority_Records" title="Priority Records">Priority Records</a>, as was the follow-up, <i><a href="/wiki/Rasassination" title="Rasassination">Rasassination</a></i>, which featured beats from <a href="/wiki/Easy_Mo_Bee" title="Easy Mo Bee">Easy Mo Bee</a> and guest appearances by <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">RZA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twista" title="Twista">Twista</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xzibit" title="Xzibit">Xzibit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mack_10" title="Mack 10">Mack 10</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>. Lead single "Ghetto Fabulous" featured Dre and Mack 10 and was pushed by a lavish video shoot. The album received generally positive reviews,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and shortly afterward the MC announced his third album, <i>Van Gogh</i>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Priority_issues.2C_Van_Gogh_and_Goldyn_Chyld"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Priority_issues,_Van_Gogh_and_Goldyn_Chyld">Priority issues, <i>Van Gogh</i> and <i>Goldyn Chyld</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ras_Kass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Priority issues, Van Gogh and Goldyn Chyld">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the recording of Ras's intended third album, <i>Van Gogh</i>, Priority Records merged with <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a>, which acquired the emcee's contract and his material.<sup id="cite_ref-craveonline.com_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-craveonline.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Initially near completion, the album was heavily bootlegged before any single or promotion could be prepared. In fact, the would-be single "Van Gogh" was even played on an episode of <a href="/wiki/Mr._Ruggerio%27s_Neighborhood" title="Mr. Ruggerio&#39;s Neighborhood">The Sopranos</a>. Ras went back to work overhauling the project, procuring tracks from <a href="/wiki/DJ_Premier" title="DJ Premier">DJ Premier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hi-Tek" title="Hi-Tek">Hi-Tek</a>, and Dr. Dre and retaining songs from <a href="/wiki/Rockwilder" title="Rockwilder">Rockwilder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battlecat_(record_producer)" title="Battlecat (record producer)">Battlecat</a>. Tensions arose during the re-recording, from budget restrictions to lack of promotion: Despite the fact that "Van Gogh" remained shelved the album's singles "Back It Up" and "Goldyn Chyld" received a decent number of spins on Los Angeles urban contemporary radio stations KKBT (The Beat) and KPWR (Power 106) during the course of 2001–02.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I would always tell Priority executives, "You give me a road kill cow and pair of scissors but you expect a pair of Air Jordans. It's not fair." Ironically, I would still somehow manage to make a couple pairs.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8202;<cite>Ras Kass</cite></div></blockquote>
<p>Ras Kass was also involved in <a href="/wiki/The_HRSMN" class="mw-redirect" title="The HRSMN">The HRSMN</a>, sometimes called the 4 Hrsmn, consisting of himself, <a href="/wiki/Kurupt" title="Kurupt">Kurupt</a> (of Tha Dogg Pound), <a href="/wiki/Killah_Priest" title="Killah Priest">Killah Priest</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Canibus" title="Canibus">Canibus</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Horsemen_Project" title="The Horsemen Project">The Horsemen Project</a></i>, a white-label of rough tracks by the four, was released in 2003, but no other releases have been subsequently forthcoming. Finally, nearing the completion of his album, the MC and Priority disagreed over the lead single. Originally pushing "Goldyn Chyld," produced by DJ Premier, Priority executives decided to try to release the Dr. Dre-produced "The Whoop" instead, against the wishes of both Ras Kass and Dr. Dre. As he was finishing up <i>Goldyn Chyld</i>, Kass was pulled over in California and arrested for a <a href="/wiki/D.U.I." class="mw-redirect" title="D.U.I.">D.U.I.</a>, marking his third; though sentenced to jail time, he was given an extension. Two weeks before the start of his sentence, Priority informed him of their decision not to put out <i>Goldyn Chyld</i> after all. Becoming a fugitive, Ras attained the masters to his project, recorded some music, and finally turned himself in to police.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> During this time, he also had minor problems with producer <a href="/wiki/The_Alchemist_(producer)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Alchemist (producer)">the Alchemist</a>, who sold Ras a beat that he later re-sold to rapper <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">Jadakiss</a>, which ultimately formed the basis for the track "<a href="/wiki/We_Gonna_Make_It" title="We Gonna Make It">We Gonna Make It</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Incarceration.2C_Institutionalized_I_and_II.2C_Priority_release"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Incarceration,_Institutionalized_I_and_II,_Priority_release">Incarceration, <i>Institutionalized I and II</i>, Priority release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ras_Kass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Incarceration, Institutionalized I and II, Priority release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After serving only 19 months for his D.U.I. charge, Ras Kass recorded the album <i>Institutionalized</i> and began seeking release from his contract with Priority/Capitol Records. Though intended to be an album, it was released as a <a href="/wiki/Mixtape" title="Mixtape">mixtape</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> generating a moderate buzz<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cloutonline.com_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cloutonline.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> despite Capitol's alleged reluctance.<sup id="cite_ref-cloutonline.com_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cloutonline.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He would go on to release two more mixtapes in 2006, <i>Revenge of the Spit</i> and <i>Eat or Die</i>, and got into a fight with former <a href="/wiki/G-Unit" title="G-Unit">G-Unit</a> rapper <a href="/wiki/The_Game_(rapper)" title="The Game (rapper)">The Game</a> over an alleged reference to the rapper's son in a freestyle.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In October 2007, Kass finally succeeded in being released from his record contract.<sup id="cite_ref-craveonline.com_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-craveonline.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, after rumors of a deal with <a href="/wiki/Def_Jam" class="mw-redirect" title="Def Jam">Def Jam</a> or <a href="/wiki/G-Unit_Records" title="G-Unit Records">G-Unit Records</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> he was again incarcerated, this time for violating his parole by flying to the 2007 <a href="/wiki/BET_Awards" title="BET Awards">BET Awards</a>. During this time, he put out the album <i><a href="/wiki/Institutionalized_Vol._2" title="Institutionalized Vol. 2">Institutionalized Volume 2</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Babygrande_Records" title="Babygrande Records">Babygrande Records</a>. After nearly two years of incarceration, Ras Kass was released from jail at the end of May 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ras Kass talks about his arrest and incarceration in the 2011 film <i>Rhyme and Punishment</i>, which documents rap artists who have spent time behind bars.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Quarterly"><i>The Quarterly</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ras_Kass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Quarterly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Featured in HipHopDX's Underground Report, Ras Kass revealed that his next release would be a project called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Quarterly_(album)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Quarterly (album)">The Quarterly</a></i>. Originally intended to be completed within the fourth quarter of 2009, it comprises a song a week released in the fashion of fellow west coast emcee <a href="/wiki/Crooked_I" title="Crooked I">Crooked I</a>'s <i>Hip-Hop Weekly</i> and <a href="/wiki/Freeway_(rapper)" title="Freeway (rapper)">Freeway</a>'s <i>Month of Madness</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> "The Quarterly" was released on November 23, 2009, via www.raskass-central.com and includes nineteen tracks featuring collaborations by Killah Priest, <a href="/wiki/Mistah_F.A.B." title="Mistah F.A.B.">Mistah F.A.B.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Krondon" title="Krondon">Krondon</a> of <a href="/wiki/Strong_Arm_Steady" title="Strong Arm Steady">Strong Arm Steady</a>, with production from <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rock" title="Pete Rock">Pete Rock</a>, Veterano, and others.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A.D.I.D.A.S._Kickstarter_project_and_Save_The_Ras_Kass">A.D.I.D.A.S. Kickstarter project and Save The Ras Kass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ras_Kass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: A.D.I.D.A.S. Kickstarter project and Save The Ras Kass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Ras_Kass-ADIDAS_tour.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Ras_Kass-ADIDAS_tour.jpg/200px-Ras_Kass-ADIDAS_tour.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="256" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Ras_Kass-ADIDAS_tour.jpg/300px-Ras_Kass-ADIDAS_tour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Ras_Kass-ADIDAS_tour.jpg/400px-Ras_Kass-ADIDAS_tour.jpg 2x" data-file-width="588" data-file-height="752" /></a><figcaption>Flyer with the South African dates of the Ras Kass A.D.I.D.A.S. album tour, 2011</figcaption></figure>
<p>In early 2010 Ras Kass launched a Kickstarter campaign to fund the manufacture of 1,000 CDs and 500 records for his <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=A.D.I.D.A.S._(All_Day_I_Dream_About_Spittin)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="A.D.I.D.A.S. (All Day I Dream About Spittin) (page does not exist)">A.D.I.D.A.S. (All Day I Dream About Spittin)</a></i> project and a viral marketing campaign called "Save The Ras Kass", which included a series of satirical web episodes about the plight of the endangered emcee.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In an interview with MTV UK writer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Han_O%27Connor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Han O&#39;Connor (page does not exist)">Han O'Connor</a> he explained his decision to use Kickstarter, stating "we started trying different angles at solving old problems. Kickstarter was the natural evolution of trying creative new business models. When we put out <i><a href="/wiki/The_Quarterly_(album)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Quarterly (album)">The Quarterly</a></i> there were people that said, ‘well how come you didn’t create a CD for this?’ and I'm like ‘well if I have to spend that $5000 and you want one, I appreciate it but there’s the small problem that I spent $5000.’"<sup id="cite_ref-mtv.co.uk_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv.co.uk-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> His webisodes document the downfall of an emcee, as he explained, "You just see this downward spiral; it’s just that fall from grace and I’m kind of making fun of that and using me as the vehicle for it."<sup id="cite_ref-mtv.co.uk_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv.co.uk-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Finally, Ras Kass exceeded the $3,800 needed for the record in less than five days,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> with the project being released as a <a href="/wiki/Limited_edition" class="mw-redirect" title="Limited edition">limited edition</a> double CD and LP on July 20, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barmageddon"><i>Barmageddon</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ras_Kass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Barmageddon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2012 Ras Kass stated that he had changed the name of his upcoming LP from <i>P.U.M.A. (Pushin Underground Music Always)</i> to <i>Barmageddon</i>. It was released on February 12, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-hiphopdx_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiphopdx-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <i>Barmageddon</i> includes features from <a href="/wiki/Kendrick_Lamar" title="Kendrick Lamar">Kendrick Lamar</a>, <a href="/wiki/MK_Asante" class="mw-redirect" title="MK Asante">MK Asante</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Lamont" title="Bishop Lamont">Bishop Lamont</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">Ice-T</a>, <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">Too Short</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Talib_Kweli" title="Talib Kweli">Talib Kweli</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soul_on_Ice_2"><i>Soul on Ice 2</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ras_Kass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Soul on Ice 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During an interview with Sway Calloway, Ras Kass announced that he is working on a sequel to his debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Soul_on_Ice_(album)" title="Soul on Ice (album)">Soul on Ice</a></i>, called <i>Soul on Ice 2</i>. The album was released in September 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_Contributions">Political Contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ras_Kass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Political Contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On July 28, 2020, Ras Kass teamed up with DJ Free Leonard to advocate for the release of Political Prisoner Leonard Peltier, by dropping the single "Freedom", that world premiered on the <a href="/wiki/DJ_Kay_Slay" title="DJ Kay Slay">DJ Kay Slay</a> "Straight Stuntin" Show on XM Sirius Shade 45 Radio.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
