<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>

<p><b>Jonathan Moore</b> (April 21, 1969 – March 8, 2017), also known by his stage name, <b>Wordsayer</b>, was a rapper, DJ and producer born in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle, Washington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Known as Seattle's "hip-hop ambassador" and "cultural mayor", Moore was influential in the Northwest hip-hop scene and founded the group <a href="/wiki/Source_of_Labor" title="Source of Labor">Source of Labor</a> in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:5_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Moore_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moore grew up in the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_City,_Seattle" title="Columbia City, Seattle">Columbia City</a> neighborhood of Seattle and attended <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_High_School_(Seattle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Roosevelt High School (Seattle)">Roosevelt High School</a>. After he graduated in 1987, he moved to Atlanta, Georgia to attend Morehouse College.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Moore worked at Sevananda, a natural grocery store while he attended <a href="/wiki/Morehouse_College" title="Morehouse College">Morehouse College</a>. When he moved back to Seattle after college in Atlanta, he rented a house in the Central District with his brother, designer Upendo Tookas (a.k.a. Negus I), and friend, DJ Kamikaze, who also changed the hip-hop game in Seattle.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Moore_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moore, Tookas and Kamikaze began performing at <a href="/wiki/Langston_Hughes_Performing_Arts_Center" title="Langston Hughes Performing Arts Center">Langston Hughes Performing Arts Center</a> in the Central District as Source of Labor (SOL) in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>SOL (with Derrick Brown (a.k.a. Vitamin D) replacing Kamikaze in 1997), began the music label, Jasiri Media Group, although Jasiri began as a collective in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Also in '93 more popular venues and clubs downtown were booked with <a href="/wiki/Grunge" title="Grunge">grunge</a> shows and <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangster rap</a> was big at that time, making it a challenge for SOL to gain footing.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> As Wordsayer, Moore was known for his politically conscious verse and life.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> SOL and Jasiri were the center of Seattle's second wave of hip hop in the early 90s, the first wave being Nastymix Recording artist <a href="/wiki/Sir_Mix-a-Lot" title="Sir Mix-a-Lot">Sir-Mix-a-Lot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Moore is credited with bringing hip-hop into the mainstream in Seattle and bringing notoriety to the city's hip hop scene.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Not only did he move hip hop into downtown Seattle, but to help sustain his effort, he also began booking and promoting other nationally notable acts for the same downtown clubs. He brought such artists as <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a> in 1995, <a href="/wiki/Blackalicious" title="Blackalicious">Blackalicious</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saul_Williams" title="Saul Williams">Saul Williams</a> (for whom Moore managed a national tour in 2001).<sup id="cite_ref-:4_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Moore also fought for all-ages venues in Seattle.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Teen Dance Ordinance of the early 1990s required a venue have one million dollar liability insurance and hire two off duty police officers in order to put on a show, making it nearly impossible for under aged kids to see music live. Moore was part of the movement to overturn that law in part by hosting <i>Sure Shot Sundays</i> at a local café/laundromat where <a href="/wiki/Macklemore" title="Macklemore">Macklemore</a> first performed at age 15.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Moore met <a href="/wiki/I_Self_Devine" title="I Self Devine">Chaka Mkali</a> while visiting his alma mater and connected him to the scrappy and burgeoning Seattle hip-hop scene which led to national attention and touring groups for the city.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 2000 Source of Labor played a show with <a href="/wiki/Mos_Def" title="Mos Def">Mos Def</a> and local groups, Black Anger and Beyond Reality for <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr." title="Martin Luther King Jr.">Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.</a>'s birthday at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>'s Hub Ballroom co-hosted by Student Hip-Hop Organization of Washington (the SHOW).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Source of Labor disbanded in 2004 to focus on individual projects. Moore continued managing other emerging artists to provide them what he missed when he was new to the scene.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He managed many third wave Seattle hip hop groups such as <a href="/wiki/THEESatisfaction" title="THEESatisfaction">THEESatisfaction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shabazz_Palaces" title="Shabazz Palaces">Shabazz Palaces</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/KUBE_(FM)" class="mw-redirect" title="KUBE (FM)">KUBE 93</a>, Seattles mainstream pop station featured Moore and DJ Hyphen co-hosting <i>Sunday Night Sound Sessions.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></i><sup id="cite_ref-:3_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Moore worked until the end of his life. He was working on the video for rapper <a href="/wiki/Brother_Ali" title="Brother Ali">Brother Ali</a>'s "Own Light (What Hearts Are For)" in which Moore's sister, Jen, dances in celebration of his life (filmed the day after he passed away). The video is dedicated in his memory.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Moore_(musician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moore taught creative writing at <a href="/wiki/Franklin_High_School_(Seattle)" title="Franklin High School (Seattle)">Franklin High School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Upendo Moore, his oldest son, whom he had with his ex partner in life and music, Erika Kylea White (MC Kylea of Beyond Reality), began following in his parents' musical footsteps at 18 months old, playing the drums, and at 4, onstage with the Roots.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> His younger son, Miles, he had with his ex partner, Caitlin Brower.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> 
Jonathan Moore died on March 8, 2017, of kidney failure at age 47.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
