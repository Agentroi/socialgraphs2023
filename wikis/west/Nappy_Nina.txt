					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">American rapper</div>
<p><b>Nappy Nina</b> is an American rapper and producer. Raised in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a>, she performed <a href="/wiki/Poetry_slam" title="Poetry slam">slam poetry</a> as an emcee in her adolescence. She moved to Brooklyn, New York in 2012 and has released the albums <i>Naptime</i>, <i>Extra Ordinary</i>, <i>The Tree Act</i>, and <i>Dumb Doubt</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-grammylookingglass_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammylookingglass-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nappy_Nina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a>, California, she spent part of her childhood there before moving to nearby <a href="/wiki/Union_City,_California" title="Union City, California">Union City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-amadeus_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amadeus-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Her father, Greg Bridges, is a popular local journalist and jazz radio host.<sup id="cite_ref-amadeus_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amadeus-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Because of her father's work in music she spent time around local musicians like Eesu Orundide and <a href="/wiki/D%27wayne_Wiggins" class="mw-redirect" title="D&#39;wayne Wiggins">D'wayne Wiggins</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tony!_Toni!_Ton%C3%A9!" title="Tony! Toni! Toné!">Tony! Toni! Toné!</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-amadeus_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amadeus-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Bridges played several different instruments growing up, which included piano and jazz clarinet.
</p><p>As a teenager, she got involved in slam poetry through Youth Works and rapped in her slam performances.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 2012, Nappy Nina moved to <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> with $500 to pursue music.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> She aspired to become one of the few nationally-recognized rappers from Oakland.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nappy_Nina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nappy Nina released her debut EP <i>Naptime</i> in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Her next EP <i>Extra Ordinary</i> was released in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-bitch2017_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bitch2017-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In a positive review, Jamara Mychelle Wakefield wrote for <i><a href="/wiki/Bitch_(magazine)" title="Bitch (magazine)">Bitch</a></i>: "<i>Extra Ordinary</i> is a quality manifesto from a new artist who wanted to prove herself as a “capable and worthy mc.” Nappy Nina’s debut EP floats in the air between Oakland and Brooklyn—refined like <a href="/wiki/Coconut_oil" title="Coconut oil">coconut oil</a>, but also rough and biting."<sup id="cite_ref-bitch2017_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bitch2017-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> José Vadi of <i>Amadeus Magazine</i> referred to it as "a project as diverse in sonic structure and cadence as it is in content matter and lyrical depth."<sup id="cite_ref-amadeus_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amadeus-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nappy Nina's rhymes frequently cover political and social issues, as well as her perspectives as a Black queer woman.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Her delivery was described as "smooth-sultry...that's almost jazz-like" by Pendarvis Harshaw of <a href="/wiki/KQED_(TV)" title="KQED (TV)">KQED</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Her album <i>The Tree Act</i> (2019) was reviewed as combining a "righteous pen game with ace production" by <a href="/wiki/Okayplayer" title="Okayplayer">Okayplayer</a>'s Karas Lamb.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2020 she released the album <i>Dumb Doubt,</i> which Taylor Crumpton of <a href="/wiki/Bandcamp" title="Bandcamp">Bandcamp</a> Daily said "marries airy, breathable beats with soulful jazz and Black radical tradition themes."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> That year she was the featured artist on the track "Money Can't Buy" on <a href="/wiki/Yaeji" title="Yaeji">Yaeji</a>'s debut mixtape <i>What We Drew</i> 우리가 그려왔던.<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> She also produced the track "Weight" with <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Summer" title="Mexican Summer">Mexican Summer</a>'s Looking Glass digital music series for Brooklyn-based artists affected by the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic" title="COVID-19 pandemic">COVID-19 pandemic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-grammylookingglass_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammylookingglass-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 1, 2021, Nappy Nina released the track "Pandemy Stimmy" with Stas Thee Boss. Nina plans to release an EP titled <i>Double Down</i> in 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> On March 5, 2021, she released the single "The Real Tea" featuring Stas Thee Boss, which Philippe Roberts of Pitchfork reviewed as "formed of equal parts fear and fury, “The Real Tea” finds balance in contradiction, airing grievances with an eye on the future."<sup id="cite_ref-pitchfork-therealtea_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitchfork-therealtea-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nappy_Nina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nappy Nina is queer.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
